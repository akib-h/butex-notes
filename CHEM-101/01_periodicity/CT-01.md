1. Mendeleev’s Periodic Law and Periodic Table

* Mendeleev’s Periodic Law:
  Dmitri Mendeleev formulated his law in 1869, stating:

  > "The physical and chemical properties of the elements are a periodic function of their atomic weights."

  This means that if elements are arranged in order of increasing atomic mass, elements with similar properties recur at regular intervals.

* Mendeleev’s Periodic Table:
  * Arrangement: He arranged the 63 known elements at the time into horizontal rows (periods) and vertical columns (groups) based on atomic mass.
  * Gaps for unknown elements: A notable feature of his table was that he left gaps for elements that had not yet been discovered (e.g., "eka-silicon", later identified as germanium). He predicted properties of those undiscovered elements and estimated their atomic masses.
  * Correction of atomic masses: He corrected the atomic masses of several elements (like beryllium and indium) based on their positions in the table.

2. Short Note on Ionization Potential & Electron Affinity

* Ionization Potential (IP) / Ionization Energy (IE):
  * Definition: The minimum amount of energy required to remove the most loosely bound electron from an isolated gaseous atom to form a cation.
  * Trend: Generally increases across a period (left to right) due to decreasing atomic size and increasing effective nuclear charge. It decreases down a group (top to bottom) due to increased shielding and larger atomic size.
  * Example: Helium has the highest IP among the elements because it is small and tightly bound; alkali metals (Group 1) have low IPs.

* Electron Affinity (EA):
  * Definition: The amount of energy released (usually) when an electron is added to a neutral gaseous atom to form an anion.
  * Trend: Generally becomes more negative (more exothermic) across a period as atoms gain electrons to approach a filled shell (halogens have high EA). It becomes less negative down a group.
  * Note: Noble gases have positive or less negative electron affinities because they already have filled shells and resist gaining electrons.

3. Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle & Aufbau Principle

It is impossible to determine both the exact position (Δx) and exact momentum (Δp)
of a microscopic particle (like an electron) simultaneously with arbitrary precision.

**Formula:**

Δx · Δp ≥ h / 4π

**Significance:**
- Rules out fixed circular orbits (Bohr model)
- Introduces orbitals as regions of probability

* Aufbau Principle:
  ('Aufbau' is German for "building up".)
  * Rule: In the ground state of an atom, electrons fill atomic orbitals of the lowest available energy before occupying higher-energy orbitals.
  * Order of filling (approximate, follows the (n + l) rule):
    1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → ...
  (Note: Exceptions occur for some transition elements due to electron-electron interactions and small energy differences between orbitals.)

4. When n = 4, Calculate the Other Quantum Numbers

Given the principal quantum number $n = 4$.

* Azimuthal (angular momentum) quantum number, $l$:
  * Values range from 0 to $n-1$.
  * For $n = 4$, $l$ can be: 0, 1, 2, 3.
    * $l = 0$ → 4s orbital
    * $l = 1$ → 4p orbitals
    * $l = 2$ → 4d orbitals
    * $l = 3$ → 4f orbitals

* Magnetic quantum number, $m_l$:
  * Values range from $-l$ to $+l$ in integer steps.
  * If $l = 0$: $m_l = 0$ (1 orbital)
  * If $l = 1$: $m_l = -1, 0, +1$ (3 orbitals)
  * If $l = 2$: $m_l = -2, -1, 0, +1, +2$ (5 orbitals)
  * If $l = 3$: $m_l = -3, -2, -1, 0, +1, +2, +3$ (7 orbitals)
  * Total number of orbitals for $n = 4$: $n^2 = 4^2 = 16$ orbitals.

* Spin quantum number, $m_s$:
  * For every orbital there are two possible spin values: $+\tfrac{1}{2}$ and $-\tfrac{1}{2}$.
  * Maximum number of electrons for a shell: $2n^2 = 2(4)^2 = 32$ electrons.

5. Discuss the Radius of Fe, Fe²⁺, Fe³⁺

This compares the ionic/atomic radii of iron in different oxidation states.

* Order of size: Fe > Fe²⁺ > Fe³⁺

* Explanation:
  * Nuclear charge: All three species have the same number of protons ($Z = 26$), so the attractive force from the nucleus is the same in all cases.
  * Number of electrons:
    * Neutral Fe: 26 electrons.
    * Fe²⁺: 24 electrons (lost 2).
    * Fe³⁺: 23 electrons (lost 3).
  * Effective nuclear charge ($Z_\text{eff}$): As electrons are removed, the remaining electrons experience a greater average attraction from the nucleus (reduced shielding and fewer electron–electron repulsions). This increases $Z_\text{eff}$ on a per-electron basis and pulls the electron cloud closer to the nucleus.
  * Result: Higher positive charge (fewer electrons) leads to a smaller radius. Thus Fe³⁺ (most positively charged) is smallest, and neutral Fe is largest.
