1. Mendeleev’s Periodic Law and Periodic Table
 * Mendeleev’s Periodic Law:
   Dmitri Mendeleev formulated his law in 1869, stating:
   > "The physical and chemical properties of elements are a periodic function of their atomic weights."
   > This means that if elements are arranged in order of increasing atomic mass, elements with similar properties will repeat at regular intervals.
   > 
 * Mendeleev’s Periodic Table:
   * Arrangement: He arranged the 63 known elements at the time into horizontal rows (periods) and vertical columns (groups) based on atomic mass.
   * Gaps for Unknown Elements: A genius feature of his table was that he left gaps for elements that had not yet been discovered (e.g., Eka-silicon, which later turned out to be Germanium). He predicted their properties with high accuracy.
   * Correction of Atomic Masses: He corrected the atomic masses of several elements (like Beryllium and Indium) based on their positions in the table.
2. Short Note on Ionization Potential & Electron Affinity
 * Ionization Potential (IP) / Ionization Energy (IE):
   * Definition: The minimum amount of energy required to remove the most loosely bound electron from an isolated gaseous atom to form a cation.
   * Trend: generally increases across a period (left to right) due to smaller atomic size and higher nuclear charge. It decreases down a group (top to bottom) due to increased shielding and atomic size.
   * Example: Helium has the highest IP because it is small and stable; Alkali metals (Group 1) have low IPs.
 * Electron Affinity (EA):
   * Definition: The amount of energy released (usually) when an electron is added to a neutral gaseous atom to form an anion.
   * Trend: It generally becomes more negative (more exothermic) across a period as atoms want to gain electrons to fill their shells (Halogens have high EA). It becomes less negative down a group.
   * Note: Noble gases have positive electron affinity because they are already stable and resist adding electrons.
3. Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle & Aufbau Principle
 * Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle:
   Proposed by Werner Heisenberg, it states that it is impossible to determine both the exact position (\Delta x) and exact momentum (\Delta p) of a microscopic particle (like an electron) simultaneously with absolute accuracy.
   * Formula: \Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \frac{h}{4\pi}
   * Significance: This principle ruled out the idea of fixed circular orbits (Bohr model) and introduced the concept of "orbitals" (regions of probability).
 * Aufbau Principle:
   The word 'Aufbau' is German for "building up."
   * Rule: In the ground state of an atom, electrons fill atomic orbitals of the lowest available energy levels before occupying higher levels.
   * Order of Filling: The energy follows the (n + l) rule. The filling order is:
     1s \rightarrow 2s \rightarrow 2p \rightarrow 3s \rightarrow 3p \rightarrow 4s \rightarrow 3d \rightarrow 4p \dots
4. When n = 4, Calculate the Other Quantum Numbers
Given the Principal Quantum Number (n) = 4.
 * Azimuthal Quantum Number (l):
   * Values range from 0 to (n - 1).
   * For n=4, l can be: 0, 1, 2, 3.
     * l=0 (4s orbital)
     * l=1 (4p orbitals)
     * l=2 (4d orbitals)
     * l=3 (4f orbitals)
 * Magnetic Quantum Number (m_l):
   * Values range from -l to +l.
   * If l=0: m_l = \mathbf{0} (1 orbital)
   * If l=1: m_l = \mathbf{-1, 0, +1} (3 orbitals)
   * If l=2: m_l = \mathbf{-2, -1, 0, +1, +2} (5 orbitals)
   * If l=3: m_l = \mathbf{-3, -2, -1, 0, +1, +2, +3} (7 orbitals)
   * Total Orbitals: n^2 = 4^2 = 16 orbitals.
 * Spin Quantum Number (m_s):
   * For every orbital, there are two possible spin values: +\frac{1}{2} and -\frac{1}{2}.
   * Total Electrons: Maximum capacity is 2n^2 = 2(4)^2 = 32 electrons.
5. Discuss the Radius of Fe, Fe²⁺, Fe³⁺
This question asks for the comparison of ionic radii for Iron (Fe) in different oxidation states.
 * Order of Size: \text{Fe} > \text{Fe}^{2+} > \text{Fe}^{3+}
 * Explanation:
   * Nuclear Charge: In all three species, the number of protons (nuclear charge) is the same (Atomic number Z = 26). They all pull on the electrons with the same force from the nucleus.
   * Number of Electrons:
     * Neutral Fe: 26 electrons.
     * Fe²⁺: 24 electrons (lost 2).
     * Fe³⁺: 23 electrons (lost 3).
   * Effective Nuclear Charge (Z_{\text{eff}}): As electrons are removed, the remaining electrons experience a stronger attraction to the nucleus because the shielding effect decreases (less repulsion between electrons).
   * Conclusion: The nucleus pulls the remaining 23 electrons in Fe³⁺ much tighter than the 26 electrons in neutral Fe. Therefore, the cation with the higher positive charge is always smaller.
