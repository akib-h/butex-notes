1. Mendeleev’s Periodic Law and Periodic Table

* Mendeleev’s Periodic Law:
  Dmitri Mendeleev formulated his law in 1869, stating:

  > "The physical and chemical properties of the elements are a periodic function of their atomic weights."

  This means that if elements are arranged in order of increasing atomic mass, elements with similar properties recur at regular intervals.

* Mendeleev’s Periodic Table:
  * Arrangement: He arranged the 63 known elements at the time into horizontal rows (periods) and vertical columns (groups) based on atomic mass.
  * Gaps for unknown elements: A notable feature of his table was that he left gaps for elements that had not yet been discovered (e.g., "eka-silicon", later identified as germanium). He predicted properties of those undiscovered elements and estimated their atomic masses.
  * Correction of atomic masses: He corrected the atomic masses of several elements (like beryllium and indium) based on their positions in the table.

2. Short Note on Ionization Potential & Electron Affinity

* Ionization Potential (IP) / Ionization Energy (IE):
  * Definition: The minimum amount of energy required to remove the most loosely bound electron from an isolated gaseous atom to form a cation.
  * Trend: Generally increases across a period (left to right) due to decreasing atomic size and increasing effective nuclear charge. It decreases down a group (top to bottom) due to increased shielding and larger atomic size.
  * Example: Helium has the highest IP among the elements because it is small and tightly bound; alkali metals (Group 1) have low IPs.

* Electron Affinity (EA):
  * Definition: The amount of energy released (usually) when an electron is added to a neutral gaseous atom to form an anion.
  * Trend: Generally becomes more negative (more exothermic) across a period as atoms gain electrons to approach a filled shell (halogens have high EA). It becomes less negative down a group.
  * Note: Noble gases have positive or less negative electron affinities because they already have filled shells and resist gaining electrons.

3. Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle & Aufbau Principle

It is impossible to determine both the exact position (Δx) and exact momentum (Δp)
of a microscopic particle (like an electron) simultaneously with arbitrary precision.

**Formula:**

Δx · Δp ≥ h / 4π

**Significance:**
- Rules out fixed circular orbits (Bohr model)
- Introduces orbitals as regions of probability

* Aufbau Principle:
  ('Aufbau' is German for "building up".)
  * Rule: In the ground state of an atom, electrons fill atomic orbitals of the lowest available energy before occupying higher-energy orbitals.
  * Order of filling (approximate, follows the (n + l) rule):
    1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → ...
  (Note: Exceptions occur for some transition elements due to electron-electron interactions and small energy differences between orbitals.)

4. When n = 4, Calculate the Other Quantum Numbers

Given the principal quantum number $n = 4$.

* Azimuthal (angular momentum) quantum number, $l$:
  * Values range from 0 to $n-1$.
  * For $n = 4$, $l$ can be: 0, 1, 2, 3.
    * $l = 0$ → 4s orbital
    * $l = 1$ → 4p orbitals
    * $l = 2$ → 4d orbitals
    * $l = 3$ → 4f orbitals

* Magnetic quantum number, $m_l$:
  * Values range from $-l$ to $+l$ in integer steps.
  * If $l = 0$: $m_l = 0$ (1 orbital)
  * If $l = 1$: $m_l = -1, 0, +1$ (3 orbitals)
  * If $l = 2$: $m_l = -2, -1, 0, +1, +2$ (5 orbitals)
  * If $l = 3$: $m_l = -3, -2, -1, 0, +1, +2, +3$ (7 orbitals)
  * Total number of orbitals for $n = 4$: $n^2 = 4^2 = 16$ orbitals.

* Spin quantum number, $m_s$:
  * For every orbital there are two possible spin values: $+\tfrac{1}{2}$ and $-\tfrac{1}{2}$.
  * Maximum number of electrons for a shell: $2n^2 = 2(4)^2 = 32$ electrons.

5. Discuss the Radius of Fe, Fe²⁺, Fe³⁺

This compares the ionic/atomic radii of iron in different oxidation states.

* Order of size: Fe > Fe²⁺ > Fe³⁺

* Explanation:
  * Nuclear charge: All three species have the same number of protons ($Z = 26$), so the attractive force from the nucleus is the same in all cases.
  * Number of electrons:
    * Neutral Fe: 26 electrons.
    * Fe²⁺: 24 electrons (lost 2).
    * Fe³⁺: 23 electrons (lost 3).
  * Effective nuclear charge ($Z_\text{eff}$): As electrons are removed, the remaining electrons experience a greater average attraction from the nucleus (reduced shielding and fewer electron–electron repulsions). This increases $Z_\text{eff}$ on a per-electron basis and pulls the electron cloud closer to the nucleus.
  * Result: Higher positive charge (fewer electrons) leads to a smaller radius. Thus Fe³⁺ (most positively charged) is smallest, and neutral Fe is largest.

# Exam Answer: Ionic Radii of Fe, Fe²⁺, and Fe³⁺

## Question: Discuss the radius of Fe, Fe²⁺, and Fe³⁺

## Complete Exam Answer

### Introduction

The atomic/ionic radius of iron species decreases as the oxidation state increases. This trend can be explained by considering the electronic configuration, effective nuclear charge, and electron-electron repulsion in each species.

---

### Radius Comparison

**Order of Radius (from largest to smallest):**

```
Fe > Fe²⁺ > Fe³⁺
```

**Approximate Values:**
- **Fe (metallic radius):** 126 pm (picometers)
- **Fe²⁺ (ionic radius):** 78 pm (high spin, coordination number 6)
- **Fe³⁺ (ionic radius):** 64.5 pm (high spin, coordination number 6)

---

### Detailed Explanation

#### **1. Fe (Neutral Iron Atom)**

**Electronic Configuration:** [Ar] 3d⁶ 4s²

**Radius: ~126 pm**

- Contains **26 electrons** balanced by **26 protons** in the nucleus
- Electrons occupy both 3d and 4s orbitals
- The outermost electrons are in the 4s orbital
- **Largest radius** among the three species because:
  - Maximum number of electrons (26)
  - Maximum electron-electron repulsion
  - Shielding effect from inner electrons reduces effective nuclear charge felt by outer electrons

---

#### **2. Fe²⁺ (Ferrous Ion)**

**Electronic Configuration:** [Ar] 3d⁶

**Radius: ~78 pm**

- Lost **2 electrons** from the 4s orbital
- Now has **24 electrons** but still **26 protons**
- All remaining electrons are in 3d and inner orbitals

**Why smaller than Fe?**

1. **Loss of 4s electrons:** The outermost 4s orbital is now empty, so the outer boundary is now the 3d orbital (closer to nucleus)

2. **Increased effective nuclear charge (Zeff):**
   - Same number of protons (26+) pulling on fewer electrons (24)
   - Each electron feels a stronger attraction to the nucleus
   - Zeff = Z - S (where Z = nuclear charge, S = shielding constant)
   - Fewer electrons means less electron-electron repulsion

3. **Decreased electron-electron repulsion:**
   - With 2 fewer electrons, repulsive forces are reduced
   - Electrons are pulled closer to the nucleus

**Calculation Example:**
```
Zeff (approximate) = 26 - (shielding electrons)
With fewer electrons, Zeff increases, pulling remaining electrons closer
```

---

#### **3. Fe³⁺ (Ferric Ion)**

**Electronic Configuration:** [Ar] 3d⁵

**Radius: ~64.5 pm**

- Lost **3 electrons** (2 from 4s, 1 from 3d)
- Now has **23 electrons** but still **26 protons**

**Why smaller than Fe²⁺?**

1. **Further loss of electron:**
   - One additional electron removed from 3d orbital
   - Even fewer electrons experiencing the same nuclear charge

2. **Maximum effective nuclear charge:**
   - 26 protons attracting only 23 electrons
   - Highest Zeff among the three species
   - Each electron is pulled most strongly toward the nucleus

3. **Minimal electron-electron repulsion:**
   - Least number of electrons
   - Reduced repulsive forces allow greater nuclear attraction

4. **Half-filled d⁵ configuration:**
   - All five 3d orbitals contain one electron (high spin)
   - Electrons are distributed evenly, minimizing repulsion
   - More compact arrangement

**Comparative Zeff:**
```
Fe³⁺ has the highest Zeff → smallest radius
Fe²⁺ has intermediate Zeff → intermediate radius
Fe has the lowest Zeff → largest radius
```

---

### Key Principles Explaining the Trend

#### **1. Effective Nuclear Charge (Zeff)**

As electrons are removed:
- Nuclear charge remains constant (26+)
- Shielding decreases (fewer electrons)
- Each remaining electron feels stronger nuclear attraction
- **Result:** Electrons are pulled closer → radius decreases

**Formula:**
```
Zeff = Z - σ

Where:
Z = atomic number (26 for Fe)
σ = shielding constant (decreases as electrons are removed)
```

#### **2. Electron-Electron Repulsion**

- More electrons = more repulsion = expanded electron cloud
- Fewer electrons = less repulsion = contracted electron cloud

**Trend:**
```
Fe (26e⁻) → Maximum repulsion → Largest radius
Fe²⁺ (24e⁻) → Reduced repulsion → Smaller radius
Fe³⁺ (23e⁻) → Minimum repulsion → Smallest radius
```

#### **3. Isoelectronic Comparison**

Consider isoelectronic species (same number of electrons):
- **Ca²⁺, Sc³⁺, Ti⁴⁺** all have 18 electrons [Ar] configuration
- Radii: Ca²⁺ (100 pm) > Sc³⁺ (74.5 pm) > Ti⁴⁺ (60.5 pm)
- Higher nuclear charge → smaller radius

Similarly for iron:
- Same nuclear charge (26+)
- Fewer electrons → smaller radius

---

### Mathematical Representation

The relationship can be approximately represented as:

```
r ∝ n²/Zeff

Where:
r = ionic radius
n = principal quantum number
Zeff = effective nuclear charge
```

For Fe, Fe²⁺, Fe³⁺:
- **n** remains approximately the same (electrons in 3d region)
- **Zeff increases** as electrons are removed
- Therefore, **r decreases**

---

### Factors Affecting Ionic Radius

#### **Coordination Number Effect**

Ionic radius varies with coordination number:

**For Fe²⁺:**
- CN = 4 (tetrahedral): 63 pm
- CN = 6 (octahedral): 78 pm (high spin), 61 pm (low spin)
- CN = 8: 92 pm

**For Fe³⁺:**
- CN = 4 (tetrahedral): 49 pm
- CN = 6 (octahedral): 64.5 pm (high spin), 55 pm (low spin)
- CN = 8: 78 pm

Higher coordination number → larger radius (more ligands push electrons outward)

#### **Spin State Effect**

**High Spin vs. Low Spin:**

- **High spin** (weak field ligands like H₂O): Larger radius
  - Electrons occupy more orbitals
  - Maximum electron-electron repulsion
  
- **Low spin** (strong field ligands like CN⁻): Smaller radius
  - Electrons pair up in lower energy orbitals
  - More compact arrangement
  - Example: [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻ is low spin, smaller than [Fe(H₂O)₆]²⁺

---

### Practical Implications

#### **1. Crystal Structure**

- Smaller Fe³⁺ fits into smaller interstitial sites in crystal lattices
- Fe²⁺ occupies larger sites
- Example: In magnetite (Fe₃O₄), both Fe²⁺ and Fe³⁺ occupy different positions

#### **2. Hydration Energy**

Smaller ions have higher hydration energy:
```
Hydration Energy: Fe³⁺ > Fe²⁺ > Fe

ΔHhydration (Fe³⁺) ≈ -4430 kJ/mol
ΔHhydration (Fe²⁺) ≈ -1946 kJ/mol
```

Smaller ions attract water molecules more strongly (higher charge density).

#### **3. Reactivity and Acidity**

- Fe³⁺ is more polarizing due to smaller size and higher charge
- [Fe(H₂O)₆]³⁺ is more acidic than [Fe(H₂O)₆]²⁺
- Smaller size = higher charge density = stronger polarization of water

### Conclusion

The ionic radius decreases from Fe to Fe²⁺ to Fe³⁺ due to:

1. **Increased effective nuclear charge** as electrons are removed
2. **Decreased electron-electron repulsion** with fewer electrons
3. **Loss of outer shell electrons**, bringing the outer boundary closer to the nucleus
4. **Same nuclear charge** (26 protons) acting on fewer electrons

This trend is fundamental to understanding the chemical behavior, coordination chemistry, and reactivity of iron in different oxidation states. The smaller, more highly charged Fe³⁺ ion exhibits higher charge density, leading to stronger interactions with ligands, higher hydration energy, and greater polarizing power compared to Fe²⁺ and neutral Fe.
