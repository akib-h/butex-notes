# Properties of Metallic Bonds

## Overview

The unique characteristics of metallic bonding directly explain the physical and chemical properties observed in metals. Understanding these property-structure relationships is crucial for materials science and engineering applications.

## 1. Electrical Conductivity

### Mechanism
- **Delocalized electrons** can move freely when an electric field is applied
- Without field: electrons move randomly in all directions (no net current)
- With field: electrons drift toward positive terminal → net current flow

### Quantitative Description

**Ohm's Law**: V = IR

**Conductivity (σ)**: 
```
σ = neμ
```
Where:
- n = electron density (electrons/m³)
- e = electron charge (1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C)
- μ = electron mobility (m²/V·s)

### Temperature Dependence

For metals:
```
σ(T) = σ₀/(1 + α(T - T₀))
```

- Conductivity **decreases** with increasing temperature
- Reason: increased lattice vibrations scatter electrons
- Opposite to semiconductors!

### Experimental Data

| Metal | Conductivity (σ) at 20°C (S/m) | Resistivity (ρ) (Ω·m) |
|-------|--------------------------------|----------------------|
| Silver (Ag) | 6.30 × 10⁷ | 1.59 × 10⁻⁸ |
| Copper (Cu) | 5.96 × 10⁷ | 1.68 × 10⁻⁸ |
| Gold (Au) | 4.52 × 10⁷ | 2.21 × 10⁻⁸ |
| Aluminum (Al) | 3.77 × 10⁷ | 2.65 × 10⁻⁸ |
| Iron (Fe) | 1.04 × 10⁷ | 9.61 × 10⁻⁸ |

## 2. Thermal Conductivity

### Mechanism
- Heat transferred by both:
  1. **Electron motion** (primary in metals)
  2. **Lattice vibrations** (phonons)

### Wiedemann-Franz Law

```
κ/σT = L = constant
```

Where:
- κ = thermal conductivity (W/m·K)
- σ = electrical conductivity (S/m)
- T = absolute temperature (K)
- L = Lorenz number ≈ 2.44 × 10⁻⁸ W·Ω/K²

This law shows that **good electrical conductors are also good thermal conductors**.

### Thermal Conductivity Values

| Metal | Thermal Conductivity (W/m·K) at 25°C |
|-------|--------------------------------------|
| Silver | 429 |
| Copper | 401 |
| Gold | 318 |
| Aluminum | 237 |
| Iron | 80 |

## 3. Malleability and Ductility

### Definitions
- **Malleability**: Ability to be hammered into thin sheets
- **Ductility**: Ability to be drawn into wires

### Mechanism

```
Before stress:          Under stress:
M⁺  M⁺  M⁺             M⁺  M⁺  M⁺
M⁺  M⁺  M⁺      →      M⁺  M⁺
M⁺  M⁺  M⁺                M⁺  M⁺  M⁺
(electron sea throughout)
```

Key points:
1. Metal layers can **slide past each other**
2. Electron sea **adjusts instantly** to maintain bonding
3. **No directional bonds** to break
4. Local bonds easily broken and reformed

### Quantitative Measures

**Percent Elongation**:
```
% Elongation = [(L_final - L_initial)/L_initial] × 100%
```

**Reduction in Area**:
```
% RA = [(A_initial - A_final)/A_initial] × 100%
```

### Examples
- Gold: Can be beaten into sheets 0.0001 mm thick (most malleable)
- Copper: Can be drawn into wires <0.025 mm diameter
- Aluminum: Used for foil (typical thickness: 0.016 mm)

## 4. Metallic Luster (Shininess)

### Mechanism
1. Light photons strike metal surface
2. Delocalized electrons absorb photon energy
3. Electrons transition to higher energy levels
4. Electrons immediately return to ground state
5. Photons re-emitted (reflected) from surface

### Characteristics
- Metals reflect all visible wavelengths → appear "shiny"
- **Plasma frequency** (ωₚ): frequency above which metal becomes transparent

```
ωₚ = √(ne²/ε₀m)
```

Where:
- n = electron density
- ε₀ = permittivity of free space
- m = electron mass

For most metals, ωₚ is in UV range → metals opaque to visible light.

## 5. Melting and Boiling Points

### Trends and Explanation

**General Trend**: Melting points correlate with bond strength

#### Across a Period (Example: Period 3)
| Element | Na | Mg | Al |
|---------|----|----|-----|
| Valence e⁻ | 1 | 2 | 3 |
| m.p. (°C) | 98 | 650 | 660 |

Reason: More valence electrons → stronger metallic bonding

#### Transition Metals (Groups 6-9)
- Have **highest melting points**
- Example: Tungsten (W): 3422°C
- Reason: Optimal balance of bonding/antibonding orbital occupation

### Quantitative Relationship

```
ΔH_fusion ∝ (number of valence electrons) × (1/atomic radius)
```

### Examples with High Melting Points

| Metal | Melting Point (°C) | Reason |
|-------|-------------------|---------|
| Tungsten (W) | 3422 | Many d-electrons, small atomic radius |
| Chromium (Cr) | 1907 | 6 valence electrons |
| Iron (Fe) | 1538 | 8 valence electrons |
| Osmium (Os) | 3033 | Densest element, many valence electrons |

### Examples with Low Melting Points

| Metal | Melting Point (°C) | Reason |
|-------|-------------------|---------|
| Mercury (Hg) | -39 | Filled d-subshell, weak bonding |
| Cesium (Cs) | 28 | Large atomic radius, 1 valence electron |
| Gallium (Ga) | 30 | Unique crystal structure |

## 6. Mechanical Strength and Hardness

### Definitions
- **Tensile Strength**: Maximum stress before breaking
- **Hardness**: Resistance to deformation
- **Young's Modulus**: Measure of stiffness

### Factors Affecting Strength

1. **Bond Strength**
   - More valence electrons → stronger
   - Smaller atomic radius → stronger

2. **Crystal Structure**
   - FCC (Face-Centered Cubic): Most ductile
   - BCC (Body-Centered Cubic): Moderate strength
   - HCP (Hexagonal Close-Packed): Less ductile

3. **Defects and Grain Boundaries**
   - Dislocations: weaken material
   - Grain boundaries: can strengthen (Hall-Petch effect)

### Hall-Petch Relationship

```
σ_y = σ₀ + kd^(-1/2)
```

Where:
- σ_y = yield strength
- σ₀ = friction stress
- k = Hall-Petch coefficient
- d = average grain size

**Smaller grains → Higher strength**

## 7. Density and Crystal Structure

### Close-Packed Structures

Most metals adopt close-packed structures to maximize bonding:

1. **Face-Centered Cubic (FCC)**
   - Coordination number: 12
   - Packing efficiency: 74%
   - Examples: Cu, Ag, Au, Al, Ni

2. **Hexagonal Close-Packed (HCP)**
   - Coordination number: 12
   - Packing efficiency: 74%
   - Examples: Mg, Zn, Ti, Co

3. **Body-Centered Cubic (BCC)**
   - Coordination number: 8
   - Packing efficiency: 68%
   - Examples: Fe, Cr, W, Na, K

### Density Calculation

```
ρ = (Z × M)/(N_A × a³)
```

Where:
- ρ = density (g/cm³)
- Z = atoms per unit cell
- M = atomic mass (g/mol)
- N_A = Avogadro's number
- a = lattice parameter (cm)

## 8. Alloy Formation

### Definition
**Alloy**: Mixture of two or more metals (or metal with nonmetal) with metallic properties

### Types of Alloys

#### 1. Substitutional Alloys
- Atoms of one metal replace another in lattice
- **Hume-Rothery Rules** for solid solution:
  1. Atomic radii differ by <15%
  2. Similar crystal structures
  3. Similar electronegativity
  4. Same valence

Example: Brass (Cu-Zn), Bronze (Cu-Sn)

#### 2. Interstitial Alloys
- Smaller atoms fit into spaces between larger atoms
- Example: Steel (Fe-C)

### Properties of Alloys vs. Pure Metals

| Property | Pure Metal | Alloy |
|----------|-----------|-------|
| Strength | Lower | Higher |
| Hardness | Lower | Higher |
| Melting point | Sharp | Range |
| Ductility | Higher | Lower |
| Corrosion resistance | Variable | Often improved |

## 9. Chemical Reactivity

### Trends
- Metallic bonding strength affects reactivity
- **Weaker bonding → More reactive**

### Ionization in Solution

Metals dissolve by losing electrons:
```
M(s) → M^(n+)(aq) + ne⁻
```

Ease of ionization depends on:
1. First ionization energy
2. Sublimation energy
3. Hydration enthalpy

### Reactivity Series (Partial)

Most reactive:
- K, Na (Group 1 metals)
- Mg, Al
- Zn, Fe
- Cu, Ag
Least reactive: Au, Pt

## Practice Problems

### Problem 1: Conductivity Calculation

**Question**: A copper wire has a cross-sectional area of 1.0 mm² and length 1.0 m. Calculate the resistance at 20°C. (Resistivity of Cu = 1.68 × 10⁻⁸ Ω·m)

**Solution**:
```
R = ρL/A
R = (1.68 × 10⁻⁸ Ω·m)(1.0 m)/(1.0 × 10⁻⁶ m²)
R = 0.0168 Ω = 16.8 mΩ
```

### Problem 2: Density Calculation

**Question**: Calculate the density of aluminum (FCC structure). Lattice parameter a = 4.05 Å, atomic mass = 26.98 g/mol.

**Solution**:
```
For FCC: Z = 4 atoms/unit cell
ρ = ZM/(N_A × a³)
ρ = (4 × 26.98 g/mol)/[(6.022 × 10²³)(4.05 × 10⁻⁸ cm)³]
ρ = 2.70 g/cm³
```

### Problem 3: Wiedemann-Franz Law

**Question**: A metal at 300 K has electrical conductivity of 5.0 × 10⁷ S/m. Estimate its thermal conductivity.

**Solution**:
```
κ/σT = L = 2.44 × 10⁻⁸ W·Ω/K²
κ = LσT
κ = (2.44 × 10⁻⁸)(5.0 × 10⁷)(300)
κ = 366 W/m·K
```

### 1. Electrical Wiring
- Copper used for conductivity + ductility
- Aluminum for lightweight applications

- Steel (Fe-C alloy) for construction
- Titanium alloys for aerospace

- Copper heat sinks in electronics
- Aluminum radiators

- Gold, silver for jewelry
- Chromium plating for corrosion resistance
