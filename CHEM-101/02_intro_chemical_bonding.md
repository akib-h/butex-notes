# Introduction to Chemical Bonding

## 1.1 Fundamental Concepts

### What is a Chemical Bond?

A **chemical bond** is the attractive force that holds atoms together in molecules or compounds. When atoms combine, they achieve a more stable, lower-energy configuration.

**Definition:** A chemical bond is a lasting attraction between atoms that enables the formation of chemical compounds, resulting from the electrostatic force between oppositely charged particles.

### Energy Considerations

The formation of a chemical bond is fundamentally an energy-driven process:

**Potential Energy Curve:**
The potential energy (PE) of two atoms varies with distance according to:

$$PE = \frac{-A}{r} + \frac{B}{r^n}$$

Where:
- $A$ = attractive term constant
- $B$ = repulsive term constant
- $r$ = internuclear distance
- $n$ = typically 9-12

At the equilibrium bond length ($r_0$), the potential energy is at a minimum, and the system is most stable.

**Mathematical Relationship:**

$$\Delta E_{bond} = E_{products} - E_{reactants} < 0$$

A negative value indicates bond formation is exothermic and spontaneous.

### Bond Strength

**Bond Dissociation Energy (BDE):** The energy required to break one mole of bonds in gaseous molecules.

Example for H₂:
$$H_2(g) \rightarrow 2H(g) \quad \Delta H = +436 \text{ kJ/mol}$$

**General Principle:** 
- Stronger bonds → Higher bond dissociation energy
- Shorter bonds → Stronger bonds (generally)

## 1.2 Historical Development

### Early Theories

1. **Democritus (460-370 BCE):** Proposed atoms with "hooks and spikes" that allowed combination
2. **John Dalton (1808):** Atomic theory - atoms combine in simple ratios
3. **Jöns Jakob Berzelius (1812):** Electrochemical theory of bonding
4. **August Kekulé (1858):** Fixed valence theory for organic compounds
5. **Gilbert N. Lewis (1916):** Electron pair bond theory
6. **Irving Langmuir (1919):** Expanded Lewis theory, coined "covalent bond"

### Modern Understanding

Current bonding theory combines:
- Quantum mechanics
- Electron configuration
- Molecular orbital theory
- Valence bond theory

## 1.3 Types of Chemical Bonds

### Primary Bonds (Strong)

#### 1. Ionic Bonds
- **Strength:** 400-4000 kJ/mol
- **Mechanism:** Complete electron transfer
- **Example:** NaCl, MgO, CaF₂
- **Characteristics:** 
  - High melting/boiling points
  - Conduct electricity when molten or dissolved
  - Hard but brittle
  - Formed between metals and nonmetals

**Formation:**
$$Na(g) \rightarrow Na^+(g) + e^- \quad \text{(ionization)}$$
$$Cl(g) + e^- \rightarrow Cl^-(g) \quad \text{(electron gain)}$$
$$Na^+(g) + Cl^-(g) \rightarrow NaCl(s) \quad \text{(lattice formation)}$$

#### 2. Covalent Bonds
- **Strength:** 150-1000 kJ/mol
- **Mechanism:** Electron sharing
- **Example:** H₂, O₂, CH₄, H₂O
- **Types:**
  - Single bond (e.g., H-H)
  - Double bond (e.g., O=O)
  - Triple bond (e.g., N≡N)
- **Characteristics:**
  - Lower melting points than ionic
  - Poor electrical conductors
  - Formed between nonmetals

#### 3. Metallic Bonds
- **Strength:** 100-800 kJ/mol
- **Mechanism:** Delocalized electron sea
- **Example:** Na, Cu, Fe, Al
- **Characteristics:**
  - Good electrical/thermal conductors
  - Malleable and ductile
  - Lustrous
  - Formed between metal atoms

### Secondary Bonds (Weak)

#### Intermolecular Forces
- Van der Waals forces (1-10 kJ/mol)
- Dipole-dipole interactions (5-25 kJ/mol)
- Hydrogen bonds (10-40 kJ/mol)
- London dispersion forces (0.05-40 kJ/mol)

## 1.4 Factors Affecting Bond Formation

### 1. Electronegativity Difference (Δχ)

**Pauling Scale:**
$$\Delta \chi = |\chi_A - \chi_B|$$

**Bond Type Prediction:**
- Δχ < 0.5 → Nonpolar covalent
- 0.5 ≤ Δχ < 1.7 → Polar covalent
- Δχ ≥ 1.7 → Ionic

**Example:**
- H₂: Δχ = 0 (nonpolar covalent)
- HCl: Δχ = 0.9 (polar covalent)
- NaCl: Δχ = 2.1 (ionic)

### 2. Ionization Energy (IE)

Energy required to remove an electron:
$$M(g) \rightarrow M^+(g) + e^- \quad \Delta H = IE$$

Lower IE → More likely to form cations

**Example:**
- Na: IE₁ = 496 kJ/mol (readily forms Na⁺)
- Cl: IE₁ = 1251 kJ/mol (prefers to gain electrons)

### 3. Electron Affinity (EA)

Energy released when an electron is added:
$$X(g) + e^- \rightarrow X^-(g) \quad \Delta H = -EA$$

Higher EA → More likely to form anions

**Example:**
- Cl: EA = -349 kJ/mol (readily forms Cl⁻)
- F: EA = -328 kJ/mol (readily forms F⁻)

### 4. Atomic/Ionic Size

- Smaller atoms → Stronger overlap → Stronger bonds
- Larger atoms → Weaker overlap → Weaker bonds

**Example:**
Bond energies: H-F (568 kJ/mol) > H-Cl (432 kJ/mol) > H-Br (366 kJ/mol) > H-I (298 kJ/mol)

## 1.5 The Octet Rule

**Statement:** Atoms tend to gain, lose, or share electrons to achieve a stable configuration of 8 valence electrons (like noble gases).

**Lewis's Postulate:**
$$\text{Stability} \propto \text{Noble gas configuration}$$

### Examples Following Octet Rule:

1. **Water (H₂O):**
   - O: 6 valence e⁻ + 2 shared = 8 e⁻
   - Each H: 1 e⁻ + 1 shared = 2 e⁻ (duet rule)

2. **Methane (CH₄):**
   - C: 4 valence e⁻ + 4 shared = 8 e⁻
   - Each H: 1 e⁻ + 1 shared = 2 e⁻

3. **Sodium Chloride (NaCl):**
   - Na: Loses 1 e⁻ → Na⁺ (2,8 configuration)
   - Cl: Gains 1 e⁻ → Cl⁻ (2,8,8 configuration)

### Exceptions to Octet Rule:

#### 1. Incomplete Octet
- **BF₃:** Boron has only 6 electrons
- **BeCl₂:** Beryllium has only 4 electrons
- **Reason:** Insufficient electrons to complete octet

#### 2. Expanded Octet (Hypervalency)
- **PCl₅:** Phosphorus has 10 electrons
- **SF₆:** Sulfur has 12 electrons
- **Reason:** Available d-orbitals for bonding (Period 3 and beyond)

#### 3. Odd-Electron Molecules
- **NO:** 11 valence electrons
- **NO₂:** 17 valence electrons
- **ClO₂:** 19 valence electrons

## 1.6 Solved Examples

### Example 1: Energy Released in Bond Formation

**Problem:** Calculate the energy released when one mole of HCl molecules is formed from atoms.

**Given:**
- Bond dissociation energy of HCl = 432 kJ/mol

**Solution:**
$$H(g) + Cl(g) \rightarrow HCl(g)$$
$$\Delta H = -432 \text{ kJ/mol}$$

The negative sign indicates energy is released (exothermic process).

### Example 2: Predicting Bond Type

**Problem:** Predict the bond type in the following compounds:
a) KBr
b) N₂
c) HF

**Solution:**

a) **KBr:**
- χ(K) = 0.8, χ(Br) = 2.8
- Δχ = 2.0
- **Bond type:** Ionic

b) **N₂:**
- χ(N) = 3.0 for both atoms
- Δχ = 0
- **Bond type:** Nonpolar covalent (triple bond)

c) **HF:**
- χ(H) = 2.1, χ(F) = 4.0
- Δχ = 1.9
- **Bond type:** Polar covalent (with significant ionic character)

### Example 3: Stability Comparison

**Problem:** Which molecule is more stable: H₂ or He₂? Justify your answer.

**Solution:**

**H₂:**
- Each H atom has 1 electron
- Total: 2 electrons
- Configuration: (σ1s)²
- Bond order = (2-0)/2 = 1
- **Stable molecule**

**He₂:**
- Each He atom has 2 electrons
- Total: 4 electrons
- Configuration: (σ1s)²(σ*1s)²
- Bond order = (2-2)/2 = 0
- **Unstable molecule** (does not exist)

**Conclusion:** H₂ is stable with bond order 1, while He₂ cannot exist due to zero bond order.

## 1.7 Key Concepts Summary

1. **Chemical bonds form to minimize system energy**
2. **Three primary bond types:** Ionic, Covalent, Metallic
3. **Electronegativity difference determines bond character**
4. **Octet rule guides bonding (with exceptions)**
5. **Bond strength inversely proportional to bond length**
6. **Quantum mechanics provides modern bonding theory**
