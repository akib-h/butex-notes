# 01 — Introduction to Complex Compounds

> **Last Updated:** April 10, 2025
> **Reference:** Hand notes (Sayim, 2023) | LibreTexts | Wikipedia – Coordination Complex

## 1. Definition

A **complex compound** (also called a **co-ordination compound**) is a class of chemical compound in which a central metal atom or ion is surrounded by a set of ions or neutral molecules called **ligands**, which are bonded to the metal via **coordinate (dative) covalent bonds**.

> The word *ligand* is derived from the Latin *ligare*, meaning **"to bind"**.

### Formal Definition

> A coordination compound consists of a central metal atom or ion bonded to one or more ligands. The resulting species can be neutral, positively charged (cation), or negatively charged (anion).

---

## 2. Components of a Complex Compound

A coordination compound has two essential parts:

```
[Co(NH₃)₆]Cl₃
 ───────────   ───
 Coordination  Counter
    Sphere       Ions
```

| Component | Description | Example (in [Co(NH₃)₆]Cl₃) |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------------|
| **Central Metal Atom/Ion** | Transition metal at the centre; acts as a Lewis acid | Co³⁺ |
| **Ligands** | Molecules or ions donating lone pairs; Lewis bases | NH₃ (×6) |
| **Co-ordination Sphere** | Central metal + ligands enclosed in `[ ]` | [Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺ |
| **Counter Ions** | Ions outside the bracket maintaining charge neutrality | Cl⁻ (×3) |

---

## 3. Examples

```
[Fe(NH₃)₆]²⁺        — Iron-hexaammine complex (cationic)
[Co(NH₃)₆]Cl₃       — Hexaamminecobalt(III) chloride (neutral)
[Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻         — Hexacyanoferrate(II) (anionic / complex ion)
[Cu(H₂O)₆]²⁺        — Hexaaquacopper(II) (aqua complex)
[PtCl₂(NH₃)₂]       — Cisplatin / Transplatin (neutral; anticancer drug)
```

## 4. Historical Background

- **Alfred Werner** (Swiss chemist, 1866–1919) was the first to correctly describe the structure of coordination compounds in **1893**.
- He distinguished two types of valency in metal complexes — *primary* (ionisable) and *secondary* (non-ionisable, directional).
- He was awarded the **Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1913** — the first inorganic chemist to receive it.

---

## 5. General Characteristics of Complex Compounds

1. **Charge:** Can be neutral, cationic, or anionic.
2. **Stability in solution:** Unlike double salts, complex ions do **not** dissociate completely in aqueous solution.
3. **Colour:** Many exhibit vivid colours due to d-d electronic transitions (explained by Crystal Field Theory).
4. **Magnetic behaviour:** Some are paramagnetic (unpaired electrons), others diamagnetic (all paired).
5. **Geometry:** Determined by the coordination number and the nature of ligands (octahedral, tetrahedral, square planar, etc.).

---

## 6. Types of Complex Ions by Charge

| Type | Example | Charge |
|------|---------|--------|
| Cationic complex | [Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺ | Positive |
| Anionic complex | [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻ | Negative |
| Neutral complex | [Co(NH₃)₃Cl₃] | Zero |

---

## 7. Biological and Industrial Importance (Overview)

Complex compounds are ubiquitous in nature and industry:

- **Haemoglobin** — Iron complex; carries O₂ in blood
- **Chlorophyll** — Magnesium complex; essential for photosynthesis
- **Vitamin B₁₂** — Cobalt complex; prevents pernicious anaemia
- **Cisplatin** — Platinum complex; anticancer drug
- **Catalysts** — Titanium and aluminium complexes catalyse polymerisation (polyethylene, polypropylene)
- **Electroplating** — Metal complexes used as plating baths

---

## 8. Notation Convention

Complex compounds are written with the coordination sphere inside **square brackets `[ ]`**:

```
[Central Metal(Ligands)]^(charge)   Counter Ion

Example:
[Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺   Cl⁻₃
```

When writing the formula inside the bracket:
1. Write the **metal symbol** first.
2. Then **anionic ligands**, then **neutral ligands** (alphabetical order by convention, IUPAC 2005).
3. The overall charge of the complex is written as a superscript on the closing bracket.
