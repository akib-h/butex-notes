# 02 — Ligands and Their Types

> **Last Updated:** April 10, 2025
> **Reference:** Hand notes (Sayim, 2023) | LibreTexts | Wikipedia

## 1. What is a Ligand?

A **ligand** is a neutral molecule or ion that donates **one or more lone pairs of electrons** to the central metal atom or ion in a complex compound, forming **coordinate (dative) covalent bonds**.

- Ligands act as **Lewis bases** (electron pair donors).
- The central metal acts as a **Lewis acid** (electron pair acceptor).
- The atom in the ligand that directly donates the electron pair is called the **donor atom**.

### Illustration

```
         Ligand
           ↓
[Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺
       ↑
  Central Metal Ion
  (Co³⁺, Z = 27)
```

In `[Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺`, **NH₃** is the ligand. Nitrogen is the donor atom.

---

## 2. Classification of Ligands

Ligands are classified based on the **number of donor atoms** (denticity) and their **structural features**.

---

## 3. Type 1 — By Denticity (Number of Donor Atoms)

### 3.1 Monodentate Ligands

> A ligand that donates **only one lone pair** from a **single donor atom** to the central metal is called a **monodentate ligand** (Greek: *mono* = one, *dent* = tooth).

| Ligand | Formula | Donor Atom |
|--------|---------|------------|
| Water | H₂O | O |
| Ammonia | NH₃ | N |
| Chloride ion | Cl⁻ | Cl |
| Fluoride ion | F⁻ | F |
| Cyanide ion | CN⁻ | C (usually) |
| Carbon monoxide | CO | C |
| Hydroxide ion | OH⁻ | O |

**Example:**
```
[Fe(H₂O)₆]²⁺  — H₂O is monodentate (6 separate O donors)
[Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺  — NH₃ is monodentate (6 separate N donors)
```

---

### 3.2 Bidentate Ligands

> A ligand that donates **two lone pairs** from **two donor atoms** simultaneously to the same central metal is called a **bidentate ligand** (*bi* = two).

| Ligand | Formula | Donor Atoms |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| Oxalate ion | C₂O₄²⁻ | 2 × O |
| Ethylenediamine (en) | H₂N–CH₂–CH₂–NH₂ | 2 × N |
| Acetylacetonate (acac) | CH₃COCHCOCH₃⁻ | 2 × O |
| 2,2'-Bipyridyl (bipy) | (C₅H₄N)₂ | 2 × N |

**Example:**
```
[Co(en)₃]³⁺  — ethylenediamine (en) is bidentate
                Each en occupies 2 coordination sites
                Total coordination number = 6
```

**Ring formation in bidentate:**

```
     CH₂—NH₂
    /         \
Metal          Metal
    \         /
     CH₂—NH₂

Ethylenediamine forms a 5-membered chelate ring
```

---

### 3.3 Polydentate Ligands

> A ligand that donates **more than two lone pairs** from **more than two donor atoms** to the central metal is called a **polydentate ligand**.

The most important polydentate ligand is **EDTA (Ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid)**:

```
Structure of EDTA⁴⁻:

   ⁻OOC–CH₂         CH₂–COO⁻
          \           /
           N–CH₂–CH₂–N
          /           \
   ⁻OOC–CH₂         CH₂–COO⁻

Donor atoms: 2 × N + 4 × O = Hexadentate (6 donor atoms)
```

**Example:**
```
[Co(EDTA)]⁻  — EDTA wraps around Co³⁺ using all 6 donor atoms
               Coordination number of Co = 6
```

| Ligand | Formula | Denticity |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| EDTA | C₁₀H₁₂N₂O₈⁴⁻ | Hexadentate (6) |
| DTPA | — | Octadentate (8) |
| Triethylenetetramine (trien) | — | Tetradentate (4) |

---

## 4. Type 2 — Special Structural Categories (Subtypes of Polydentate)

### 4.1 Chelating Ligands

> A ligand that is **attached to the central atom at two or more points**, forming a **cyclic or ring structure** (chelate ring), is called a **chelating ligand**. The resulting complex is called a **chelate**.

The term comes from the Greek *chele* = claw.

**Key features:**
- Must be bidentate or polydentate to chelate.
- Forms **5- or 6-membered rings** (most stable).
- **Chelate effect:** Chelate complexes are more stable than analogous non-chelate complexes (thermodynamic advantage from entropy).

**Example:**
```
EDTA forming a chelate with Fe³⁺:

          N ——— CH₂ ——— COO⁻
         / \
     Fe³⁺   CH₂
         \ /
          N ——— CH₂ ——— COO⁻

[Fe(EDTA)]⁻  (very stable chelate complex)
```

**Why are chelates more stable? (Entropy Argument)**

When a monodentate ligand is replaced by a bidentate chelate:

```
[M(H₂O)₂]²⁺ + en  →  [M(en)]²⁺ + 2H₂O

Reactants: 2 particles     Products: 3 particles
ΔS > 0  (entropy increases) → ΔG more negative → more stable
```

---

### 4.2 Macrocyclic Ligands

> A ligand that forms a **large cyclic (ring) structure** containing at least **9 atoms in the ring** and having **3 or more donor sites** is called a **macrocyclic ligand**.

The stability gained from using a macrocyclic ligand is called the **macrocyclic effect**, which is even greater than the chelate effect.

**Examples:**

| Ligand | Ring Size | Donor Atoms |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| 18-Crown-6 | 18 atoms | 6 × O |
| 2,2'-Bipyridine (bipy) | — | 2 × N |
| Porphyrin | 20 atoms | 4 × N |
| Phthalocyanine | Large | 4 × N |

**18-Crown-6 structure (schematic):**
```
     O       O
    / \     / \
   /   \   /   \
  O     C-C     O
  |     |  |    |
  O     C-C     O
   \   /   \   /
    \ /     \ /
     O       O

(18 atoms in ring: 6 O + 12 C)
Selectively binds K⁺ ions (matches cavity size)
```

**Biological macrocyclic ligands:**
- **Haem** (porphyrin ring) — binds Fe²⁺ in haemoglobin
- **Chlorophyll** — binds Mg²⁺

---

## 5. Type 3 — Ambidentate Ligands

> Ligands that can donate electron pairs from **two different donor atoms** (i.e., they have more than one potential donor site but bind through only **one donor at a time**) are called **ambidentate ligands**.

> Note: Unlike chelating ligands, ambidentate ligands use only **one** donor site at a time.

| Ligand | Formula | Bonding via N | Bonding via O/S |
|--------|---------|--------------|-----------------|
| Thiocyanate | SCN⁻ | –NCS (N-bonded) | –SCN (S-bonded) |
| Nitrite | NO₂⁻ | –NO₂ (N-bonded, nitro) | –ONO (O-bonded, nitrito) |
| Sulfur dioxide | SO₂ | — | S or O |

**Example — Linkage Isomerism due to Ambidentate ligand:**
```
[Co(NH₃)₅(NO₂)]²⁺  — nitro form (N-bonded)   → yellow
[Co(NH₃)₅(ONO)]²⁺  — nitrito form (O-bonded) → red
```
Both have the same formula but different connectivity → **linkage isomers**.

---

## 6. Summary Table

| Type | Donor Sites | Example | Key Feature |
|------|-------------|---------|-------------|
| Monodentate | 1 | NH₃, H₂O, Cl⁻ | Single bond to metal |
| Bidentate | 2 | en, oxalate (C₂O₄²⁻) | Forms 5- or 6-membered ring |
| Polydentate | >2 | EDTA (6 sites) | Multiple bonds; very stable |
| Chelating | ≥2 (cyclic) | EDTA, en | Ring formation; chelate effect |
| Macrocyclic | ≥3 (large ring) | 18-crown-6, porphyrin | Macrocyclic effect |
| Ambidentate | 1 (at a time) | SCN⁻, NO₂⁻ | Two possible donor atoms |

## 7. Practice Problems
