# 04 — Factors Affecting Complex Compounds

> **Last Updated:** April 10, 2025
> **Reference:** Hand notes (Sayim, 2023) | LibreTexts | Wikipedia

---

## Overview

The stability, structure, and properties of a complex compound are governed by several factors related to:
1. The **nature of the central metal ion**
2. The **nature of the ligand**

These factors are divided into two groups in the syllabus:
- **Factors affecting complex compounds (general)**
- **Effects of ligand on complex compounds (ligand-specific)**

---

## Part A: General Factors Affecting Complex Compounds

### Factor 1 — Size and Charge of the Metal Ion

The central metal ion's **ionic radius** and **charge (oxidation state)** are the two most critical factors:

**Effect of Charge:**
- Higher charge (higher oxidation state) → stronger attraction to ligand electrons → **greater stability**.
- Example:
  ```
  [Fe(CN)₆]³⁻  (Fe³⁺, charge = +3)  is less stable than
  [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻  (Fe²⁺, charge = +2)  ... Wait — actually:
  Fe³⁺ has stronger electrostatic pull → K[Fe³⁺(CN)₆] is kinetically stable.
  ```
  In general: *higher metal charge → stronger M–L bond*.

**Effect of Size:**
- Smaller ions have higher **charge density** → stronger electrostatic attraction to ligand → more stable complex.
- However, very large ions can accommodate **higher coordination numbers**:
  ```
  Al³⁺ (small) → CN = 6
  La³⁺ (large) → CN = 9 or more
  ```

**Summary:**

| Metal Ion Property | Effect on Stability |
|--------------------|---------------------|
| Higher charge | More stable complex |
| Smaller size | Stronger M–L bond |
| Larger size | Higher CN possible |

---

### Factor 2 — Basicity of the Ligands

The **basicity** of a ligand is its tendency to donate electrons (Lewis base strength).

- **Stronger base** (higher electron density on donor atom) → more readily donates electrons → **more stable complex**.
- Basicity follows the ability to donate lone pairs.

**Example:**
```
NH₃ is a stronger base than H₂O
→ [Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺ is more stable than [Co(H₂O)₆]³⁺
```

**Order of basicity (approx. for common ligands):**
```
CN⁻ > NO₂⁻ > en > NH₃ > H₂O > F⁻ > Cl⁻ > Br⁻ > I⁻
(strong field)                              (weak field)
```

---

### Factor 3 — Stability of the Ligand Field

The overall arrangement of ligands around the metal creates a **ligand field** (electrostatic environment). The stability of this field depends on:
- The **symmetry** of the coordination sphere
- The **geometry** of the complex (octahedral > square planar > tetrahedral in general stability)
- The **crystal field splitting energy (Δ)** — discussed in detail in File 07 (CFT)

---

### Factor 4 — Macrocyclic Effect

Macrocyclic ligands (forming large rings around the metal) provide **exceptional stability** due to:

1. **Entropy advantage** (similar to chelate effect but stronger)
2. **Preorganisation** — the ligand cavity is pre-formed to accept the metal ion
3. Greater **kinetic inertness** — harder for other ligands to displace the macrocyclic ligand

**Example:**
```
[Ni(18-crown-6)]²⁺  is far more stable than
[Ni(H₂O)₆]²⁺       even though both are 6-coordinate
```

---

### Factor 5 — Denticity of Ligands

**Denticity** = number of donor atoms per ligand.

Higher denticity → more bonds formed → greater stability:

```
Stability order:
Hexadentate (EDTA) > Tetradentate > Bidentate (en) > Monodentate (NH₃)
```

This is the basis of the **chelate effect** — explained thermodynamically:
- More bonds released during complex formation → greater entropy gain (ΔS > 0)
- More negative ΔG = ΔH − TΔS → more spontaneous formation

**Quantitative example:**
```
[Ni(H₂O)₆]²⁺ + 3en → [Ni(en)₃]²⁺ + 6H₂O

Reactant species: 4   Product species: 7
ΔS > 0  → TΔS term increases → ΔG is more negative
```

---

### Factor 6 — Size and Bulk of Ligands

**Steric effects** of bulky ligands can:
- **Reduce** the coordination number (bulky ligands physically cannot all fit)
- **Destabilise** the complex by steric repulsion between adjacent large ligands

**Example:**
```
Large ligands like P(C₆H₅)₃ (triphenylphosphine) 
→ complexes often have CN = 2 or 4 (not 6)
  because 6 bulky ligands cannot fit around most metals
```

## Part B: Effects of Ligand on Complex Compounds

These factors are specifically about how the **nature of the ligand** influences complex properties:

### Ligand Effect 1 — Nature of the Ligand

- Ligands that can donate via σ-bonds only (σ-donors) form different complexes from those that also participate in π-bonding.
- **π-acceptor ligands** (like CO, CN⁻) can form very stable complexes via **back-bonding** (metal donates electron density back into empty ligand π* orbitals).

```
Metal → Ligand π-back donation:
M: (filled d-orbital) → CN⁻ (empty π* orbital)
This extra bonding makes [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻ very stable.
```

---

### Ligand Effect 2 — Basicity of the Ligand

(Same as Factor 2 above — repeated here as it directly affects ligand field strength)

Stronger Lewis bases produce stronger metal–ligand bonds → more stable complexes.

---

### Ligand Effect 3 — Chelation

The **chelate effect** increases stability:
- Formation of 5- or 6-membered chelate rings is thermodynamically and kinetically favoured.

---

### Ligand Effect 4 — Size of the Chelating Ring

- **5-membered chelate rings** (formed by bidentate ligands with 2-atom chain, e.g., ethylenediamine) are generally most stable.
- **6-membered chelate rings** (e.g., acetylacetonate) are also stable.
- Very large or very small rings are strained and less stable.

```
5-membered ring (en):
    M — N — C — C — N (back to M) → very stable

6-membered ring (acac):
    M — O — C — C — C — O (back to M) → also stable
```

---

### Ligand Effect 5 — Entropy Effect

As discussed under denticity — more dentate ligands → greater entropy gain on complex formation → more negative ΔG.

---

### Ligand Effect 6 — Steric Effect

Bulky substituents on the ligand:
- Create steric hindrance → reduce coordination number
- Can force unusual geometries
- Example: Ligands with large flanking groups force **tetrahedral** over octahedral geometry

---

### Ligand Effect 7 — π-Bonding (Back-Bonding) Effect

Ligands with **empty π*-orbitals** (e.g., CO, CN⁻, NO, phosphines) can accept electron density from filled metal d-orbitals. This is called **π-back bonding** or **retrodative bonding**.

```
         σ donation          π back-donation
  Ligand ─────────► Metal ─────────► Ligand
  (lone pair)              (from d orbital to π*)

Net result: Stronger and more stable M–L bond
            Large crystal field splitting (strong field ligands)
```

**Spectrochemical series** (from weak-field to strong-field):
```
I⁻ < Br⁻ < Cl⁻ < F⁻ < OH⁻ < H₂O < NH₃ < en < NO₂⁻ < CN⁻ ≈ CO
(weak field, small Δ)                              (strong field, large Δ)
```

## Summary Table

| Factor | How It Affects Stability / Properties |
|--------|---------------------------------------|
| Metal charge ↑ | Stability ↑ |
| Metal size ↑ | CN ↑ |
| Ligand basicity ↑ | Stability ↑ |
| Denticity ↑ (chelate effect) | Stability ↑↑ |
| Macrocyclic ligand | Stability ↑↑↑ |
| Larger chelate ring | Stability ↓ |
| Bulkier ligand | CN ↓, may distort geometry |
| π-back bonding ligand (CN⁻, CO) | Very stable, large Δ |
