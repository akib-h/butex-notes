# 06 — Werner's Theory of Coordination Compounds

## 1. Historical Context

**Alfred Werner** (12 December 1866 – 15 November 1919) was a Swiss chemist who in **1893** proposed the first systematic theory to explain the structure and formation of coordination compounds. His theory, known as **Werner's Theory** (or the **Co-ordination Theory**), resolved major inconsistencies in the known formulas and behaviours of these compounds.

- He was awarded the **Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1913** — the first inorganic chemist to receive it.
- He is called the **"Father of Coordination Chemistry"**.

---

## 2. The Core Problem Werner Solved

Before Werner, chemists could not explain why compounds like:

```
CoCl₃ · 6NH₃    (yellow)
CoCl₃ · 5NH₃    (purple)
CoCl₃ · 4NH₃    (green)
CoCl₃ · 3NH₃    (violet)
```
...all had the same empirical composition but behaved so differently (different colours, different numbers of ions in solution, different conductivities). Werner's theory answered this precisely.

## 3. Werner's Postulates

### Postulate 1 — Two Types of Valency

> In co-ordination compounds, metal atoms exhibit **two types of valency**:
> 1. **Primary valency (Hauptvalenz)**
> 2. **Secondary valency (Nebenvalenz)**

---

#### (i) Primary Valency

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Nature | **Ionisable** (dissociates in solution) |
| Directionality | **Non-directional** |
| Satisfied by | **Negative ions only** |
| Represents | **Oxidation state** of the metal |
| Position | Outside the co-ordination sphere |

```
Example in [Co(NH₃)₆]Cl₃:
Primary valency of Co = 3 (Co³⁺)
Satisfied by 3 Cl⁻ ions (outside bracket)
These Cl⁻ ionise freely in solution.
```

---

#### (ii) Secondary Valency

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Nature | **Non-ionisable** (does not dissociate in solution) |
| Directionality | **Directional** (determines geometry) |
| Satisfied by | **Negative ions OR neutral molecules** |
| Represents | **Co-ordination number** of the metal |
| Position | Inside the co-ordination sphere |

```
Example in [Co(NH₃)₆]Cl₃:
Secondary valency of Co = 6 (co-ordination number = 6)
Satisfied by 6 NH₃ molecules (inside bracket)
These NH₃ do NOT ionise.
```

---

### Postulate 2 — Central Atom at the Centre

> Every complex compound has a **central atom** situated at the **centre of the co-ordination sphere**.

---

### Postulate 3 — Co-ordination Number is Essential

> The fulfilment of the **co-ordination number** is essential for complex compound formation.

If there are not enough ligands to satisfy the secondary valency, some ions may fill dual roles (satisfying both primary and secondary valency simultaneously).

---

### Postulate 4 — Secondary Valencies are Directional

> The **secondary valences are directed in space** about the central metal ion. This directionality determines the **geometry** of the complex.

| Secondary Valency (CN) | Geometry |
|------------------------|----------|
| 6 | Octahedral |
| 4 | Tetrahedral or Square Planar |
| 2 | Linear |

```
For [Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺ (CN = 6):
Six NH₃ molecules are arranged octahedrally.

     NH₃
      |
NH₃—Co—NH₃
   /  |  \
NH₃  NH₃  NH₃
(Simplified octahedral representation)
```

---

## 4. Primary vs Secondary Valency — Key Distinctions

| Feature | Primary Valency | Secondary Valency |
|---------|----------------|------------------|
| Also called | Hauptvalenz / Ionisable valency | Nebenvalenz / Non-ionisable valency |
| Modern equivalent | Oxidation number | Co-ordination number |
| Satisfying species | Anions only | Anions OR neutral molecules |
| Ionisation in water | Yes | No |
| Directionality | Non-directional | Directional |
| Position in formula | Outside [ ] | Inside [ ] |

---

## 5. Notes on Primary and Secondary Valency (from notes)

> - **Primary valency = Oxidation number / charge of central atom**
> - **Secondary valency = Co-ordination number** (number of groups attached to central atom)
> - In K₄[Fe(CN)₆]: Secondary valency of Fe = 6, Primary valency = 2 (Fe²⁺)

## 6. Werner's Experimental Proof — The Silver Nitrate Test

Werner added excess AgNO₃ to solutions of the cobalt-ammine chlorides and measured the amount of AgCl precipitate:

```
CoCl₃ · 6NH₃  →  [Co(NH₃)₆]Cl₃    →  3 AgCl ↓  (3 Cl⁻ ionised, outside sphere)
CoCl₃ · 5NH₃  →  [Co(NH₃)₅Cl]Cl₂  →  2 AgCl ↓  (2 Cl⁻ outside, 1 inside sphere)
CoCl₃ · 4NH₃  →  [Co(NH₃)₄Cl₂]Cl  →  1 AgCl ↓  (1 Cl⁻ outside, 2 inside sphere)
CoCl₃ · 3NH₃  →  [Co(NH₃)₃Cl₃]    →  0 AgCl ↓  (0 Cl⁻ outside, all 3 inside sphere)
```

This confirmed that:
- Cl⁻ inside the coordination sphere does **not** ionise (secondary valency).
- Cl⁻ outside the coordination sphere **does** ionise (primary valency).

**Molar conductance also supported this:**

```
Complex                     Ions in solution   Molar Conductance
[Co(NH₃)₆]Cl₃              4 ions (1³⁺ + 3Cl⁻)    Highest
[Co(NH₃)₅Cl]Cl₂            3 ions (1²⁺ + 2Cl⁻)    High
[Co(NH₃)₄Cl₂]Cl            2 ions (1⁺ + 1Cl⁻)     Moderate
[Co(NH₃)₃Cl₃]              0 ions (non-electrolyte) Zero
```

## 7. Worked Example: Types of Valency in `[Co(NH₃)₆]Cl₃`

From notes (2019 exam question):

```
[Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺  (Cl⁻)₃

Structure:
           NH₃ NH₃
            |  /
     NH₃ — Co³⁺ — NH₃
            |  \
           NH₃  NH₃
         [inside bracket]  (Cl⁻)₃
                            [outside bracket]

Primary valency of Co = 3  (= oxidation number; Co³⁺)
  → Satisfied by 3 Cl⁻ (outside)

Secondary valency of Co = 6  (= co-ordination number)
  → Satisfied by 6 NH₃ (inside the coordination sphere)
```

---

## 8. How Werner's Theory Explains Non-ionic Behaviour: CoCl₃ · 3NH₃

From notes:

```
CoCl₃ · 3NH₃  →  [Co(Cl)₃(NH₃)₃]

Here, all 3 Cl⁻ satisfy BOTH primary (ionisable character) AND secondary valency.
→ All Cl⁻ are inside the coordination sphere.
→ No free ions → non-electrolyte → no precipitate with AgNO₃.

The complex behaves as a neutral, non-conducting molecule.
Can be written as [Co(Cl₃)(NH₃)₃].
```

---

## 9. Limitations of Werner's Theory

| # | Limitation |
|---|-----------|
| 1 | **No theoretical reasons** for assuming two types of valency — just empirical |
| 2 | **No explanation** for why specific space configurations (geometry) occur |
| 3 | **No explanation** for the particular co-ordination number adopted by a metal |
| 4 | **No explanation** of how ligands are actually attached to the metal (bond nature) |
| 5 | Cannot explain **colour** of the complex |
| 6 | Cannot explain **magnetic properties** |
| 7 | Cannot explain **optical properties** or spectral data |

---

## 10. Significance of Werner's Theory

Despite its limitations, Werner's theory:
- Introduced the concept of **co-ordination number** and **co-ordination sphere**
- Correctly predicted the **geometries** of complexes (octahedral, square planar, tetrahedral)
- Explained **isomerism** in coordination compounds
- Provided the foundation for modern **coordination chemistry**
- Led to Werner winning the **Nobel Prize in Chemistry (1913)**
