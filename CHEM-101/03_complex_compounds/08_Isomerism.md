## 1. Definition of Isomerism

> **Isomers** are molecules that have the **same chemical formula** (same kinds and numbers of atoms) but **different chemical and/or physical properties** due to different arrangements of atoms.

In coordination chemistry, isomerism arises from:
- Different positions of ligands and counter ions
- Different connectivity of ligands to the metal
- Different spatial arrangements of ligands

---

## 2. Classification of Isomerism

Isomerism in complex compounds is classified into **two major categories**:

```
ISOMERISM IN COMPLEX COMPOUNDS
│
├── A. STRUCTURAL ISOMERISM
│       (same formula, different connectivity)
│       ├── 1. Ionisation Isomerism
│       ├── 2. Hydrate (Solvate) Isomerism
│       ├── 3. Linkage Isomerism
│       ├── 4. Co-ordination Isomerism
│       ├── 7. Co-ordination Position Isomerism
│       └── 8. Polymerisation Isomerism
│
└── B. STEREOISOMERISM
        (same formula, same connectivity, different spatial arrangement)
        ├── 5. Geometrical Isomerism (cis-trans)
        └── 6. Optical Isomerism
```

## 3. Structural Isomerism

### Type 1 — Ionisation Isomerism (2019 exam)

> Complexes that have the **same overall chemical composition** but produce **different ions in solution** are called **ionisation isomers**.

This occurs when an ion that is a **counter ion** (outside the coordination sphere) in one isomer becomes a **ligand** (inside the coordination sphere) in the other.

**Example:**

```
[Co(NH₃)₅Br]SO₄    — Bromopentaamminecobalt(III) sulphate
                      In solution: [Co(NH₃)₅Br]²⁺ and SO₄²⁻

[Co(NH₃)₅SO₄]Br    — Sulphatopentaamminecobalt(III) bromide
                      In solution: [Co(NH₃)₅SO₄]⁺ and Br⁻
```

These are **ionisation isomers**:
- Both have same atoms: 1 Co, 5 NH₃, 1 Br, 1 SO₄
- But Br and SO₄ swap positions (inside ↔ outside the bracket)
- They produce **different ions** in solution

**Test:** Add BaCl₂ → precipitates SO₄²⁻ (if outside bracket);
         Add AgNO₃ → precipitates Br⁻ (if outside bracket).

---

### Type 2 — Hydrate (Solvate) Isomerism (2019 exam)

> Isomerism arising from **different combinations of water molecules** — some as ligands inside the coordination sphere and some as lattice water (or free water) outside.

Also called **solvate isomerism** when other solvents are involved.

**Example:**

```
[Cr(H₂O)₆]Cl₃          — violet     — 6 H₂O inside, 0 outside
[Cr(H₂O)₅Cl]Cl₂ · H₂O  — blue-green — 5 H₂O + 1 Cl inside; 1 H₂O, 2 Cl outside
[Cr(H₂O)₄Cl₂]Cl · 2H₂O — dark green — 4 H₂O + 2 Cl inside; 2 H₂O, 1 Cl outside
```

All three have the same empirical formula **CrCl₃ · 6H₂O**, but differ in the distribution of Cl⁻ and H₂O between the inner and outer spheres.

**Historical note:** These compounds correspond to the old formula CrCl₃ · 6H₂O (Reinecke salts) and were one of the key puzzles solved by Werner.

---

### Type 3 — Linkage Isomerism

> Isomerism arising when an **ambidentate ligand** bonds through **different donor atoms** in two otherwise identical complexes.

Requires an ambidentate ligand (one that has two different potential donor atoms — see File 02).

**Example (from notes):**

```
[Co(NH₃)₅(NO₂)]²⁺  — nitro form (N-bonded, yellow)
                       NO₂⁻ bonds through N

[Co(NH₃)₅(ONO)]²⁺  — nitrito form (O-bonded, red)
                       NO₂⁻ bonds through O
```

Other ambidentate ligands that can give linkage isomers:

| Ligand | Bonding via atom 1 | Bonding via atom 2 |
|--------|--------------------|--------------------|
| SCN⁻ | N → thiocyanato (N-bonded) | S → isothiocyanato (S-bonded) |
| NO₂⁻ | N → nitro | O → nitrito |
| SO₂ | S-bonded | O-bonded |
| CN⁻ | C → cyano | N → isocyano |

---

### Type 4 — Co-ordination Isomerism

> Isomerism in which the **distribution of ligands between two metal centres** differs, while the overall composition of the compound remains the same.

Requires a compound with **two different metal complexes** (both cationic and anionic complexes present).

**Example (from notes):**

```
[Co(NH₃)₆][Cr(CN)₆]  ←→  [Cr(NH₃)₆][Co(CN)₆]
[Cr(NH₃)₆][Co(CN)₆]  ←→  [Cr(CN)₆][Co(NH₃)₆]  (same as above, reordered)
```

Here, NH₃ and CN⁻ swap between Co and Cr centres.

**Another example:**

```
[Cu(NH₃)₄][PtCl₄]  ←→  [Pt(NH₃)₄][CuCl₄]
```

---

### Type 5 — Geometrical (cis-trans) Isomerism (2019 exam)

> Isomerism in which the same ligands are arranged **differently in space** around the central metal — either on the **same side** (cis) or **opposite sides** (trans) of the coordination sphere.

Occurs in:
- **Square planar** complexes of type MA₂B₂ or MA₂BC
- **Octahedral** complexes with appropriate ligand combinations

#### Square Planar Example: `[Pt(NH₃)₂Cl₂]`

```
Cis isomer:                    Trans isomer:
                               
H₃N    Cl                    H₃N    Cl
    \  /                          \  /
     Pt            vs              Pt
    /  \                          /  \
H₃N    Cl                      Cl    NH₃

Both NH₃ on same side      NH₃ on opposite sides
(Cisplatin — anticancer)    (Transplatin — inactive)
```

**Cisplatin** is the famous anticancer drug — only the *cis* isomer is active!

#### Octahedral Example: `[Co(NH₃)₄Cl₂]⁺`

```
Cis isomer:                    Trans isomer:
   NH₃                            NH₃
    |                              |
Cl—Co—NH₃         vs          NH₃—Co—NH₃
   /|                              |
NH₃|                              NH₃
   |                              
   Cl (adjacent to first Cl)   Cl on opposite side from Cl
```

### Type 6 — Optical Isomerism

> Isomerism in which the two isomers are **non-superimposable mirror images** of each other (enantiomers). The molecule and its mirror image cannot be superimposed.

- One isomer rotates plane-polarised light to the **right** (+, d- or dextrorotatory).
- The other rotates it to the **left** (−, l- or laevorotatory).

This occurs in complexes with **no plane of symmetry (chiral centres)**.

**Classic example: `[Co(en)₃]³⁺`**

```
       Cis isomer (Δ form)           Mirror image (Λ form)
           
           en                           en
          / \                          / \
        en   Co   en     ←→          en   Co   en
          \ /                          \ /
           en                           en
           
These are non-superimposable → optical isomers (enantiomers)
```

**Key requirement:** A complex must be **chiral** (no plane of symmetry) to show optical isomerism.

**Common chiral complexes:**
- `[Co(en)₃]³⁺` — tris(ethylenediamine)cobalt(III)
- `[Cr(ox)₃]³⁻` — tris(oxalato)chromate(III)
- Cis-`[Co(en)₂Cl₂]⁺`

**Werner's achievement:** In **1911**, Werner first resolved the cobalt/ammonia complex hexol into optical isomers — proving that chirality is not exclusive to carbon compounds.

---

### Type 7 — Co-ordination Position Isomerism

> Isomerism in which ligands can be **interchanged between metal atoms** in a bridged, dinuclear complex.

**Example (from notes):**

```
[(NH₃)₄Co—NH₂—Co(NH₃)₂Cl₂]²⁺  — asymmetrical form (Unsymmetrical)
                O₂

[(NH₃)₃ClCo—NH₂—Co(NH₃)₃Cl]²⁺  — symmetrical form
                O₂
```

Here the Cl ligand swaps between the two Co centres in a bridged dinuclear system.

---

### Type 8 — Polymerisation Isomerism

> Isomerism in which the **isomers have the same empirical formula** but **different molecular masses** — the repeat units are polymers of each other.

Strictly speaking, these are not true isomers (since they have different molecular masses) but are of the same empirical (simplest) formula.

**Example (from notes):**

```
Empirical formula: [Co(OH)₂CoX₃ · 6NH₃]

Isomers:
[Co(NH₃)₆][Co(OH)₂Cl₄]  (monomer, MW = x)
[Co(NH₃)₄Cl₂]₂[Co(NH₃)₂Cl₄]  (trimer, MW = 3x)
```

More examples:
```
[Pt(NH₃)₂Cl₂]   (n=1)
[Pt(NH₃)₄][PtCl₄]  (n=2, contains two Pt atoms per formula unit)
[Pt(NH₃)₄][Pt(NH₃)Cl₃]₂  (n=3)
```

## 4. Exam Question: Identify Isomers with Reasons (2019)

**Q: Discuss the isomerism exhibited by the following:**

**i) CrCl₃ · 6H₂O:**

```
[Cr(H₂O)₆]Cl₃          — all H₂O inside, all Cl⁻ outside — 3 AgCl ppt
[Cr(H₂O)₅Cl]Cl₂ · H₂O — one Cl inside — 2 AgCl ppt
[Cr(H₂O)₄Cl₂]Cl · 2H₂O — two Cl inside — 1 AgCl ppt
→ Type: HYDRATE ISOMERISM
```

**ii) `[Co(NH₃)₅Br]SO₄` and `[Co(NH₃)₅SO₄]Br`:**

```
Same formula: Co + 5NH₃ + Br + SO₄ + 3 positive charges
But Br and SO₄ swap between inner and outer sphere.
→ Type: IONISATION ISOMERISM
```

**iii) `[Co(H₂O)₆]Cl₃` and `[Co(H₂O)₅Cl]Cl₂ · H₂O`:**

```
→ Type: HYDRATE ISOMERISM
```

**iv) `[Co(NH₃)₅SO₄]Br` and `[Co(NH₃)₅Br]SO₄`:**

```
→ Type: IONISATION ISOMERISM (same pair as ii)
```

## 5. Summary Table

| Type | Key Cause | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| 1. Ionisation | Counter ion ↔ ligand swap | `[Co(NH₃)₅Br]SO₄` vs `[Co(NH₃)₅SO₄]Br` |
| 2. Hydrate | H₂O inside ↔ outside sphere | `[Cr(H₂O)₆]Cl₃` vs `[Cr(H₂O)₅Cl]Cl₂·H₂O` |
| 3. Linkage | Ambidentate ligand binds differently | `[Co(NH₃)₅NO₂]²⁺` vs `[Co(NH₃)₅ONO]²⁺` |
| 4. Co-ordination | Ligands swap between two metals | `[Co(NH₃)₆][Cr(CN)₆]` vs `[Cr(NH₃)₆][Co(CN)₆]` |
| 5. Geometrical | cis vs trans spatial arrangement | Cisplatin vs Transplatin |
| 6. Optical | Non-superimposable mirror images | Δ- vs Λ-[Co(en)₃]³⁺ |
| 7. Co-ordination Position | Ligands swap between bridged metals | Bridged dinuclear Co complexes |
| 8. Polymerisation | Same empirical formula, different MW | `[Pt(NH₃)₂Cl₂]` monomer vs dimer |
