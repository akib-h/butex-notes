## Part A: Importance / Applications of Complex Compounds

Complex compounds play crucial roles across chemistry, biology, medicine, and industry.

### Application 1 — Qualitative and Quantitative Chemical Analysis

Complex compounds are widely used in **analytical chemistry**:

- **EDTA (Ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid)** is used as a hexadentate chelating agent to determine water hardness (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺ ions) via complexometric titration.
- **Prussian Blue** `[Fe₄[Fe(CN)₆]₃]` is a precipitate used to detect Fe³⁺ in qualitative analysis.
- **Dimethylglyoxime (dmgH)** forms a bright red precipitate with Ni²⁺ for its detection.
- **SCN⁻** forms blood-red `[Fe(SCN)]²⁺` — a sensitive test for Fe³⁺.

```
Fe³⁺ + SCN⁻ → [Fe(SCN)]²⁺  (blood-red colour → qualitative test)
Ca²⁺ + EDTA → [Ca(EDTA)]²⁻  (complexometric titration for water hardness)
```

---

### Application 2 — Treatment of Lead Poisoning (EDTA chelation therapy)

> **EDTA** is used in the treatment of **heavy metal poisoning** (lead, mercury, arsenic).

EDTA forms highly stable, water-soluble chelate complexes with heavy metal ions, allowing them to be excreted from the body via urine.

```
Pb²⁺ (in blood) + EDTA⁴⁻ → [Pb(EDTA)]²⁻  (stable, non-toxic, excreted)
```

This process is called **chelation therapy** and is also used for arsenic, mercury, and cadmium poisoning.

---

### Application 3 — Purification of Metals

Complex compounds are used to **refine and purify metals**:

**Mond Process (Purification of Nickel):**
```
Ni (crude) + 4CO → [Ni(CO)₄]   (volatile, at 50°C — nickel tetracarbonyl)
                    ↓ (heat to 230°C)
                   Ni (pure) + 4CO
```

The volatile complex `[Ni(CO)₄]` separates impurities, then pure Ni is recovered by decomposition.

**Purification of Gold:**
```
Au + CN⁻ + O₂ + H₂O → [Au(CN)₂]⁻  (leaching — hydrometallurgy)
                         ↓ (reduction with Zn)
                        Au (pure)
```

### Application 5 — Catalysts in Industrial Processes

Many important industrial catalysts are transition metal complexes:

| Process | Catalyst | Product |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Ziegler–Natta polymerisation | TiCl₄/Al(C₂H₅)₃ | Polyethylene, Polypropylene |
| Hydroformylation (Oxo process) | [HCo(CO)₄] or Rh complex | Aldehydes from alkenes |
| Wacker process | [PdCl₄]²⁻ | Acetaldehyde from ethylene |
| Monsanto process | [Rh(CO)₂I₂]⁻ | Acetic acid (CH₃COOH) |
| Homogeneous hydrogenation | Wilkinson's catalyst [RhCl(PPh₃)₃] | Reduction of alkenes |

```
Ziegler-Natta:
n CH₂=CH₂  ──[TiCl₄/AlR₃]──→  (−CH₂−CH₂−)ₙ  (polyethylene)
```

### Application 6 — Electroplating

Metal complex solutions are used as **plating baths** in electroplating:

```
Gold plating:   [Au(CN)₂]⁻  + e⁻ → Au (deposited) + 2CN⁻
Silver plating: [Ag(CN)₂]⁻  + e⁻ → Ag (deposited) + 2CN⁻
Copper plating: [Cu(CN)₃]²⁻ + e⁻ → Cu (deposited) + 3CN⁻
```

Using complex ions (rather than simple metal ions) gives **smoother, more adherent** metal deposits.

### Application 7 — Biological Systems (Bioinorganic Chemistry)

Some of the most important molecules in biology are metal complexes:

| Molecule | Metal | Function |
|----------|-------|---------|
| Haemoglobin | Fe²⁺ (porphyrin) | Oxygen transport in blood |
| Myoglobin | Fe²⁺ (porphyrin) | Oxygen storage in muscle |
| Cytochromes | Fe²⁺/³⁺ (porphyrin) | Electron transfer in mitochondria |
| Chlorophyll | Mg²⁺ (porphyrin) | Photosynthesis in plants |
| Vitamin B₁₂ | Co³⁺ (corrin ring) | Prevention of pernicious anaemia |
| Carbonic anhydrase | Zn²⁺ | CO₂/H₂O interconversion |
| Nitrogenase | Fe, Mo | N₂ fixation in bacteria |

```
Haemoglobin:
    Fe²⁺ (in haem group) + O₂ → Fe²⁺···O₂ (oxyhaemoglobin)
    
    The porphyrin ring (a macrocyclic ligand) holds Fe²⁺ 
    at the centre with 4 N donors. A 5th N comes from His 
    (histidine) residue of the protein. O₂ binds at the 6th site.
```

## Part B: Difference Between Double Salt and Complex Compound

From notes (2019 exam material):

### What is a Double Salt?

A **double salt** is a salt formed by the crystallisation of two different salts from the same solution in a fixed molar ratio.

**Example:** Potash alum = K₂SO₄ · Al₂(SO₄)₃ · 24H₂O = KAl(SO₄)₂ · 12H₂O

### What is a Complex Compound?

A **complex compound** contains a central metal ion surrounded by ligands held by coordinate bonds — the complex ion does not dissociate in solution.

**Example:** K₄[Fe(CN)₆] — the [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻ complex ion stays intact in solution.

### Comparison Table

| # | Double Salt | Complex Compound |
|---|------------|-----------------|
| 1 | Does **NOT** have co-ordinate bonds | **Has** co-ordinate bonds |
| 2 | Exists **only** in the solid state | Exists in both **solid state and aqueous solution** |
| 3 | **Loses identity** in aqueous solution (all ions dissociate freely) | Does **NOT** lose identity in aqueous solution (complex ion stays intact) |
| 4 | Contains **two salts in equimolar ratio** | Contains ions which **may or may not** be in equimolar ratio |
| 5 | **Example:** K₂SO₄ · Al₂(SO₄)₃ (alum) | **Example:** K₄[Fe(CN)₆] |

**Dissolution behaviour:**

```
Double salt: KAl(SO₄)₂ · 12H₂O  →  K⁺ + Al³⁺ + 2SO₄²⁻ + 12H₂O
             (loses identity — gives individual free ions)

Complex:     K₄[Fe(CN)₆]  →  4K⁺ + [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻
             (complex ion remains intact — does NOT give free Fe²⁺ or CN⁻)
```

## Part C: Difference Between Ionic Bond and Covalent Bond

From notes (2020 exam):

### Ionic Bond

An **ionic bond** forms when electrons are **transferred** from one atom to another, creating oppositely charged ions (cation and anion) that attract each other by electrostatic forces.

### Covalent Bond

A **covalent bond** forms when two atoms **share** a pair of electrons.

### Comparison Table

| # | Ionic Bond | Covalent Bond |
|---|-----------|--------------|
| 1 | Formed by **transfer** of electrons between a cation and anion | Formed by **sharing** of electrons between neutral atoms |
| 2 | **Non-directional** (electrostatic force acts equally in all directions) | **Directional** (bond has specific direction in space) |
| 3 | **Electrostatic** in nature | **Not electrostatic** — quantum mechanical sharing |
| 4 | **Always polar** | **May or may not** be polar (depends on electronegativity difference) |
| 5 | **Strongest** bond type (high lattice energy) | Quite **weak** compared to ionic (in general) — though exceptions exist |
| 6 | **Metallic elements** tend to form ionic bonds (lose electrons easily) | **Non-metallic elements** tend to form covalent bonds (share electrons) |

---

### Why Do Ionic Compounds Have High Melting Points? (from notes, 2020 exam)

> Ions are held together by **strong ionic bonds**. A large amount of energy is required to overcome the strong electrostatic forces between the ions. That is why ionic compounds have **high melting points**.

**Mathematical basis:**

Lattice energy (U) gives the energy released when one mole of ionic solid forms from gaseous ions:

$$U = \frac{N_A \cdot M \cdot z^+ \cdot z^- \cdot e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 \cdot r_0} \left(1 - \frac{1}{n}\right)$$

Where:
- $N_A$ = Avogadro's number
- $M$ = Madelung constant (geometry-dependent)
- $z^+$, $z^-$ = charges on ions
- $r_0$ = nearest-neighbour distance
- $n$ = Born exponent

Higher lattice energy → more energy needed to melt → higher melting point.

**Example comparison:**

| Compound | Type | Melting Point |
|----------|------|--------------|
| NaCl | Ionic | 801°C |
| MgO | Ionic (higher charge) | 2852°C |
| H₂O | Covalent (polar) | 0°C |
| CH₄ | Covalent (non-polar) | −182°C |

## Part D: Coordinate (Dative) Bond in Complexes

Complex compounds involve a special type of covalent bond:

### Coordinate Bond (Dative Covalent Bond)

> A bond in which **both electrons** of the shared pair are provided by **only one atom** (the donor/ligand), while the other atom (acceptor/metal) contributes no electrons.

```
NH₃  →  Co³⁺   (both electrons from N of NH₃)

N: ──→ Co   (dative bond, sometimes shown with →)
```

In complex formation:
- **Ligand** = electron pair donor (Lewis base)
- **Metal** = electron pair acceptor (Lewis acid)

## Summary

| Topic | Key Points |
|-------|-----------|
| Applications | Analysis, medicine, purification, catalysis, electroplating, biology |
| Double salt | No coordinate bonds; loses identity in solution |
| Complex compound | Has coordinate bonds; retains identity in solution |
| Ionic bond | Electron transfer; non-directional; high MP |
| Covalent bond | Electron sharing; directional; lower MP |
| Coordinate bond | Both electrons from one atom (ligand → metal) |
