## Introduction

The concept of **acids and bases** is one of the most fundamental in chemistry. Over centuries, chemists have developed increasingly sophisticated and general definitions to explain acid-base behaviour across different chemical environments.

The progression of theories is from the most specific (Arrhenius — aqueous systems only) to the most general (Lewis — all electron interactions), and each successive definition encompasses and extends the previous one.

```
Most Specific ←————————————→ Most General

Arrhenius ⊂ Brønsted-Lowry ⊂ Lewis
```

> 📌 **Key Principle:** Understanding the hierarchy of acid-base theories allows chemists to explain reactions in aqueous solutions, non-aqueous solvents, and even the gas phase.

## 1. Arrhenius Concept (1884)

### 📖 Historical Background

Proposed by Swedish chemist **Svante Arrhenius** in 1884. This foundational work earned him the **Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1903**. It was the first modern, quantitative theory of acids and bases.

### Definition

| Species | Definition | Condition |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| **Arrhenius Acid** | A substance that **produces H⁺ (hydrogen ions)** when dissolved in water | Must be in aqueous solution |
| **Arrhenius Base** | A substance that **produces OH⁻ (hydroxide ions)** when dissolved in water | Must be in aqueous solution |

### Ionization Reactions

**Acids:**

```
HCl  (aq)  →  H⁺(aq)  +  Cl⁻(aq)
HNO₃ (aq)  →  H⁺(aq)  +  NO₃⁻(aq)
H₂SO₄(aq)  →  2H⁺(aq) +  SO₄²⁻(aq)
```

**Bases:**

```
NaOH  (aq) →  Na⁺(aq)  +  OH⁻(aq)
KOH   (aq) →  K⁺(aq)   +  OH⁻(aq)
Ca(OH)₂(aq)→  Ca²⁺(aq) +  2OH⁻(aq)
```

### Examples

**Common Arrhenius Acids:**

1. Hydrochloric acid: `HCl → H⁺ + Cl⁻`
2. Sulfuric acid: `H₂SO₄ → 2H⁺ + SO₄²⁻`
3. Nitric acid: `HNO₃ → H⁺ + NO₃⁻`
4. Phosphoric acid: `H₃PO₄ → 3H⁺ + PO₄³⁻`
5. Acetic acid: `CH₃COOH ⇌ H⁺ + CH₃COO⁻` (weak, partial dissociation)

**Common Arrhenius Bases:**

1. Sodium hydroxide: `NaOH → Na⁺ + OH⁻`
2. Potassium hydroxide: `KOH → K⁺ + OH⁻`
3. Calcium hydroxide: `Ca(OH)₂ → Ca²⁺ + 2OH⁻`
4. Ammonia (special case): `NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻` *(acts as base by producing OH⁻)*

### ✅ Advantages

1. Simple and intuitive model — directly relates to observable H⁺ and OH⁻ ions
2. Explains common color/behavior of acids (sour taste, corrosive) and bases (slippery, bitter)
3. Accurately describes acid-base neutralization producing salts and water

### ❌ Limitations

1. **Only applies to aqueous solutions** — cannot explain acid-base behaviour in non-aqueous solvents (e.g., gas phase, alcohols, liquid ammonia)
2. **Cannot explain basic character of NH₃** — ammonia has no OH⁻ in its formula, yet it is a base. Similarly, Na₂CO₃ and amines are bases but contain no OH group
3. **Ignores solvent role** — the theory neglects the role of the solvent in determining acidity
4. **Cannot explain why CuSO₄, AlCl₃, CO₂, SO₂** show acidic properties — these contain no H⁺

> 🧠 **Memory Tip:** "Arrhenius Acids Add H⁺; Arrhenius Bases Bring OH⁻"

## 2. Brønsted–Lowry Concept (1923)

### 📖 Historical Background

Proposed independently and simultaneously by Danish chemist **Johannes Nicolaus Brønsted** and English chemist **Thomas Martin Lowry** in **1923**. This theory extends Arrhenius by redefining acids and bases based on **proton transfer** rather than dissociation in water.

### Definition

| Species | Definition |
|---------|-----------|
| **Brønsted-Lowry Acid** | A **proton (H⁺) donor** — any substance that can donate a hydrogen ion to another species |
| **Brønsted-Lowry Base** | A **proton (H⁺) acceptor** — any substance that can accept a hydrogen ion from another species |

> The key idea: an acid-base reaction is fundamentally a **proton transfer reaction**.

### General Equation

```
  HA    +    B    ⇌    A⁻   +   BH⁺
(acid)    (base)    (conj. base) (conj. acid)
```

### Reactions and Examples

**Example 1 — HCl in water:**

```
HCl   +   H₂O   →   H₃O⁺   +   Cl⁻
(acid)    (base)   (conj. acid)  (conj. base)
```

HCl donates H⁺ to water → water is the **base** (proton acceptor).

**Example 2 — NH₃ in water:**

```
NH₃   +   H₂O   ⇌   NH₄⁺   +   OH⁻
(base)    (acid)   (conj. acid)  (conj. base)
```

Water donates H⁺ to NH₃ → water acts as an **acid** here!

**Example 3 — HCl + NH₃ (non-aqueous, gas phase):**

```
HCl  +  NH₃  →  NH₄⁺  +  Cl⁻   (→ NH₄Cl salt)
(acid)  (base)
```

⚠️ **No water is involved** — this reaction cannot be explained by Arrhenius theory, but Brønsted-Lowry handles it perfectly.

**Example 4 — Polyprotic acid (H₂SO₄):**

```
H₂SO₄  +  H₂O  →  H₃O⁺  +  HSO₄⁻   (Step 1, strong)
HSO₄⁻  +  H₂O  ⇌  H₃O⁺  +  SO₄²⁻   (Step 2, weak)
```

### Conjugate Acid-Base Pairs

In Brønsted-Lowry theory, every acid-base reaction produces a **conjugate acid** and a **conjugate base**:

```
Acid₁  +  Base₂  ⇌  Conjugate Base₁  +  Conjugate Acid₂
```

| Acid | Conjugate Base | Base | Conjugate Acid |
|------|---------------|------|---------------|
| HCl | Cl⁻ | H₂O | H₃O⁺ |
| CH₃COOH | CH₃COO⁻ | NH₃ | NH₄⁺ |
| H₂SO₄ | HSO₄⁻ | H₂O | H₃O⁺ |
| H₂O | OH⁻ | NH₃ | NH₄⁺ |
| HF | F⁻ | F⁻ | HF |

> 📌 **Rule:** A conjugate pair **differs by exactly one proton (H⁺)**. The acid has one more H⁺ than its conjugate base.

### Water as an Amphoteric Species

Water is **amphiprotic** — it can act as either a Brønsted-Lowry acid or base:

```
As acid:  H₂O  +  NH₃   →  OH⁻   +  NH₄⁺   (donates H⁺)
As base:  H₂O  +  HCl   →  H₃O⁺  +  Cl⁻    (accepts H⁺)
```

**Autoionization of water:**

```
H₂O  +  H₂O  ⇌  H₃O⁺  +  OH⁻     Kw = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ at 25°C
```

### Non-Aqueous Solvents

Brønsted-Lowry theory applies in any protic solvent. In **liquid ammonia (NH₃)**:

```
NH₃  +  NH₃  ⇌  NH₄⁺  +  NH₂⁻       (K ≈ 10⁻³⁰)
```

- Strongest acid possible in NH₃: **NH₄⁺**
- Strongest base possible in NH₃: **NH₂⁻**
- Acetic acid (weak in water) is a **strong acid** in liquid ammonia

### ✅ Advantages

1. **Not restricted to water** — works in any solvent capable of proton transfer
2. Explains basic character of **NH₃, Na₂CO₃, amines** without needing OH⁻ in formula
3. Introduces the elegant concept of **conjugate acid-base pairs**
4. Explains amphoteric behaviour of water and ions like HCO₃⁻, HSO₄⁻

### ❌ Limitations

1. Does **not apply to aprotic solvents** (benzene, dioxane) — no proton transfer occurs
2. **Cannot explain reactions between acid oxides and basic oxides:** e.g., `CaO + SO₃ → CaSO₄` (no proton involved)
3. **Fails to recognize acidic character** of proton-less species like `AlCl₃`, `BF₃`, `FeCl₃`

## 3. Lewis Concept (1923)

### 📖 Historical Background

Proposed by American chemist **Gilbert Newton Lewis** in **1923** (the same year as Brønsted-Lowry!). Lewis theory is the **most general** of the three major theories, defining acids and bases in terms of **electron pair transfer** rather than proton transfer. It encompasses all previous definitions.

### Definition

| Species | Definition |
|---------|-----------|
| **Lewis Acid** | An **electron pair acceptor** — a species with a vacant orbital that can accept a lone pair of electrons |
| **Lewis Base** | An **electron pair donor** — a species that can donate a lone pair of electrons to form a coordinate covalent bond |

> The product of a Lewis acid-base reaction is called an **adduct** or **Lewis adduct**.

```
Lewis Acid  +  Lewis Base  →  Adduct
  (A)              (:B)         (A←B)
```

### Mechanism

In a Lewis acid-base reaction, the base donates an electron pair into an empty or accessible orbital on the acid:

```
       lone pair
          ↓
  A  +  :B   →   A:B
(empty orbital)   (coordinate bond formed)
```

### Examples

**Example 1 — BF₃ + F⁻:**

```
BF₃  +  :F⁻  →  [BF₄]⁻
(Lewis acid)  (Lewis base)  (adduct)
```

Boron in BF₃ is electron-deficient (only 6 electrons) and accepts an electron pair from F⁻ to complete its octet.

**Example 2 — AlCl₃ + Cl⁻:**

```
AlCl₃  +  :Cl⁻  →  [AlCl₄]⁻
```

AlCl₃ is a classic Lewis acid — widely used in Friedel-Crafts reactions.

**Example 3 — Proton (H⁺) as Lewis Acid:**

```
H⁺  +  :OH⁻  →  H₂O
```

H⁺ is an extreme Lewis acid — it has **no electrons at all** and readily accepts a lone pair.

**Example 4 — Metal Ion Complex Formation:**

```
Fe³⁺  +  6:H₂O  →  [Fe(H₂O)₆]³⁺
(Lewis acid) (Lewis base)  (coordination complex)
```

This explains why metal salt solutions can be acidic — the metal ion is a Lewis acid.

**Example 5 — CO₂ + OH⁻ (no proton transfer):**

```
CO₂  +  :OH⁻  →  HCO₃⁻
```

CO₂ is a Lewis acid (carbon accepts lone pair from OH⁻). This reaction **cannot** be explained by Brønsted-Lowry theory.

**Example 6 — BF₃ + NH₃:**

```
BF₃  +  :NH₃  →  H₃N→BF₃
```

NH₃ is the Lewis base (donates lone pair); BF₃ is the Lewis acid.

### Types of Lewis Acids

| Type | Examples | Reason |
|------|---------|--------|
| **Electron-deficient** | BF₃, BCl₃, AlCl₃ | Incomplete octet (6 electrons on central atom) |
| **Metal cations** | Fe³⁺, Cu²⁺, Zn²⁺, Al³⁺ | Empty d-orbitals available |
| **Molecules with polar bonds** | CO₂, SO₃, SO₂ | Carbon/Sulfur has partial + charge |
| **Halogen molecules** | I₂, Br₂ | Can accept lone pair into σ* orbital |

### Types of Lewis Bases

| Type | Examples | Reason |
|------|---------|--------|
| **Neutral molecules with lone pairs** | NH₃, H₂O, ROH, R₂O | Lone pairs available for donation |
| **Anions** | OH⁻, Cl⁻, CN⁻, F⁻ | Lone pairs and negative charge |
| **π-system donors** | Benzene, alkenes | π electrons can donate to Lewis acids |

### ✅ Advantages

1. **Most general acid-base theory** — explains reactions without protons or even water
2. Explains **metal complex formation** (coordination chemistry)
3. Explains why compounds like **BF₃, AlCl₃, FeCl₃** are acids even with no H⁺
4. **Covers all Brønsted-Lowry and Arrhenius** reactions as special cases
5. Foundation for **organic reaction mechanisms** (electrophiles are Lewis acids, nucleophiles are Lewis bases)

### ❌ Limitations

1. **No quantitative scale** — hard to rank Lewis acids/bases by strength
2. **Very broad** — almost everything is a Lewis acid or base; reduces predictive power
3. Doesn't explain why some reactions between Lewis pairs don't occur easily
4. Doesn't directly give pH or quantitative equilibrium data

## 4. Solvent System Concept

### Definition

The **solvent system concept** generalizes acids and bases based on the autoionization of the solvent. A solvent can ionize to give a **cation (solvo-acid)** and an **anion (solvo-base)**:

- **Acid** = a substance that increases the concentration of the **cation** characteristic of the solvent
- **Base** = a substance that increases the concentration of the **anion** characteristic of the solvent

### Examples

**In Water (H₂O):**

```
H₂O + H₂O  ⇌  H₃O⁺  +  OH⁻
Acid = anything producing H₃O⁺
Base = anything producing OH⁻
```

**In Liquid Ammonia (NH₃):**

```
NH₃ + NH₃  ⇌  NH₄⁺  +  NH₂⁻
Acid = anything producing NH₄⁺
Base = anything producing NH₂⁻
```

**In Liquid SO₂:**

```
2SO₂  ⇌  SO²⁺  +  SO₃²⁻
Acid = produces SO²⁺ (thionyl cation)
Base = produces SO₃²⁻ (sulfite ion)
```

**In Liquid HF:**

```
HF + HF  ⇌  H₂F⁺  +  F⁻
```

## 5. Lux–Flood Concept

### Definition

Proposed by **Lux** (1939) and extended by **Flood** (1947). This concept applies specifically to **high-temperature oxide reactions** in melts and geochemistry.

- **Acid** = an **oxide ion (O²⁻) acceptor**
- **Base** = an **oxide ion (O²⁻) donor**

### Examples

```
CaO  +  SiO₂  →  CaSiO₃
(base)   (acid)   (salt)
CaO donates O²⁻; SiO₂ accepts O²⁻

SO₃  +  CaO  →  CaSO₄
(acid)  (base)   (salt)
```

> 📌 **Application:** The Lux-Flood concept is important in **geology, metallurgy**, and understanding slag formation in **blast furnaces**.

---

## 6. Usanovich Concept (Generalized Acid-Base Theory)

Proposed by **Usanovich** (1939). This is the **most general** of all acid-base theories.

- **Acid** = a species that donates **cations** or accepts **anions or electrons**
- **Base** = a species that donates **anions or electrons** or accepts **cations**

This definition unifies acid-base and **redox chemistry**. However, it is so general that it is rarely used in practice.

## Comparison of Acid-Base Theories

| Theory | Acid | Base | Medium | Scope |
|--------|------|------|--------|-------|
| **Arrhenius** | Produces H⁺ in H₂O | Produces OH⁻ in H₂O | Aqueous only | Very narrow |
| **Brønsted-Lowry** | Proton (H⁺) donor | Proton (H⁺) acceptor | Any protic solvent | Moderate |
| **Lewis** | Electron pair acceptor | Electron pair donor | Any | Very broad |
| **Solvent System** | Produces solvent cation | Produces solvent anion | Non-aqueous | Moderate |
| **Lux-Flood** | O²⁻ acceptor | O²⁻ donor | High-temp melts | Specialized |
| **Usanovich** | Cation donor/anion acceptor | Anion donor/cation acceptor | Any | Universal |

```
                  [Usanovich]
              [Lux-Flood] [Solvent System]
                    [Lewis]
                [Brønsted-Lowry]
                  [Arrhenius]

     ← Specific                       General →
```

## Conjugate Acid-Base Pairs

In Brønsted-Lowry theory, when an acid donates a proton it forms its **conjugate base**, and when a base accepts a proton it forms its **conjugate acid**:

```
        Acid₁  +  Base₂  ⇌  Conjugate Acid₂  +  Conjugate Base₁
                                  ↑                     ↑
                           (Base₂ + H⁺)           (Acid₁ − H⁺)
```

### Conjugate Pair Table

| Acid | Conjugate Base | Ka (25°C) |
|------|---------------|-----------|
| HClO₄ | ClO₄⁻ | Very large (strong) |
| HCl | Cl⁻ | Very large (strong) |
| H₂SO₄ | HSO₄⁻ | Very large (strong) |
| HNO₃ | NO₃⁻ | Very large (strong) |
| H₃O⁺ | H₂O | 55 |
| HSO₄⁻ | SO₄²⁻ | 1.2 × 10⁻² |
| HF | F⁻ | 6.8 × 10⁻⁴ |
| CH₃COOH | CH₃COO⁻ | 1.8 × 10⁻⁵ |
| H₂CO₃ | HCO₃⁻ | 4.3 × 10⁻⁷ |
| NH₄⁺ | NH₃ | 5.6 × 10⁻¹⁰ |
| H₂O | OH⁻ | 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ |
| NH₃ | NH₂⁻ | ~10⁻³⁸ |

> 📌 **Key Relationship:** The stronger the acid, the weaker its conjugate base. Conversely, the stronger the base, the weaker its conjugate acid.

## Amphoteric and Amphiprotic Species

### Amphiprotic Species

Species that can **both donate and accept a proton**:

- **H₂O** — donates H⁺ (acting as acid) and accepts H⁺ (acting as base)
- **HCO₃⁻** — can donate H⁺ (→ CO₃²⁻) or accept H⁺ (→ H₂CO₃)
- **HSO₄⁻** — can donate H⁺ (→ SO₄²⁻) or accept H⁺ (→ H₂SO₄)
- **HS⁻**, **HPO₄²⁻**, **H₂PO₄⁻**

### Amphoteric Species

Species that can act as **acid or base** depending on conditions (broader term that includes Lewis sense):

```
Al(OH)₃ + 3HCl → AlCl₃ + 3H₂O   (acts as base)
Al(OH)₃ + NaOH → NaAl(OH)₄       (acts as acid)
```

> All amphiprotic substances are amphoteric, but some amphoteric substances are not amphiprotic (e.g., ZnO in Lewis sense).

## Practice Problems

**Problem 1:** Identify the Brønsted-Lowry acid and base in the following reaction:

```
HNO₂  +  H₂O  ⇌  NO₂⁻  +  H₃O⁺
```

**Answer:** HNO₂ is the acid (donates H⁺); H₂O is the base (accepts H⁺). Conjugate base is NO₂⁻; conjugate acid is H₃O⁺.

---

**Problem 2:** Classify the following as Lewis acid or Lewis base:

1. `BF₃` — Lewis **acid** (empty p-orbital on B)
2. `NH₃` — Lewis **base** (lone pair on N)
3. `Cu²⁺` — Lewis **acid** (empty d-orbitals)
4. `I⁻` — Lewis **base** (lone pairs on I)
5. `CO₂` — Lewis **acid** (C accepts electrons)

---

**Problem 3:** Which of the following is NOT an Arrhenius base but IS a Brønsted-Lowry base?

`NH₃`, `KOH`, `NaOH`, `Ca(OH)₂`

**Answer:** `NH₃` — it has no OH⁻ in its formula, so it is not an Arrhenius base. But it **accepts a proton from water** to form NH₄⁺ and OH⁻, making it a Brønsted-Lowry base.

---

**Problem 4:** Write the Lux-Flood acid-base reaction occurring in a blast furnace.

```
CaO  (base, O²⁻ donor)  +  SiO₂  (acid, O²⁻ acceptor)  →  CaSiO₃  (slag)
```
