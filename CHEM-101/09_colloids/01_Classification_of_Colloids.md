## 1. Introduction & Definition

A **colloid** (from Greek: *kólla* = glue, *eidos* = form) is a **heterogeneous system** in which one substance, called the **dispersed phase**, is distributed as fine particles within another substance, called the **dispersion medium** (or continuous phase).

### Key Size Criterion

| System Type     | Particle Size       |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| True solution    | < 1 nm (< 10 Å)    |
| **Colloidal solution** | **1 nm – 1000 nm** |
| Suspension      | > 1000 nm (> 1 µm) |

Colloidal particles are **too small to be seen by the naked eye** but **large enough to scatter light** (Tyndall Effect) and **cannot pass through semi-permeable membranes**.

> **Key distinction:** Unlike true solutions, colloidal particles do not settle under gravity under normal conditions, yet unlike coarse suspensions, they are not filterable by ordinary filter paper.

## 2. Classification Based on Physical State

Since both the dispersed phase and dispersion medium can exist in three physical states (solid, liquid, gas), we get **8 possible combinations** (gas-in-gas is a true mixture, not a colloid).

| S.No. | Dispersed Phase | Dispersion Medium | Type Name       | Examples                                      |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|-----------------|-----------------------------------------------|
| 1     | Solid           | Gas               | **Aerosol**     | Smoke, dust in air, fog from dry ice          |
| 2     | Solid           | Liquid            | **Sol**         | Paint, ink, gold sol, starch solution, muddy water |
| 3     | Solid           | Solid             | **Solid Sol**   | Gemstones (ruby = Cr³⁺ in Al₂O₃), alloys (steel) |
| 4     | Liquid          | Gas               | **Liquid Aerosol** | Fog, mist, cloud, aerosol sprays           |
| 5     | Liquid          | Liquid            | **Emulsion**    | Milk, mayonnaise, cream, cold cream           |
| 6     | Liquid          | Solid             | **Gel**         | Cheese, butter, jelly, boot polish            |
| 7     | Gas             | Liquid            | **Foam**        | Whipped cream, soap lather, shaving foam      |
| 8     | Gas             | Solid             | **Solid Foam**  | Pumice stone, bread, foam rubber, aerogel     |

### Special Notes:

- **Sol** is the most commonly discussed colloidal system in chemistry.
- If the dispersion medium is **water**, the sol is called a **hydrosol** or **aquasol**.
- If the dispersion medium is **alcohol**, it is called an **alcosol**.
- If the dispersion medium is **benzene**, it is called a **benzosol**.

## 3. Classification Based on Nature of Interaction with Dispersion Medium

This is one of the most **physically significant** classifications. It divides colloids into two broad categories:

### 3.1 Lyophilic Colloids ("Solvent-loving")

- *Lyo* = solvent; *philic* = loving
- The dispersed phase has a **strong affinity** for the dispersion medium.
- Particles are **heavily solvated** (surrounded by solvent molecules).
- **Preparation:** Simply dissolve the substance in the dispersion medium (e.g., dissolve gelatin in warm water).
- **Stability:** Very **stable**; difficult to coagulate.
- **Reversibility:** If the sol is evaporated or dried, it can be reconstituted by re-adding the solvent → **reversible colloids**.
- **Examples:** Starch in water, gelatin in water, gum arabic, egg albumin, rubber in benzene, proteins in water.

**Properties at a glance:**
| Property | Lyophilic |
|----------|-----------|
| Preparation | Easy (direct mixing) |
| Stability | High |
| Reversibility | Reversible |
| Viscosity | High (close to medium) |
| Surface tension | Similar to medium |
| Coagulation | Requires large amount of electrolyte |
| Tyndall effect | Less distinct |

### 3.2 Lyophobic Colloids ("Solvent-hating")

- *Lyo* = solvent; *phobic* = hating
- The dispersed phase has **little or no affinity** for the dispersion medium.
- Particles are **not solvated**.
- **Preparation:** Requires special methods (condensation, dispersion, electrical methods).
- **Stability:** **Unstable**; require trace amounts of electrolytes or stabilizers (protective colloids) for stability.
- **Reversibility:** Once coagulated, **cannot be reconstituted** → **irreversible colloids**.
- **Examples:** Gold sol, arsenic sulfide (As₂S₃) sol, silver sol, ferric hydroxide (Fe(OH)₃) sol, sulfur sol.

**Properties at a glance:**
| Property | Lyophobic |
|----------|-----------|
| Preparation | Requires special methods |
| Stability | Low (need stabilizer) |
| Reversibility | Irreversible |
| Viscosity | Similar to medium |
| Surface tension | Lower than medium |
| Coagulation | Small amount of electrolyte sufficient |
| Tyndall effect | More pronounced |

### 3.3 Comparison: Lyophilic vs. Lyophobic

```
Lyophilic Sol                          Lyophobic Sol
─────────────────────────────          ──────────────────────────────
Particles solvated                     Particles NOT solvated
Stable (self-stabilizing)              Unstable (need stabilizer)
Reversible                             Irreversible
High viscosity                         Low viscosity
Needs large electrolyte to coagulate   Easily coagulated
Examples: gelatin, starch              Examples: Au sol, Fe(OH)₃ sol
```

## 4. Classification Based on Type of Particles (Colloidal Structure)

### 4.1 Multimolecular Colloids

- Formed when a **large number of small molecules** (atoms or molecules with diameter < 1 nm) **aggregate** together to form particles within the colloidal range.
- The individual molecules are held together by **van der Waals forces**.
- **Examples:**
  - Gold sol: many gold atoms cluster together.
  - Sulfur sol: S₈ molecules aggregate → S_n clusters in 1–1000 nm range.

### 4.2 Macromolecular Colloids

- The **molecules themselves** are large enough (polymers, biopolymers) to fall in the colloidal range.
- Each dissolved molecule acts as a single colloidal particle.
- These are generally **lyophilic** in nature.
- **Examples:**
  - Natural: Starch, proteins (hemoglobin, albumin), cellulose, rubber, DNA.
  - Synthetic: Nylon, polythene, polyvinyl chloride (PVC), polystyrene in organic solvents.

### 4.3 Associated Colloids (Micellar Colloids)

- Formed by certain substances called **surfactants** (surface active agents) or **amphiphilic molecules** that possess both a **hydrophilic head** (water-loving) and a **hydrophobic tail** (water-hating).
- At low concentrations, they behave as true solutes (electrolytes).
- Above a critical concentration called the **Critical Micelle Concentration (CMC)**, the molecules **self-assemble** into aggregates called **micelles**.

#### Micelle Formation:

```
Below CMC:  Individual amphiphilic molecules dispersed in solution

Above CMC:  Molecules aggregate → MICELLE
            [Hydrophobic tails point inward (away from water)]
            [Hydrophilic heads point outward (toward water)]

Structure:
         ___________
       /  head heads \      ← hydrophilic heads face water
      | tail  tails  |
      | tail  tails  |      ← hydrophobic tails sequestered inside
       \___________/
```

- **CMC** is typically in the range of 10⁻⁴ to 10⁻² mol/L for common surfactants.
- **Aggregation number:** Number of monomer units in a micelle (typically 20–200).
- **Examples:** Soaps (sodium stearate, C₁₇H₃₅COONa), detergents (sodium dodecyl sulfate, SDS), bile salts.

**Formula for CMC determination (Krafft equation approach):**

The degree of micellization can be monitored via **surface tension vs. concentration** plots. The CMC is identified as the **break point** (inflection point) in the surface tension–log[concentration] curve.

## 5. Classification Based on Charge on Particles

Colloidal particles carry a net surface charge, which determines their electrical behavior.

### 5.1 Positively Charged Sols

Particles carry a **positive charge** due to adsorption of cations or loss of anions.

| Colloid              | Medium | Charge Source                                |
|----------------------|--------|----------------------------------------------|
| Fe(OH)₃ sol          | Water  | Adsorption of Fe³⁺ ions                      |
| Al(OH)₃ sol          | Water  | Adsorption of Al³⁺ ions                      |
| TiO₂ sol             | Water  | Adsorption of Ti⁴⁺ ions                      |
| Methylene blue sol   | Water  | Nature of dye molecule                       |
| Haemoglobin (in acid)| Water  | Protonation of –NH₂ groups (below isoelectric point) |

### 5.2 Negatively Charged Sols

Particles carry a **negative charge** due to adsorption of anions or ionization of surface groups.

| Colloid              | Medium | Charge Source                               |
|----------------------|--------|---------------------------------------------|
| Gold sol (Au sol)    | Water  | Adsorption of AuO₂⁻ or citrate ions        |
| Arsenic sulfide (As₂S₃) sol | Water | Adsorption of S²⁻ or HS⁻ ions       |
| Starch sol           | Water  | Adsorption of OH⁻ ions                     |
| Clay (kaolin) particles | Water | Ionization of silanol groups (Si-OH → Si-O⁻ + H⁺) |
| Proteins (above isoelectric point) | Water | Dissociation of –COOH groups |

## 6. Comparison Table

| Feature | True Solution | Colloidal Solution | Suspension |
|---------|---------------|--------------------|------------|
| Particle size | < 1 nm | 1–1000 nm | > 1000 nm |
| Visibility | Invisible (even under microscope) | Invisible to naked eye; visible under ultramicroscope | Visible to naked eye |
| Filterability | Passes through filter paper & semipermeable membrane | Passes through filter paper; does NOT pass through semipermeable membrane | Does NOT pass through either |
| Stability | Perfectly stable | Moderately stable | Unstable (settles) |
| Tyndall Effect | Absent | Present | Absent (scatters but not Tyndall) |
| Diffusion | Diffuses rapidly | Diffuses slowly | Does not diffuse |
| Osmotic Pressure | Measurable | Very low | Negligible |
| Example | NaCl in water | Starch in water | Sand in water |

## 7. Numerical Examples & Problem Solving

### Problem 1 — Particle Size Classification
**Q:** A colloidal gold sol has particles of radius 15 nm. Calculate the number of gold atoms per particle, given: density of gold = 19,300 kg/m³, atomic mass of Au = 197 g/mol.

**Solution:**
```
Volume of one spherical particle:
V = (4/3)πr³ = (4/3) × π × (15 × 10⁻⁹)³
  = (4/3) × 3.1416 × 3.375 × 10⁻²⁴ m³
  = 1.414 × 10⁻²³ m³

Mass of one particle:
m = ρ × V = 19,300 kg/m³ × 1.414 × 10⁻²³ m³
  = 2.729 × 10⁻¹⁹ kg = 2.729 × 10⁻¹⁶ g

Mass of one gold atom:
m_atom = 197 g/mol ÷ (6.022 × 10²³ atoms/mol)
       = 3.272 × 10⁻²² g/atom

Number of atoms per particle:
N = 2.729 × 10⁻¹⁶ g ÷ 3.272 × 10⁻²² g/atom
  ≈ 8.34 × 10⁵ atoms per particle ≈ 834,000 atoms
```

### Problem 2 — CMC Calculation
**Q:** The surface tension of SDS (sodium dodecyl sulfate) solution was measured at various concentrations. The CMC was found at 8.2 × 10⁻³ mol/L. At this point, the aggregation number is 62. Calculate the concentration of micelles at twice the CMC.

**Solution:**
```
At 2 × CMC = 16.4 × 10⁻³ mol/L total SDS

Monomers still at CMC = 8.2 × 10⁻³ mol/L (equilibrium)

SDS in micellar form = (16.4 – 8.2) × 10⁻³ = 8.2 × 10⁻³ mol/L

Concentration of micelles = (8.2 × 10⁻³ mol/L) ÷ 62
                           = 1.32 × 10⁻⁴ mol/L
```

## 8. Real-World Applications

1. **Food Industry:** Milk (O/W emulsion), mayonnaise (O/W), butter (W/O) — classified as liquid-in-liquid colloids (emulsions).
2. **Medicine:** Blood is a sol of proteins and cells in plasma. Drug delivery systems use colloidal carriers (liposomes, nanoparticles).
3. **Environment:** Fog (liquid aerosol) affects visibility; smog (solid + liquid aerosol) is an air quality hazard.
4. **Photography:** Silver halide grains in gelatin on photographic film are solid-in-solid colloids.
5. **Geology:** Clay sols and soil colloids determine soil fertility and irrigation behavior.
6. **Cosmetics:** Creams and lotions are emulsions; lipstick is a solid sol.
