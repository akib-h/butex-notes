## 1. Overview

The preparation of colloidal solutions requires achieving a particle size in the range of **1–1000 nm**. Two fundamental strategies exist:

```
PREPARATION METHODS
       │
       ├──► DISPERSION METHODS   (Break down bulk matter → colloidal size)
       │         │
       │         ├── Mechanical Dispersion (Colloidal Mill, Ultrasonic)
       │         ├── Electrodispersion (Bredig's Arc)
       │         └── Peptization
       │
       └──► CONDENSATION METHODS (Build up from atomic/molecular scale)
                 │
                 ├── Chemical Methods (Double decomposition, Oxidation,
                 │                    Reduction, Hydrolysis)
                 └── Physical Methods (Solvent exchange, Cooling)
```

**Key Principle:** Both methods rely on the fact that colloidal stability requires either:
- Electrostatic stabilization (surface charge creates repulsion between particles), or
- Steric stabilization (adsorbed polymer layers prevent aggregation).

## 2. Methods of Preparation — Dispersion Methods

In dispersion methods, **coarser particles are broken down** to colloidal size using mechanical, electrical, or chemical energy.

### 2.1 Mechanical Dispersion

#### 2.1.1 Colloidal Mill

- A **colloidal mill** (also called a ball mill or colloid mill) consists of two rapidly rotating steel discs set very close together.
- The material to be dispersed (suspended in liquid) is passed between the discs.
- The **high shear forces** tear the particles apart to colloidal size.
- Typical gap between discs: **0.001–0.05 mm**.
- Rotation speed: **1000–20,000 rpm**.

**Applications:**
- Preparation of paints, printing inks, pigments, pharmaceutical creams.
- Preparation of graphite lubricant colloid.

**Limitation:** Cannot produce very small particles (< 50 nm); not suitable for hard crystalline materials.

#### 2.1.2 Ultrasonic Dispersion

- **High-frequency sound waves** (ultrasound, > 20 kHz) are passed through the dispersion.
- The **cavitation** (rapid formation and collapse of bubbles) creates intense local shear forces and temperatures.
- The collapse of cavitation bubbles generates transient pressures of ~1000 atm and temperatures of ~5000 K locally.
- Very effective for producing sols of metals, rubber, and biological materials.

**Frequency used:** 20 kHz – 1 MHz

**Examples:** Silica sols, metal sols, pharmaceutical nanosuspensions.

### 2.2 Electrodispersion — Bredig's Arc Method

This is used for the preparation of **metal sols** (gold, silver, platinum, copper) in water.

#### Apparatus & Procedure:

```
          ┌───────────────────────────────┐
          │         AC Power Supply       │
          │    (High Voltage, 100V)        │
          └────────────┬──────────────────┘
                       │
              ┌────────┴────────┐
           Metal            Metal
          electrode          electrode
          (e.g., Au)        (e.g., Au)
              │                │
              └───────┬────────┘
                      │ ← Electric Arc
                  ~~~~~~~~~~
              ┌───────────────────┐
              │   Distilled Water │
              │  (with traces of  │
              │    KOH or NaOH)   │
              │                   │
              │  ··Au·· ··Au··    │  ← Colloidal gold particles
              └───────────────────┘
```

#### Mechanism:
1. Two metal electrodes (same metal as desired sol) are immersed in **ice-cold distilled water**.
2. An **electric arc** is struck between the electrodes.
3. The intense heat (**~4000°C**) vaporizes metal at the arc point.
4. Metal vapor immediately condenses in the cold water → forms colloidal particles.
5. Traces of alkali (KOH) act as **stabilizers** by imparting a charge to the particles.

**Chemical equation (for Gold sol):**
```
2Au(s) --[electric arc]--> 2Au(g) --[condensation in H₂O]--> Au colloid
```

**Stabilization:**
```
Au particles adsorb OH⁻ ions → negatively charged gold sol
```

### 2.3 Peptization

**Peptization** is the process of converting a **freshly prepared precipitate** into a colloidal sol by the action of a small amount of an **electrolyte** (called the **peptizing agent**).

#### Mechanism:
1. A freshly formed precipitate has particles slightly larger than colloidal range.
2. When a suitable electrolyte is added, **ions are adsorbed** on the surface of the precipitate.
3. This imparts a **charge** to the particles, creating electrostatic repulsion between them.
4. Repulsion causes the aggregate to break apart → colloidal dispersion.

#### Examples:

**Example 1: Fe(OH)₃ sol from ferric hydroxide precipitate**
```
Fe(OH)₃ precipitate + FeCl₃ (small amount, peptizing agent)
→ Fe(OH)₃ particles adsorb Fe³⁺ ions
→ Positively charged Fe(OH)₃ sol
```

**Example 2: As₂S₃ sol from arsenic sulfide precipitate**
```
As₂S₃ precipitate + H₂S (traces, peptizing agent)
→ As₂S₃ particles adsorb S²⁻ or HS⁻ ions
→ Negatively charged As₂S₃ sol
```

**Example 3: AgCl sol**
```
AgCl precipitate + dilute HCl
→ Cl⁻ ions adsorbed on AgCl
→ Negatively charged AgCl sol
```

**Important:** The peptizing agent must share a **common ion** with the precipitate for effective adsorption (Fajans–Paneth adsorption rules).

## 3. Methods of Preparation — Condensation Methods

In condensation methods, **atoms, ions, or molecules** aggregate from a lower dispersity to produce colloidal particles. These are **chemical or physical methods**.

### 3.1 Chemical Condensation Methods

#### 3.1.1 Double Decomposition (Metathesis)

A reaction between two soluble salts that produces an insoluble product in colloidal form.

**Example: As₂S₃ sol**
```
As₂O₃ + 3H₂S → As₂S₃ (colloidal) + 3H₂O
```

**Example: AgI sol**
```
AgNO₃ (dilute) + KI (dilute) → AgI (colloidal sol) + KNO₃
```

**Condition:** Both reactant solutions must be **dilute** to prevent precipitation of large particles.

#### 3.1.2 Oxidation

Sulfur sol can be prepared by **oxidation of H₂S** in the presence of dissolved SO₂:

```
2H₂S + SO₂ → 3S (colloidal) + 2H₂O
```

Or by oxidation with dilute HNO₃:
```
H₂S + [O] → S (colloidal) + H₂O
```

#### 3.1.3 Reduction

Metal sols are prepared by **reducing metal salts** with suitable reducing agents.

**Gold sol (Faraday's method / Zsigmondy's method):**
```
2HAuCl₄ + 3HCHO → 2Au (colloidal) + 8HCl + 3CO + 3H₂O
                   formaldehyde (reducing agent)

Or using sodium citrate as reductant and stabilizer:
HAuCl₄ + Na₃citrate → Au (colloidal, citrate-stabilized) + ...
```

**Silver sol:**
```
2AgNO₃ + tannic acid → 2Ag (colloidal) + ...
```

**Platinum sol:**
```
PtCl₄ + 2FeSO₄ → Pt (colloidal) + Fe₂(SO₄)₃ + 2FeCl₂
         (reducing agent)
```

#### 3.1.4 Hydrolysis

Ions of certain metals undergo **hydrolysis** in aqueous solution to form colloidal hydroxide sols.

**Ferric hydroxide sol (most important example):**

When FeCl₃ is **boiled** in water, it hydrolyzes:
```
FeCl₃ + 3H₂O → Fe(OH)₃ (colloidal sol) + 3HCl
```

This gives a **red-brown colored** positively charged Fe(OH)₃ sol.

**Aluminium hydroxide sol:**
```
AlCl₃ + 3H₂O → Al(OH)₃ (colloidal) + 3HCl
```

**Silicic acid sol:**
```
Na₂SiO₃ + 2HCl (dilute) → H₂SiO₃ (colloidal) + 2NaCl
```

#### 3.1.5 Exchange of Solvent (Physical Condensation)

If a substance is **soluble in one solvent but insoluble in another**, adding its solution dropwise to the second solvent produces a colloidal sol.

**Sulfur sol:**
- Dissolve sulfur in alcohol → Add this alcoholic solution dropwise to excess water.
- Sulfur molecules condense from supersaturation → colloidal sulfur particles form.

**Resin sols:**
- Dissolve resin (e.g., mastic) in alcohol → Slowly add to water → Milky white sol.

## 4. Preparation of Specific Colloids

| Colloid | Method | Reagents | Product Charge |
|---------|--------|----------|----------------|
| Fe(OH)₃ sol | Hydrolysis (boiling) | FeCl₃ + H₂O | Positive |
| As₂S₃ sol | Double decomposition | As₂O₃ + H₂S | Negative |
| Gold sol | Reduction | HAuCl₄ + HCHO (or citrate) | Negative |
| Silver sol | Reduction | AgNO₃ + tannic acid | Negative |
| Sulfur sol | Oxidation / Solvent exchange | H₂S + SO₂ or S in alcohol + water | Negative |
| Silicic acid sol | Double decomposition | Na₂SiO₃ + HCl | Negative |
| Platinum sol | Reduction | PtCl₄ + FeSO₄ | Negative |
| Al(OH)₃ sol | Hydrolysis | AlCl₃ + H₂O | Positive |
| AgCl sol | Double decomposition | AgNO₃ + NaCl | Negative (excess Cl⁻) or Positive (excess Ag⁺) |

## 5. Purification of Colloids

Freshly prepared colloidal solutions contain **impurities**: excess electrolytes, unreacted reagents, and small molecules. Excess electrolytes are especially dangerous as they can cause **coagulation** of the sol. The following methods are used to purify colloids:

### 5.1 Dialysis

**Principle:** Colloidal particles **cannot pass through a semi-permeable membrane** (e.g., parchment paper, cellophane, animal bladder membrane), but small molecules and ions **can**.

**Apparatus:**
```
         ┌─────────────────────────┐
         │      Flowing water      │
         │   (constantly renewed)  │
         │                         │
         │   ┌─────────────────┐   │
         │   │ Semi-permeable  │   │
         │   │    membrane     │   │
         │   │  (e.g., cellophane)│ │
         │   │                 │   │
         │   │  Colloidal sol  │   │
         │   │  + impurities   │   │
         │   │                 │   │
         │   │ ···col··· ions→ │→──┼──→ ions diffuse out
         │   │ ···col···       │   │
         │   └─────────────────┘   │
         └─────────────────────────┘
              (Dialyzer)
```

**Process:**
1. The colloidal sol is placed inside a bag/sac made of semi-permeable membrane.
2. The bag is suspended in a vessel of distilled water.
3. Small ions and molecules diffuse outward through the membrane.
4. Colloidal particles are retained inside.
5. Water is frequently changed (or flowing water used) to maintain the concentration gradient.

**Efficiency:** Rate of dialysis ∝ temperature, membrane area, and inversely ∝ membrane thickness.

**Application of Dialysis:**
- **Kidney dialysis (Hemodialysis):** Blood is passed through a cellophane membrane in a dialysis machine. Waste products (urea, creatinine) diffuse out; proteins and blood cells are retained.

### 5.2 Electrodialysis

**Principle:** When an **electric field** is applied across the dialysis membrane, ions (which are charged) **migrate much faster** toward the oppositely charged electrode.

```
         [–] Cathode          [+] Anode
              │                    │
              ↓                    ↓
         ┌────┼────────────────────┼────┐
         │    │  Semi-perm.        │    │
         │  ──┼── membranes ───────┼──  │
         │    │                    │    │
         │    │  Colloidal sol     │    │
         │    │  + electrolyte     │    │
         │    │                    │    │
         │  cations →  ←  anions  │    │
         └────────────────────────────┘
```

- **Cations** migrate toward cathode, **anions** toward anode.
- **Colloidal particles** (which are also charged but much larger) remain in the central compartment.
- **Electrodialysis is much faster** than simple dialysis.
- Used commercially for the **desalination of water** and purification of colloidal silica.

### 5.3 Ultrafiltration

**Principle:** A special filter (ultrafilter) with extremely fine pores is used. The pore size is adjusted to allow the passage of small molecules and ions but **retain colloidal particles**.

**Types of ultrafilter membranes:**

| Membrane Type | Pore Size | Retains |
|---------------|-----------|---------|
| Microfiltration | 0.1–10 µm | Bacteria, coarse colloids |
| **Ultrafiltration** | **2–100 nm** | **Colloidal particles, macromolecules** |
| Nanofiltration | 1–2 nm | Divalent ions, large organics |
| Reverse osmosis | < 1 nm | Nearly all solutes |

**Procedure:**
1. The colloidal sol is forced through the ultrafilter membrane under **pressure** (or suction).
2. Small ions and molecules pass through.
3. Colloidal particles are retained on the filter.
4. The particles are then redispersed in pure dispersion medium.

**Applications:**
- Purification of proteins and enzyme solutions.
- Concentration of viruses and large biomolecules.
- Water treatment (removal of humic acids, which are colloidal).

### 5.4 Comparison of Purification Methods

| Method | Principle | Speed | Removes | Limitation |
|--------|-----------|-------|---------|------------|
| Dialysis | Semi-perm. membrane, diffusion | Slow | Ions, small molecules | Very slow |
| Electrodialysis | Semi-perm. membrane + electric field | Fast | Electrolytes (ions) | More complex apparatus |
| Ultrafiltration | Physical filtration under pressure | Moderate | Ions, small molecules | Membrane may clog |

## 6. Numerical Examples

### Problem 1 — Dialysis Rate
**Q:** In dialysis, the flux (J) of NaCl across a cellophane membrane is given by Fick's first law:

$$J = -D \cdot A \cdot \frac{\Delta C}{\Delta x}$$

Where:
- D = diffusion coefficient of NaCl = 1.61 × 10⁻⁹ m²/s
- A = membrane area = 50 cm² = 50 × 10⁻⁴ m²
- ΔC = concentration difference = 0.1 mol/L = 100 mol/m³
- Δx = membrane thickness = 0.1 mm = 1 × 10⁻⁴ m

**Calculate the flux and time to reduce NaCl concentration to 1/10 of initial (assuming volume = 100 mL).**

**Solution:**
```
J = D × A × ΔC/Δx
  = 1.61 × 10⁻⁹ m²/s × 50 × 10⁻⁴ m² × (100 mol/m³) / (1 × 10⁻⁴ m)
  = 1.61 × 10⁻⁹ × 50 × 10⁻⁴ × 10⁶ mol/s
  = 8.05 × 10⁻⁶ mol/s

Initial moles of NaCl = 0.1 mol/L × 0.1 L = 0.01 mol
Moles to remove = 0.009 mol (to reduce to 1/10)

Time (approximate, first order estimate) = 0.009 / (8.05 × 10⁻⁶)
                                         ≈ 1118 s ≈ 18.6 minutes
```

### Problem 2 — Bredig's Arc
**Q:** In Bredig's arc method, a current of 2.5 A at 110 V is passed for 5 minutes. If 3% of the electrical energy goes into dispersing gold (latent heat of vaporization of Au = 1578 J/g), estimate the mass of gold dispersed.

**Solution:**
```
Total electrical energy = V × I × t = 110 V × 2.5 A × 300 s = 82,500 J

Energy used for dispersion = 3% × 82,500 = 2475 J

Mass of Au dispersed = Energy / Latent heat = 2475 J / 1578 J/g
                     ≈ 1.57 g
```

## 7. Industrial & Laboratory Applications

1. **Industrial Gold Recovery:** Gold sols are prepared for use in staining glass red (colloidal gold gives ruby-red color — used in stained glass windows since medieval times).
2. **Pharmaceutical Manufacturing:** Colloidal mills are used to prepare fine drug suspensions (e.g., insulin colloidal preparations, hydrocortisone suspensions).
3. **Food Processing:** Homogenizers (mechanical dispersers) are used to prepare milk, cream, and dressings.
4. **Water Treatment:** Dialysis and ultrafiltration are used in water purification plants.
5. **Kidney Dialysis:** Direct application of dialysis principle in medicine — the artificial kidney machine.
6. **Purification of Enzymes:** Ultrafiltration concentrates and purifies enzymes, proteins, and antibodies in biotechnology.
