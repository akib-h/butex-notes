## 1. Protective Action of Colloids

### 1.1 Background

Lyophobic colloids (e.g., gold sol, silver sol, As₂S₃ sol) are **inherently unstable**. They are easily coagulated by:
- Addition of small quantities of electrolytes
- Changes in temperature
- Mechanical agitation

However, when a **lyophilic colloid** (e.g., gelatin, starch, gum arabic, egg albumin, casein) is added to a lyophobic sol, the lyophobic sol becomes **significantly more resistant to coagulation**. This stabilizing effect is called **protective action**, and the lyophilic colloid that provides this protection is called a **protective colloid**.

### 1.2 Definition

> **Protective action** is the phenomenon whereby a **lyophilic colloid** (the protective colloid) prevents the **coagulation of a lyophobic colloid** by electrolytes or other destabilizing agents.

The lyophilic colloid is said to "protect" the lyophobic sol.

### 1.3 Common Protective Colloids and Their Uses

| Protective Colloid | Protects | Application |
|--------------------|----------|-------------|
| Gelatin | Gold, silver, platinum sols | Photography (AgBr in gelatin) |
| Casein | Fat globules (oil droplets) | Milk stability, ice cream |
| Egg albumin | Various colloidal particles | Pharmaceutical preparations |
| Gum arabic (acacia) | Pigments, resins | Paints, inks, confectionery |
| Starch | Iodine sol | Iodine formulations |
| Dextrin | Metal sols | Industrial metal colloids |
| Sodium alginate | Food colloids | Thickening, stabilizing food emulsions |

## 2. Gold Number

### 2.1 Definition

The concept of **Gold Number** was introduced by **Zsigmondy (1901)** to quantitatively express the protective power of a lyophilic colloid.

> **Gold Number** is defined as the **minimum mass (in milligrams) of a protective colloid required to prevent the coagulation of 10 mL of a standard red gold sol** when 1 mL of 10% NaCl solution is added.

**Unit:** mg (milligrams)

**Notation:** Denoted by symbol **G** or **Z** (Zsigmondy number).

**Important Note on Definition:**
- A smaller gold number means a **more efficient** protective colloid (less is needed to prevent coagulation).
- The coagulation is detected by a change in color: **red sol → blue/violet** (aggregation causes red-shift in plasmon resonance) → **gray/colorless precipitate**.

### 2.2 Gold Numbers of Common Protective Colloids

| Protective Colloid | Gold Number (mg) | Protection Power |
|--------------------|------------------|------------------|
| Gelatin | 0.005–0.01 | **Highest** |
| Haemoglobin | 0.03–0.07 | Very high |
| Egg albumin | 0.08–0.10 | Very high |
| Casein | 0.01–0.02 | Very high |
| Gum arabic | 0.15–0.25 | Moderate |
| Starch | 10–25 | Low |
| Dextrin | 20–50 | Low |
| Potato starch | > 25 | Low |

**Interpretation:** Gelatin has the lowest gold number (0.005–0.01 mg) → it is the **most effective protective colloid**. Starch requires much more (10–25 mg) → it is a **poor protective colloid**.

**Inversely:** Gold Number ∝ 1/(Protective Power)

### 2.3 Practical Significance of Gold Number

1. **Identifying unknown proteins:** The gold number of a sample can help identify the type of protein or biopolymer present.
2. **Quality control in photography:** Gelatin quality in photographic films is assessed partly by its gold number.
3. **Drug formulation:** Choosing the right protective colloid (low gold number) for stabilizing pharmaceutical sol preparations.
4. **Standardization of colloid formulations:** Ensures consistent protective ability of stabilizer batches.

## 3. Mechanism of Protection

### 3.1 Steric Stabilization (Main Mechanism)

1. Lyophilic colloid (polymer/protein) molecules **adsorb** onto the surface of the lyophobic colloidal particles.
2. This forms a **thick polymer layer** (hydration shell) around each lyophobic particle.
3. When two such protected particles approach each other:
   - The **polymer layers overlap** → unfavorable thermodynamically (entropy decrease + osmotic repulsion).
   - This creates a **steric repulsion force** that keeps the particles apart.
4. The electrostatic double layer is no longer the sole stabilizer.

```
WITHOUT protective colloid:        WITH protective colloid:
                                   
   [Au particle]                   [  (gelatin)   Au particle   (gelatin)  ]
        +                                     +
   [Au particle]                   [  (gelatin)   Au particle   (gelatin)  ]
        ↓                                     ↓
  COAGULATION occurs               Steric barrier PREVENTS COAGULATION
  (electrolyte compresses          (polymer layers repel each other)
   double layer → particles
   collide irreversibly)
```

### 3.2 Electrostatic Component

- The adsorbed lyophilic polymer also carries **ionizable groups** (e.g., –COOH, –NH₂ in proteins) that add charge to the particle surface.
- This **enhances the electrostatic repulsion** in addition to steric stabilization.

### 3.3 Solvation Layer

- Lyophilic polymer chains are **highly solvated** (surrounded by water molecules in case of aqueous systems).
- This solvation layer acts as an additional physical barrier.

## 4. Industrial Applications of Colloids

### 4.1 Food Industry

1. **Milk:** A natural **oil-in-water emulsion** stabilized by casein (a protein protective colloid). Homogenization reduces fat globule size to < 1 µm.
2. **Mayonnaise and salad dressings:** O/W emulsions stabilized by egg yolk lecithin (a natural emulsifier/protective colloid).
3. **Ice cream:** A complex colloidal system — fat emulsion stabilized by milk proteins, with air bubbles trapped in a partially frozen aqueous phase.
4. **Chocolate:** A suspension of cocoa particles in fat, stabilized by lecithin.
5. **Bread and cakes:** The gluten network in dough is a macromolecular colloid; fermentation produces CO₂ gas bubbles creating foam.

### 4.2 Paints and Inks

1. **Paints** are sols of pigments (finely divided solids) in a liquid medium (water or oil), stabilized by protective colloids (resins, gums).
2. **Printing inks** are sols of carbon black or pigments in oils, often with gum arabic as a protective colloid.
3. **India ink** = carbon black sol in water, stabilized by shellac and gum arabic.

### 4.3 Rubber Industry

1. **Vulcanization:** Natural rubber is a macromolecular colloid of polyisoprene chains. Vulcanization (treatment with sulfur at high temperature) creates crosslinks.
2. **Latex:** Natural rubber latex is a colloid of rubber particles in water, stabilized by proteins and fatty acid soaps.
3. **Synthetic rubber manufacture:** Emulsion polymerization produces synthetic rubbers (SBR, NBR) as colloidal dispersions.

### 4.4 Photography

1. Photographic film contains **silver halide (AgBr, AgCl, AgI)** crystals of colloidal size (0.1–1 µm) suspended in **gelatin** (the protective colloid).
2. Gelatin provides:
   - Mechanical support for the crystals.
   - Protective action to prevent premature aggregation.
   - A hydrophilic matrix for developer chemicals to penetrate.

### 4.5 Leather Industry (Tanning)

1. Raw hide contains **collagen fibers** (protein, lyophilic colloid).
2. Treatment with **tannin** (tannic acid solution) or **chrome tanning salts** causes controlled coagulation of collagen.
3. This makes leather hard, water-resistant, and durable.
4. The process involves electrostatic and covalent interactions between tannin and collagen.

### 4.6 Paper and Textile Industry

1. **Paper sizing:** Rosin is used as a colloidal coating on paper fibers to make paper water-resistant.
2. **Textile dyeing:** Mordants (colloidal metal hydroxides like Cr(OH)₃) are fixed to textile fibers and then bind dye molecules, providing deep color.
3. **Textile finishing:** Starch and gum solutions (colloids) are used to provide stiffness.

### 4.7 Medicines and Pharmaceuticals

1. **Colloidal silver** (AgNPs): Used as antimicrobial agents; silver nanoparticles kill bacteria by disrupting cell membranes and releasing Ag⁺ ions.
2. **Colloidal sulfur:** Used in skin treatments for fungal infections and acne.
3. **Colloidal bismuth:** Pepto-Bismol contains bismuth subsalicylate in colloidal form for stomach coating.
4. **Liposomal drug delivery:** Drugs are encapsulated in liposomes (colloidal vesicles of phospholipids) for targeted delivery — reduces side effects.
5. **Blood substitutes:** Colloidal preparations of albumin or hydroxyethyl starch are used as plasma expanders in surgery.

### 4.8 Colloidal Fuel (Industrial)

1. **Colloidal fuel** is a suspension of fine coal or carbon particles in fuel oil, acting as a colloidal system.
2. These are used in certain industrial burners and provide more even combustion.

## 5. Biological Applications of Colloids

### 5.1 Blood as a Colloidal System

Human blood is an extremely complex colloidal system:

| Component | Dispersed Phase | Dispersion Medium | Type |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|------|
| Red blood cells (RBCs) | Solid (cells) | Plasma | Suspension/sol |
| Plasma proteins (albumin, globulin) | Macromolecular colloid | Water (plasma) | Lyophilic sol |
| Lipoproteins (LDL, HDL) | Lipid particles | Plasma | Lyophobic sol |
| Blood clotting | Fibrin gel formation | Plasma | Sol → Gel transition |

### 5.2 Plasma Proteins as Protective Colloids

- **Albumin** (most abundant plasma protein, ~40 g/L) stabilizes the other colloidal components of blood.
- It acts as a protective colloid for insoluble lipids and fat-soluble vitamins in circulation.
- **Blood viscosity** is largely determined by its colloidal nature.

### 5.3 Cell Membranes

- Cell membranes are **bilayer colloidal structures** (liposomes) of phospholipids with embedded proteins.
- Transport, signaling, and structural integrity all depend on their colloidal properties.

### 5.4 Cytoplasm and Intracellular Colloids

- Cytoplasm is a **sol-gel** system — it can switch between more fluid (sol) and more solid-like (gel) states.
- This **sol-gel transformation** is critical for cell movement, division, and organelle transport.

## 6. Medical Applications of Colloids

### 6.1 Drug Delivery Systems

| System | Type | Drug | Advantage |
|--------|------|------|-----------|
| Liposomes | Colloidal vesicle | Doxorubicin (cancer) | Targeted delivery, reduced toxicity |
| Nanoparticles | Polymer colloid | Various | Extended release, oral/IV delivery |
| Microemulsions | O/W or W/O | Cyclosporin | Enhanced solubility of lipophilic drugs |
| Colloidal gold | Metal nanoparticle | siRNA, antibodies | Theranostics (therapy + diagnostics) |

### 6.2 Diagnostics

1. **Colloidal gold in rapid tests:** Pregnancy tests (hCG detection) and COVID-19 rapid antigen tests use colloidal gold particles conjugated with antibodies. When antigen binds, particles aggregate → visible colored line.
2. **Turbidimetry and nephelometry:** Measure the turbidity of colloidal sols to quantify proteins and other analytes in blood.

### 6.3 Blood Coagulation

- Blood coagulation is a controlled colloidal coagulation process.
- When blood vessels are damaged, the sol-like fibrinogen (protein colloid) converts to fibrin gel, trapping platelets → clot forms.
- Anticoagulants (heparin, warfarin) interfere with this process.

## 7. Environmental Applications of Colloids

### 7.1 Water Treatment

1. **Coagulation-flocculation-sedimentation:** A multi-step process:
   - **Coagulation:** Alum (KAl(SO₄)₂) is added → Al³⁺ neutralizes negative charge on clay/silt colloids.
   - **Flocculation:** Gentle stirring causes Al(OH)₃ floc to grow by sweep flocculation.
   - **Sedimentation:** Heavy floc settles, taking colloidal particles with it.
   - **Filtration:** Remaining particles removed by sand filter.

2. **Algae/cyanobacteria treatment:** Colloidal clay or polyelectrolytes coagulate algae cells for removal.

### 7.2 Air Pollution Control

1. **Electrostatic precipitator (Cottrell precipitator):** Used in industrial chimneys to remove colloidal smoke and dust particles.
   - **Principle:** Electrophoresis — charged smoke particles are attracted to high-voltage electrodes (50,000–100,000 V) and deposited.
   - Removes >99% of particulates from flue gases.
   - Used in cement plants, coal-fired power plants, smelters.

### 7.3 Soil Science

1. Clay colloids in soil control nutrient retention, water holding capacity, and soil structure.
2. **Soil colloidal particles** (predominantly clay minerals: kaolinite, montmorillonite, illite) carry negative charges → attract and hold positively charged nutrient ions (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, K⁺, NH₄⁺).
3. **Soil flocculation by lime (CaO/Ca(OH)₂):** Adding lime to acidic clay soils causes flocculation → improves soil structure for agriculture.

## 8. Numerical Examples

### Problem 1 — Gold Number Calculation
**Q:** In a gold number experiment, 0.004 mg of gelatin is found to protect 10 mL of gold sol from coagulation by NaCl. What is the gold number of this gelatin? Is this a good protective colloid?

**Solution:**
```
Gold Number = minimum mass of protective colloid (mg) that prevents coagulation
            = 0.004 mg

Comparing with typical values:
- Gelatin gold number: 0.005–0.01 mg (typical range)
- This sample (0.004 mg) is within/slightly below the typical range.

Conclusion: This gelatin is an EXCELLENT protective colloid (very low gold number).
```

### Problem 2 — Cottrell Precipitator Efficiency
**Q:** A Cottrell precipitator treats 1000 m³/min of flue gas containing 50 mg/m³ of colloidal particles. If the efficiency is 99.2%, calculate the mass of particles deposited per hour.

**Solution:**
```
Total particle load = 1000 m³/min × 50 mg/m³ = 50,000 mg/min

Mass deposited per minute = 99.2% × 50,000 = 49,600 mg/min

Mass deposited per hour = 49,600 × 60 = 2,976,000 mg/hr = 2.976 kg/hr ≈ 2.98 kg/hr
```

### Problem 3 — Colloidal Silver Drug Formulation
**Q:** A colloidal silver preparation for wound treatment contains 100 mg of Ag per 100 mL. If the average particle radius is 8 nm, estimate the number of silver particles per mL and the total surface area per mL.

```
Volume of one particle = (4/3)π(8×10⁻⁹)³ = 2.145 × 10⁻²⁴ m³

Density of silver = 10,490 kg/m³ = 10.49 g/cm³

Mass of one particle = 10.49 g/cm³ × 2.145×10⁻¹⁸ cm³ = 2.25 × 10⁻¹⁷ g

Mass of Ag per mL = 100 mg/100 mL = 1 mg/mL = 10⁻³ g/mL

Number of particles per mL = 10⁻³ g / 2.25×10⁻¹⁷ g
                            = 4.44 × 10¹³ particles/mL

Surface area of one particle = 4πr² = 4π(8×10⁻⁹)² = 8.04×10⁻¹⁶ m²

Total surface area per mL = 4.44×10¹³ × 8.04×10⁻¹⁶ m² = 0.0357 m²/mL = 357 cm²/mL

A remarkably large surface area → explains the high antimicrobial activity of colloidal silver!
```
