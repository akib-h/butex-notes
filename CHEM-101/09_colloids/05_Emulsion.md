## 1. Definition and Introduction

### 1.1 Definition

An **emulsion** is a **colloidal system** in which **two immiscible or partially miscible liquids** are dispersed in each other. One liquid forms the **dispersed phase (internal phase)** and the other forms the **continuous phase (external phase or dispersion medium)**.

> An emulsion is thus a **liquid-in-liquid colloid**.

Emulsions are thermodynamically **unstable** (due to high interfacial area) but can be **kinetically stabilized** by the use of emulsifying agents.

### 1.2 Why Are Immiscible Liquids Important?

The two most common immiscible liquid pairs are:
- **Oil and water** (O/W or W/O emulsions)

"Oil" in colloid chemistry refers to **any hydrophobic, water-insoluble liquid** — not just cooking oil. This includes:
- Hydrocarbons (petroleum, mineral oil)
- Fats and triglycerides
- Waxes
- Chlorinated solvents (CCl₄, CHCl₃)
- Essential oils (terpenes)

### 1.3 Droplet Size in Emulsions

| Emulsion Type | Droplet Size |
|---------------|-------------|
| Macroemulsion (conventional) | 0.1 – 100 µm |
| **Nanoemulsion** | **20 – 500 nm** |
| Microemulsion | 5 – 100 nm (thermodynamically stable) |
| Miniemulsion | 50 – 500 nm |

*Note:* **Microemulsions** are thermodynamically **stable** (spontaneous formation with very low interfacial tension), unlike regular emulsions. They are considered either a type of emulsion or a separate category depending on the textbook.

## 2. Components of an Emulsion

Every emulsion consists of at least **three essential components**:

### 2.1 Dispersed Phase (Internal Phase)

- The liquid that is broken up into droplets.
- In an **O/W emulsion**: oil is the dispersed phase.
- In a **W/O emulsion**: water is the dispersed phase.

### 2.2 Dispersion Medium (External/Continuous Phase)

- The liquid in which the droplets are dispersed.
- In an **O/W emulsion**: water is the continuous phase.
- In a **W/O emulsion**: oil is the continuous phase.

### 2.3 Emulsifying Agent (Emulsifier / Surfactant)

- A substance that **reduces the interfacial tension** between oil and water, making it possible to disperse one liquid in the other and stabilizing the resulting emulsion.
- Without an emulsifier, the emulsion quickly separates.
- Also called: **emulgents**, **surfactants**, **emulsifying agents**, **amphiphiles**.

**Why is an emulsifier necessary?**
```
Without emulsifier:               With emulsifier:

Oil + Water                        Oil + Water + Emulsifier
  ↓ shake                            ↓ shake
  ↓ (unstable)                       ↓
Oil layer                          STABLE EMULSION
Water layer                        (hours, days, months stable)
(separated in seconds/minutes)
```

## 3. Emulsifying Agents (Emulsifiers)

### 3.1 Definition

An **emulsifying agent (emulsifier)** is an amphiphilic substance that **adsorbs at the oil-water interface** and:
1. Reduces the **interfacial tension** (γ) between oil and water.
2. Creates a **mechanical barrier** (interfacial film) around each droplet.
3. Imparts **charge** (electrostatic repulsion) or **steric repulsion** to prevent coalescence.

### 3.2 Classification of Emulsifiers

#### 3.2.1 Surfactant (Small Molecule) Emulsifiers

These are amphiphilic molecules with a **hydrophilic head group** and a **hydrophobic tail**.

**A) Anionic Surfactants:**
- Carry a **negative charge** in solution.
- Favor **O/W emulsions**.
- Examples:
  - Sodium stearate (soap): CH₃(CH₂)₁₆COO⁻ Na⁺
  - Sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS): C₁₂H₂₅OSO₃⁻ Na⁺
  - Sodium lauryl ether sulfate (SLES): Used in shampoos

**B) Cationic Surfactants:**
- Carry a **positive charge** in solution.
- Can form W/O or O/W emulsions depending on the system.
- Examples:
  - Cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB): C₁₆H₃₃N(CH₃)₃⁺ Br⁻
  - Benzalkonium chloride: Used as antiseptic emulsifier

**C) Nonionic Surfactants:**
- **No net charge**; stabilize by steric repulsion.
- Less sensitive to ionic strength → more widely used.
- Examples:
  - Tween series (Polysorbates): Tween 20, Tween 80 — used in food, pharma
  - Span series (Sorbitan esters): Span 20, Span 80 — used in cosmetics
  - Polyoxyethylene glycol ethers (Brij series)

**D) Zwitterionic (Amphoteric) Surfactants:**
- Have both positive and negative charges.
- Examples:
  - Lecithin (phosphatidylcholine): Found in egg yolk — forms zwitterionic head group.
  - Betaines: Used in personal care products.

#### 3.2.2 Macromolecular (Polymeric) Emulsifiers

- **Proteins:** Casein (milk), whey protein, egg albumin, gelatin — natural emulsifiers.
- **Polysaccharides:** Gum arabic, pectin, modified starch, xanthan gum.
- **Synthetic polymers:** Polyvinyl alcohol (PVA), polyethylene glycol (PEG) block copolymers.

These act primarily by **steric stabilization** — they form a thick, viscoelastic film at the interface.

#### 3.2.3 Solid Particle Emulsifiers (Pickering Emulsions)

- **Fine solid particles** (instead of surfactants) can stabilize emulsions by adsorbing at the oil-water interface.
- Called **Pickering emulsions** (Pickering, 1907).
- The solid must be partially wetted by both oil and water (contact angle near 90°).
- Examples:
  - Silica nanoparticles
  - Clay platelets (montmorillonite, Laponite)
  - Starch granules
  - Carbon black particles
- **Advantage:** No chemical surfactant needed → very stable; used in food, pharmaceuticals, and oilfield applications.

## 4. Mechanism of Emulsification

### 4.1 Surface Energy Consideration

The **interfacial energy (G_interface)** required to create an emulsion is:
$$\Delta G = \gamma_{OW} \cdot \Delta A$$

Where:
- $\gamma_{OW}$ = oil-water interfacial tension (for pure water-oil: ~50 mN/m)
- $\Delta A$ = increase in interfacial area on emulsification

**Example calculation:**
If 10 mL of oil is emulsified into droplets of radius 1 µm:
```
Volume of oil = 10 mL = 10 × 10⁻⁶ m³

Volume per droplet = (4/3)π(10⁻⁶)³ = 4.19 × 10⁻¹⁸ m³

Number of droplets = 10⁻⁵ / 4.19 × 10⁻¹⁸ = 2.39 × 10¹²

Surface area of all droplets = N × 4πr²
                              = 2.39 × 10¹² × 4π × (10⁻⁶)² = 30.0 m²

Energy = γ × ΔA = 50 × 10⁻³ N/m × 30 m² = 1.5 J

But with emulsifier (reduces γ to ~1 mN/m):
Energy = 1 × 10⁻³ × 30 = 0.03 J ← 50-fold reduction!
```

This shows why emulsifiers are essential — they dramatically reduce the energy needed and the thermodynamic driving force for phase separation.

### 4.2 Interfacial Film Theory

When an emulsifier adsorbs at the oil-water interface, it:
1. Forms a **condensed, coherent interfacial film** (monomolecular layer or thick multilayer for polymers).
2. This film has **mechanical strength** (elasticity and viscosity) — it resists rupture when droplets collide.
3. The **Gibbs-Marangoni effect:** If the film is locally thinned (by a collision), the surfactant concentration drops locally → surface tension rises locally → liquid flows from lower surface tension region to higher surface tension region → **repairs the film automatically** (self-healing).

### 4.3 Bancroft's Rule

**Bancroft's Rule (1913):** The emulsifier preferentially **dissolves in the continuous phase**.

- If the emulsifier is **more soluble in water** → forms **O/W emulsion** (oil drops in water).
- If the emulsifier is **more soluble in oil** → forms **W/O emulsion** (water drops in oil).

**Explanation using geometry (Israelachvili Packing Parameter):**

The **critical packing parameter (CPP)** determines the type of aggregate/emulsion formed:

$$CPP = \frac{v}{a_0 \cdot l_c}$$

Where:
- $v$ = volume of hydrophobic tail
- $a_0$ = optimal head group area
- $l_c$ = critical chain length

| CPP Value | Preferred Structure | Emulsion Type |
|-----------|--------------------|----|
| CPP < 1/3 | Spherical micelles | O/W |
| 1/3 < CPP < 1/2 | Cylindrical micelles | O/W |
| CPP ≈ 1 | Bilayer / vesicles | — |
| CPP > 1 | Inverted micelles | W/O |

## 5. HLB (Hydrophilic-Lipophilic Balance) System

### 5.1 Definition

The **HLB number** (introduced by Griffin, 1949) is a **dimensionless scale (0–20)** that describes the balance of the hydrophilic and hydrophobic parts of an emulsifier molecule.

$$\text{HLB} = 20 \times \frac{M_H}{M}$$

Where:
- $M_H$ = molecular mass of the hydrophilic portion
- $M$ = total molecular mass

For polyoxyethylene (POE) nonionic surfactants:
$$\text{HLB} = \frac{\% \text{POE content}}{5}$$

### 5.2 HLB Scale and Applications

| HLB Range | Behavior | Application |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| 0–3 | Strongly lipophilic | Antifoaming agents |
| 4–6 | Lipophilic | **W/O emulsifier** |
| 7–9 | Intermediate | Wetting agent |
| 8–18 | Hydrophilic | **O/W emulsifier** |
| 13–15 | Very hydrophilic | Detergent, cleaner |
| 15–18 | Strongly hydrophilic | Solubilizer |

### 5.3 Required HLB (RHLB)

Each oil phase has a **required HLB** (the HLB of emulsifier needed to make a stable emulsion with that oil):

| Oil | Required HLB (for O/W) | Required HLB (for W/O) |
|-----|------------------------|------------------------|
| Mineral oil | 10 | 4 |
| Beeswax | 9 | 5 |
| Lanolin | 15 | 8 |
| Castor oil | 14 | — |
| Paraffin wax | 10 | 4 |

### 5.4 Blending Emulsifiers to Achieve Required HLB

Mixed emulsifiers can be blended to achieve any required HLB:

$$\text{HLB}_{blend} = f_A \cdot \text{HLB}_A + f_B \cdot \text{HLB}_B$$

**Example:** Required HLB = 12. Using Span 80 (HLB = 4.3) and Tween 80 (HLB = 15.0):
```
12 = f_A × 4.3 + (1 - f_A) × 15.0
12 = 4.3f_A + 15 - 15f_A
-3 = -10.7f_A
f_A = 0.280 (28% Span 80, 72% Tween 80)
```

## 6. Stability and Instability of Emulsions

### 6.1 Causes of Emulsion Instability

Emulsions can undergo several forms of instability:

#### 6.1.1 Creaming
- Droplets **rise to the top** (for O/W, oil is less dense) due to buoyancy — Stokes' law.
- **Not true phase separation** — the emulsion can be re-homogenized by shaking.
- Prevented by: reducing droplet size, increasing viscosity, density matching.

#### 6.1.2 Sedimentation
- Droplets **sink to the bottom** (dispersed phase denser than continuous phase).
- Common in W/O emulsions.

#### 6.1.3 Flocculation
- Droplets **aggregate (cluster)** without losing their individual identity.
- **Reversible** — gentle agitation can redisperse.
- Leads to creaming or sedimentation.

#### 6.1.4 Coalescence
- Two droplets **merge** to form one larger droplet.
- **Irreversible** — once droplets merge, they cannot be separated without re-homogenization.
- Requires rupture of the interfacial film.
- Ultimately leads to complete phase separation (Ostwald ripening → total separation).

#### 6.1.5 Ostwald Ripening (Disproportionation)
- Small droplets **dissolve** (higher chemical potential due to curvature → Kelvin effect) and the dissolved material **deposits on larger droplets**.
- Net effect: small droplets disappear, large droplets grow → emulsion destabilizes.
- Rate described by the **LSW (Lifshitz-Slyozov-Wagner) equation**:
$$\frac{dr^3}{dt} = \frac{8\gamma_{OW} D C_\infty V_m}{9RT}$$

#### 6.1.6 Phase Inversion
- The **continuous and dispersed phases switch roles** (O/W → W/O or vice versa).
- Triggered by:
  - Changing the phase volume ratio
  - Changing temperature (phase inversion temperature, PIT)
  - Changing electrolyte concentration
  - Adding a different emulsifier

### 6.2 Preventing Instability

| Problem | Prevention |
|---------|-----------|
| Creaming/sedimentation | Reduce droplet size; add viscosity modifier (xanthan gum) |
| Flocculation | Ensure adequate zeta potential (> ±30 mV) |
| Coalescence | Use strong interfacial film (protein/polymer emulsifier) |
| Ostwald ripening | Add slightly soluble co-emulsifier; match refractive index |
| Phase inversion | Control phase volume ratio (< 74%) |

## 7. Methods of Preparation of Emulsions

### 7.1 Dry Gum (Continental) Method
1. Mix oil and emulsifier (gum arabic) in dry vessel first (4:1 ratio).
2. Add water rapidly in one portion → primary emulsion forms.
3. Dilute to desired concentration.
- **Used for:** Pharmaceutical O/W preparations with gum arabic.

### 7.2 Wet Gum (English) Method
1. Triturate (grind) gum with water first → form mucilage.
2. Add oil slowly with continuous trituration → O/W emulsion.
- **Used for:** Same as dry gum, but different order of mixing.

### 7.3 High-Speed (Mechanical) Homogenization
1. Pre-mix all components with coarse mixing.
2. Pass through **homogenizer** at high pressure (50–300 MPa).
3. Homogenizer forces mixture through a narrow gap → extreme shear forces → fine droplets.
- **Produces:** Nanoemulsions and fine macroemulsions with d < 1 µm.
- **Used in:** Food (milk homogenization), pharma, cosmetics.

### 7.4 Ultrasonication
1. Apply high-frequency ultrasound (20–100 kHz) to the pre-emulsion.
2. Cavitation creates enormous shear → fine droplets.
- **Advantage:** Produces very fine, stable nanoemulsions.
- **Limitation:** Suitable for small volumes only (scale-up difficult).

### 7.5 Phase Inversion Methods

**Phase Inversion Composition (PIC):**
- Add water to an oil/emulsifier mixture.
- Passes through W/O phase → inversion point → O/W nanoemulsion with very fine droplets.

**Phase Inversion Temperature (PIT):**
- Use nonionic surfactants whose HLB changes with temperature.
- Heat the system → passes through PIT (Winsor III microemulsion state with very low γ).
- Quench rapidly → fine O/W nanoemulsion forms.

## 8. Identification Tests for Emulsion Type

| Test | O/W Emulsion | W/O Emulsion |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Dilution test** | Miscible with water (can be diluted with water) | Miscible with oil (can be diluted with oil) |
| **Conductivity test** | High conductivity (water is conductive continuous phase) | Low conductivity (oil is non-conductive continuous phase) |
| **Dye test (water-soluble dye, e.g., methylene blue)** | Continuous phase stains uniformly blue | Only droplets stain blue (seen as blue dots in microscope) |
| **Dye test (oil-soluble dye, e.g., Sudan III)** | Only droplets stain red | Continuous phase stains red uniformly |
| **Filter paper wetting** | Leaves wet water stain | Leaves oil stain |
| **Cobalt chloride paper** | Turns pink (water is external) | Remains blue (oil is external) |
| **Fluorescence** | Fluorescent dye (in water phase) appears continuous | Fluorescent dye appears as discrete droplets |

## 9. Numerical Examples

### Problem 1 — HLB Calculation
**Q:** Calculate the HLB of polysorbate 80 (Tween 80) knowing:
- Molecular formula: C₆₄H₁₂₄O₂₆
- Approximate composition: 65.7% polyoxyethylene (POE) groups

```
HLB = % POE content / 5 = 65.7 / 5 = 13.14 ≈ 13.1

This value (13.1) indicates Tween 80 is a good O/W emulsifier
(O/W emulsifiers require HLB 8–18).
```

### Problem 2 — Droplet Size from Stokes Creaming
**Q:** An O/W emulsion contains oil droplets of radius 2 µm. The oil density is 900 kg/m³, water density is 1000 kg/m³, and water viscosity is 10⁻³ Pa·s. Calculate the creaming velocity (the rate at which oil droplets rise).

```
Creaming velocity = 2r²(ρ_water - ρ_oil)g / (9η)   [Stokes' Law, upward]
= 2 × (2×10⁻⁶)² × (1000 - 900) × 9.81 / (9 × 10⁻³)
= 2 × 4×10⁻¹² × 100 × 9.81 / 9×10⁻³
= 7.85×10⁻⁹ / 9×10⁻³
= 8.72 × 10⁻⁷ m/s ≈ 0.075 m/day ≈ 7.5 cm/day

Conclusion: This emulsion would show significant creaming within a day!
To reduce: homogenize to r = 0.2 µm → velocity = 8.72×10⁻⁹ m/s → 0.075 cm/day (100× less)
```

### Problem 3 — Interfacial Area Calculation
**Q:** 50 mL of oil is emulsified into 950 mL of water to form a O/W emulsion with average droplet diameter 500 nm. Calculate the interfacial area.

```
Volume of dispersed oil = 50 mL = 50 × 10⁻⁶ m³
Radius of droplet = 250 nm = 2.5 × 10⁻⁷ m

Volume per droplet = (4/3)π r³ = (4/3) × π × (2.5×10⁻⁷)³ = 6.545 × 10⁻²⁰ m³

Number of droplets = 50×10⁻⁶ / 6.545×10⁻²⁰ = 7.643 × 10¹⁴

Surface area per droplet = 4πr² = 4π(2.5×10⁻⁷)² = 7.854 × 10⁻¹³ m²

Total interfacial area = N × area = 7.643×10¹⁴ × 7.854×10⁻¹³ = 600 m²

ENORMOUS interfacial area from just 50 mL of oil!
```
