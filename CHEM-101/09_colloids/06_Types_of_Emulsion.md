## 1. Classification Overview

Emulsions can be classified on **multiple bases**:

```
EMULSIONS
    │
    ├── Based on PHASES (Most fundamental)
    │       ├── Oil-in-Water (O/W)
    │       ├── Water-in-Oil (W/O)
    │       └── Multiple (W/O/W or O/W/O)
    │
    ├── Based on DROPLET SIZE
    │       ├── Macroemulsion (0.1–100 µm)
    │       ├── Nanoemulsion (20–500 nm)
    │       └── Microemulsion (5–100 nm, thermodynamically stable)
    │
    ├── Based on STABILIZER TYPE
    │       ├── Surfactant-stabilized
    │       ├── Polymer/protein-stabilized
    │       └── Pickering (solid-particle-stabilized)
    │
    └── Based on CONSISTENCY
            ├── Liquid emulsions
            ├── Semi-solid emulsions (creams)
            └── Solid emulsions (some ointments)
```

## 2. Type 1 — Oil-in-Water (O/W) Emulsions

### 2.1 Definition

In an **O/W emulsion**, **oil droplets** (dispersed phase) are dispersed in a **continuous aqueous (water) phase**.

```
  Continuous phase = WATER
  Dispersed phase = OIL

  [Water]  ○  ○  ○  [Water]  ○  ○
  ○  ○  [Water]  ○  ○  [Water]  ○
         ↑
       Oil droplets suspended in water
```

### 2.2 Emulsifiers for O/W

- **High-HLB surfactants** (HLB 8–18): Tween 20, Tween 80, SDS (sodium dodecyl sulfate).
- **Protein emulsifiers:** Whey protein, casein, egg albumin (all water-soluble → favor O/W).
- **Anionic surfactants:** Soap (sodium stearate) — more soluble in water than oil.

### 2.3 Preparation Considerations

**Bancroft's Rule:** Emulsifier must be **more soluble in water** → creates O/W emulsion.

**Phase volume ratio:** O/W emulsions are generally favored when the **oil volume fraction (φ)** is less than 0.74 (the theoretical maximum packing of spheres).

### 2.4 Properties

| Property | Value/Characteristic |
|----------|---------------------|
| Continuous phase | Water |
| Electrical conductivity | **High** (water conducts electricity) |
| Dilutability | Miscible with water; immiscible with oil |
| Feel on skin | Non-greasy, light, easily absorbed |
| Evaporation | Evaporates (due to water content) — cooling effect |
| Microbial susceptibility | Higher (water supports microbial growth) |

### 2.5 Examples and Applications

**Natural O/W Emulsions:**
- **Milk:** ~3.5% fat droplets (d ≈ 1–10 µm) in water, stabilized by casein micelles and whey proteins.
- **Cream (fresh):** Similar to milk but higher fat content.
- **Human blood:** Complex O/W-type system (lipoproteins dispersed in aqueous plasma).

**Processed/Formulated O/W Emulsions:**
- **Mayonnaise:** ~80% oil-in-water emulsion; egg yolk lecithin is the emulsifier.
- **Salad dressings:** O/W emulsions with various emulsifiers.
- **Oil-in-water pharmaceutical preparations:** Injectable emulsions (fat emulsions for parenteral nutrition).
- **Sunscreen O/W:** Light, non-greasy formulations.
- **Vanishing creams (cosmetics):** O/W emulsions that seem to "vanish" into the skin.

## 3. Type 2 — Water-in-Oil (W/O) Emulsions

### 3.1 Definition

In a **W/O emulsion**, **water droplets** (dispersed phase) are dispersed in a **continuous oil phase**.

```
  Continuous phase = OIL
  Dispersed phase = WATER

  [Oil]  ●  ●  ●  [Oil]  ●  ●
  ●  ●  [Oil]  ●  ●  [Oil]  ●
         ↑
       Water droplets suspended in oil
```

### 3.2 Emulsifiers for W/O

- **Low-HLB surfactants** (HLB 3–8): Span 80 (sorbitan monooleate, HLB=4.3), Span 20, lanolin.
- **Metallic soaps:** Calcium, magnesium, or zinc soaps (divalent soaps are more oil-soluble).
- **Natural W/O emulsifiers:** Wool fat (lanolin), beeswax.
- **Nonionic polymeric stabilizers at low HLB**.

### 3.3 Properties

| Property | Value/Characteristic |
|----------|---------------------|
| Continuous phase | Oil |
| Electrical conductivity | **Low** (oil doesn't conduct) |
| Dilutability | Miscible with oil; immiscible with water |
| Feel on skin | **Greasy, occlusive** — forms barrier |
| Evaporation | Minimal (oil doesn't evaporate easily) |
| Occlusion | High — traps moisture in skin |
| Microbial susceptibility | Lower (oil phase doesn't support microbes) |

### 3.4 Examples and Applications

**Natural W/O Emulsions:**
- **Butter:** ~20% water droplets in ~80% milk fat (continuous phase).
- **Margarine:** Manufactured W/O emulsion of water in partially hydrogenated vegetable oil.
- **Crude petroleum emulsions:** Water droplets in crude oil — a major problem in the oil industry (must be broken).

**Formulated W/O Emulsions:**
- **Cold cream (cosmetics):** The original cosmetic cream (W/O); leaves a greasy, protective film.
- **Barrier creams:** W/O emulsions that protect skin from water-based irritants (used in industrial settings).
- **Water-repellent sunscreens:** W/O formulations that resist washing off in water.
- **Ointments (pharmaceutical):** W/O semi-solid emulsions for skin drug delivery.
- **Water-in-diesel emulsions:** Used as emulsified fuel to reduce NOₓ emissions.

## 4. Type 3 — Multiple Emulsions

### 4.1 Definition

**Multiple emulsions** (also called **double emulsions**) are complex systems where droplets of one emulsion are themselves dispersed in another liquid. They are "emulsions within emulsions."

### 4.2 Types

**A) W/O/W (Water-in-Oil-in-Water):**
- Small water droplets are dispersed in oil droplets, which are dispersed in a continuous water phase.
- The **inner aqueous phase** may contain a dissolved drug, flavor, or nutrient.

```
  Outer water phase (W₂)
      ╭───────────────────────╮
      │  [OIL GLOBULE]        │
      │  ╭─────────────────╮  │
      │  │  ●  ●  ●  ●     │  │  ← Inner water droplets (W₁) containing drug
      │  │  ●  ●  ●        │  │
      │  ╰─────────────────╯  │
      │  [OIL GLOBULE]        │
      │  ╭─────────────────╮  │
      │  │  ●  ●  ●        │  │
      │  ╰─────────────────╯  │
      ╰───────────────────────╯
```

**B) O/W/O (Oil-in-Water-in-Oil):**
- Oil droplets in a water phase, which is itself dispersed in a continuous oil phase.
- Less common than W/O/W.

### 4.3 Preparation

Multiple emulsions are prepared in **two steps**:
1. **Step 1:** Prepare the primary emulsion (W/O for W/O/W type) using a **lipophilic (low-HLB) emulsifier** (e.g., Span 80).
2. **Step 2:** Re-emulsify the primary W/O emulsion in water using a **hydrophilic (high-HLB) emulsifier** (e.g., Tween 80).

**Critical requirement:** The emulsifiers for the two steps must be carefully chosen so they don't interfere with each other.

### 4.4 Applications

1. **Controlled drug release:** The inner aqueous phase acts as a reservoir; the oil membrane controls release rate. Used for:
   - Insulin delivery
   - Vaccine adjuvants
   - Sustained release of water-soluble drugs
2. **Flavor encapsulation:** Volatile flavors in inner water phase — protected from oxidation by oil membrane.
3. **Reduced-calorie food products:** Oil is "hidden" inside a water-in-oil-in-water matrix — appears to be oil-rich but contains significant water (fewer calories).
4. **Targeted delivery:** The double membrane provides protection to sensitive cargo (peptides, proteins).

## 5. Type 4 — Microemulsions

### 5.1 Definition

A **microemulsion** is a **thermodynamically stable**, optically **isotropic** (transparent or translucent), and **thermodynamically spontaneous** dispersion of two immiscible liquids stabilized by an interfacial film of surfactant (often with a co-surfactant).

**Key difference from regular emulsions:** Microemulsions form **spontaneously** (ΔG_formation < 0) due to ultralow interfacial tension.

### 5.2 Characteristics

| Property | Microemulsion | Regular Emulsion |
|----------|--------------|-----------------|
| Thermodynamics | Stable (ΔG < 0) | Metastable (ΔG > 0) |
| Formation | Spontaneous | Requires energy input |
| Droplet size | 5–100 nm | 100 nm – 100 µm |
| Appearance | Transparent/translucent | Milky white (opaque) |
| Long-term stability | Indefinite | Hours to months |

### 5.3 Components

Microemulsions typically require **four components**:
1. Oil
2. Water
3. Surfactant (typically at high concentration, 10–30%)
4. **Co-surfactant** (short-chain alcohol like 1-butanol, 1-pentanol) — reduces interfacial tension to near zero (< 0.01 mN/m)

### 5.4 Winsor Classification

Microemulsion phases were classified by **Winsor (1954)**:

| Type | Structure | Phase behavior |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| Winsor I | O/W microemulsion | Lower-phase microemulsion + excess oil |
| Winsor II | W/O microemulsion | Upper-phase microemulsion + excess water |
| Winsor III | Bicontinuous | Middle-phase microemulsion + excess oil + excess water |
| Winsor IV | Single-phase microemulsion | No excess phase |

### 5.5 Applications

1. **Enhanced oil recovery (EOR):** Microemulsions injected into oil reservoirs reduce interfacial tension → mobilize trapped oil.
2. **Drug solubilization (pharma):** Poorly water-soluble drugs are dissolved in the oil core of microemulsions. Example: **Neoral** (cyclosporin A microemulsion) for immunosuppression.
3. **Cleaning products:** Microemulsions clean both water-soluble and oil-soluble dirt simultaneously.
4. **Nanoreactor chemistry:** Chemical reactions can be carried out in microemulsion droplets → produces nanoparticles of controlled size.

## 6. Type 5 — Nanoemulsions

### 6.1 Definition

**Nanoemulsions** are emulsions with droplet diameters in the range of **20–500 nm** (often 50–200 nm). They are **kinetically stable** (not thermodynamically) but have exceptional long-term stability due to the small droplet size (which greatly reduces gravitational effects and Ostwald ripening).

### 6.2 Advantages Over Conventional Emulsions

| Property | Nanoemulsion | Conventional Emulsion |
|----------|-------------|-----------------------|
| Appearance | Translucent to transparent | Milky white |
| Stability | Months to years (no creaming) | Hours to weeks |
| Drug bioavailability | Enhanced | Standard |
| Skin penetration | Better (small size) | Limited |

### 6.3 Preparation Methods

1. **High-energy methods:** Microfluidizers, high-pressure homogenizers, ultrasonication.
2. **Low-energy methods:** Phase inversion temperature (PIT), phase inversion composition (PIC), spontaneous emulsification.

### 6.4 Applications

1. **Pharmaceutical:** Parenteral nutrition, anti-cancer drug delivery (Abraxane™).
2. **Food:** Premium beverages (fortified with vitamins/omega-3 in nanoemulsion form).
3. **Cosmetics:** Transparent serum formulations with nano-sized droplets for better skin feel.
4. **Sanitizers/disinfectants:** Nanoemulsions of essential oils with antimicrobial activity.

## 7. Type 6 — Pickering Emulsions

### 7.1 Definition

**Pickering emulsions** are emulsions stabilized by **solid particles** (instead of traditional surfactants) that adsorb at the oil-water interface.

Named after **S.U. Pickering** who described the phenomenon in 1907.

### 7.2 Mechanism

A solid particle adsorbs at the oil-water interface if its **three-phase contact angle (θ)** (measured through the water phase) is:
- **θ < 90°:** Particle is predominantly wetted by water → prefers to sit in water → stabilizes **O/W emulsion**.
- **θ > 90°:** Particle is predominantly wetted by oil → prefers to sit in oil → stabilizes **W/O emulsion**.
- **θ ≈ 90°:** Particle sits exactly at interface → maximum adsorption energy → most stable emulsion.

**Adsorption energy of particle at interface:**
$$\Delta G_{adsorption} = -\pi r_p^2 \gamma_{OW} (1 \pm \cos\theta)^2$$

Where $r_p$ is particle radius, $\gamma_{OW}$ is oil-water interfacial tension, and the ± depends on the direction of removal.

**Key feature:** For a 10-nm silica particle at a hexadecane-water interface with θ = 70°:
```
ΔG ≈ π × (10⁻⁸)² × 50×10⁻³ × (1-cos70°)²
   ≈ π × 10⁻¹⁶ × 0.05 × 0.117
   ≈ 1.84 × 10⁻¹⁸ J ≈ 447 k_BT at 25°C
```
This enormous adsorption energy means particles are **essentially irreversibly adsorbed** at the interface!

### 7.3 Examples of Pickering Emulsifiers

| Particle | Contact Angle | Emulsion Type | Application |
|----------|--------------|--------------|-------------|
| Hydrophilic silica (SiO₂) | 20–40° | O/W | Cosmetics, pharmaceuticals |
| Modified silica | 60–90° | O/W or W/O | Food emulsions |
| Laponite clay | ~20° | O/W | Paints, oilfield |
| Starch granules | ~60° | O/W | Food (clean label) |
| Chitosan particles | variable | O/W | Biomedical |
| Wax particles | ~90° | W/O | Food, cosmetics |

### 7.4 Advantages

1. **No chemical surfactant** required → important for clean-label food products and biocompatible pharmaceuticals.
2. **Extremely stable** (irreversible adsorption of particles).
3. Can create **pH-responsive or temperature-responsive** emulsions by using stimuli-responsive particles.

## 8. Type 7 — Solid Emulsions (Semi-Solid)

### 8.1 Creams (Semi-Solid Emulsions)

Pharmaceutical and cosmetic **creams** are semi-solid emulsions:

| Type | Structure | Properties |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| **O/W cream** (aqueous cream) | Oil in water, semi-solid | Water-washable, non-greasy |
| **W/O cream** (oily cream) | Water in oil, semi-solid | Greasy, occlusive, protective |

The semi-solid consistency arises from a **gel network** in the continuous phase (thickened with cetyl alcohol, carbomer, or other rheology modifiers).

### 8.2 Ointments

- **Anhydrous ointments:** Pure W/O systems (e.g., petrolatum-based) with no true emulsification.
- **Emulsifying ointments:** True semi-solid W/O or O/W emulsions with crystalline emulsifier structure.

## 10. Numerical Examples

### Problem 1 — Phase Volume and Emulsion Type Prediction
**Q:** An emulsion is prepared with 30 mL of olive oil and 70 mL of water using sodium lauryl sulfate (SDS) as emulsifier (HLB = 40). Predict the type of emulsion formed and its key characteristics.

```
Phase volume ratio: oil/total = 30/100 = 0.30 (30% oil phase volume)
Emulsifier: SDS, HLB = 40 (very high, strongly hydrophilic)

Bancroft's rule: SDS is highly water-soluble → will stabilize O/W emulsion.
Phase volume: oil is minority phase (30%) → dispersed phase.

Prediction: O/W emulsion (oil droplets in water)
Properties:
  - Milky white appearance
  - High electrical conductivity
  - Miscible with water
  - Non-greasy feel
```

### Problem 2 — Blending Emulsifiers for W/O Cream
**Q:** Prepare a W/O emulsion cream for a cosmetic product. Required HLB = 5.0. Available emulsifiers: Span 80 (HLB = 4.3) and Span 20 (HLB = 8.6). Calculate the blend ratio.

```
Required HLB = 5.0
Let fraction of Span 80 = x, fraction of Span 20 = (1-x)

5.0 = 4.3x + 8.6(1-x)
5.0 = 4.3x + 8.6 - 8.6x
5.0 - 8.6 = -4.3x
-3.6 = -4.3x
x = 3.6/4.3 = 0.837

Blend: 83.7% Span 80 + 16.3% Span 20

Verification: 0.837 × 4.3 + 0.163 × 8.6 = 3.60 + 1.40 = 5.00 ✓
```

### Problem 3 — Microemulsion Identification
**Q:** An oil-water-surfactant system is prepared. It forms spontaneously, is transparent, and has a conductivity of 0.1 µS/cm. Droplet size measured by DLS = 18 nm. Classify this system.

```
Analysis:
1. Spontaneous formation → thermodynamically stable (not a regular emulsion)
2. Transparent → droplet size << λ_light (400–700 nm) → very small droplets confirmed
3. Droplet size = 18 nm → 5–100 nm range
4. Conductivity = 0.1 µS/cm → very low → oil is the continuous phase
   (pure water: ~0.05 µS/cm; even slight ionic content → W/O type)
   
Classification: MICROEMULSION (W/O type, Winsor II or IV)
```
