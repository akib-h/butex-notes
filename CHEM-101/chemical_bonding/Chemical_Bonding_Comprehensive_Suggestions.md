## 1. Introduction to Chemical Bonding

Chemical bonding describes the attractive forces that hold atoms together in molecules and compounds. These forces arise from the interaction of electrons between atoms, leading to stable configurations.

### Types of Chemical Bonds

1. **Ionic Bonds** - Transfer of electrons from one atom to another
2. **Covalent Bonds** - Sharing of electrons between atoms
3. **Metallic Bonds** - Delocalized electrons in a "sea" of electrons
4. **Coordinate/Dative Bonds** - Both electrons in the bond come from one atom

### Historical Development

- **1916**: G.N. Lewis proposed the electron pair bond concept
- **1916**: Walther Kossel proposed ionic bonding theory
- **1927**: Walter Heitler and Fritz London developed quantum mechanical approach
- **1931**: Linus Pauling published "On the Nature of the Chemical Bond"
- **1920s-1930s**: Friedrich Hund and Robert Mulliken developed Molecular Orbital Theory

## 2. Valence Bond Theory (VBT)

### 2.1 Fundamental Concepts

Valence Bond Theory, developed by Heitler, London, Pauling, and Slater (1927-1931), describes chemical bonding through the overlap of atomic orbitals.

### 2.2 Main Postulates of VBT

1. **Orbital Overlap**: Covalent bonds form when half-filled atomic orbitals overlap
2. **Electron Pairing**: Electrons in overlapping orbitals must have opposite spins
3. **Maximum Overlap**: Bonds form in the direction of maximum orbital overlap
4. **Localized Electrons**: Electrons are localized in the bond region between atoms
5. **Bond Strength**: Greater overlap leads to stronger bonds

### 2.3 Key Features

- **Hybridization**: Atomic orbitals mix to form new hybrid orbitals (sp, sp², sp³, sp³d, sp³d²)
- **Directional Bonds**: Bonds have specific orientations in space
- **Sigma (σ) and Pi (π) Bonds**: Different types based on orbital overlap
- **Resonance**: Multiple Lewis structures contribute to actual structure

### 2.4 Hybridization Types

| Hybridization | Atomic Orbitals Mixed | Geometry | Bond Angle | Example |
|---------------|----------------------|----------|------------|---------|
| sp | 1s + 1p | Linear | 180° | BeF₂, C₂H₂ |
| sp² | 1s + 2p | Trigonal planar | 120° | BF₃, C₂H₄ |
| sp³ | 1s + 3p | Tetrahedral | 109.5° | CH₄, NH₃ |
| sp³d | 1s + 3p + 1d | Trigonal bipyramidal | 90°, 120° | PCl₅ |
| sp³d² | 1s + 3p + 2d | Octahedral | 90° | SF₆ |

### 2.5 Advantages of VBT

- Intuitive and easy to visualize
- Explains molecular geometry well
- Consistent with Lewis structures
- Predicts bond angles accurately for simple molecules
- Explains hybridization concept effectively

### 2.6 Limitations of VBT

1. Cannot explain paramagnetism of O₂
2. Fails to predict magnetic properties accurately
3. Cannot explain color in coordination compounds
4. Doesn't account for delocalized electrons
5. Struggles with resonance structures
6. Cannot explain fractional bond orders
7. Incorrectly predicts dissociation of H₂ into ions

## 3. Molecular Orbital Theory (MOT)

### 3.1 Fundamental Concepts

Molecular Orbital Theory describes chemical bonding through the formation of molecular orbitals that extend over the entire molecule, treating electrons as delocalized.

**Key Developers**: Friedrich Hund, Robert Mulliken, John Lennard-Jones (1920s-1930s)

### 3.2 Main Postulates of MOT

1. **Linear Combination of Atomic Orbitals (LCAO)**: Atomic orbitals combine mathematically to form molecular orbitals
2. **Number Conservation**: Number of molecular orbitals formed = Number of atomic orbitals combined
3. **Energy Ordering**: Bonding MOs are lower in energy; antibonding MOs are higher in energy
4. **Electron Filling**: Follow Aufbau principle, Pauli exclusion, and Hund's rule
5. **Delocalization**: Electrons occupy molecular orbitals spanning the entire molecule

### 3.3 Types of Molecular Orbitals

**Bonding Molecular Orbitals (BMO)**
- Lower in energy than parent atomic orbitals
- Increase electron density between nuclei
- Stabilize the molecule
- Denoted without asterisk (σ, π)

**Antibonding Molecular Orbitals (ABMO)**
- Higher in energy than parent atomic orbitals
- Decrease electron density between nuclei
- Destabilize the molecule
- Denoted with asterisk (σ*, π*)

**Non-bonding Molecular Orbitals**
- Same energy as parent atomic orbitals
- Neither stabilize nor destabilize

### 3.4 MO Energy Level Diagrams

**For H₂ to N₂** (Li₂, Be₂, B₂, C₂, N₂):
```
Energy Order: σ1s < σ*1s < σ2s < σ*2s < π2px = π2py < σ2pz < π*2px = π*2py < σ*2pz
```

**For O₂ to Ne₂** (O₂, F₂, Ne₂):
```
Energy Order: σ1s < σ*1s < σ2s < σ*2s < σ2pz < π2px = π2py < π*2px = π*2py < σ*2pz
```

**Note**: The order changes due to s-p mixing. For lighter atoms (Li-N), significant 2s-2p orbital mixing occurs because these orbitals are close in energy, causing π orbitals to be lower than σ2p.

### 3.5 Three Criteria for MO Formation

1. **Symmetry Criterion**: Orbitals must have compatible symmetry to overlap
2. **Overlap Criterion**: Better overlap leads to stronger interaction
3. **Energy Criterion**: Orbitals must be similar in energy to interact effectively

### 3.6 Advantages of MOT

- Explains paramagnetism and diamagnetism correctly
- Predicts bond order including fractional values
- Explains delocalized bonding (benzene, resonance)
- Correctly predicts magnetic properties
- Explains spectroscopic data (photoelectron spectroscopy)
- More accurate for complex molecules

### 3.7 Limitations of MOT

- Mathematically complex
- Less intuitive than VBT
- Difficult to apply without computational tools
- Doesn't easily explain bond breaking/formation during reactions
- Requires extensive calculations for large molecules

## 4. Differences Between VBT and MOT

### 4.1 Comprehensive Comparison Table

| Aspect | Valence Bond Theory (VBT) | Molecular Orbital Theory (MOT) |
|--------|---------------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Basic Concept** | Bonds form by overlap of atomic orbitals | Atomic orbitals combine to form molecular orbitals |
| **Electron Location** | Localized between bonded atoms | Delocalized over entire molecule |
| **Orbital Type** | Atomic orbitals with hybridization | Molecular orbitals (bonding and antibonding) |
| **Bond Formation** | Direct overlap of half-filled orbitals | LCAO - mathematical combination of AOs |
| **Electron Pairing** | Electrons paired in overlapping region | Electrons fill MOs according to energy |
| **Bond Description** | Sigma (σ) and Pi (π) bonds | Bonding and antibonding orbitals |
| **Magnetic Properties** | Cannot explain paramagnetism | Successfully explains paramagnetic behavior |
| **O₂ Molecule** | Predicts diamagnetic (incorrect) | Correctly predicts paramagnetic |
| **Bond Order** | Integer values only (1, 2, 3) | Can be fractional (0.5, 1.5, 2.5) |
| **Resonance** | Requires multiple structures | Single MO description |
| **Mathematical Complexity** | Relatively simple | Complex, requires computation |
| **Applicability** | Best for simple molecules | Excellent for all molecules |
| **Bond Energy** | Based on overlap extent | Based on MO occupation |
| **Molecular Geometry** | VSEPR + hybridization | Less direct prediction |
| **Dissociation** | Predicts atoms correctly | Simple models predict ions (incorrect) |
| **Delocalization** | Difficult to describe | Natural consequence of MO theory |
| **Historical Development** | Pauling (1931) | Hund, Mulliken (1920s-30s) |
| **Chemical Intuition** | High - aligns with Lewis structures | Lower - more abstract |
| **Computational Use** | Limited until 1980s | Dominant since 1960s |

### 4.2 Specific Example: Oxygen Molecule (O₂)

**VBT Prediction**:
- Electronic configuration: All electrons paired
- Magnetic behavior: Diamagnetic (INCORRECT)
- Bond order: 2 (from O=O double bond)

**MOT Prediction**:
- Electronic configuration: σ1s² σ*1s² σ2s² σ*2s² σ2pz² π2px² π2py² π*2px¹ π*2py¹
- Magnetic behavior: Paramagnetic with 2 unpaired electrons (CORRECT)
- Bond order: (8-4)/2 = 2

**Experimental Evidence**: Liquid O₂ is attracted to magnetic fields, confirming MOT's prediction.

### 4.3 Convergence of Theories

Modern quantum chemistry shows that at higher levels of theory (with configuration interaction), VBT and MOT converge to describe the same wavefunction. They are complementary approaches:

- **VBT**: Better for understanding reaction mechanisms and structural changes
- **MOT**: Better for spectroscopic properties and magnetic behavior

## 5. Fajan's Rules

### 5.1 Introduction

**Fajan's Rules**, formulated by Kazimierz Fajans in 1923, predict whether a chemical bond will be predominantly ionic or covalent based on the polarization of the anion by the cation.

### 5.2 Concept of Polarization

**Polarization** is the distortion of the electron cloud of an anion by the electric field of a cation.

- **Polarizing Power**: Ability of a cation to distort an anion's electron cloud
- **Polarizability**: Ease with which an anion's electron cloud can be distorted

**Key Principle**: Greater polarization → Greater covalent character

### 5.3 The Three Main Rules

#### Rule 1: Size of Ions

**Statement**: Smaller cations and larger anions favor covalent character.

**Explanation**:
- **Small cation**: High charge density → High polarizing power
- **Large anion**: Loosely held outer electrons → High polarizability

**Charge Density** = Charge / Volume (or Charge / Radius)

**Examples**:
- LiCl > NaCl > KCl > RbCl > CsCl (decreasing covalent character)
- NaF < NaCl < NaBr < NaI (increasing covalent character)

#### Rule 2: Charge on Ions

**Statement**: Higher charges on both cation and anion increase covalent character.

**Explanation**:
- Higher cation charge → Stronger polarizing power
- Higher anion charge → Greater polarizability

**Examples**:
- SnCl₄ (Sn⁴⁺) is more covalent than SnCl₂ (Sn²⁺)
- AlCl₃ is more covalent than MgCl₂

#### Rule 3: Electronic Configuration of Cation

**Statement**: For cations of the same size and charge, those with pseudo-noble gas configuration (18-electron) have greater polarizing power than those with noble gas configuration (8-electron).

**Electronic Configurations**:
- **Noble gas configuration**: ns² np⁶ (e.g., Ca²⁺: [Ar])
- **Pseudo-noble gas configuration**: ns² np⁶ nd¹⁰ (e.g., Zn²⁺: [Ar] 3d¹⁰)

**Explanation**: d-electrons shield nuclear charge poorly, resulting in higher effective nuclear charge and greater polarizing power.

**Examples**:
- HgCl₂ (Hg²⁺: 5d¹⁰) is more covalent than CaCl₂ (Ca²⁺: noble gas)
- ZnCl₂ (Zn²⁺: 3d¹⁰) is more covalent than MgCl₂ (Mg²⁺: noble gas)
- AgCl is more covalent than KCl (both have similar ionic radii)

### 5.4 Summary Table

| Factor | Favors Ionic Character | Favors Covalent Character |
|--------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| **Cation Size** | Large | Small |
| **Anion Size** | Small | Large |
| **Cation Charge** | Low (+1) | High (+3, +4) |
| **Anion Charge** | Low (-1) | High (-2, -3) |
| **Cation Configuration** | Noble gas (ns²np⁶) | Pseudo-noble gas (nd¹⁰) |

### 5.5 Consequences of Covalent Character

1. **Melting Point**: Decreases with increasing covalent character
   - LiF (870°C) > LiCl (613°C) > LiBr (547°C) > LiI (446°C)

2. **Solubility**: 
   - More covalent → Less soluble in water, more soluble in non-polar solvents
   - LiF is more soluble in water than LiI

3. **Electrical Conductivity**: Decreases with covalent character

4. **Heat of Sublimation**: Lower for compounds with higher covalent character

### 5.6 Applications

1. **Predicting bond type** in ionic compounds
2. **Explaining anomalous properties** of certain compounds
3. **Understanding thermal decomposition** of compounds
4. **Explaining solubility trends**

### 5.7 Percentage Ionic Character

Can be estimated from dipole moments:

**% Ionic Character** = (Observed Dipole Moment / Theoretical Dipole Moment) × 100

Where theoretical dipole moment assumes complete charge separation.

## 6. Bond Order

### 6.1 Definition

**Bond Order** is a measure of the number of chemical bonds between a pair of atoms, indicating bond strength and stability.

### 6.2 Bond Order in Lewis/VBT

In Lewis structures and VBT:
- Single bond: Bond order = 1
- Double bond: Bond order = 2
- Triple bond: Bond order = 3

### 6.3 Bond Order in MOT

**Formula**:
```
Bond Order (BO) = ½ × (Nb - Na)
```

Where:
- **Nb** = Number of electrons in bonding molecular orbitals
- **Na** = Number of electrons in antibonding molecular orbitals

### 6.4 Significance of Bond Order

1. **Bond Strength**: Higher bond order → Stronger bond
2. **Bond Length**: Higher bond order → Shorter bond
3. **Stability**: Bond order > 0 → Molecule exists; Bond order = 0 → Molecule doesn't exist
4. **Magnetic Properties**: Related to unpaired electrons in MO diagram

### 6.5 Relationship Between Bond Properties

| Bond Order | Bond Type | Bond Length | Bond Strength | Stability |
|------------|-----------|-------------|---------------|-----------|
| 0 | No bond | - | None | Does not exist |
| 0.5 | Weak bond | Very long | Very weak | Marginal |
| 1 | Single | Long | Weak | Stable |
| 2 | Double | Medium | Strong | Very stable |
| 3 | Triple | Short | Very strong | Extremely stable |

### 6.6 Bond Order Calculations - Examples

#### Example 1: N₂ (Nitrogen)

**Atomic Number**: N = 7; Total electrons in N₂ = 14

**Electronic Configuration**:
σ1s² σ*1s² σ2s² σ*2s² π2px² π2py² σ2pz²

**Calculation**:
- Bonding electrons (Nb): σ1s² + σ2s² + π2px² + π2py² + σ2pz² = 10
- Antibonding electrons (Na): σ*1s² + σ*2s² = 4

**Bond Order** = ½(10 - 4) = **3**

**Properties**:
- Triple bond (N≡N)
- Diamagnetic (all electrons paired)
- Very stable, unreactive
- Short bond length

#### Example 2: O₂ (Oxygen)

**Atomic Number**: O = 8; Total electrons in O₂ = 16

**Electronic Configuration**:
σ1s² σ*1s² σ2s² σ*2s² σ2pz² π2px² π2py² π*2px¹ π*2py¹

**Calculation**:
- Bonding electrons (Nb): σ1s² + σ2s² + σ2pz² + π2px² + π2py² = 10
- Antibonding electrons (Na): σ*1s² + σ*2s² + π*2px¹ + π*2py¹ = 6

**Bond Order** = ½(10 - 6) = **2**

**Properties**:
- Double bond (O=O)
- Paramagnetic (2 unpaired electrons in π* orbitals)
- Moderately reactive
- Intermediate bond length

#### Example 3: Ne₂ (Neon)

**Atomic Number**: Ne = 10; Total electrons in Ne₂ = 20

**Electronic Configuration**:
σ1s² σ*1s² σ2s² σ*2s² σ2pz² π2px² π2py² π*2px² π*2py² σ*2pz²

**Calculation**:
- Bonding electrons (Nb): 10
- Antibonding electrons (Na): 10

**Bond Order** = ½(10 - 10) = **0**

**Properties**:
- No bond exists
- Ne₂ does not exist as a stable molecule
- Neon exists only as monatomic gas

#### Example 4: F₂ (Fluorine)

**Atomic Number**: F = 9; Total electrons in F₂ = 18

**Electronic Configuration**:
σ1s² σ*1s² σ2s² σ*2s² σ2pz² π2px² π2py² π*2px² π*2py²

**Calculation**:
- Bonding electrons (Nb): 10
- Antibonding electrons (Na): 8

**Bond Order** = ½(10 - 8) = **1**

**Properties**:
- Single bond (F-F)
- Diamagnetic
- Reactive
- Relatively weak single bond

### 6.7 Comparison Table: N₂, O₂, and Ne₂

| Property | N₂ | O₂ | Ne₂ |
|----------|----|----|-----|
| **Total Electrons** | 14 | 16 | 20 |
| **Bonding Electrons** | 10 | 10 | 10 |
| **Antibonding Electrons** | 4 | 6 | 10 |
| **Bond Order** | 3 | 2 | 0 |
| **Bond Type** | Triple | Double | None |
| **Magnetic Property** | Diamagnetic | Paramagnetic | - |
| **Unpaired Electrons** | 0 | 2 | 0 |
| **Stability** | Very stable | Stable | Does not exist |
| **Bond Length (pm)** | 110 | 121 | - |
| **Bond Energy (kJ/mol)** | 945 | 498 | 0 |
| **Reactivity** | Very low | Moderate | - |

### 6.8 Fractional Bond Orders

Some molecules and ions have fractional bond orders:

| Species | Bond Order | Bond Type |
|---------|------------|-----------|
| H₂⁺ | 0.5 | Half bond |
| O₂⁺ | 2.5 | Between double and triple |
| O₂⁻ | 1.5 | Between single and double |
| NO | 2.5 | Between double and triple |
| Benzene (C-C) | 1.5 | Resonance structure |

### 6.9 Bond Order and Molecular Properties

**General Trends**:

1. **Bond Order ∝ Bond Strength**
   - N₂ (BO = 3): 945 kJ/mol
   - O₂ (BO = 2): 498 kJ/mol
   - F₂ (BO = 1): 158 kJ/mol

2. **Bond Order ∝ 1/Bond Length**
   - N₂ (BO = 3): 110 pm
   - O₂ (BO = 2): 121 pm
   - F₂ (BO = 1): 142 pm

3. **Bond Order ∝ Stability**
   - Higher bond order = More stable molecule

## 7. Orbital Overlapping

### 7.1 Introduction

**Orbital Overlapping** is the interpenetration of atomic orbitals from different atoms, leading to bond formation. The extent of overlap determines bond strength.

### 7.2 Types of Overlap

#### 7.2.1 Positive/Constructive Overlap
- Orbitals with same phase/sign overlap
- Increases electron density between nuclei
- Forms bonding molecular orbitals
- Stabilizes the system

#### 7.2.2 Negative/Destructive Overlap
- Orbitals with opposite phases overlap
- Decreases electron density between nuclei
- Forms antibonding molecular orbitals
- Destabilizes the system

#### 7.2.3 Zero/No Overlap
- Incompatible orbital symmetry
- No net interaction
- No bond formation

### 7.3 Classification by Bond Type

### A. Sigma (σ) Bonds

**Definition**: Bonds formed by head-on (end-to-end) overlap of atomic orbitals along the internuclear axis.

**Characteristics**:
- Cylindrically symmetric about bond axis
- Strongest type of covalent bond
- Allow free rotation
- Present in all single, double, and triple bonds
- Electron density concentrated between nuclei

**Types of σ Overlap**:

1. **s-s Overlap**
   - Example: H₂ molecule (H-H)
   - Two 1s orbitals overlap
   - Forms σ1s bonding orbital

2. **s-p Overlap**
   - Example: HF molecule (H-F)
   - 1s orbital of H overlaps with 2p orbital of F
   - Forms σ bonding orbital

3. **p-p (Head-on) Overlap**
   - Example: F₂ molecule (F-F)
   - Two 2p orbitals overlap along axis
   - Forms σp bonding orbital
   - Can be px-px, py-py, or pz-pz (along bond axis)

4. **Hybrid-Hybrid Overlap**
   - Example: C-C in ethane
   - sp³-sp³ overlap
   - Most common in organic molecules

5. **Hybrid-s Overlap**
   - Example: C-H bond in methane
   - sp³-1s overlap

6. **Hybrid-p Overlap**
   - Various combinations possible

### B. Pi (π) Bonds

**Definition**: Bonds formed by sideways (lateral) overlap of parallel p orbitals perpendicular to the internuclear axis.

**Characteristics**:
- Two regions of electron density (above and below σ bond)
- Weaker than σ bonds (less overlap)
- Prevent free rotation
- Always accompanied by σ bond
- Present in double and triple bonds

**Types of π Overlap**:

1. **p-p (Parallel/Sideways) Overlap**
   - Most common type
   - Example: C=C in ethene
   - px-px or py-py overlap (perpendicular to bond axis)

2. **p-d Overlap**
   - Less common
   - Found in some coordination compounds

3. **d-d Overlap**
   - In metal-metal bonding

**π Bond Formation**:
- First bond between atoms is always σ
- Second bond (in double bond) is π
- Third bond (in triple bond) is another π (perpendicular to first π)

### C. Delta (δ) Bonds

**Definition**: Bonds formed by face-to-face overlap of d orbitals.

**Characteristics**:
- Two nodal planes containing internuclear axis
- Weakest type
- Only in metal-metal bonding
- Rare - found in quadruple and quintuple bonds

**Example**: Mo₂ (molybdenum dimer) has quadruple bond (1σ + 2π + 1δ)

### 7.4 Extent of Overlap

**Overlap Strength Order**: σ > π > δ

**Factors Affecting Overlap**:

1. **Size of Orbitals**
   - Similar sizes → Better overlap
   - Size mismatch → Poor overlap

2. **Orientation**
   - Proper alignment → Maximum overlap
   - Poor alignment → Weak overlap

3. **Energy of Orbitals**
   - Similar energies → Better interaction
   - Energy mismatch → Poor MO formation

4. **Nuclear Distance**
   - Optimal distance → Maximum overlap
   - Too close or too far → Reduced overlap

### 7.5 Bond Strength and Overlap

**General Relationship**:
- Greater overlap → Stronger bond
- Head-on overlap (σ) > Sideways overlap (π)

**Bond Strength Order**:
```
C-C (σ): ~350 kJ/mol
C=C (σ + π): ~610 kJ/mol (additional π: ~260 kJ/mol)
C≡C (σ + 2π): ~835 kJ/mol (each π: ~260 kJ/mol)
```

**Note**: The additional bond strength from π bonds is less than σ bonds due to less effective overlap.

### 7.6 Overlapping in Multiple Bonds

#### Single Bond (e.g., Ethane, C₂H₆)
```
C-C: 1 σ bond (sp³-sp³ overlap)
Total: 1 σ bond
```

#### Double Bond (e.g., Ethene, C₂H₄)
```
C=C: 1 σ bond (sp²-sp² overlap) + 1 π bond (p-p sideways overlap)
Total: 1 σ + 1 π bond
```

#### Triple Bond (e.g., Ethyne, C₂H₂)
```
C≡C: 1 σ bond (sp-sp overlap) + 2 π bonds (two sets of p-p overlap, perpendicular)
Total: 1 σ + 2 π bonds
```

### 7.7 Hybridization and Overlap

| Hybridization | σ Bonds | π Bonds | Example | Geometry |
|---------------|---------|---------|---------|----------|
| sp³ | 4 | 0 | CH₄ | Tetrahedral |
| sp² | 3 | 1 | C₂H₄ | Trigonal planar |
| sp | 2 | 2 | C₂H₂ | Linear |

### 7.8 Molecular Orbital Perspective

**Bonding MO**:
- In-phase overlap
- Constructive interference
- Lower energy
- Increases electron density

**Antibonding MO**:
- Out-of-phase overlap
- Destructive interference
- Higher energy
- Node between nuclei

**Non-bonding**:
- No overlap
- Same energy as AO
- Perpendicular orientations

### 7.9 Examples of Overlap

#### Example 1: Ammonia (NH₃)
- N: sp³ hybridization
- H: 1s orbital
- **N-H bonds**: sp³-1s overlap (3 σ bonds)
- Lone pair in sp³ orbital (no overlap)

#### Example 2: Water (H₂O)
- O: sp³ hybridization
- H: 1s orbital
- **O-H bonds**: sp³-1s overlap (2 σ bonds)
- Two lone pairs in sp³ orbitals

#### Example 3: Benzene (C₆H₆)
- C: sp² hybridization
- **C-C bonds**: sp²-sp² overlap (σ bonds)
- **C-H bonds**: sp²-1s overlap (σ bonds)
- **π system**: 6 p orbitals form delocalized π molecular orbitals

### 7.10 Rotational Freedom

**σ Bonds**: 
- Allow free rotation
- Cylindrically symmetric
- Rotation doesn't break overlap

**π Bonds**:
- Restrict rotation
- Sideways overlap
- Rotation would break π overlap
- Require energy to rotate (~260 kJ/mol for C=C)

This is why:
- Ethane (single bond) can rotate freely
- Ethene (double bond) cannot rotate without breaking π bond
- cis-trans isomerism exists in alkenes

## 8. Practice Questions and Answers

### Section A: Valence Bond Theory

**Q1. Explain the concept of hybridization. Why does carbon undergo sp³ hybridization in methane?**

**Answer**:
Hybridization is the process where atomic orbitals mix to form new hybrid orbitals of equal energy and specific geometry. In methane:
- Carbon has 4 valence electrons: 2s² 2p²
- To form 4 equivalent C-H bonds, one 2s and three 2p orbitals mix
- Forms 4 sp³ hybrid orbitals, each with 25% s and 75% p character
- These arrange tetrahedrally (109.5°) to minimize repulsion
- Each sp³ orbital overlaps with H 1s orbital forming 4 equivalent σ bonds

**Q2. Distinguish between sigma and pi bonds.**

**Answer**:

| Aspect | Sigma (σ) Bond | Pi (π) Bond |
|--------|----------------|-------------|
| Overlap type | Head-on/end-to-end | Sideways/lateral |
| Strength | Stronger | Weaker |
| Rotation | Free rotation possible | Restricted |
| Electron density | Between nuclei along axis | Above and below axis |
| Orbitals involved | s-s, s-p, p-p (head-on), hybrid | p-p (parallel) only |
| Number | One in every bond | Additional in multiple bonds |

### Section B: Molecular Orbital Theory

**Q3. Why is O₂ paramagnetic? Explain using MO theory.**

**Answer**:
O₂ is paramagnetic because it has unpaired electrons, as shown by MO theory:

Electronic configuration: σ1s² σ*1s² σ2s² σ*2s² σ2pz² π2px² π2py² π*2px¹ π*2py¹

The two antibonding π* orbitals (π*2px and π*2py) are degenerate (equal energy). According to Hund's rule, electrons occupy these orbitals singly with parallel spins before pairing.

Therefore, O₂ has 2 unpaired electrons in π* orbitals, making it paramagnetic. This is confirmed by liquid oxygen being attracted to magnetic fields.

VBT incorrectly predicts O₂ to be diamagnetic with all electrons paired.

**Q4. Calculate the bond order of the following species: (a) N₂⁺ (b) O₂⁻**

**Answer**:

**(a) N₂⁺**
- N₂ has 14 electrons; N₂⁺ has 13 electrons
- Configuration: σ1s² σ*1s² σ2s² σ*2s² π2px² π2py² σ2pz¹
- Nb = 9, Na = 4
- **Bond order = ½(9-4) = 2.5**

**(b) O₂⁻**
- O₂ has 16 electrons; O₂⁻ has 17 electrons
- Configuration: σ1s² σ*1s² σ2s² σ*2s² σ2pz² π2px² π2py² π*2px² π*2py¹
- Nb = 10, Na = 7
- **Bond order = ½(10-7) = 1.5**

**Q5. Which molecule has the highest bond order: N₂, O₂, or F₂? Explain.**

**Answer**:
**N₂ has the highest bond order = 3**

Comparison:
- N₂: Bond order = 3 (triple bond)
- O₂: Bond order = 2 (double bond)
- F₂: Bond order = 1 (single bond)

N₂ has:
- Shortest bond length (110 pm)
- Highest bond energy (945 kJ/mol)
- Greatest stability
- All bonding orbitals filled, minimal antibonding occupation

### Section C: Fajan's Rules

**Q6. Arrange the following in order of increasing covalent character: NaF, NaCl, NaBr, NaI. Justify your answer.**

**Answer**:
**Order: NaF < NaCl < NaBr < NaI** (increasing covalent character)

Justification using Fajan's Rules:
- Cation (Na⁺) is same in all
- Anion size increases: F⁻ < Cl⁻ < Br⁻ < I⁻
- Larger anions have:
  - More loosely held outer electrons
  - Higher polarizability
  - Greater covalent character

Evidence:
- Melting points: NaF (993°C) > NaCl (801°C) > NaBr (747°C) > NaI (661°C)
- Solubility in water decreases down the series
- NaI is more soluble in organic solvents than NaF

**Q7. Why is HgCl₂ more covalent than CaCl₂ despite both cations having similar sizes and charges?**

**Answer**:
This is explained by **Fajan's Rule 3** (electronic configuration effect):

- **Ca²⁺**: Noble gas configuration [Ar] (1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶)
- **Hg²⁺**: Pseudo-noble gas configuration [Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰

The d and f electrons in Hg²⁺ provide poor shielding of nuclear charge compared to s and p electrons in Ca²⁺. This results in:
- Higher effective nuclear charge for Hg²⁺
- Greater polarizing power
- More covalent character in HgCl₂

Evidence:
- HgCl₂ is covalent (melts at 277°C, soluble in organic solvents)
- CaCl₂ is ionic (melts at 772°C, highly soluble in water)

**Q8. Which compound would you expect to be more covalent: AlF₃ or AlI₃? Explain.**

**Answer**:
**AlI₃ is more covalent than AlF₃**

Reasoning:
- Same cation (Al³⁺) with high charge (+3) and small size
- Different anions: F⁻ vs I⁻
- Anion size: I⁻ (206 pm) >> F⁻ (133 pm)

According to Fajan's Rules:
- I⁻ is highly polarizable (large, loosely held outer electrons)
- F⁻ is poorly polarizable (small, tightly held electrons)
- Al³⁺ can significantly distort I⁻ electron cloud but not F⁻

Result:
- AlI₃: Highly covalent, exists as discrete molecules, soluble in organic solvents
- AlF₃: Predominantly ionic, high melting point (1291°C), ionic lattice structure

### Section D: Bond Order and Properties

**Q9. How does bond order relate to bond length and bond energy? Provide examples.**

**Answer**:

**Relationships**:
1. **Bond Order ∝ Bond Energy** (direct relationship)
2. **Bond Order ∝ 1/Bond Length** (inverse relationship)

**Examples**:

| Bond | Bond Order | Bond Length (pm) | Bond Energy (kJ/mol) |
|------|------------|------------------|---------------------|
| C-C | 1 | 154 | 348 |
| C=C | 2 | 134 | 614 |
| C≡C | 3 | 120 | 839 |

| Molecule | Bond Order | Bond Length (pm) | Bond Energy (kJ/mol) |
|----------|------------|------------------|---------------------|
| F₂ | 1 | 142 | 158 |
| O₂ | 2 | 121 | 498 |
| N₂ | 3 | 110 | 945 |

**Explanation**:
- Higher bond order means more electron pairs between atoms
- More electron pairs → stronger attraction → shorter distance
- More bonds to break → higher energy required

**Q10. Predict whether Be₂ molecule exists. Justify using MO theory.**

**Answer**:
**Be₂ does not exist as a stable molecule**

MO Analysis:
- Each Be atom has 2 valence electrons (2s²)
- Total electrons in Be₂ = 4

**Electronic configuration**: σ2s² σ*2s²

Calculation:
- Bonding electrons (Nb) = 2 (in σ2s)
- Antibonding electrons (Na) = 2 (in σ*2s)
- **Bond order = ½(2-2) = 0**

**Conclusion**:
- Bond order of 0 means no net bonding
- Bonding and antibonding effects cancel out
- Be₂ is unstable and does not exist under normal conditions
- Beryllium exists as monatomic gas or metal lattice

### Section E: Orbital Overlap

**Q11. Explain why pi bonds are weaker than sigma bonds.**

**Answer**:

**Reasons**:

1. **Extent of Overlap**:
   - σ bonds: Head-on overlap → maximum overlap area
   - π bonds: Sideways overlap → smaller overlap area
   - Less overlap = weaker interaction

2. **Electron Density Location**:
   - σ bonds: Electron density directly between nuclei along internuclear axis
   - π bonds: Electron density above and below the axis
   - Direct attraction is stronger

3. **Orbital Geometry**:
   - σ: Cylindrically symmetric → uniform attraction
   - π: Two lobes → divided electron density

**Evidence**:
- C-C single bond (σ): ~348 kJ/mol
- Additional π bond in C=C: ~266 kJ/mol
- π bond is ~76% of σ bond strength

**Q12. What types of orbital overlap can occur between the following pairs?**
**(a) s and p orbitals**
**(b) px and py orbitals**
**(c) pz and pz orbitals (along z-axis)**

**Answer**:

**(a) s and p orbitals**:
- **Sigma (σ) bond**
- s orbital overlaps with p orbital head-on along internuclear axis
- Example: H-F bond (H 1s with F 2p)

**(b) px and py orbitals**:
- **No bond** (non-bonding)
- These orbitals are perpendicular to each other
- No net overlap occurs
- One orbital has node where the other has maximum density

**(c) pz and pz orbitals (along z-axis)**:
- **Sigma (σ) bond**
- Head-on overlap along the bond axis (z-axis)
- Example: F₂ molecule (F 2pz with F 2pz)

### Section F: Comprehensive Problems

**Q13. For the molecule C₂H₄ (ethene):**
**(a) Describe the hybridization of carbon atoms**
**(b) Explain the types of bonds present**
**(c) Why is rotation about the C=C bond restricted?**

**Answer**:

**(a) Hybridization**:
- Each carbon atom is **sp² hybridized**
- One 2s orbital mixes with two 2p orbitals
- Forms three sp² hybrid orbitals (120° apart, trigonal planar)
- One 2p orbital remains unhybridized (perpendicular to plane)

**(b) Types of Bonds**:

**C=C double bond**:
- 1 σ bond: sp² (C) - sp² (C) overlap
- 1 π bond: 2p (C) - 2p (C) sideways overlap

**C-H bonds** (4 total):
- σ bonds: sp² (C) - 1s (H) overlap

**Total**: 5 σ bonds and 1 π bond

**(c) Restricted Rotation**:
- The π bond consists of sideways overlap of p orbitals
- These p orbitals must remain parallel for overlap
- Rotating about C=C axis would:
  - Break the sideways overlap
  - Require ~260 kJ/mol energy
  - Destroy the π bond
- σ bond alone would allow rotation, but π bond prevents it
- This leads to cis-trans isomerism in alkenes

**Q14. Compare and contrast the bonding in N₂ and O₂ molecules using both VBT and MOT perspectives.**

**Answer**:

### N₂ Molecule

**VBT**:
- N≡N triple bond
- 1 σ bond (head-on overlap)
- 2 π bonds (sideways overlap, perpendicular to each other)
- Bond order = 3
- Predicts all electrons paired (diamagnetic) ✓

**MOT**:
- Electronic configuration: σ1s² σ*1s² σ2s² σ*2s² π2px² π2py² σ2pz²
- Bond order = ½(10-4) = 3
- All electrons paired → diamagnetic ✓
- Explains stability and low reactivity

### O₂ Molecule

**VBT**:
- O=O double bond
- 1 σ bond + 1 π bond
- Bond order = 2
- Predicts all electrons paired (diamagnetic) ✗ WRONG

**MOT**:
- Electronic configuration: σ1s² σ*1s² σ2s² σ*2s² σ2pz² π2px² π2py² π*2px¹ π*2py¹
- Bond order = ½(10-6) = 2
- Two unpaired electrons in π* orbitals → paramagnetic ✓ CORRECT
- Explains paramagnetism and color

**Comparison**:
- Both theories correctly predict N₂ properties
- Only MOT correctly predicts O₂ paramagnetism
- MOT provides more accurate description for O₂
- N₂ is more stable (higher bond order, no antibonding electrons in valence MOs)

**Q15. Using Fajan's Rules, explain the following observations:**
**(a) CsF has the highest ionic character among alkali halides**
**(b) SnCl₄ is more covalent than SnCl₂**

**Answer**:

**(a) CsF - Highest Ionic Character**

Analysis using Fajan's Rules:
- **Cation**: Cs⁺ is the largest alkali metal cation (265 pm)
  - Large size → Low charge density
  - Low polarizing power
- **Anion**: F⁻ is the smallest halide anion (133 pm)
  - Small size → Low polarizability
  - Difficult to distort

**Result**:
- Minimal polarization of anion
- Maximum ionic character
- High melting point (682°C)
- High solubility in water

**(b) SnCl₄ vs SnCl₂ - Covalent Character**

Using **Fajan's Rule 2** (charge effect):

**SnCl₂**:
- Sn²⁺ cation
- Charge = +2
- Moderate polarizing power

**SnCl₄**:
- Sn⁴⁺ cation
- Charge = +4
- High polarizing power (smaller ionic radius, higher charge density)

**Explanation**:
- Higher cation charge (Sn⁴⁺) → Higher polarizing power
- Cl⁻ is more polarized by Sn⁴⁺ than by Sn²⁺
- Greater polarization → Greater covalent character

**Evidence**:
- SnCl₄: Liquid at room temperature, soluble in organic solvents (covalent)
- SnCl₂: Crystalline solid, more ionic character

## Appendix A: Key Formulas

### Bond Order (MOT)
```
Bond Order = ½ × (Nb - Na)
```
Where:
- Nb = Number of electrons in bonding MOs
- Na = Number of electrons in antibonding MOs

### Charge Density
```
Charge Density = Charge / Radius
```
Higher charge density → Greater polarizing power

### Ionic Character Percentage
```
% Ionic Character = (μobserved / μtheoretical) × 100
```
Where μ = dipole moment

## Appendix C: Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing VBT and MOT predictions for O₂**
   - Remember: Only MOT correctly predicts O₂ is paramagnetic

2. **Forgetting antibonding electrons in bond order**
   - Always subtract antibonding from bonding electrons

3. **Assuming all ionic compounds are purely ionic**
   - Use Fajan's Rules to assess covalent character

4. **Thinking π bonds can exist alone**
   - π bonds always accompany σ bonds in multiple bonds

5. **Ignoring s-p mixing for light atoms**
   - For B₂, C₂, N₂: π orbitals lower than σ2p

6. **Using wrong energy ordering for O₂**
   - O₂ follows "normal" ordering: σ2p < π2p
