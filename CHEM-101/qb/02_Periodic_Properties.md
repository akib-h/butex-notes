## 1. Atomic Size (Atomic Radius)

### Definition
**Atomic radius** is defined as half the internuclear distance between two identical atoms bonded together (covalent radius), or half the distance between nuclei of adjacent atoms in a metallic crystal (metallic radius).

### Periodic Trends

#### Across a Period (Left → Right)
- Nuclear charge (Z) **increases** with each element.
- Electrons are added to the **same shell** (same n).
- Shielding from inner electrons remains approximately constant.
- **Effective nuclear charge (Z_eff) increases** → electrons pulled closer to nucleus.
- → **Atomic radius decreases** across a period.

#### Down a Group (Top → Bottom)
- Electrons added to **higher energy shells** (n increases).
- Inner shells shield the valence electrons.
- The principal quantum number (n) increases.
- → **Atomic radius increases** down a group.

### Arrangement: F, Mg, Cl in order of increasing atomic size

| Element | Z | Period | Group | Atomic Radius (pm) |
|---------|---|--------|-------|-------------------|
| F | 9 | 2 | VIIA | 64 |
| Cl | 17 | 3 | VIIA | 99 |
| Mg | 12 | 3 | IIA | 160 |

**Order of increasing atomic size: F < Cl < Mg**

**Reasoning:**
- F and Cl are both halogens (Group VIIA). Cl is in Period 3 (larger n) → Cl > F.
- Mg is in Period 3, Group IIA, to the left of Cl in the same period. Moving left across Period 3 decreases Z_eff → Mg is larger than Cl.
- Therefore: **F < Cl < Mg** ✓

### Arrangement: Ar, S, Se in order of increasing ionization energy
*(Also applies to atomic size)*

| Element | Atomic radius (pm) | Group | Period |
|---------|--------------------|-------|--------|
| Se | 119 | VIA | 4 |
| S | 103 | VIA | 3 |
| Ar | 71 | VIIIA | 3 |

**Increasing atomic size: Ar < S < Se**

## 2. Ionization Energy (Ionization Potential)

### Definition
**Ionization Energy (IE)** is the minimum energy required to remove the most loosely bound electron from a gaseous atom in its ground state:

$$M(g) \rightarrow M^+(g) + e^- \quad \Delta H = IE_1$$

### Successive Ionization Energies
IE₁ < IE₂ < IE₃ < … (each subsequent electron is harder to remove — higher effective nuclear charge on remaining electrons).

A **large jump** in successive IEs indicates crossing into a new (inner) shell.

### Periodic Trends

#### Across a Period
- Z_eff increases → valence electrons held more tightly → **IE increases** across a period (generally).
- **Exceptions:**
  - Group IIA > Group IIIA (e.g., Mg > Al): Removing a 2p electron (Al) is easier than a 2s electron (Mg) because 2p is higher in energy and better shielded.
  - Group VA > Group VIA (e.g., N > O): N has a half-filled 2p³ (extra stability); O has paired electrons in 2p → pairing repulsion makes one easier to remove.

#### Down a Group
- n increases → valence electrons farther from nucleus and more shielded → **IE decreases** down a group.

### Why IE of Mg > IE of Al (same period)?

| Element | Config | Electron removed from |
|---------|--------|-----------------------|
| Mg | [Ne] 3s² | 3s orbital |
| Al | [Ne] 3s² 3p¹ | 3p orbital |

- 3p electrons are **higher in energy** and **more effectively shielded** by the 3s electrons than 3s electrons are.
- Therefore, less energy is needed to remove the 3p electron of Al than the 3s electron of Mg.
- **IE(Mg) > IE(Al)** despite Al having a higher Z. ✓

### Why IE of N > IE of O?

| Element | 2p config | Situation |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| N | ↑ ↑ ↑ | Half-filled — extra stability |
| O | ↑↓ ↑ ↑ | One paired electron |

- O has one paired 2p electron that experiences **extra electron–electron repulsion**.
- Less energy needed to remove this "forced-pair" electron from O.
- **IE(N) > IE(O)** ✓

### Why IE of Mg > IE of Na?

| Element | Z | 3s electrons | Shielding |
|---------|---|-------------|-----------|
| Na | 11 | 1 (3s¹) | from 10 inner e⁻ |
| Mg | 12 | 2 (3s²) | from 10 inner e⁻ |

- Both are Period 3 elements.
- Mg has Z = 12 vs Na's Z = 11, so **Z_eff(Mg) > Z_eff(Na)**.
- Valence electron in Mg is held more tightly → **IE(Mg) > IE(Na)** ✓

## 3. Electron Affinity

### Definition
**Electron affinity (EA)** is the energy change when a gaseous atom in its ground state gains an electron:

$$X(g) + e^- \rightarrow X^-(g) \quad \Delta H = -EA$$

(Negative ΔH = exothermic = favorable for most elements)

### Periodic Trends
- **Across a period:** EA generally increases (more negative) because Z_eff increases → nucleus attracts incoming electron more strongly.
  - Exceptions: Noble gases (complete octets, EA ≈ 0 or positive), N (half-filled 2p³ — incoming electron must pair), Be, Mg (complete s-subshell).
- **Down a group:** EA generally decreases (less negative) — incoming electron goes to a larger shell, farther from nucleus.
  - Exception: F < Cl in EA because F is very small → added electron experiences high electron–electron repulsion in compact 2p subshell.

### 1st vs. 2nd Electron Affinity
- **1st EA:** Exothermic (energy released) — atom is neutral, nucleus attracts e⁻.
- **2nd EA:** Endothermic (energy must be supplied) — adding e⁻ to an already negative ion requires overcoming repulsion from negative charge.

Example: O → O⁻ (EA₁ = −141 kJ/mol, exothermic)
O⁻ → O²⁻ (EA₂ = +798 kJ/mol, endothermic)

### Factors Controlling Electron Affinity Across a Period

| Factor | Effect on EA |
|--------|-------------|
| Increasing Z_eff | Increases EA (stronger nuclear attraction) |
| Decreasing atomic radius | Increases EA (e⁻ closer to nucleus) |
| Subshell stability (half-filled/full) | Decreases EA (N, Be exceptions) |
| Electron–electron repulsion | Decreases EA (very small atoms like F) |

## 4. Electronegativity

### Definition
**Electronegativity** is the tendency of an atom in a molecule to attract the shared pair of electrons towards itself (Pauling scale).

### Periodic Trends

```
Increasing electronegativity →
↑ Period 2: Li(1.0) < Be(1.5) < B(2.0) < C(2.5) < N(3.0) < O(3.5) < F(4.0)
↓ Group: F(4.0) > Cl(3.2) > Br(3.0) > I(2.7)

Most electronegative: F (4.0)
Least electronegative: Cs / Fr (~0.7)
```

### How Electronegativity Controls Ionic Properties in Covalent Compounds

When two atoms of different electronegativities share electrons:
- The more electronegative atom pulls the shared pair closer.
- Creates a **dipole moment** (δ+ on less electronegative, δ− on more electronegative).
- If ΔEN > 1.7 → mostly ionic; if ΔEN < 0.4 → mostly nonpolar covalent.
- **Bond polarity** controls: melting points, solubility, boiling points, reactivity.

### Relative Strength of Oxyacids (HOF, HOCl, HOBr, HOI)

For oxyacids of halogens (HOX), acidity depends on the **electronegativity of X**:
- More electronegative X → withdraws electrons from O–H bond → weakens O–H → easier H⁺ release → **stronger acid**.

| Acid | X | EN(X) | Acid Strength |
|------|---|-------|--------------|
| HOF | F | 4.0 | Strongest |
| HOCl | Cl | 3.2 | Strong |
| HOBr | Br | 3.0 | Moderate |
| HOI | I | 2.7 | Weakest |

**Order of decreasing acid strength: HOF > HOCl > HOBr > HOI**

## 5. Screening Effect & Effective Nuclear Charge

### Screening (Shielding) Effect

**Definition:** The reduction in nuclear attraction experienced by valence electrons due to repulsion from inner-shell (core) electrons is called the **screening (shielding) effect**.

```
Nucleus (+Z)
   ↕  ← shielded by inner electrons
Inner electrons (core)
   ↕  ← reduced attraction reaches here
Valence electrons
```

The **effective nuclear charge (Z_eff)** experienced by a valence electron:

$$Z_{eff} = Z - \sigma$$

Where:
- **Z** = actual atomic number
- **σ (sigma)** = shielding constant

### Slater's Rules for Calculating σ

Electrons are grouped as:
```
(1s) | (2s, 2p) | (3s, 3p) | (3d) | (4s, 4p) | (4d) | (4f) | (5s, 5p)...
```

Rules for the **shielding constant σ** of a given electron:

| Shielding electrons | Contribution per electron |
|--------------------|--------------------------| 
| Same group (same n, same l) | 0.35 (0.30 for 1s) |
| One group lower (n−1) for s or p electron | 0.85 |
| Two or more groups lower | 1.00 |
| All lower groups for d or f electron | 1.00 |

### Calculation: Z_eff for outermost shell electron of Zn (Z = 30)

**Electronic configuration of Zn:** [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s²

The outermost electron is in **4s**.

Slater grouping:
```
(1s²)(2s²2p⁶)(3s²3p⁶)(3d¹⁰)(4s²)
```

Shielding contributions on one 4s electron:
- **Same group (4s):** 1 other 4s electron → 1 × 0.35 = 0.35
- **One group lower (n−1, i.e., 3s3p3d):** 18 electrons → 18 × 0.85 = 15.30
- **Two+ groups lower (n ≤ 2, i.e., 1s2s2p):** 10 electrons → 10 × 1.00 = 10.00

$$\sigma = 0.35 + 15.30 + 10.00 = 25.65$$

$$Z_{eff}(Zn, 4s) = 30 - 25.65 = \boxed{4.35}$$

## 6. Noble-Gas Core & Valence Electrons

### Noble-Gas Core
The **noble-gas core** refers to the inner electrons of an atom that correspond to the configuration of the preceding noble gas. It is written in square brackets.

**Examples:**
```
Na (Z=11):  [Ne] 3s¹       → core = [Ne] = 1s²2s²2p⁶ (10 e⁻)
Ca (Z=20):  [Ar] 4s²       → core = [Ar] = 1s²2s²2p⁶3s²3p⁶ (18 e⁻)
Fe (Z=26):  [Ar] 3d⁶4s²   → core = [Ar]
```

### Valence Electrons
**Valence electrons** are the electrons in the outermost principal energy level (highest n) that participate in chemical bonding.

| Element | Full Config | Noble-gas notation | Valence e⁻ |
|---------|------------|-------------------|------------|
| Na | 1s²2s²2p⁶3s¹ | [Ne] 3s¹ | 1 |
| Cl | 1s²2s²2p⁶3s²3p⁵ | [Ne] 3s²3p⁵ | 7 |
| Ca | 1s²2s²2p⁶3s²3p⁶4s² | [Ar] 4s² | 2 |
| Zn (NH₃)₄]²⁺ | [Ar] 3d¹⁰4s² | [Ar] 3d¹⁰4s² | 2 (4s) or 12 (4s+3d) |

### Q5. How effective nuclear charge controls atomic sizes across a period? *(2018, Q1a — 4.5 marks)*

As we move across a period from left to right:
1. Each element has one more proton (Z increases by 1).
2. Each additional electron enters the **same shell** (same n value).
3. Inner electrons don't change → shielding remains nearly constant.
4. **Z_eff = Z − σ increases** because Z increases but σ stays roughly constant.
5. Higher Z_eff → greater nuclear pull on valence electrons → electrons drawn closer to nucleus → **atomic radius decreases**.

```
Period 3:   Na    Mg    Al    Si    P     S     Cl    Ar
Z_eff:     2.51  3.31  4.07  4.29  4.89  5.48  6.12  6.75
Radius(pm): 186   160   143   118   110   103    99    71
```

Clear inverse relationship: as Z_eff ↑, radius ↓.

### Q7. Explain why the first ionization potential of N is more than that of O. *(2013, Q2c)*

*(See Section 2)*

N (1s²2s²2p³): half-filled 2p subshell has extra stability (Hund's rule — all 3 electrons unpaired). No pairing repulsion.

O (1s²2s²2p⁴): one electron pair in 2p. The paired electrons repel each other → one electron is easier to remove.

**IE₁(N) = 1402 kJ/mol > IE₁(O) = 1314 kJ/mol** ✓

### Q8. Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle — show it is valid only for small particles. *(2017, Q2a; 2015, Q1b; 2013, Q2a)*

**Statement:** It is impossible to simultaneously determine both the exact position (Δx) and exact momentum (Δp) of a subatomic particle:

$$\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \frac{h}{4\pi}$$

**For an electron (m = 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg):**
- If Δv = 1 m/s, then Δp = mΔv = 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg·m/s
- Δx ≥ h/(4π·Δp) = 6.626×10⁻³⁴ / (4π × 9.11×10⁻³¹) ≈ **5.8 × 10⁻⁵ m = 58 μm**
- This uncertainty (~58 μm) is huge compared to atomic size (~100 pm = 10⁻¹⁰ m).
- The principle is **very significant** for electrons.

**For a cricket ball (m = 0.15 kg):**
- If Δv = 1 m/s, then Δp = 0.15 kg·m/s
- Δx ≥ h/(4π × 0.15) = 6.626×10⁻³⁴ / (1.885) ≈ **3.5 × 10⁻³⁴ m**
- This is immeasurably small — completely negligible.
- The principle is **insignificant** for macroscopic objects.

**Conclusion:** Heisenberg's principle matters only at the atomic/subatomic scale. ✓

### Q9. Hund's rule of maximum multiplicity — justify as lowest energy principle. *(2017, Q2c)*

**Hund's Rule:** When electrons occupy degenerate orbitals (same energy, e.g., 2px, 2py, 2pz), they distribute singly and with **parallel spins** before any pairing occurs.

**Justification:**
- Electrons in the same orbital repel each other (same space → large repulsion).
- Electrons in **separate** degenerate orbitals occupy different regions of space → **minimize electron–electron repulsion**.
- Parallel spins are further stabilized by **quantum-mechanical exchange energy** (positive exchange integral K for same-spin electrons).
- The ground state (lowest energy) has maximum spin multiplicity (2S+1).

**Example — Carbon (Z=6): [He] 2s² 2p²**
```
Correct (lower energy):   2p: ↑_ ↑_ __   (parallel spins, separate orbitals)
Incorrect (higher energy): 2p: ↑↓ __ __   (paired, high repulsion)
```

**Q2.** Calculate Z_eff for the 3d electron of Fe (Z = 26).

<details>
<summary>✅ Solution</summary>

Fe: [Ar] 3d⁶ 4s²

For a **3d electron**:
Slater groups: (1s²)(2s²2p⁶)(3s²3p⁶)(3d⁶)(4s²) — but for d-electron, all same-group means all 3d except the one in question.

- Same group (3d): 5 electrons × 0.35 = 1.75
- Groups lower (n ≤ 2: 1s2s2p and 2s3p, i.e., groups 1 and 2): 10 electrons × 1.00 = 10.00

Wait — for d electrons, the rule is: all lower groups contribute 1.00.
Groups lower than 3d: (1s²)(2s²2p⁶)(3s²3p⁶) → 18 electrons × 1.00 = 18.00

σ = 1.75 + 18.00 = **19.75**

Z_eff(Fe, 3d) = 26 − 19.75 = **6.25**

</details>

**Q3.** Why is the atomic radius of Cl⁻ larger than that of Cl?

<details>
<summary>✅ Solution</summary>

- Cl: 17 electrons, 17 protons → Z_eff pulls electrons tightly.
- Cl⁻: 18 electrons, 17 protons → One **extra electron** is added, increasing electron–electron repulsion without increasing nuclear charge.
- The increased repulsion causes the electron cloud to **expand** → larger radius.
- Also, Z_eff decreases from 6.12 (Cl) to a smaller value for Cl⁻.
- **Cl⁻ (181 pm) > Cl (99 pm)**

</details>

---

**Q4.** Which has higher electron affinity — N or P? Explain.

<details>
<summary>✅ Solution</summary>

Normally, EA decreases going down a group. However:
- **N** has a half-filled 2p³ configuration — extra stability makes it resist gaining an electron → **EA(N) ≈ 0 kJ/mol** (actually slightly negative).
- **P** also has half-filled 3p³ but is larger → the incoming e⁻ faces **less repulsion** in the larger 3p subshell.

Actually, EA(P) ≈ −72 kJ/mol; EA(N) ≈ 0 kJ/mol
**P has a more negative (larger exothermic) EA than N** despite being below it in the group, for the same reason F < Cl in EA.

</details>

**Q5.** Why do noble gases have very high ionization energies?

<details>
<summary>✅ Solution</summary>

Noble gases (He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe) have completely **filled valence shells**:
- He: 1s² (duet)
- Ne: 2s²2p⁶, Ar: 3s²3p⁶ (octets)

The completely filled subshells provide maximum symmetry and electron–electron exchange energy stability. Removing an electron would destroy this stable configuration. Therefore, a **very large amount of energy** is required, giving noble gases the highest IE in their respective periods.

</details>

**Q6.** What is the (n + l) rule? Write five orbitals in increasing energy order.

<details>
<summary>✅ Solution</summary>

The **(n + l) rule (Madelung's rule / Aufbau principle):**
- Lower (n + l) value → lower energy → fills first.
- If (n + l) is equal for two orbitals → lower **n** fills first.

| Orbital | n | l | n + l | Fill order |
|---------|---|---|-------|------------|
| 1s | 1 | 0 | 1 | 1st |
| 2s | 2 | 0 | 2 | 2nd |
| 2p | 2 | 1 | 3 | 3rd |
| 3s | 3 | 0 | 3 | 4th |
| 3p | 3 | 1 | 4 | 5th |
| 4s | 4 | 0 | 4 | 6th (before 3d!) |
| 3d | 3 | 2 | 5 | 7th |
| 4p | 4 | 1 | 5 | 8th |

**Five orbitals in increasing energy:** 1s < 2s < 2p < 3s < 3p

</details>

**Q7.** An electron is in the 4f orbital. What possible values can n, l, m, and s have?

<details>
<summary>✅ Solution</summary>

For **4f** orbital:
- **n = 4** (principal quantum number)
- **l = 3** (f subshell, l = 0,1,2,3 for s,p,d,f)
- **m = −3, −2, −1, 0, +1, +2, +3** (7 possible values)
- **s = +½ or −½** (spin quantum number)

Total 4f orbitals = 7; total electrons = 14.

</details>

**Q8.** Why is the first ionization energy of Mg higher than that of Na?

<details>
<summary>✅ Solution</summary>

Both are Period 3 elements:
- Na (Z=11): [Ne] 3s¹ — Z_eff(3s) = 11 − 8.85 = 2.15 (Slater)
- Mg (Z=12): [Ne] 3s² — Z_eff(3s) = 12 − 8.85 = 3.15

Mg has a higher effective nuclear charge on its 3s electrons than Na, so its valence electrons are held more tightly. More energy is needed to remove the first electron from Mg than from Na.

**IE₁(Mg) = 738 kJ/mol > IE₁(Na) = 496 kJ/mol** ✓

</details>

**Q9.** Explain why electron affinity of F is less than that of Cl (an anomaly in the trend).

<details>
<summary>✅ Solution</summary>

Normally EA increases as you go up a group (smaller atom → stronger nuclear attraction for incoming e⁻). We'd expect F > Cl.

However: **EA(Cl) = −349 kJ/mol > EA(F) = −328 kJ/mol**

This anomaly is because:
- F is an **extremely small** atom (r = 64 pm).
- When an electron is added to the tiny 2p subshell, the resulting high **electron–electron repulsion** in the compact space partially offsets the nuclear attraction.
- Cl is larger → incoming electron enters roomier 3p → less repulsion → higher (more exothermic) EA.

</details>

**Q10.** What are the four quantum numbers? Describe each with their allowed values.

<details>
<summary>✅ Solution</summary>

| Quantum Number | Symbol | Describes | Allowed Values |
|---------------|--------|-----------|----------------|
| Principal | n | Shell / energy level | 1, 2, 3, 4, ... |
| Azimuthal (angular momentum) | l | Subshell / orbital shape | 0 to (n−1) |
| Magnetic | m or m_l | Orbital orientation in space | −l to +l (including 0) |
| Spin | s or m_s | Electron spin direction | +½ or −½ |

**Example:** An electron in 3p: n=3, l=1, m=−1,0,or+1, s=±½

**Pauli Exclusion Principle:** No two electrons in an atom can have all four quantum numbers identical.

</details>

**Q11.** Describe how electronegativity difference between bonded atoms relates to bond polarity and ionic character.

<details>
<summary>✅ Solution</summary>

| ΔEN (Pauling) | Bond Type | Example |
|---------------|-----------|---------|
| 0 | Nonpolar covalent | H₂, Cl₂ |
| 0.1–0.4 | Slightly polar covalent | C–H |
| 0.4–1.7 | Polar covalent | H–Cl (ΔEN = 0.9) |
| > 1.7 | Predominantly ionic | NaCl (ΔEN = 2.1) |

As ΔEN increases:
- The shared electrons shift toward the more electronegative atom.
- Bond dipole moment (μ = q × d) increases.
- % ionic character increases: % ionic ≈ 1 − e^(−0.25 ΔEN²) × 100

</details>
