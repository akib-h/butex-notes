## 1. Arrhenius Concept

**Svante Arrhenius (1884):**
- **Acid:** A substance that produces HвЃє (Hв‚ѓOвЃє) ions in aqueous solution.
- **Base:** A substance that produces OHвЃ» ions in aqueous solution.

$$\text{HCl} \rightarrow H^+ + Cl^- \quad \text{(Acid)}$$
$$\text{NaOH} \rightarrow Na^+ + OH^- \quad \text{(Base)}$$

### Limitations
- Restricted to aqueous solutions only.
- Cannot explain basic behaviour of NHв‚ѓ (no OHвЃ» group).
- Cannot explain acidвЂ“base reactions in non-aqueous or gas phase.

## 2. BrГёnsted-Lowry Theory

**BrГёnsted and Lowry (1923):**
- **Acid (proton donor):** Any species that can donate a proton (HвЃє).
- **Base (proton acceptor):** Any species that can accept a proton (HвЃє).

$$\text{Acid} \rightleftharpoons \text{Base} + H^+$$

### Examples
$$HCl + H_2O \rightleftharpoons Cl^- + H_3O^+$$
$$\underbrace{HCl}_{\text{acid}} + \underbrace{H_2O}_{\text{base}} \rightleftharpoons \underbrace{Cl^-}_{\text{conj. base}} + \underbrace{H_3O^+}_{\text{conj. acid}}$$

$$NH_3 + H_2O \rightleftharpoons NH_4^+ + OH^-$$
$$\underbrace{H_2O}_{\text{acid}} + \underbrace{NH_3}_{\text{base}} \rightleftharpoons \underbrace{OH^-}_{\text{conj. base}} + \underbrace{NH_4^+}_{\text{conj. acid}}$$

### Amphoteric Species
Water, HCOв‚ѓвЃ», HSOв‚„вЃ» can act as both acid and base (amphoteric/amphiprotic):

$$H_2O + HCl \rightleftharpoons H_3O^+ + Cl^- \quad (H_2O = base)$$
$$H_2O + NH_3 \rightleftharpoons OH^- + NH_4^+ \quad (H_2O = acid)$$

### Advantages over Arrhenius
- Works in non-aqueous solvents.
- Explains NHв‚ѓ as a base (accepts HвЃє).
- Defines conjugate pairs.

### Limitation
- Restricted to proton transfer reactions only.
- Cannot explain Lewis acid-base reactions (no proton transfer).

## 3. Lewis Theory

**G.N. Lewis (1923):**
- **Lewis acid:** Electron-pair **acceptor**.
- **Lewis base:** Electron-pair **donor**.

$$\text{Lewis Acid} + \text{Lewis Base} \rightarrow \text{Lewis Acid-Base complex}$$

### Examples

| Lewis Acid | Type | Lewis Base | Product |
|-----------|------|-----------|---------|
| BFв‚ѓ | Empty p orbital | NHв‚ѓ | Fв‚ѓBв†ђNHв‚ѓ |
| AlClв‚ѓ | Empty orbital | ClвЃ» | [AlClв‚„]вЃ» |
| FeВівЃє | Empty d-orbitals | CNвЃ» | [Fe(CN)в‚†]ВівЃ» |
| HвЃє | Empty 1s | OHвЃ» | Hв‚‚O |
| SOв‚ѓ | Electrophilic S | OВІвЃ» | SOв‚„ВІвЃ» |

### Lewis Acid Classification by Electronic Configuration

1. **Molecules with incomplete octets:** BFв‚ѓ, BClв‚ѓ, AlClв‚ѓ (6 electrons around central atom).
2. **Molecules with expandable octet (using d-orbitals):** SiFв‚„, PFв‚…, SFв‚„ (can accept more pairs).
3. **Metal cations:** All metal ions are Lewis acids (empty d-orbitals or s/p orbitals).
4. **Molecules with multiple bonds:** COв‚‚, SOв‚ѓ (ПЂ electron density can shift to accommodate incoming base).

### Identifying Lewis Acid and Base

In: OВІвЃ» + COв‚‚ в†’ COв‚ѓВІвЃ»

- OВІвЃ»: has lone pairs в†’ **Lewis base** (electron-pair donor)
- COв‚‚: accepts the lone pair through the electrophilic carbon в†’ **Lewis acid** (electron-pair acceptor)

In: OВІвЃ» + SOв‚ѓ в†’ SOв‚„ВІвЃ» (also Lewis acid-base)

## 4. HSAB Theory (Pearson Concept)

**Ralph Pearson (1963) вЂ” Hard and Soft Acids and Bases:**

### Definitions

**Hard species:** Small, compact, not easily polarized (high charge density).
- Hard acids: High charge, small size, low polarizability (e.g., HвЃє, LiвЃє, MgВІвЃє, AlВівЃє, CrВівЃє, FeВівЃє)
- Hard bases: Small, electronegative, not easily polarized (e.g., FвЃ», OHвЃ», Hв‚‚O, NHв‚ѓ, OВІвЃ», ClвЃ»)

**Soft species:** Large, polarizable, easily distorted (low charge density).
- Soft acids: Low charge, large size, high polarizability (e.g., CuвЃє, AgвЃє, AuвЃє, HgВІвЃє, PtВІвЃє, PdВІвЃє)
- Soft bases: Large, polarizable, low electronegativity (e.g., IвЃ», SВІвЃ», SHвЃ», CNвЃ», CO, phosphines PRв‚ѓ)

### HSAB Rule (Pearson's Principle)
> **Hard acids prefer hard bases; soft acids prefer soft bases.**

Hard-Hard: Ionic interactions (electrostatic)
Soft-Soft: Covalent interactions (polarization, orbital overlap)

### HSAB Classification Table

| | Hard Acids | Borderline | Soft Acids |
|--|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Examples | HвЃє, AlВівЃє, CrВівЃє, FeВівЃє | FeВІвЃє, CoВІвЃє, CuВІвЃє, ZnВІвЃє | CuвЃє, AgвЃє, HgВІвЃє, PtВІвЃє |

| | Hard Bases | Borderline | Soft Bases |
|--|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Examples | FвЃ», OHвЃ», Hв‚‚O, NHв‚ѓ | BrвЃ», NOв‚‚вЃ», SOв‚ѓВІвЃ» | IвЃ», SCNвЃ», SВІвЃ», CNвЃ», CO |

### Applications of HSAB

**1. Arranging AgF, AgCl, AgBr, AgI in order of decreasing solubility:**
- AgвЃє is a **soft acid**.
- FвЃ» (hard base) в†’ AgF is more stable with AgвЃє? No вЂ” **soft-hard match is unfavorable**.
- IвЃ» (soft base) в†’ AgI is most stable (soft-soft match) в†’ **least soluble**.
- Decreasing solubility: **AgF > AgCl > AgBr > AgI**

**2. Distribution of hard and soft bases by donor atom in periodic table:**
- Moving down Group VIA: O (hard) в†’ S (borderline) в†’ Se, Te (soft)
- Moving down Group VIIA: F (hard) в†’ Cl (borderline-hard) в†’ Br (borderline) в†’ I (soft)
- In general: **heavier, more polarizable donor atoms в†’ softer bases**

**3. PtвЃґвЃє with SCNвЃ»:**
- PtвЃґвЃє = soft acid в†’ prefers S (soft base) end of SCNвЃ» в†’ **S-bonded** вњ“

## 5. Lux-Flood Concept

**H. Lux (1939) and H. Flood (1947)** вЂ” applicable to **anhydrous molten systems** and high-temperature reactions (smelting, ceramics).

- **Acid:** OВІвЃ» ion **acceptor** (oxide acceptor).
- **Base:** OВІвЃ» ion **donor** (oxide donor).

$$\text{Base} \rightleftharpoons \text{Acid} + O^{2-}$$

### Examples

**CaO + SOв‚ѓ в‡Њ CaВІвЃє + SOв‚„ВІвЃ»**

- SOв‚ѓ accepts OВІвЃ» from CaO в†’ SOв‚ѓ is the **Lux-Flood acid**
- CaO donates OВІвЃ» в†’ CaO is the **Lux-Flood base**

**Identifying from SOв‚„ВІвЃ» в‡Њ SOв‚ѓ + OВІвЃ»:**
- SOв‚ѓ = acid (loses OВІвЃ»)
- OВІвЃ» = base (oxide ion)

### Application: CaO + SOв‚ѓ в‡Њ CaВІвЃє + SOв‚„ВІвЃ»

| Species | Role |
|---------|------|
| SOв‚ѓ | **Lux-Flood Acid** (accepts OВІвЃ») |
| CaO | **Lux-Flood Base** (donates OВІвЃ») |
| SOв‚„ВІвЃ» | Product |
| CaВІвЃє | Counter cation |

### Lux-Flood Scale
Acid strength = tendency to accept OВІвЃ»:

```
COв‚‚ < SOв‚‚ < SOв‚ѓ < NOв‚‚ < SiOв‚‚ < Pв‚‚Oв‚…
Weakest acid                    Strongest acid

CaO > MgO > BeO > Alв‚‚Oв‚ѓ > SiOв‚‚
Strongest base               Amphoteric
```

## 6. Conjugate Acid-Base Pairs

A **conjugate pair** differs by exactly one proton:

| Acid | в‡Њ | Conjugate Base |
|------|---|----------------|
| HCl | в‡Њ | ClвЃ» |
| Hв‚‚SOв‚„ | в‡Њ | HSOв‚„вЃ» |
| Hв‚‚SOв‚ѓ | в‡Њ | HSOв‚ѓвЃ» |
| HSOв‚„вЃ» | в‡Њ | SOв‚„ВІвЃ» |
| Hв‚‚COв‚ѓ | в‡Њ | HCOв‚ѓвЃ» |
| HCOв‚ѓвЃ» | в‡Њ | COв‚ѓВІвЃ» |
| NHв‚„вЃє | в‡Њ | NHв‚ѓ |
| Hв‚ѓOвЃє | в‡Њ | Hв‚‚O |
| Hв‚‚O | в‡Њ | OHвЃ» |

**Inverse relationship:** Stronger acid в†’ weaker conjugate base; weaker acid в†’ stronger conjugate base.

### Determining Direction of Acid-Base Reaction
**Rule:** Equilibrium favors the side with the **weaker acid and weaker base**.

For: SOв‚„ВІвЃ» + HCN в‡Њ HSOв‚„вЃ» + CNвЃ»

- HCN (pKa = 9.2) is a **weaker acid** than HSOв‚„вЃ» (pKa = 1.9)
- CNвЃ» is a **stronger base** than SOв‚„ВІвЃ»
- Forward reaction: HCN в†’ HSOв‚„вЃ» (stronger acid on right side)
- **Reaction favors the reverse direction** (products are stronger acid/base в†’ equilibrium lies to the left)

## 7. Strength of Oxyacids

### Pauling's Rules for Oxyacids H_nXO_m

1. If n = 0 (no non-OH oxygens): **weak acid** (Ka ~10вЃ»вЃ·)
2. If n = 1: **moderate acid** (Ka ~10вЃ»ВІ)
3. If n = 2: **strong acid** (Ka ~10Ві)
4. If n = 3: **very strong acid** (Ka ~10вЃё)

Where n = number of non-OH oxygen atoms (=mв€’n for H_nXO_m).

### Comparing HSOв‚ѓвЃ» and Hв‚‚SOв‚ѓ

- Hв‚‚SOв‚ѓ (sulfurous acid): S with 1 non-OH oxygen в†’ stronger (pKaв‚Ѓ = 1.81)
- HSOв‚ѓвЃ» (bisulfite): after losing 1 HвЃє, bears negative charge в†’ harder to lose another HвЃє в†’ weaker (pKaв‚‚ = 6.91)
- **Hв‚‚SOв‚ѓ is a stronger acid than HSOв‚ѓвЃ»**

### Comparing HSOв‚„вЃ» and HSeOв‚„вЃ»

Both have 3 oxygens and same oxidation state:
- Hв‚‚SOв‚„/HSOв‚„вЃ»: S is more electronegative than Se в†’ greater electron withdrawal from O-H в†’ weaker O-H в†’ stronger acid
- **HSOв‚„вЃ» (pKa=1.99) > HSeOв‚„вЃ» (pKa=1.66)** вЂ” actually HSeOв‚„вЃ» is slightly stronger in some references; this is debated.

| Salt from | pH | Example |

## 9. Acidity of Metal Aquo-Complexes

Metal ions in water form **aquo complexes** like [Fe(Hв‚‚O)в‚†]ВІвЃє and [Fe(Hв‚‚O)в‚†]ВівЃє.

### Comparing [Fe(Hв‚‚O)в‚†]ВІвЃє vs [Fe(Hв‚‚O)в‚†]ВівЃє

### Q1. Discuss various types of probability samples. *(2019, Q4a вЂ” 3 marks)*

This question belongs to analytical chemistry. **Types of samples:**

1. **Simple random sample:** Each item has equal probability of selection.
2. **Systematic sample:** Items selected at fixed intervals (every nth item).
3. **Stratified sample:** Population divided into strata; random sample from each stratum.
4. **Cluster sample:** Population divided into clusters; entire clusters selected randomly.
5. **Grab/spot sample:** Single sample taken at one time and place.
6. **Composite sample:** Mix of multiple spot samples taken at different times or places.
