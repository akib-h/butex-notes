## ⚡ Types of Chemical Bonds

### Definition
> A **chemical bond** is the force of attraction that holds two or more atoms together to form a stable molecule or compound.

### Classification

```
Chemical Bonds
├── Ionic Bond (electrovalent)
├── Covalent Bond
│   ├── Polar covalent
│   └── Non-polar covalent
├── Coordinate Covalent Bond (dative bond)
├── Metallic Bond
└── Secondary Bonds
    ├── Hydrogen Bond
    ├── Van der Waals forces
    └── Dipole-dipole interactions
```

---

### 1. Ionic Bond
> Formed by the **complete transfer of electrons** from a metal (low IP) to a non-metal (high EA).

```
Na  +  Cl  →  Na⁺ + Cl⁻  →  NaCl
[Ne]3s¹  [Ne]3s²3p⁵    [Ne]   [Ar]
```

**Conditions for ionic bond formation:**
- Large electronegativity difference (> 1.7)
- Low ionization potential of cation
- High electron affinity of anion
- High lattice energy of the ionic compound

---

### 2. Covalent Bond
> Formed by **mutual sharing of electron pairs** between two non-metal atoms.

```
H· + ·H  →  H:H  →  H−H
         (shared pair)

:Cl· + ·Cl:  →  :Cl:Cl:  (Cl₂)
```

**Types:**
| Type | Pairs Shared | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Single bond (σ) | 1 | H−H, C−C |
| Double bond (σ+π) | 2 | O=O, C=C |
| Triple bond (σ+2π) | 3 | N≡N, C≡C |

---

### 3. Coordinate Covalent Bond (Dative Bond)
> A covalent bond where **both electrons are donated by one atom** (the donor/Lewis base) to another (acceptor/Lewis acid).

```
H₃N: + BF₃  →  H₃N→BF₃
(donor)  (acceptor)

H⁺ + :NH₃  →  [NH₄]⁺
         ↑
   Coordinate bond
```

## 🧲 Fajan's Rules

### Definition
> Fajan's rules predict the **degree of covalent character** in an ionic compound.

### Rules — Covalent character increases when:

| Rule | Condition | Explanation |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| 1 | **Small cation** | High charge density → high polarizing power |
| 2 | **Large anion** | Diffuse electron cloud → high polarizability |
| 3 | **High charge on cation** | Greater electrostatic attraction on anion |
| 4 | **18-electron configuration** of cation | Greater polarizing power than 8-electron config |

### Polarization Concept

```
Ionic compound:
  M⁺  ──  X⁻           (pure ionic, spherical anion)

With increasing covalency:
  M⁺  ──  X⁻            →    M⁺──X  →   M─X
  (anion distorted)          (covalent)
       ↑
   Polarization
```

### Examples

#### Why is MgCl₂ more covalent than NaCl?

| Property | Na⁺ | Mg²⁺ |
|----------|-----|------|
| Ionic radius | 102 pm | 72 pm |
| Charge | +1 | +2 |
| Charge density | Lower | **Higher** |
| Polarizing power | Lower | **Higher** |

**∴ Mg²⁺ polarizes Cl⁻ more → MgCl₂ is more covalent**

#### Why is SnF₄ ionic but SnCl₄ covalent?

| Factor | F⁻ | Cl⁻ |
|--------|----|-----|
| Ionic radius | 133 pm | 181 pm |
| Polarizability | **Low** | **High** |
| Result | Less polarized → ionic | More polarized → covalent |

**∴ SnF₄ is ionic, SnCl₄ is covalent**

## 🔵 Valence Bond Theory (VBT)

### Main Postulates

1. A covalent bond forms when **half-filled orbitals of two atoms overlap**
2. The two electrons must have **opposite spins**
3. Greater overlap → **stronger bond**
4. Bonds form along the **axis of maximum overlap**
5. Accounts for **hybridization** and **directional bonding**

### σ vs π Bonds in VBT

```
σ Bond (Sigma):
  ●━━━● (head-on overlap)
  Formed by: s-s, s-p, p-p (axial), sp³-sp³

π Bond (Pi):
  ≡≡●≡≡
  ≡≡●≡≡   (lateral overlap of parallel p-orbitals)
  Formed by: p-p sideways overlap
```

### VBT — HF Example

```
H:  1s¹
F:  1s² 2s² 2px² 2py² 2pz¹   (one half-filled 2pz)

H(1s¹) overlaps with F(2pz¹):
       σ bond
H ──────── F
     ↑↓ (shared pair)

Non-bonding electrons on F:
  2px [↑↓]  2py [↑↓]  (lone pairs — non-bonding)
```

**HF MO Diagram:**
```
Energy
  │
  │    ──── σ* (antibonding — empty)
  │   /
H 1s ─── + ─── F 2pz
  │   \
  │    ──── σ (bonding — 2 electrons)
  │
  │    ──── F 2px, 2py (non-bonding — 4 electrons as lone pairs)
  │
```

## 🌐 Molecular Orbital Theory (MOT)

### Basic Principles

1. **Atomic orbitals combine** (by LCAO method) to form Molecular Orbitals (MOs)
2. Number of MOs formed = Number of AOs combined
3. **Bonding MOs** (σ, π) — lower energy, electron density between nuclei
4. **Antibonding MOs** (σ*, π*) — higher energy, node between nuclei
5. MOs are filled following **Aufbau, Pauli, and Hund's rules**
6. MOs are associated with the **entire molecule** (delocalized)

### MO Energy Diagram — General Order

```
For O₂, F₂:
σ1s < σ*1s < σ2s < σ*2s < σ2pz < π2px = π2py < π*2px = π*2py < σ*2pz

For B₂, C₂, N₂:
σ1s < σ*1s < σ2s < σ*2s < π2px = π2py < σ2pz < π*2px = π*2py < σ*2pz
```

## 📊 Bond Order Calculations

### Formula

$$\text{Bond Order} = \frac{(\text{Bonding electrons}) - (\text{Antibonding electrons})}{2}$$

### Bond Order of N₂ (= 3)

**N₂** has 14 electrons total.

```
MO Configuration of N₂:
σ1s² σ*1s² σ2s² σ*2s² π2px² π2py² σ2pz²

Bonding electrons:  σ1s(2) + σ2s(2) + π2px(2) + π2py(2) + σ2pz(2) = 10
Antibonding electrons: σ*1s(2) + σ*2s(2) = 4

Bond Order = (10 - 4) / 2 = 6/2 = 3 ✓
```

**MO Diagram of N₂:**
```
Energy
         σ*2pz ────       (empty)
         π*2px,π*2py ════ (empty)
         σ2pz ────[↑↓]   (bonding)
  N2s    π2px,π2py ════[↑↓][↑↓]  (bonding)
   │     σ*2s ────[↑↓]   (antibonding)
   │     σ2s ────[↑↓]    (bonding)
   │     σ*1s ────[↑↓]   (antibonding)
   │     σ1s ────[↑↓]    (bonding)
```

---

### Bond Order of O₂ (= 2)

**O₂** has 16 electrons total.

```
MO Configuration of O₂:
σ1s² σ*1s² σ2s² σ*2s² σ2pz² π2px² π2py² π*2px¹ π*2py¹

Bonding electrons:  σ1s(2) + σ2s(2) + σ2pz(2) + π2px(2) + π2py(2) = 10
Antibonding electrons: σ*1s(2) + σ*2s(2) + π*2px(1) + π*2py(1) = 6

Bond Order = (10 - 6) / 2 = 4/2 = 2 ✓
```

**Paramagnetism of O₂:**
```
π*2px [↑]   π*2py [↑]   → 2 unpaired electrons → PARAMAGNETIC
```

> 💡 VBT predicts O₂ as diamagnetic (wrong), but **MOT correctly predicts it as paramagnetic** — a key triumph of MOT!

---

### Bond Order of HF

```
HF has 10 electrons total.
H: 1s¹,  F: 1s² 2s² 2p⁵

Only H(1s) and F(2pz) interact; others are non-bonding.

MO Config: [σ1s²] [σ*1s²] [σ2s²] [σ*2s²] [σ(HF)]² [nb(F2px)]² [nb(F2py)]²

Bond order = (2-0)/2 = 1
```

**HF MO Diagram:**
```
Energy
      σ*(HF) ──── (empty, antibonding)
     /
H(1s) ─── ── F(2pz)     ← interact and form bonds
     \
      σ(HF) ────[↑↓]    (bonding — 2e)

F(2px), F(2py) ────[↑↓][↑↓]   (non-bonding — 4e, lone pairs)
F(2s)  ────[↑↓]              (non-bonding — core)
```

---

### Bond Order of MgO

```
MgO has 20 electrons total.
Configuration: σ1s² σ*1s² σ2s² σ*2s² σ2pz² π2px² π2py² π*2px² π*2py²

Bonding = 10,  Antibonding = 8
Bond Order = (10-8)/2 = 1
```

---

### Why H₂ is Stable but He₂ is Not

```
H₂: σ1s²
  BO = (2-0)/2 = 1  → STABLE ✓

He₂: σ1s² σ*1s²
  BO = (2-2)/2 = 0  → DOES NOT EXIST ✗
```

### Why Mg₂ is Impossible

```
Mg₂ would have 24 electrons (2 × Mg with 12e each)
All bonding MOs are matched by antibonding MOs.
Bond order = 0 → Mg₂ does not exist.
```

## 🔶 Hybridization

### Definition
> Hybridization is the **intermixing of atomic orbitals of comparable energy** to form new hybrid orbitals of equivalent energy and shape, oriented to minimize repulsion.

### Types of Hybridization

| Hybridization | Orbitals mixed | Shape | Bond angle | Example |
|---------------|----------------|-------|-----------|---------|
| sp | s + p | Linear | 180° | BeCl₂, C₂H₂ |
| sp² | s + 2p | Trigonal planar | 120° | BF₃, C₂H₄ |
| sp³ | s + 3p | Tetrahedral | 109.5° | CH₄, CCl₄ |
| sp³d | s + 3p + d | Trigonal bipyramidal | 90°/120° | PCl₅ |
| sp³d² | s + 3p + 2d | Octahedral | 90° | SF₆ |

### Hybrid Bond vs Bond Order

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Hybrid bond** | A bond formed between hybridized orbitals (e.g., sp³ of C with 1s of H in CH₄) |
| **Bond order** | Number of covalent bonds between two atoms (single=1, double=2, triple=3) |

## 💧 Hydrogen Bonding

### Definition
> A hydrogen bond is a **weak electrostatic attraction** between a partially positive H atom (bonded to electronegative atom) and a lone pair of electrons on another electronegative atom.

```
X-H ··· Y  (hydrogen bond — dotted line)
where X, Y = F, O, N
```

### Types

#### 1. Intermolecular Hydrogen Bonding
> Between **different molecules** of the same or different substances.

```
H₂O molecules:
H─O···H─O···H─O
  H     H     H
  ↑
  Intermolecular H-bond
  (responsible for high boiling point of water)
```

#### 2. Intramolecular Hydrogen Bonding
> Within the **same molecule** (chelation).

```
o-nitrophenol:
    O─H···O
   / \   ‖
  /   \  N⁺
 ●     \ /
  benzene ring

(H-bond between OH and NO₂ within same molecule → lower BP)
```

### Comparison

| Property | Intermolecular | Intramolecular |
|----------|---------------|----------------|
| Location | Between molecules | Within one molecule |
| Effect on BP | Increases | Decreases (less association) |
| Example | H₂O, HF, NH₃ | o-nitrophenol, salicylaldehyde |

## ⚛️ Bonding, Antibonding and Non-bonding Electrons

| Type | Location | Energy | Effect | Example |
|------|----------|--------|--------|---------|
| **Bonding** | Between nuclei | Lower than AOs | Stabilizes molecule | σ, π electrons |
| **Antibonding** | Outside/beside nuclei | Higher than AOs | Destabilizes molecule | σ*, π* electrons |
| **Non-bonding** | On one atom only | Same as AOs | No effect on bond | Lone pairs (F in HF) |

```
In N₂:
  σ1s²  σ*1s²  σ2s²  σ*2s²  π2px²  π2py²  σ2pz²
  bond  antib  bond  antib  bond   bond   bond

  Bonding electrons = 10
  Antibonding electrons = 4
  Non-bonding electrons = 0
```

```
In HF:
  σ(HF)²      → bonding
  σ*(HF)⁰     → antibonding (empty)
  F(2px)² F(2py)²  → non-bonding (lone pairs)
```

## ⚖️ VBT vs MOT Comparison

| Feature | VBT | MOT |
|---------|-----|-----|
| **Proposed by** | Heitler & London (1927) | Hund & Mulliken (1932) |
| **Bond formation** | Overlap of half-filled AOs | LCAO of atomic orbitals |
| **Electrons** | Localized between atoms | Delocalized over molecule |
| **Orbital types** | Atomic + Hybrid orbitals | Molecular orbitals (BMO, ABMO) |
| **Magnetic properties** | Cannot explain O₂ paramagnetism | Correctly explains O₂ paramagnetism |
| **Bond order** | From Lewis structure | (Nb − Na)/2 |
| **Lone pairs** | Remain in atomic orbitals | Occupy non-bonding MOs |
| **Resonance** | Requires resonance structures | Automatically delocalized |
| **Complexity** | Simpler, intuitive | More complex, more accurate |

## 🧩 Ligands and Chelate Groups

### Ligands
> Species that donate lone pairs to a central metal ion to form a **coordinate bond** in a complex.

**Types:**

| Type | Donor sites | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Monodentate | 1 | Cl⁻, NH₃, H₂O, CN⁻ |
| Bidentate | 2 | en (ethylenediamine), oxalate |
| Polydentate | > 2 | EDTA (hexadentate) |

### Chelate Groups
> A **chelate** is a cyclic complex formed when a **polydentate ligand** bonds to the metal through two or more donor atoms, forming a ring.

```
M²⁺ + en (H₂N-CH₂-CH₂-NH₂):
     NH₂
    /    \
   M       CH₂
    \    /
     NH₂─CH₂
   (5-membered chelate ring)
```

**Chelate effect:** Chelate complexes are **more stable** than corresponding complexes with monodentate ligands due to increased entropy.

## 🧪 Practice Questions with Solutions

### Q1. Why is the bond angle in H₂O (104.5°) less than in CH₄ (109.5°)?

**Solution:**
- H₂O: O has sp³ hybridization but with **2 lone pairs**
- Lone pair–lone pair repulsion > lone pair–bond pair > bond pair–bond pair
- Lone pairs compress the H–O–H angle from 109.5° → 104.5°
- CH₄ has no lone pairs, so bond angle remains 109.5°

---

### Q2. Why does AgF have ionic character while AgI is covalent? (Fajan's Rule)

**Solution:**

| Anion | Radius | Polarizability | Character |
|-------|--------|----------------|-----------|
| F⁻ | 133 pm | Low | **Ionic** |
| I⁻ | 220 pm | High | **Covalent** |

Ag⁺ has a small radius and 18-electron configuration (high polarizing power). It distorts the large I⁻ more than the small F⁻. Hence AgI is more covalent.

---

### Q3. Calculate bond order of O₂⁻ (superoxide ion)

**Solution:**
O₂⁻ has 17 electrons (16+1).

```
MO config: σ1s² σ*1s² σ2s² σ*2s² σ2pz² π2px² π2py² π*2px² π*2py¹

Bonding = 10
Antibonding = 7
Bond Order = (10-7)/2 = 1.5
```

O₂⁻ has bond order = **1.5** (between single and double bond)

---

### Q4. Draw the MO diagram of Li₂ and find its bond order

**Solution:**
Li₂ has 6 electrons.

```
MO: σ1s² σ*1s² σ2s²

Bonding = 4
Antibonding = 2
Bond order = (4-2)/2 = 1
```

**Li₂ exists as a diatomic molecule** with BO = 1 (single bond), confirmed experimentally.

---

### Q5. Explain why a coordinate covalent bond is also called a dative bond

**Solution:**
The word "dative" comes from Latin *dare* (to give). In a coordinate bond, **one atom donates both electrons** of the shared pair. This one-directional donation distinguishes it from regular covalent bonds where each atom contributes one electron. Hence it is also called a **dative bond**. After formation, the coordinate bond is indistinguishable from a regular covalent bond in terms of length and strength.
