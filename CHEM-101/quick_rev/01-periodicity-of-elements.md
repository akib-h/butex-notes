## 1. Modern Periodic Law

**Mendeleev's Periodic Law (1869):**
> *"The physical and chemical properties of elements are a periodic function of their atomic weights."*

Mendeleev arranged 63 known elements in order of increasing atomic weight. Elements with similar properties were placed in the same vertical column (group). He left gaps for undiscovered elements and correctly predicted their properties (e.g., Eka-silicon → Germanium).

**Limitations of Mendeleev's law:**
- Could not explain the position of isotopes
- Wrong order of atomic weights (Ar before K; Co before Ni)
- Could not assign a proper place to hydrogen

**Modern Periodic Law (Moseley, 1913):**
> *"The physical and chemical properties of elements are a periodic function of their **atomic numbers**."*

The modern periodic table has **7 periods** and **18 groups**, arranged by increasing atomic number (Z).

```
Period 1:  Z = 1–2      (2 elements)
Period 2:  Z = 3–10     (8 elements)
Period 3:  Z = 11–18    (8 elements)
Period 4:  Z = 19–36    (18 elements)
Period 5:  Z = 37–54    (18 elements)
Period 6:  Z = 55–86    (32 elements)
Period 7:  Z = 87–118   (32 elements)
```

## 2. Quantum Numbers

Quantum numbers describe the **state of an electron** in an atom. There are four quantum numbers:

### 2.1 Principal Quantum Number (n)
- Defines the **shell / energy level**
- $n = 1, 2, 3, 4, \ldots$
- Determines the size and energy of the orbital
- Maximum electrons in shell = $2n^2$

### 2.2 Azimuthal (Angular Momentum) Quantum Number (ℓ)
- Defines the **subshell / shape** of the orbital
- $\ell = 0, 1, 2, \ldots, (n-1)$
- $\ell = 0 \rightarrow s$, $\ell = 1 \rightarrow p$, $\ell = 2 \rightarrow d$, $\ell = 3 \rightarrow f$

### 2.3 Magnetic Quantum Number (mₗ)
- Defines the **orientation** of orbital in space
- $m_\ell = -\ell, \ldots, 0, \ldots, +\ell$ → total $(2\ell + 1)$ values

### 2.4 Spin Quantum Number (mₛ)
- Describes the **spin** of electron
- $m_s = +\frac{1}{2}$ (spin up, ↑) or $m_s = -\frac{1}{2}$ (spin down, ↓)

### 2.5 Summary Table

| n | ℓ values | Subshells | mₗ values | Number of orbitals | Max electrons |
|---|---------|-----------|----------|-------------------|--------------|
| 1 | 0 | 1s | 0 | 1 | 2 |
| 2 | 0, 1 | 2s, 2p | 0; −1,0,+1 | 1+3 = 4 | 8 |
| 3 | 0, 1, 2 | 3s, 3p, 3d | 0; −1,0,+1; −2,−1,0,+1,+2 | 1+3+5 = 9 | 18 |
| 4 | 0, 1, 2, 3 | 4s, 4p, 4d, 4f | 0; −1,0,+1; −2..+2; −3..+3 | 1+3+5+7 = 16 | 32 |

### 2.6 When n = 4 (Detailed)

$$n = 4$$

| Subshell | ℓ | mₗ values | No. of orbitals | Max electrons |
|---------|---|-----------|----------------|---------------|
| 4s | 0 | 0 | 1 | 2 |
| 4p | 1 | −1, 0, +1 | 3 | 6 |
| 4d | 2 | −2, −1, 0, +1, +2 | 5 | 10 |
| 4f | 3 | −3, −2, −1, 0, +1, +2, +3 | 7 | 14 |
| **Total** | | | **16** | **32** |

**Electrons in specific orbitals:**
- 3s: max **2**
- 4p: max **6**
- 5d: max **10**
- 3d: max **10**
- 5f: max **14**

## 3. Orbital Shapes and Orientations

### 3.1 s Orbital (ℓ = 0)
- **Shape:** Spherical
- One orientation (mₗ = 0)
- Probability of finding electron is equal in all directions

```
        ___
      /     \
     |   ●   |   ← nucleus at centre
      \___/

      s orbital (spherical)
```

### 3.2 p Orbital (ℓ = 1)
- **Shape:** Dumbbell / figure-8
- Three orientations: $p_x, p_y, p_z$ along x, y, z axes
- Has one nodal plane

```
         z
         |
    ___  |  ___
   /   \ | /   \
  |  ●  |+|  ●  |   ← pz orbital (along z-axis)
   \___/ | \___/
         |
```

### 3.3 d Orbital (ℓ = 2)
- **Shape:** Cloverleaf (4-lobed), except $d_{z^2}$ (dumbbell + torus)
- Five orientations: $d_{xy}, d_{xz}, d_{yz}, d_{x^2-y^2}, d_{z^2}$
- Has two nodal planes

![Orbital Shapes](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Atomic-orbital-clouds_spdf_m0.png/800px-Atomic-orbital-clouds_spdf_m0.png)
*Figure 1.2: Shapes of s, p, d, f orbitals (Source: Wikimedia Commons)*

| Orbital | Shape | Nodal planes | Orientations |
|---------|-------|-------------|-------------|
| s | Sphere | 0 | 1 |
| p | Dumbbell | 1 | 3 (x, y, z) |
| d | Cloverleaf / $d_{z^2}$ | 2 | 5 |
| f | Complex | 3 | 7 |

## 4. Aufbau Principle, Pauli Exclusion & Hund's Rule

### 4.1 Aufbau Principle
> *"Electrons fill orbitals in order of increasing energy, from lowest to highest."*

**Energy order:**
$$1s < 2s < 2p < 3s < 3p < 4s < 3d < 4p < 5s < 4d < 5p < 6s < 4f < 5d \ldots$$

The **(n + ℓ) rule**: lower (n + ℓ) → lower energy. If equal, lower n fills first.

```
         1s
        2s 2p
       3s 3p 3d
      4s 4p 4d 4f
     5s 5p 5d 5f
    6s 6p 6d
   7s 7p

Fill diagonally: 1s→2s→2p→3s→3p→4s→3d→4p...
```

### 4.2 Pauli Exclusion Principle
> *"No two electrons in an atom can have the same set of all four quantum numbers."*

- Each orbital can hold **maximum 2 electrons** with opposite spins
- If two electrons share n, ℓ, mₗ → they must differ in mₛ (one +½, one −½)

**Example:** The two electrons in 1s of He:
- Electron 1: n=1, ℓ=0, mₗ=0, mₛ=+½
- Electron 2: n=1, ℓ=0, mₗ=0, mₛ=−½ ✓ (all four not the same)

### 4.3 Hund's Rule of Maximum Multiplicity
> *"Electrons occupy degenerate (equal energy) orbitals singly before pairing; all unpaired electrons have parallel spins."*

**Example — Nitrogen (Z=7):** 1s² 2s² 2p³

```
2p:  ↑   ↑   ↑
    [px] [py] [pz]    ← 3 unpaired electrons, parallel spins ✓

Wrong arrangement:
2p:  ↑↓   ↑   _       ← violates Hund's rule ✗
```

This minimizes electron-electron repulsion and lowers energy.

## 5. Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle

> *"It is impossible to determine simultaneously, with perfect accuracy, both the position and momentum (velocity) of a particle."*

$$\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \frac{h}{4\pi}$$

Where:
- $\Delta x$ = uncertainty in position
- $\Delta p = m \cdot \Delta v$ = uncertainty in momentum
- $h$ = Planck's constant ($6.626 \times 10^{-34}$ J·s)

**Significance:**
- Applies to subatomic particles (electrons, protons)
- Makes classical planetary model of atom invalid
- The concept of **orbitals** (probability distribution) replaces definite orbits
- More precisely we know position → less precisely we know momentum, and vice versa

**Example:** If $\Delta x = 10^{-10}$ m (size of atom):
$$\Delta v \geq \frac{h}{4\pi m \Delta x} = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{4\pi \times 9.11 \times 10^{-31} \times 10^{-10}} \approx 5.79 \times 10^5 \text{ m/s}$$

## 6. Classification of Elements (s, p, d, f Blocks)

| Block | Subshell filling | Groups | Properties |
|-------|-----------------|--------|------------|
| s-block | ns¹ or ns² | 1, 2 | Alkali & alkaline earth metals; highly reactive |
| p-block | np¹ to np⁶ | 13–18 | Metals, metalloids, non-metals, noble gases |
| d-block | (n−1)d¹⁻¹⁰ ns⁰⁻² | 3–12 | Transition metals; coloured ions; variable valency |
| f-block | (n−2)f¹⁻¹⁴ | Lanthanides, Actinides | Inner transition metals; radioactive (actinides) |

| Particle | Symbol | Charge | Mass (amu) | Location |
|---------|--------|--------|-----------|---------|
| Proton | p | +1 | 1.0073 | Nucleus |
| Neutron | n | 0 | 1.0087 | Nucleus |
| Electron | e⁻ | −1 | 0.000549 | Orbitals |

## 7. Atomic and Ionic Size — Trends

### 7.1 Atomic Radius Trends

**Across a Period (left → right):**
- Atomic radius **decreases**
- Nuclear charge (Z) increases, more protons pull electrons closer
- Same principal quantum shell; no new shielding

**Down a Group (top → bottom):**
- Atomic radius **increases**
- New electron shells added; increased shielding effect

![Atomic Radius Periodic Trend](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Atomic_and_ionic_radii.svg/1200px-Atomic_and_ionic_radii.svg.png)
*Figure 1.3: Atomic radius trends in the periodic table (Source: Wikimedia Commons)*

```
Period 2:  Li > Be > B > C > N > O > F > Ne
           (atomic radius decreasing left to right)

Group 1:   Li < Na < K < Rb < Cs
           (atomic radius increasing down the group)
```

### 7.2 Na, K, Cl — Comparison

| Element | Z | Configuration | Atomic radius (pm) |
|---------|---|--------------|-------------------|
| Na | 11 | [Ne] 3s¹ | 186 |
| K | 19 | [Ar] 4s¹ | 227 |
| Cl | 17 | [Ne] 3s² 3p⁵ | 99 |

- K > Na because K has one more electron shell (n=4 vs n=3)
- Cl < Na despite same period → higher nuclear charge in Cl

### 7.3 Fe, Fe²⁺, Fe³⁺

| Species | Configuration | Radius (pm) |
|---------|--------------|-------------|
| Fe (Z=26) | [Ar] 3d⁶ 4s² | 126 |
| Fe²⁺ | [Ar] 3d⁶ | 77 |
| Fe³⁺ | [Ar] 3d⁵ | 64 |

**Order:** Fe > Fe²⁺ > Fe³⁺

*Explanation:* Losing electrons decreases electron-electron repulsion and the remaining electrons are pulled closer to the nucleus. Fe³⁺ has fewer electrons → smaller radius.

### 7.4 Mn, Mn²⁺, Mn⁴⁺

| Species | Configuration | Radius (pm) |
|---------|--------------|-------------|
| Mn (Z=25) | [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s² | 127 |
| Mn²⁺ | [Ar] 3d⁵ | 83 |
| Mn⁴⁺ | [Ar] 3d³ | 53 |

**Order:** Mn > Mn²⁺ > Mn⁴⁺

### 7.5 Pb, Pb²⁺, Pb⁴⁺

| Species | Configuration | Radius (pm) |
|---------|--------------|-------------|
| Pb (Z=82) | [Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6s² 6p² | 175 |
| Pb²⁺ | [Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6s² | 119 |
| Pb⁴⁺ | [Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ | 77 |

**Order:** Pb > Pb²⁺ > Pb⁴⁺

## 8. Ionization Potential

**Definition:** The energy required to remove the **outermost electron** from a gaseous, ground-state neutral atom.

$$M(g) \rightarrow M^+(g) + e^- \quad \Delta H = \text{IP}_1$$

- **1st IP:** Remove 1st electron from neutral atom
- **2nd IP:** Remove electron from M⁺ → M²⁺ (always IP₂ > IP₁)

### 8.1 IP of Group 1 (Alkali Metals)

| Element | 1st IP (kJ/mol) | Trend |
|---------|----------------|-------|
| Li | 520 | ↑ |
| Na | 496 | |
| K | 419 | |
| Rb | 403 | ↓ |
| Cs | 376 | |

**Trend: Decreases down the group** because:
- Atomic radius increases → outermost electron farther from nucleus
- Increased shielding → effective nuclear charge decreases
- Easier to remove the outermost electron

### 8.2 IP of 2nd Period Elements

| Element | Li | Be | B | C | N | O | F | Ne |
|---------|-----|-----|---|----|---|----|---|----|
| 1st IP (kJ/mol) | 520 | 899 | 801 | 1086 | 1402 | 1314 | 1681 | 2081 |

**General trend:** Increases left → right (greater nuclear charge)

**Anomalies:**
- **Be > B:** Be has filled 2s² (extra stability); B has one electron in 2p (easier to remove)
- **N > O:** N has half-filled 2p³ (extra stability from exchange energy); O has paired electrons in one p orbital (extra repulsion → easier to remove)

```
IP trend across Period 2:

2500 |                                              Ne
     |                                         F
2000 |                                    
     |                        N
1500 |                    C       O
     |              B
1000 |       Be
     |   Li
 500 |________________________
      Li  Be  B   C   N   O  F  Ne
```

### 8.3 Compare 1st and 2nd IP

$$\text{IP}_2 > \text{IP}_1 \text{ always}$$

Because after losing the 1st electron:
- Fewer electrons → less shielding
- Same nuclear charge → pulls remaining electrons more tightly
- 2nd electron removed from M⁺ (positively charged) requires more energy

**Exceptional jumps in successive IPs** indicate the number of valence electrons.

## 9. Electron Affinity

**Definition:** Energy released when a gaseous neutral atom **gains** an electron to form an anion.

$$X(g) + e^- \rightarrow X^-(g) \quad \Delta H = -\text{EA}$$

- Negative ΔH = exothermic = energy is released
- Higher electron affinity → greater tendency to gain an electron

### 9.1 Trends

| Direction | Trend | Reason |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| Across period (→) | Generally increases | Greater nuclear charge attracts electron more |
| Down group (↓) | Generally decreases | Larger atom; electron farther from nucleus |

**Anomalies:**
- N has very low EA (half-filled 2p³ is stable; added electron goes into filled orbital, extra repulsion)
- F < Cl in EA (F is small; added electron experiences more repulsion in compact 2p shell)

| Element | EA (kJ/mol) |
|---------|------------|
| F | 328 |
| Cl | 349 |
| Br | 325 |
| I | 295 |
| N | −7 (negative!) |
| O | 141 |

## Practice Questions

### PQ1. State the modern periodic law and explain how it differs from Mendeleev's.

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

**Mendeleev's Law (1869):** Properties are periodic functions of atomic *weights*.  
**Modern Law (Moseley, 1913):** Properties are periodic functions of atomic *numbers* (Z).

Key differences:
| Feature | Mendeleev | Modern |
|---------|-----------|--------|
| Basis | Atomic weight | Atomic number |
| Anomalies (Ar/K, Co/Ni) | Could not explain | Explained — Z increases correctly |
| Isotopes | No position | All isotopes of same element → same Z |
| Gaps | Left gaps | Filled by predicted elements |

</details>

### PQ2. Write all possible quantum numbers for an electron in 3d orbital.

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

For 3d: n = 3, ℓ = 2

mₗ can be: −2, −1, 0, +1, +2 (5 values)  
mₛ can be: +½ or −½

Total possible sets: 5 orbitals × 2 = **10 electrons** (10 sets of quantum numbers)

Example for one electron: n=3, ℓ=2, mₗ=−2, mₛ=+½

</details>

### PQ3. Which has a larger radius — Na⁺ or Mg²⁺? Explain.

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

Both Na⁺ and Mg²⁺ are isoelectronic (both have 10 electrons, configuration [Ne]).

However:
- Na⁺: Z = 11, 10 electrons → 11 protons pull 10 electrons
- Mg²⁺: Z = 12, 10 electrons → 12 protons pull 10 electrons

**Mg²⁺ has a greater nuclear charge** pulling the same electrons more strongly.

$$r(\text{Na}^+) > r(\text{Mg}^{2+})$$

Na⁺ ≈ 102 pm; Mg²⁺ ≈ 72 pm

</details>

### PQ4. Why does nitrogen have a higher ionization energy than oxygen?

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

- N (Z=7): 1s² 2s² **2p³** — half-filled p subshell (extra stability due to exchange energy)
- O (Z=8): 1s² 2s² **2p⁴** — one p orbital has a paired electron (electron-electron repulsion)

The paired electron in oxygen is easier to remove due to repulsion, so:

$$\text{IP}_1(\text{N}) > \text{IP}_1(\text{O})$$

N: 1402 kJ/mol vs O: 1314 kJ/mol

</details>

### PQ5. An element has n=4 as its outermost principal quantum number. It has 5 electrons in its d subshell. Identify the element and write its electronic configuration.

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

- Outermost shell: n = 4 → 4s filled first
- 5 electrons in d subshell (3d⁵)
- Configuration: [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s²

This is **Manganese (Mn), Z = 25**

Full configuration: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 3d⁵ 4s²

The 3d⁵ is a half-filled d subshell → extra stability.

</details>

### PQ6. How many radial and angular nodes does a 3p orbital have?

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

For an orbital with quantum numbers n and ℓ:
- **Radial nodes** = n − ℓ − 1
- **Angular nodes** = ℓ
- **Total nodes** = n − 1

For 3p (n=3, ℓ=1):
- Radial nodes = 3 − 1 − 1 = **1**
- Angular nodes = **1**
- Total nodes = 3 − 1 = **2**

</details>
