## 1. Introduction ‚ÄĒ Key Definitions

### 1.1 Complex Compound (Coordination Compound)
A compound containing a **central metal atom/ion** surrounded by a definite number of **ligands** (ions or molecules) attached by coordinate (dative) bonds.

**Example:** K‚āĄ[Fe(CN)‚āÜ] ‚ÄĒ Potassium hexacyanoferrate(II)

### 1.2 Important Terms

| Term | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Coordination sphere** | Central metal ion + ligands (enclosed in [ ]) | [Fe(CN)‚āÜ]‚Āī‚ĀĽ |
| **Central metal ion** | Metal atom that accepts lone pairs | Fe¬≤‚Āļ in [Fe(CN)‚āÜ]‚Āī‚ĀĽ |
| **Ligand** | Ion or molecule that donates lone pair to metal | CN‚ĀĽ, NH‚āÉ, H‚āāO, Cl‚ĀĽ |
| **Coordination number (CN)** | Number of donor atoms directly bonded to metal | CN = 6 in [Fe(CN)‚āÜ]‚Āī‚ĀĽ |
| **Primary valency** | Charge of the metal ion (ionizable) | Fe = +2 ‚Üí ionized by KCN |
| **Secondary valency** | Number of ligands (coordination number) | 6 in [Fe(CN)‚āÜ]‚Āī‚ĀĽ |
| **Chelate** | Ligand bonded at 2+ points to same metal | Ethylenediamine (en), EDTA |
| **Denticity** | Number of donor atoms per ligand | Monodentate, bidentate, polydentate |

### 1.3 Ligand Types

| Type | Donor atoms | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| Monodentate | 1 | Cl‚ĀĽ, NH‚āÉ, H‚āāO, CN‚ĀĽ |
| Bidentate | 2 | en (H‚āāNCH‚āāCH‚āāNH‚āā), ox¬≤‚ĀĽ (oxalate) |
| Tridentate | 3 | diethylenetriamine (dien) |
| Tetradentate | 4 | triethylenetetramine (trien) |
| Hexadentate | 6 | EDTA‚Āī‚ĀĽ |

### 1.4 IUPAC Naming (Brief)
- Ligands named before metal
- Anionic ligands: -o suffix (Cl‚ĀĽ = chloro, CN‚ĀĽ = cyano, OH‚ĀĽ = hydroxo)
- Neutral ligands: name as-is (NH‚āÉ = ammine, H‚āāO = aqua, CO = carbonyl)
- Metal oxidation state in Roman numerals in parentheses

### 1.5 Common 4- and 6-Coordinate Complexes

| Formula | Name | CN | Geometry |
|---------|------|----|---------|
| [Ni(CN)‚āĄ]¬≤‚ĀĽ | Tetracyanonickelate(II) | 4 | Square planar |

## 2. Werner's Theory

**Proposed by:** Alfred Werner (1893) ‚ÄĒ Nobel Prize 1913

### 2.1 Postulates
1. **Primary valency (ionizable):** The principal valency of a metal ion, equal to its oxidation state. Satisfied by negative ions.

2. **Secondary valency (non-ionizable):** The coordination number ‚ÄĒ number of groups (ligands) that can be bonded to the metal in the coordination sphere. Satisfied by neutral molecules or anions.

3. Secondary valencies are directed in space ‚Üí determines **geometry** of the complex.

4. Every metal has a fixed coordination number (usually 4 or 6).

### 2.2 Illustration with [Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āÜ]Cl‚āÉ

```
         Primary valency (charge) = 3+ (Co¬≥‚Āļ)
         Satisfied by 3 Cl‚ĀĽ (ionizable counterions)
         
         Secondary valency = 6
         Satisfied by 6 NH‚āÉ (non-ionizable)
         
         [Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āÜ]¬≥‚Āļ Cl‚āÉ‚ĀĽ

         Structure: Octahedral
         
              NH‚āÉ
               |
  H‚āÉN - Co - NH‚āÉ
  |      |      |
 NH‚āÉ   NH‚āÉ   NH‚āÉ
```

### 2.3 Werner's Proof of Theory
Werner prepared different isomers of [Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āôCl‚āÉ] and measured the number of ions in solution (using conductivity). This confirmed which Cl‚ĀĽ ions were inside vs outside the coordination sphere.

| Formula | Ions in solution | Conductivity |
|---------|----------------|-------------|
| [Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āÜ]Cl‚āÉ | [Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āÜ]¬≥‚Āļ + 3Cl‚ĀĽ ‚Üí 4 ions | High |
| [Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āÖCl]Cl‚āā | [Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āÖCl]¬≤‚Āļ + 2Cl‚ĀĽ ‚Üí 3 ions | Medium |
| [Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āĄCl‚āā]Cl | [Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āĄCl‚āā]‚Āļ + Cl‚ĀĽ ‚Üí 2 ions | Low |
| [Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āÉCl‚āÉ] | [Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āÉCl‚āÉ] ‚Üí 0 ions | Zero |

## 3. Sidgwick's Theory (EAN Rule)

**Proposed by:** N.V. Sidgwick (1927)

**Effective Atomic Number (EAN) Rule:**
> When a central metal atom forms coordinate bonds with ligands, it gains electrons until its total electron count equals the atomic number of the **nearest noble gas**.

$$\text{EAN} = Z_{\text{metal}} - \text{(charge on metal ion)} + \text{(electrons donated by ligands)}$$

Each ligand donates **2 electrons** to the metal.

### 3.1 EAN of Stable Complexes

| Complex | Metal | Z | Charge on metal | e‚ĀĽ from ligands | EAN | Noble gas |
|---------|-------|---|----------------|----------------|-----|-----------|
| [Fe(CN)‚āÜ]‚Āī‚ĀĽ | Fe | 26 | +2 | 6 √ó 2 = 12 | 26‚ąí2+12 = **36** | Kr ‚úď |
| [Fe(CN)‚āÜ]¬≥‚ĀĽ | Fe | 26 | +3 | 6 √ó 2 = 12 | 26‚ąí3+12 = **35** | Not noble gas ‚ÄĒ less stable |
| [Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āÜ]¬≥‚Āļ | Co | 27 | +3 | 6 √ó 2 = 12 | 27‚ąí3+12 = **36** | Kr ‚úď |
| [Ni(CO)‚āĄ] | Ni | 28 | 0 | 4 √ó 2 = 8 | 28‚ąí0+8 = **36** | Kr ‚úď |
| [Cr(CO)‚āÜ] | Cr | 24 | 0 | 6 √ó 2 = 12 | 24‚ąí0+12 = **36** | Kr ‚úď |

**Complexes obeying the EAN rule are more stable.**

### 3.2 EAN for [Cr(NH‚āÉ)‚āÜ]Cl‚āÉ

- Cr (Z=24), charge = +3, CN = 6
- EAN = 24 ‚ąí 3 + (6 √ó 2) = 24 ‚ąí 3 + 12 = **33** (‚Č† 36, so less stable ‚ÄĒ but still forms due to other factors)

## 4. Pauling's Theory (VBT of Complexes)

**Based on:** Hybridization of metal orbitals to accept ligand lone pairs.

### 4.1 Inner Orbital vs Outer Orbital Complexes

| Type | Hybridization uses | Spin state | Example |
|------|------------------|-----------|---------|
| Inner orbital (low spin) | Inner d orbitals (3d) | Low spin (more paired) | [Fe(CN)‚āÜ]‚Āī‚ĀĽ |
| Outer orbital (high spin) | Outer d orbitals (4d) or 4s,4p,4d | High spin (more unpaired) | [FeF‚āÜ]¬≥‚ĀĽ |

**Inner orbital complexes:** Stronger field ligands force 3d electron pairing ‚Üí 3d orbital available ‚Üí uses 3d, 4s, 4p orbitals (d¬≤sp¬≥ or dsp¬≤)

**Outer orbital complexes:** Weak field ligands ‚Üí electrons remain unpaired ‚Üí uses 4s, 4p, 4d orbitals (sp¬≥d¬≤ for octahedral)

## 5. Crystal Field Theory (CFT)

**Proposed by:** Bethe (1929) and Van Vleck (1935)

**Assumptions:**
1. Metal-ligand bond is **purely electrostatic** (no covalent character)
2. Ligands are treated as **point negative charges** (or dipoles)
3. The electrostatic field from ligands **splits** the degenerate d orbitals

### 5.1 Splitting of d Orbitals in Octahedral Field

In a free metal ion, all 5 d orbitals are **degenerate** (equal energy).

When **6 ligands** approach along x, y, z axes (octahedral field):
- $d_{x^2-y^2}$ and $d_{z^2}$ (the $e_g$ set) ‚Üí **point directly at ligands** ‚Üí **higher energy** (repelled more)
- $d_{xy}, d_{xz}, d_{yz}$ (the $t_{2g}$ set) ‚Üí **point between ligands** ‚Üí **lower energy**

```
        Energy
          ‚ÜĎ
          |        ___   ___
          |       dx¬≤-y¬≤  dz¬≤     ‚Üź eg set (higher energy)
          |                        ‚Üē őĒo (Crystal field splitting energy)
   - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - (barycentre)
          |  ___  ___  ___
          |  dxy  dxz  dyz        ‚Üź t2g set (lower energy)
          |
```

$$\Delta_o = \text{Crystal field splitting parameter (octahedral)}$$

$$e_g \text{ energy} = +\frac{3}{5}\Delta_o = +0.6\Delta_o$$
$$t_{2g} \text{ energy} = -\frac{2}{5}\Delta_o = -0.4\Delta_o$$

### 5.2 Strong and Weak Field Ligands (Spectrochemical Series)

$$\text{I}^- < \text{Br}^- < \text{S}^{2-} < \text{SCN}^- < \text{Cl}^- < \text{F}^- < \text{OH}^- < \text{ox} < \text{H}_2\text{O} < \text{NCS}^- < \text{py} < \text{NH}_3 < \text{en} < \text{bipy} < \text{phen} < \text{NO}_2^- < \text{CN}^- < \text{CO}$$

- **Weak field ligands** ‚Üí small őĒo ‚Üí **high spin** (electrons spread out, max unpaired)
- **Strong field ligands** ‚Üí large őĒo ‚Üí **low spin** (electrons pair up first)

### 5.3 Tetrahedral Splitting

In tetrahedral field, splitting is **reversed** and smaller ($\Delta_t \approx \frac{4}{9}\Delta_o$):
- $e$ set ($d_{z^2}$, $d_{x^2-y^2}$) ‚Üí **lower energy**
- $t_2$ set ($d_{xy}$, $d_{xz}$, $d_{yz}$) ‚Üí **higher energy**

### 5.4 Merits of CFT
1. Explains **colour** of transition metal complexes (d-d transitions)
2. Explains **magnetic properties** (unpaired electrons)
3. Predicts **stability** (CFSE ‚ÄĒ Crystal Field Stabilization Energy)
4. Explains **geometric preferences** (tetrahedral vs square planar vs octahedral)
5. Accounts for **Jahn-Teller distortion**

**Limitations:** Ignores covalent character (addressed by Ligand Field Theory)

## 6. Stability of Complex Compounds

**Stability constant (formation constant, Kf):**

$$M + nL \rightleftharpoons ML_n \quad K_f = \frac{[\text{ML}_n]}{[M][L]^n}$$

Larger $K_f$ ‚Üí more stable complex.

### 6.1 Factors Affecting Stability

1. **Nature of metal ion:** Higher charge density (charge/radius) ‚Üí stronger metal-ligand bond ‚Üí more stable
2. **Nature of ligand:** Stronger donor ‚Üí more stable
3. **Chelate effect:** Chelating (polydentate) ligands form more stable complexes than monodentate ligands of comparable donor strength ‚Üí **entropy driven**
4. **CFSE:** Larger crystal field stabilization energy ‚Üí more stable
5. **EAN compliance:** Complexes achieving noble gas configuration are more stable

**Chelate Effect Example:**
$$[\text{Ni(en)}_3]^{2+} \text{ is much more stable than } [\text{Ni(NH}_3)_6]^{2+}$$

en is bidentate ‚ÄĒ forming chelate rings increases stability due to entropy (őĒS > 0 when one chelate replaces two monodentate ligands releasing more particles).

## 7. Paramagnetic and Diamagnetic Complexes

### 7.1 Definitions

| Property | Definition | Behavior in magnetic field |
|----------|-----------|--------------------------|
| **Paramagnetic** | Has **unpaired electrons** | Attracted to magnetic field |
| **Diamagnetic** | All electrons **paired** | Weakly repelled by magnetic field |

Magnetic moment: $\mu = \sqrt{n(n+2)}$ BM (Bohr magnetons), where n = number of unpaired electrons

### 7.2 K‚āĄ[Fe(CN)‚āÜ] ‚ÄĒ Diamagnetic

- K‚āĄ[Fe(CN)‚āÜ]: Fe is **Fe¬≤‚Āļ** (4 K‚Āļ ions balance charge of [Fe(CN)‚āÜ]‚Āī‚ĀĽ)
- Fe¬≤‚Āļ configuration: [Ar] **3d‚Ā∂**
- CN‚ĀĽ is a **strong field ligand** ‚Üí large őĒo ‚Üí forces pairing of all 3d electrons

```
Fe¬≤‚Āļ free ion (3d‚Ā∂):
  ‚ÜĎ‚Üď  ‚ÜĎ  ‚ÜĎ  ‚ÜĎ  ‚ÜĎ      (4 unpaired)
  t2g    eg  (high spin)

Fe¬≤‚Āļ with CN‚ĀĽ (low spin):
  ‚ÜĎ‚Üď  ‚ÜĎ‚Üď  ‚ÜĎ‚Üď  _  _    (0 unpaired ‚Üí DIAMAGNETIC)
   t2g (6e)    eg (0e)
```

All 6 electrons in t‚āāg, fully paired ‚Üí **őľ = 0** ‚Üí **diamagnetic** ‚úď

Hybridization: d¬≤sp¬≥ (inner orbital complex)

### 7.3 K‚āÉ[Fe(CN)‚āÜ] ‚ÄĒ Paramagnetic

- K‚āÉ[Fe(CN)‚āÜ]: Fe is **Fe¬≥‚Āļ** (3 K‚Āļ ions balance charge of [Fe(CN)‚āÜ]¬≥‚ĀĽ)
- Fe¬≥‚Āļ configuration: [Ar] **3d‚ĀĶ**
- CN‚ĀĽ still strong field ‚Üí but Fe¬≥‚Āļ has only 5 d electrons

```
Fe¬≥‚Āļ with CN‚ĀĽ (low spin):
  ‚ÜĎ‚Üď  ‚ÜĎ‚Üď  ‚ÜĎ  _  _     (1 unpaired ‚Üí PARAMAGNETIC)
   t2g (5e)   eg (0e)
```

1 unpaired electron ‚Üí $\mu = \sqrt{1(1+2)} = \sqrt{3} \approx 1.73$ BM ‚Üí **paramagnetic** ‚úď

### 7.4 [Cr(NH‚āÉ)‚āÜ]Cl‚āÉ Magnetic Properties

- Cr is Cr¬≥‚Āļ (3 Cl‚ĀĽ outside coordination sphere)
- Cr¬≥‚Āļ configuration: [Ar] **3d¬≥**
- NH‚āÉ is intermediate field ligand

```
Cr¬≥‚Āļ with NH‚āÉ:
  ‚ÜĎ  ‚ÜĎ  ‚ÜĎ  _  _       (3 unpaired ‚Üí PARAMAGNETIC)
   t2g (3e)  eg (0e)
```

3 unpaired electrons ‚Üí $\mu = \sqrt{3(3+2)} = \sqrt{15} \approx 3.87$ BM ‚Üí **paramagnetic**

### 7.5 Summary Table

| Complex | Metal ion | d electrons | Unpaired e‚ĀĽ | Magnetic |
|---------|----------|------------|------------|---------|
| K‚āĄ[Fe(CN)‚āÜ] | Fe¬≤‚Āļ | 3d‚Ā∂ | 0 (low spin, CN‚ĀĽ) | **Diamagnetic** |
| K‚āÉ[Fe(CN)‚āÜ] | Fe¬≥‚Āļ | 3d‚ĀĶ | 1 (low spin, CN‚ĀĽ) | **Paramagnetic** |
| [Cr(NH‚āÉ)‚āÜ]Cl‚āÉ | Cr¬≥‚Āļ | 3d¬≥ | 3 | **Paramagnetic** |

## 8. Geometrical Isomerism

**Geometrical isomers** are compounds with the **same molecular formula and connectivity** but **different spatial arrangements** of groups (cis vs trans).

### 8.1 Square Planar Complexes [Ma‚āāb‚āā]

**Example: [Pt(NH‚āÉ)‚āāCl‚āā]**

```
    NH‚āÉ   Cl              NH‚āÉ  NH‚āÉ
     |     |               |    |
 Pt           ‚Üí   Pt  (cis)  vs  Pt  (trans)
     |     |               |    |
    NH‚āÉ   Cl              Cl   Cl
    
cis: like groups adjacent     trans: like groups opposite
(90¬į apart)                   (180¬į apart)
```

- **cis-[Pt(NH‚āÉ)‚āāCl‚āā]** = Cisplatin ‚ÄĒ anti-cancer drug
- **trans-[Pt(NH‚āÉ)‚āāCl‚āā]** = Transplatin ‚ÄĒ no anti-cancer activity

### 8.2 Octahedral Complexes [Ma‚āĄb‚āā]

**Example: [Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āĄCl‚āā]‚Āļ**

```
        NH‚āÉ               NH‚āÉ
         |                 |
 H‚āÉN-Co-NH‚āÉ          H‚āÉN-Co-Cl
  |       |             |       |
  Cl    NH‚āÉ            Cl    NH‚āÉ
  |                           
  NH‚āÉ                  

  cis (90¬į)            trans (180¬į)
```

## 9. Optical Isomerism

**Optical isomers** are non-superimposable mirror images (**enantiomers**) that rotate plane-polarized light in opposite directions.

**Requirement:** The molecule must be chiral (no plane of symmetry).

### 9.1 Example: [Co(en)‚āÉ]¬≥‚Āļ (tris(ethylenediamine)cobalt(III))

```
       őõ (Lambda)          őĒ (Delta)
     isomer                isomer
    (left-handed)         (right-handed)
    
    Mirror images that are NOT superimposable
    ‚Üí rotate polarized light in opposite directions
```

- **őõ form:** Rotates light counterclockwise (levorotatory, ‚ąí)
- **őĒ form:** Rotates light clockwise (dextrorotatory, +)

Chiral octahedral complexes with three bidentate ligands ([M(en)‚āÉ]‚ĀŅ‚Āļ type) are classic examples.

### 9.2 Geometric vs Optical ‚ÄĒ Comparison

| Property | Geometric isomers | Optical isomers |
|----------|-----------------|----------------|
| Cause | Restricted rotation, spatial arrangement | Chirality (no plane of symmetry) |
| Types | cis/trans | d (dextro) and l (laevo) |
| Physical properties | Different MP, BP, solubility, colour | Same physical properties (except optical rotation) |
| Interconversion | Not easily interconverted | Difficult |

### PQ1. Calculate the EAN of [Ni(CO)‚āĄ] and predict whether it is stable.

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

- Ni (Z=28), CO is neutral ligand ‚Üí Ni is in 0 oxidation state
- Each CO donates 2 electrons
- EAN = 28 ‚ąí 0 + (4 √ó 2) = 28 + 8 = **36**

36 = atomic number of Kr (noble gas) ‚Üí **EAN rule satisfied ‚Üí stable** ‚úď

Hybridization: sp¬≥, geometry: tetrahedral

</details>

### PQ2. Identify if [CoF‚āÜ]¬≥‚ĀĽ is paramagnetic or diamagnetic and calculate its magnetic moment.

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

- Co¬≥‚Āļ (Z=27, loses 3e‚ĀĽ): configuration [Ar] 3d‚Ā∂
- F‚ĀĽ is a **weak field ligand** ‚Üí small őĒo ‚Üí **high spin**

High spin d‚Ā∂ arrangement:
```
t2g: ‚ÜĎ‚Üď  ‚ÜĎ  ‚ÜĎ     eg: ‚ÜĎ  ‚ÜĎ
     (4e in t2g)       (2e in eg)
```
Unpaired electrons = 4

$$\mu = \sqrt{4(4+2)} = \sqrt{24} = 4.90 \text{ BM}$$

‚Üí **Paramagnetic** (4 unpaired electrons)

Contrast: [Co(CN)‚āÜ]¬≥‚ĀĽ with CN‚ĀĽ (strong field) ‚Üí low spin ‚Üí 0 unpaired ‚Üí diamagnetic

</details>

### PQ4. Draw the cis and trans isomers of [Pt(en)Cl‚āā]. Can it show optical isomerism?

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

en (ethylenediamine) is **bidentate** ‚ÄĒ occupies 2 adjacent coordination sites.

[Pt(en)Cl‚āā] has: 1 bidentate en + 2 Cl‚ĀĽ ‚Üí CN = 4, square planar

Because en must occupy *cis* positions (adjacent), [Pt(en)Cl‚āā] can only exist in the **cis** form. The trans arrangement with a bidentate ligand is geometrically impossible.

Optical isomerism: Square planar complexes generally **do not show optical isomerism** (they have a plane of symmetry). So **no optical isomers** for this complex.

</details>

### PQ5. Why do transition metal complexes show colour?

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

Transition metal complexes show colour due to **d-d electronic transitions** (crystal field theory):

1. When light falls on the complex, electrons in lower t‚āāg orbitals absorb photons of specific wavelength/energy = őĒo
2. Electrons jump from t‚āāg ‚Üí eg (d-d transition)
3. The **complementary colour** (not absorbed) is transmitted/reflected ‚Üí we see that colour

**Example:**
- [Ti(H‚āāO)‚āÜ]¬≥‚Āļ absorbs green/yellow light (őĽ ‚Čą 500 nm) ‚Üí appears **violet**
- [Cu(H‚āāO)‚āĄ]¬≤‚Āļ absorbs red light ‚Üí appears **blue**

The colour depends on:
- The metal ion (d configuration)
- The ligand field strength (stronger field ‚Üí larger őĒo ‚Üí absorbs at shorter wavelength ‚Üí different colour)

</details>
