## 1. ATOMIC STRUCTURE & QUANTUM NUMBERS

### Q: What are quantum numbers? Define them and show their relationship when n = 3 or n = 4.

**Quantum numbers** are a set of four numbers that completely describe the state and position of an electron in an atom.

| Quantum Number | Symbol | Describes | Allowed Values |
|---|---|---|---|
| Principal | n | Energy level / shell | 1, 2, 3, … |
| Azimuthal (Angular Momentum) | l | Subshell / orbital shape | 0 to (n−1) |
| Magnetic | mₗ | Orbital orientation | −l to +l |
| Spin | mₛ | Electron spin direction | +½ or −½ |

**Relationship when n = 3:**

| n | l | Subshell | mₗ | Number of orbitals |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 3 | 0 | 3s | 0 | 1 |
| 3 | 1 | 3p | −1, 0, +1 | 3 |
| 3 | 2 | 3d | −2, −1, 0, +1, +2 | 5 |

Total orbitals = n² = 9; Total electrons = 2n² = 18

**Relationship when n = 4:**

| n | l | Subshell | mₗ | No. of orbitals |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 4 | 0 | 4s | 0 | 1 |
| 4 | 1 | 4p | −1, 0, +1 | 3 |
| 4 | 2 | 4d | −2, −1, 0, +1, +2 | 5 |
| 4 | 3 | 4f | −3,−2,−1,0,+1,+2,+3 | 7 |

Total orbitals = 16; Total electrons = 32

### Q: State and explain Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle.

**Statement:** It is impossible to simultaneously determine, with perfect accuracy, both the position and the momentum (or velocity) of a microscopic particle.

**Mathematical Expression:**

$$\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \frac{h}{4\pi}$$

Where:
- Δx = uncertainty in position
- Δp = uncertainty in momentum
- h = Planck's constant (6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s)

**Explanation:**
- This is not a limitation of instruments but a fundamental property of matter.
- If Δx is made very small (precise position), Δp becomes very large (uncertain momentum), and vice versa.
- This principle makes it impossible to assign a fixed path to an electron, leading to the concept of **orbitals** (regions of probability) instead of orbits.

### Q: Define orbit and orbital. Compare them.

| Feature | Orbit (Bohr's concept) | Orbital (Quantum concept) |
|---|---|---|
| Definition | A fixed circular path around the nucleus | A 3D region in space where the probability of finding an electron is maximum (≥90%) |
| Nature | Well-defined, definite path | Probabilistic, diffuse region |
| Shape | Circular/elliptical | Depends on l: spherical (s), dumbbell (p), complex (d, f) |
| Basis | Classical mechanics | Quantum mechanics |
| Max electrons | 2n² | 2 per orbital |

### Q: Discuss any two principles governing the distribution of electrons in an atom.

**1. Aufbau Principle:**
Electrons fill atomic orbitals in order of increasing energy. The order is:
1s < 2s < 2p < 3s < 3p < 4s < 3d < 4p < 5s < 4d < 5p …

**2. Pauli Exclusion Principle:**
No two electrons in the same atom can have the same set of four quantum numbers. As a consequence, each orbital can hold a maximum of **two electrons**, and they must have opposite spins (+½ and −½).

**3. Hund's Rule of Maximum Multiplicity:**
When electrons fill orbitals of equal energy (degenerate orbitals), each orbital is first occupied by one electron with the same spin before any orbital gets a second electron.

### Q: Draw the shapes and orientation of s, p, and d orbitals.

**s-orbital:** Spherically symmetric about the nucleus. No directional preference.
```
      ___
    /     \
   |   •   |   (sphere, nucleus at centre)
    \___/
```

**p-orbitals:** Dumbbell-shaped. Three orbitals — pₓ, p_y, p_z — oriented along x, y, z axes respectively.

```
   pz:        py:        px:
   |           /          —•—
  —•—         •
   |          /
```
Each looks like two lobes on opposite sides of the nucleus.

**d-orbitals:** Five d-orbitals. Four have a clover/four-leaf shape (dxy, dxz, dyz, dx²−y²); one (dz²) has a dumbbell with a torus (donut ring) in the middle.

**General Electronic Configuration:**

### Q: How does electron affinity change in a period and a group?

**Across a Period (left → right):**
- Electron affinity generally **increases** (becomes more negative).
- Nuclear charge increases, atomic size decreases → greater attraction for incoming electron.
- Exception: Group IIA (filled s) and Group VA (half-filled p) have lower electron affinities than expected.

**Down a Group (top → bottom):**
- Electron affinity generally **decreases**.
- Atomic size increases, nuclear attraction on incoming electron decreases, shielding increases.
- Exception: Period 2 elements (F, O) have lower electron affinities than Period 3 (Cl, S) due to small size causing electron-electron repulsion.

---

### Q: How does ionization potential vary with atomic radii and across the periodic table?

**Ionization Potential (IP):** The energy required to remove the outermost electron from a gaseous atom in its ground state.

**With Atomic Radii:** IP and atomic radius are **inversely related**. Larger the radius, weaker the nuclear attraction on the outer electron → lower IP.

**Across a Period:** IP increases left to right (decreasing radius, increasing nuclear charge).
- Exception: N > O (N has stable half-filled 2p³ configuration; O loses an electron from a paired orbital which is easier).

**Down a Group:** IP decreases (increasing radius and shielding).

**Why N has higher IP than O:**
Nitrogen has the configuration 2p³ (half-filled), which is extra stable. Oxygen has 2p⁴; one orbital is doubly occupied, creating electron-electron repulsion. So O loses an electron more easily → lower IP than N.

### Q: Classify radioactive elements; state modern periodic law; discuss Zeeman effect and Pauli Exclusion Principle.

**Modern Periodic Law:** The physical and chemical properties of elements are periodic functions of their **atomic numbers** (not atomic masses as in Mendeleev's law).

**Classification of elements (electronic configuration basis):**
- **s-block:** Groups IA & IIA — outer electrons in s-subshell (e.g., Na, Ca)
- **p-block:** Groups IIIA–VIIA & Zero — outer electrons in p-subshell (e.g., C, N, Cl)
- **d-block (Transition metals):** Groups IB–VIII — (n−1)d partially filled (e.g., Fe, Cu)
- **f-block (Inner transition):** Lanthanides & Actinides — (n−2)f partially filled

**Zeeman Effect:** The splitting of spectral lines of an element when it is placed in a strong external magnetic field. This is due to the different spatial orientations of orbitals (described by the magnetic quantum number mₗ).

**Pauli Exclusion Principle:** No two electrons in an atom can have identical sets of all four quantum numbers. This limits each orbital to 2 electrons with opposite spins.

## 2. CHEMICAL BONDING (IONIC, COVALENT, VBT, MOT)

---

### Q: What do you mean by overlapping of orbitals?

**Orbital Overlapping:** The process by which atomic orbitals of two atoms come together (overlap) to share electron density between them, forming a covalent bond.

**Types of overlap:**
1. **s-s overlap:** Between two s-orbitals (e.g., H₂)
2. **s-p overlap:** Between an s and a p orbital (e.g., HCl)
3. **p-p overlap:** Between two p-orbitals — can be axial (σ bond) or lateral (π bond)

**Greater the overlap → stronger the bond**

```
Energy
  |
  |    ψ*  (antibonding MO — higher energy)
  |   /  \
  |  /    \
  | AO    AO   ← atomic orbitals (same energy)
  |  \    /
  |   \  /
  |    ψ   (bonding MO — lower energy, electrons go here)
  |
  └─────────────────────────────
```

- Two atomic s-orbitals combine to form **two molecular orbitals (MOs)**:
  - **Bonding MO (σ1s):** Lower energy — stabilizes the molecule
  - **Antibonding MO (σ*1s):** Higher energy — destabilizes

### Q: Show SP³ hybridization in CH₄ with geometrical shape.

**SP³ Hybridization in Methane (CH₄):**

**Carbon's ground state:** 1s² 2s² 2p²  
**Carbon's excited state:** 1s² 2s¹ 2p³  
**Hybridized state:** Four equivalent sp³ hybrid orbitals

- One 2s orbital + three 2p orbitals → **four sp³ hybrid orbitals**
- Each sp³ orbital overlaps with a 1s orbital of hydrogen to form 4 σ bonds
- **Geometry:** Tetrahedral
- **Bond angle:** 109.5°
- **Shape:**

```
           H
           |
    H ——— C ——— H
           |
           H
   (tetrahedral, 109.5° angles)
```

All four C–H bonds are equivalent, with equal bond lengths (109 pm) and bond energies.

### Q: What do you mean by 'hybrid bond' and 'bond order'? Why is Mg₂ impossible but H₂ is possible?

**Hybrid Bond:** A covalent bond formed by the overlap of a hybrid orbital (sp, sp², sp³ etc.) of one atom with an orbital of another atom.

**Bond Order:** The number of covalent bonds between two atoms.
$$\text{Bond Order} = \frac{\text{Number of bonding electrons} - \text{Number of antibonding electrons}}{2}$$

**H₂ molecule (bond order = 1):**
- Configuration: (σ1s)² 
- Bond order = (2 − 0)/2 = **1** → H₂ is stable ✓

**Mg₂ molecule (bond order = 0):**
- Mg has configuration: (σ1s)²(σ*1s)²(σ2s)²(σ*2s)²(σ2p)²(σ*2p)²
- All bonding MOs are cancelled by antibonding MOs
- Bond order = (8 − 8)/2 = **0** → Mg₂ does not exist ✗

### Q: Define bonding, antibonding and non-bonding electrons with examples.

**Bonding Electrons:**
- Electrons occupying **bonding molecular orbitals** (lower energy than constituent AOs).
- They stabilize the molecule by increasing electron density between nuclei.
- Example: In H₂, the 2 electrons in σ1s are bonding electrons.

**Antibonding Electrons:**
- Electrons occupying **antibonding molecular orbitals** (higher energy, marked with *).
- They destabilize the molecule by creating a node between nuclei.
- Example: In He₂, electrons in σ*1s are antibonding.

**Non-bonding Electrons:**
- Electrons that **do not participate** in bonding (lone pairs or electrons in non-bonding MOs).
- They neither stabilize nor destabilize the bond.
- Example: In HF, the fluorine lone pair electrons in 2p orbitals perpendicular to the bond axis.

### Q: Explain why the bond order of O₂ is two. Compare VBT and MOT.

**Bond Order of O₂ using MOT:**

O₂ MO configuration:
(σ1s)²(σ*1s)²(σ2s)²(σ*2s)²(σ2p)²(π2p)⁴(π*2p)²

- Bonding electrons = 2+2+2+4 = 10 (only those in bonding MOs: σ2s, σ2p, π2p counted differently)

More simply:
- Bonding electrons (ignoring core 1s): σ2s(2) + σ2p(2) + π2p(4) = 8
- Antibonding electrons: σ*2s(2) + π*2p(2) = 4
- Bond order = (8−4)/2 = **2** ✓

Also, O₂ has **2 unpaired electrons** in π*2p → it is **paramagnetic** (VBT fails to explain this; MOT explains it correctly).

**Comparison of VBT and MOT:**

| Feature | VBT (Valence Bond Theory) | MOT (Molecular Orbital Theory) |
|---|---|---|
| Basis | Atomic orbitals remain localized | Atomic orbitals combine to form delocalized molecular orbitals |
| Bond formation | Overlap of AOs | Linear combination of AOs (LCAO) |
| Magnetic properties | Cannot explain paramagnetism of O₂ | Correctly predicts paramagnetism |
| Bond order | Can only handle integral bond orders | Explains fractional bond orders |
| Electron density | Between bonded atoms only | Spread over entire molecule |
| Resonance | Requires resonance structures | Not needed — electrons are delocalized |
| Limitation | Cannot explain O₂ paramagnetism | More complex mathematically |

### Q: Discuss the salient features of Valence Bond Theory (VBT).

1. A covalent bond is formed by the **overlap of atomic orbitals** containing one electron each.
2. The overlapping orbitals must have **electrons with opposite spins**.
3. **Greater the overlap → stronger and shorter the bond**.
4. A **σ bond** is formed by head-on (axial) overlap; a **π bond** is formed by sideways (lateral) overlap.
5. The directional nature of bonds explains the **geometry** of molecules.
6. **Hybridization** concept is introduced to explain the geometry of polyatomic molecules like CH₄ (sp³), BF₃ (sp²), BeCl₂ (sp).
7. Resonance: When a single VBT structure cannot explain all properties, a resonance hybrid is used.
8. **Limitation:** VBT fails to explain paramagnetic nature of O₂ and fractional bond orders.

---

### Q: Define ionic bond, covalent bond, and co-ordinate covalent bond.

**Ionic Bond:**
Formed by complete transfer of one or more electrons from a metal to a non-metal, creating oppositely charged ions that attract each other electrostatically.
Example: Na⁺Cl⁻ (sodium chloride)

**Covalent Bond:**
Formed by the mutual sharing of electron pairs between two non-metal atoms, each atom contributing one electron to the shared pair.
Example: H–H (hydrogen gas), H–Cl

**Co-ordinate (Dative) Covalent Bond:**
A type of covalent bond where **both electrons of the shared pair are donated by one atom** (the donor/Lewis base) to another atom with an empty orbital (the acceptor/Lewis acid).
Example: NH₄⁺ — after NH₃ forms three covalent bonds with H, the lone pair on N donates to H⁺ to form the fourth bond.

### Q: Discuss intermolecular and intramolecular hydrogen bonds.

**Hydrogen Bond:**
A weak electrostatic attraction between a partially positive H atom (bonded to an electronegative atom: F, O, or N) and a lone pair on an electronegative atom of another or the same molecule.

**Intramolecular Hydrogen Bond:**
- Forms **within** the same molecule.
- Occurs when H and the electronegative atom are positioned close enough within the same molecule.
- Results in **ring-like structures**.
- Example: o-nitrophenol, salicylaldehyde
- Effect: **Lower boiling point** compared to para isomer.

**Intermolecular Hydrogen Bond:**
- Forms **between** different molecules.
- Creates a network or chain of associated molecules.
- Example: Water (H₂O), HF, alcohols, carboxylic acids
- Effect: **Abnormally high** boiling and melting points.

### Q: What are the basic principles/postulates of Molecular Orbital Theory (MOT)?

1. When atomic orbitals of atoms combine, they form **molecular orbitals (MOs)** that belong to the molecule as a whole.
2. The number of MOs formed = number of AOs combined.
3. MOs are formed by **Linear Combination of Atomic Orbitals (LCAO)**:
   - **Bonding MO (ψ):** ψ = ψ_A + ψ_B (constructive interference)
   - **Antibonding MO (ψ*):** ψ* = ψ_A − ψ_B (destructive interference)
4. Electrons in bonding MOs stabilize; electrons in antibonding MOs destabilize.
5. Electrons fill MOs according to the **Aufbau principle, Hund's rule, and Pauli Exclusion Principle**.
6. **Bond order = (Nb − Na)/2** where Nb = bonding electrons, Na = antibonding electrons.
7. A molecule is stable if bond order > 0.

### Q: Calculate the bond order of MgO by MOT diagram.

Mg (Z=12): [Ne] 3s²  
O (Z=8): [He] 2s² 2p⁴

MO configuration of MgO:
(σ1s)²(σ*1s)²(σ2s)²(σ*2s)²(σ2p)²(π2p)⁴(π*2p)²

σ2s² σ*2s² σ2p² π2p⁴

Bond order = (2 + 2 + 4 − 2)/2... 

Using formal calculation:

Bond order = (8 − 2)/2 = **3**

Actually for MgO with 8 valence electrons:
σ2s² σ*2s² σ2p² π2p⁴ → bonding: 8, antibonding: 2 → **Bond order = 3**

### Q: SnF₄ is ionic and SnCl₄ is covalent — explain by Fajans' Rule.

**Fajans' Rules** determine the degree of covalent character in ionic compounds. Covalency increases (ionic character decreases) when:

1. **Small cation** (high charge density — polarizes anion strongly)
2. **Large anion** (easily polarizable, electron cloud distorted)
3. **High charge on either ion**
4. **Cation with 18-electron or pseudo-noble gas configuration** (greater polarizing power)

**Explanation:**

| Compound | Cation | Anion | Polarization | Bond type |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| SnF₄ | Sn⁴⁺ (small) | F⁻ (small, hard to polarize) | Low | Ionic |
| SnCl₄ | Sn⁴⁺ (small) | Cl⁻ (larger, easily polarized) | High | Covalent |

- F⁻ is very small and its electron cloud is tightly held — Sn⁴⁺ cannot significantly distort it → **ionic bond** retained.
- Cl⁻ is larger than F⁻, its electron cloud is more diffuse and easily polarized by the highly charged Sn⁴⁺ → covalent character increases → **SnCl₄ is covalent**.

Similarly:
- **AgF is ionic, AgI is covalent** (I⁻ is much larger than F⁻ → more polarizable)
- **MgCl₂ is more covalent than NaCl** (Mg²⁺ has higher charge and smaller size → greater polarizing power)

---

### Q: Why does AgI is covalent but AgF is ionic? (Fajans' rule)

Both Ag⁺ (18-electron configuration: [Kr] 4d¹⁰) has high polarizing power.

- **F⁻:** Very small, low polarizability → electron cloud not significantly distorted → **AgF remains ionic**
- **I⁻:** Much larger, high polarizability → electron cloud heavily distorted by Ag⁺ → significant covalent character → **AgI is covalent**

Also, Ag⁺ has a pseudo-noble gas (18-electron) configuration, which makes it a better polarizer than Na⁺ or K⁺ (noble gas config).

## 3. FAJANS' RULE & ACIDS/BASES

---

### Q: Why is HClO₄ a stronger acid than H₃PO₄?

The strength of an oxyacid depends on the **oxidation state of the central atom** (or the number of non-bridging oxygen atoms that are not OH groups).

**Rule:** More oxygen atoms attached to the central atom (non-OH oxygens) → more electrons pulled away from O–H bond → easier proton release → stronger acid.

| Acid | Formula | Non-OH oxygens | Acid Strength |
|---|---|---|---|
| HClO₄ | Cl(=O)₃(OH) | 3 | Very strong |
| H₃PO₄ | P(=O)(OH)₃ | 1 | Weak |

HClO₄ has **3 non-bridging oxygens** (high electronegativity pull) → O–H bond greatly weakened → easy proton release → **very strong acid**.

H₃PO₄ has only **1 non-bridging oxygen** → weak pull on O–H → harder to release proton → **weak acid**.

### Q: Write the modern concepts of acid-base theory. State Lewis and Bronsted-Lowry concepts with comparison.

**Arrhenius Theory:**
- Acid: produces H⁺ in water
- Base: produces OH⁻ in water
- Limited to aqueous solutions

**Bronsted-Lowry Theory:**
- **Acid:** A proton (H⁺) **donor**
- **Base:** A proton (H⁺) **acceptor**
- Every acid has a conjugate base; every base has a conjugate acid
- Example: HCl + H₂O → H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻ (HCl = acid, H₂O = base)

**Lewis Theory:**
- **Acid:** An electron pair **acceptor** (has empty orbital)
- **Base:** An electron pair **donor** (has lone pair)
- Broader than Bronsted-Lowry — covers reactions without proton transfer
- Example: BF₃ + NH₃ → F₃B←NH₃ (BF₃ = Lewis acid, NH₃ = Lewis base)

**Comparison:**

| Theory | Acid | Base | Scope |
|---|---|---|---|
| Arrhenius | H⁺ producer | OH⁻ producer | Aqueous only |
| Bronsted-Lowry | H⁺ donor | H⁺ acceptor | Any proton transfer |
| Lewis | e⁻ pair acceptor | e⁻ pair donor | Broadest |

---

### Q: State the concept of acid and base according to Lux-Flood and Usanovich (Leuris/Luris).

**Lux-Flood Concept:**
- **Acid:** An **oxide ion (O²⁻) acceptor**
- **Base:** An **oxide ion (O²⁻) donor**
- Useful in high-temperature, non-aqueous (molten) systems (e.g., metallurgy, ceramics)
- Example: CaO + SiO₂ → CaSiO₃ (CaO = base, SiO₂ = acid)

**Usanovich Theory:**
- Most general acid-base concept.
- **Acid:** Any species that combines with a base, donates cations, or accepts anions/electrons.
- **Base:** Any species that donates anions, accepts cations, or donates electrons.
- Includes redox reactions within the acid-base framework.

---

### Q: Explain amphiprotic species with example. Write the properties of Hard and Soft acids and bases.

**Amphiprotic Species:**
A species that can **act both as an acid (proton donor) and as a base (proton acceptor)** depending on the reaction partner.

Examples:
- **H₂O:** Acts as acid with NH₃ (donates H⁺); acts as base with HCl (accepts H⁺)
- **HCO₃⁻:** Acts as acid → gives H⁺ to form CO₃²⁻; acts as base → accepts H⁺ to form H₂CO₃
- **HS⁻, HPO₄²⁻**

**HSAB Theory (Hard-Soft Acid-Base by Pearson):**

**Hard Acids:**
- Small, high charge density, low polarizability
- Examples: H⁺, Li⁺, Na⁺, Al³⁺, Fe³⁺, BF₃
- Properties: Not easily polarized; high charge/radius ratio

**Soft Acids:**
- Large, low charge density, highly polarizable
- Examples: Cu⁺, Ag⁺, Hg²⁺, Pt²⁺, I₂
- Properties: Easily polarized, low electronegativity

**Hard Bases:**
- Small, high electronegativity, low polarizability, hard to oxidize
- Examples: F⁻, Cl⁻, OH⁻, H₂O, NH₃, ROH
- Properties: Donate lone pairs from O, N, F

**Soft Bases:**
- Large, low electronegativity, highly polarizable
- Examples: I⁻, S²⁻, RS⁻, CO, PR₃, C₂H₄
- Properties: Easily polarized, donate from P, S, or C

**HSAB Rule:** Hard acids prefer hard bases; soft acids prefer soft bases.

### Q: Arrange acids in order of increasing strength: H₂SO₄, HNO₃, HClO₄, HMnO₄.

Using the principle that **more electronegative central atom and more non-OH oxygens → stronger acid:**

| Acid | Non-OH oxygens | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| H₂SO₄ | 2 | Strong |
| HNO₃ | 2 | Strong |
| HMnO₄ | 3 | Very strong |
| HClO₄ | 3 | Strongest |

**Increasing order of strength:**
$$\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 < \text{HNO}_3 < \text{HMnO}_4 < \text{HClO}_4$$

HClO₄ > HMnO₄ > H₂SO₄ ≈ HNO₃ (HClO₄ strongest because Cl is most electronegative among these central atoms and has 3 non-OH oxygens)

### Q: From neutralization theory, prove that S₁V₁ = S₂V₂.

**Neutralization:** An acid-base reaction producing salt and water.

At equivalence point: moles of acid = moles of base

$$\text{Moles} = \text{Strength (Normality, N)} \times \text{Volume (L)}$$

For acid: moles of acid = N₁ × V₁  
For base: moles of base = N₂ × V₂

At equivalence:
$$N_1 V_1 = N_2 V_2$$

Or in terms of Strength (S = Normality):
$$\boxed{S_1 V_1 = S_2 V_2}$$

This is the **law of equivalents**, used in titration calculations.

### Q: Distinguish between accuracy and precision.

| Feature | Accuracy | Precision |
|---|---|---|
| Definition | How close a measurement is to the **true/accepted value** | How close repeated measurements are to **each other** |
| Indicates | Correctness of measurement | Reproducibility / consistency |
| Error | Low systematic error | Low random error |
| Example | Hitting the bullseye | Hitting the same spot repeatedly (even if off-center) |
| Good accuracy? | Possible without precision | Not required for precision |
| Good precision? | Not required | Both accuracy AND precision = ideal |

### Q: Define types of errors in analytical analysis. How to minimize errors?

**Types of Errors:**

1. **Systematic (Determinate) Errors:**
   - Have a definite source; reproducible; can be corrected.
   - Types: instrumental errors, method errors, personal errors
   - Example: An uncalibrated balance always reading 0.2g too high.

2. **Random (Indeterminate) Errors:**
   - Unpredictable, fluctuating; cannot be eliminated, only minimized.
   - Due to: slight variations in reading, temperature fluctuations, vibrations
   - Minimized by taking **multiple measurements and averaging**.

3. **Gross Errors:**
   - Large blunders due to misreading or accidents; data discarded.

**Minimizing Errors:**
- Calibrate instruments regularly
- Perform multiple trials and use statistical analysis
- Use proper technique and clean equipment
- Conduct blank experiments
- Use standard reference materials

## 4. COMPLEX COMPOUNDS / COORDINATION CHEMISTRY

### Q: Define complex compound, ligand, co-ordination number, co-ordination sphere, and chelating agent.

**Complex Compound:**
A compound containing a **central metal atom/ion** bonded to surrounding ions or molecules (ligands) by coordinate bonds. Enclosed in square brackets in formula.
Example: [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻, [Cu(NH₃)₄]²⁺

**Ligand:**
An **ion or molecule** that donates a lone pair of electrons to the central metal atom/ion to form a co-ordinate bond.
- **Monodentate:** Donates one electron pair (e.g., NH₃, H₂O, Cl⁻, CN⁻)
- **Bidentate:** Donates two electron pairs (e.g., en = ethylenediamine)
- **Polydentate:** Donates multiple pairs (e.g., EDTA — hexadentate)

**Co-ordination Number:**
The total number of **ligand donor atoms** directly bonded to the central metal atom/ion.
Example: In [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻, coordination number = 6

**Co-ordination Sphere:**
The **central metal atom/ion along with the directly bonded ligands**, enclosed in square brackets.
Example: In K₄[Fe(CN)₆], the coordination sphere is [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻

**Chelating Agent:**
A **polydentate ligand** that forms two or more bonds with the same central metal ion, creating a ring structure (chelate ring).
Example: EDTA, ethylenediamine (en), oxalate (C₂O₄²⁻)

### Q: Define primary valency and secondary valency (Werner's Theory).

**Werner's Theory** explains the structure of complex compounds using two types of valency:

**Primary Valency (Ionizable valency):**
- The normal valency of the metal, which determines its oxidation state.
- Satisfied by negative ions (counter ions); **ionizable** — appear outside the coordination sphere.
- Example: In [Co(NH₃)₆]Cl₃, Co has primary valency = 3 (satisfied by 3 Cl⁻ ions)

**Secondary Valency (Non-ionizable / Co-ordination valency):**
- The **coordination number** of the metal; number of ligands directly attached.
- Satisfied by neutral molecules or ions in the coordination sphere; **non-ionizable**.
- Example: In [Co(NH₃)₆]Cl₃, Co has secondary valency = 6 (6 NH₃ ligands)
- Directs the spatial arrangement of ligands → determines geometry.

For [Co(NH₃)₆]Cl₃:
- Primary valency: 3 (Co³⁺)
- Secondary valency: 6 (6 NH₃)
- 3 Cl⁻ ions are outside the coordination sphere (ionizable)

### Q: Explain Effective Atomic Number (EAN) theory with example.

**EAN Theory (Sidgwick's Theory):**
The central metal atom in a complex gains electrons from ligands until its total electron count equals that of the nearest noble gas. This electron count is the **Effective Atomic Number (EAN)**.

$$\text{EAN} = \text{Atomic number of metal} - \text{Oxidation state} + \text{2 × Coordination number}$$

**Example 1: [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻**
- Fe²⁺ (26 − 2 = 24 electrons)
- 6 CN⁻ ligands donate 6 × 2 = 12 electrons
- EAN = 24 + 12 = **36** = atomic number of Kr (noble gas) ✓ → Stable

**Example 2: K₄[Fe(CN)₆]**
- Fe²⁺: 24 electrons
- 6 CN⁻ ligands: 12 electrons
- EAN = 24 + 12 = **36 (Kr)** ✓ → Stable

**Example 3: [Co(NH₃)₆]Cl₃**
- Co³⁺: 27 − 3 = 24 electrons
- 6 NH₃ ligands: 12 electrons
- EAN = 24 + 12 = **36 (Kr)** ✓ → Stable

Complexes with EAN equal to noble gas electron count are **especially stable**.

### Q: Discuss the stability of K₄[Fe(CN)₆] in terms of EAN rule.

K₄[Fe(CN)₆] = Potassium hexacyanoferrate(II)

- Metal: Fe²⁺
- Atomic number of Fe = 26
- Electrons in Fe²⁺ = 26 − 2 = 24
- Ligand: CN⁻ (6 ligands, each donates 2 electrons)
- Electrons donated = 6 × 2 = 12
- **EAN = 24 + 12 = 36**

Atomic number of Krypton (Kr) = 36 (nearest noble gas)

Since EAN = 36 = Kr, the complex has the same electron configuration as a noble gas → **maximum stability**. This is why K₄[Fe(CN)₆] is very stable and does not easily dissociate.

### Q: Discuss the stability of K₄[Fe(CN)₆] in terms of Crystal Field Theory (CFT). Why does d-orbital split into two groups?

**Crystal Field Theory:**
CFT treats ligands as **point charges** creating an electrostatic field around the central metal ion. This field causes the **five d-orbitals** (which are degenerate in free metal ion) to **split** into two groups of different energies.

**Why d-orbitals split:**
In an octahedral complex, the six ligands approach along the x, y, z axes. The d orbitals oriented **toward** the ligands (dz², dx²-y²) experience greater repulsion than those oriented **between** the axes (dxy, dxz, dyz).

Result:
- **eg group** (higher energy): dz², dx²-y² — point toward ligands — repelled more
- **t₂g group** (lower energy): dxy, dxz, dyz — point between ligands — repelled less

The energy difference = **Crystal Field Splitting Energy (Δ₀ or 10Dq)**

**K₄[Fe(CN)₆] — CFT Analysis:**
- Fe²⁺: d⁶ configuration
- CN⁻ is a **strong field ligand** (large Δ₀) → causes **low spin** configuration
- All 6 d-electrons pair in t₂g: (t₂g)⁶(eg)⁰
- No unpaired electrons → **diamagnetic**
- Large Δ₀ from CN⁻ → **high CFSE (Crystal Field Stabilization Energy)** → complex is very stable

### Q: Explain the magnetic properties of K₄[Fe(CN)₆] and K₃[Fe(CN)₆].

**K₄[Fe(CN)₆]** — Fe²⁺ (d⁶):
- CN⁻ = strong field ligand → large Δ₀ → pairing energy < Δ₀
- Low spin: (t₂g)⁶(eg)⁰
- **0 unpaired electrons → Diamagnetic** (repelled by magnetic field)

**K₃[Fe(CN)₆]** — Fe³⁺ (d⁵):
- CN⁻ = strong field ligand → large Δ₀ → low spin
- Low spin d⁵: (t₂g)⁵(eg)⁰
- **1 unpaired electron → Paramagnetic** (weakly attracted to magnetic field)

**General Rule:**
- **Paramagnetic:** Has unpaired electrons → attracted to magnetic field
- **Diamagnetic:** All electrons paired → repelled by magnetic field

### Q: What are eₓ and t₂g? Explain with example.

In Crystal Field Theory for **octahedral complexes**:

**t₂g (triply degenerate lower set):**
- Contains: dxy, dxz, dyz orbitals
- These orbitals point **between** the Cartesian axes
- They are **farther from the ligands** → less repulsion → **lower energy**
- Stabilized by −0.4Δ₀ (or −4Dq) per electron

**eg (doubly degenerate higher set):**
- Contains: dz², dx²-y² orbitals
- These orbitals point **along** the Cartesian axes (directly toward ligands)
- They experience **more repulsion** from ligands → **higher energy**
- Destabilized by +0.6Δ₀ (or +6Dq) per electron

**Example:** In [Fe(H₂O)₆]³⁺ (Fe³⁺, d⁵, weak field):
- High spin: t₂g³ eg²
- 5 unpaired electrons → strongly paramagnetic

In [Fe(CN)₆]³⁻ (Fe³⁺, d⁵, strong field):
- Low spin: t₂g⁵ eg⁰
- 1 unpaired electron → weakly paramagnetic

## 5. COLLIGATIVE PROPERTIES & SOLUTIONS

### Q: What are colligative properties? When are they valid?

**Colligative Properties:**
Properties of dilute solutions that **depend only on the number of solute particles** (concentration) and **not on the nature or identity** of the solute.

The four colligative properties are:
1. **Relative lowering of vapour pressure** (Raoult's Law)
2. **Elevation of boiling point** (ΔT_b)
3. **Depression of freezing point** (ΔT_f)
4. **Osmotic pressure** (π)

**When are they valid?**
- Only for **dilute solutions** (ideal solutions)
- When the solute is **non-volatile** and **non-electrolyte**
- When solute does **not associate or dissociate** in the solvent
- At **low concentrations** where intermolecular interactions between solute-solvent are negligible

---

### Q: State and derive Raoult's Law. Show that vapour pressure of solution is directly proportional to mole fraction of solvent.

**Raoult's Law:** The partial vapour pressure of a solvent above a solution is directly proportional to the mole fraction of the solvent in the solution.

$$P_A = P_A^0 \cdot x_A$$

Where:
- P_A = vapour pressure of solvent above solution
- P_A⁰ = vapour pressure of pure solvent
- x_A = mole fraction of solvent

**Derivation / Proof:**

If n_A = moles of solvent, n_B = moles of solute:

$$x_A = \frac{n_A}{n_A + n_B}$$

Relative lowering of vapour pressure:

$$\frac{P_A^0 - P_A}{P_A^0} = x_B = 1 - x_A$$

Therefore:

$$P_A^0 - P_A = P_A^0 \cdot x_B$$

$$P_A = P_A^0 (1 - x_B) = P_A^0 \cdot x_A$$

**Proof that P_A ∝ x_A:**

Since P_A = P_A⁰ · x_A, and P_A⁰ is a constant at a given temperature:

$$\boxed{P_A \propto x_A}$$ ✓

---

### Q: Explain Vant Hoff's Law of osmotic pressure. Derive πV = nRT.

**Osmotic Pressure (π):** The minimum pressure that must be applied to a solution to prevent the passage of solvent through a semipermeable membrane from pure solvent into the solution.

**Vant Hoff's Law:**
1. At constant temperature, osmotic pressure is directly proportional to molar concentration (C):  π ∝ C
2. At constant concentration, osmotic pressure is directly proportional to absolute temperature (T): π ∝ T
3. Combining: π = CRT

**Derivation of πV = nRT:**

Since C = n/V (molar concentration):

$$\pi = CRT = \frac{n}{V} \cdot RT$$

$$\boxed{\pi V = nRT}$$

This is analogous to the ideal gas law (PV = nRT), hence dilute solutions behave like ideal gases.

Where:
- π = osmotic pressure (atm)
- V = volume of solution (litres)
- n = moles of solute
- R = 0.0821 L·atm·mol⁻¹·K⁻¹
- T = absolute temperature (K)

---

### Q: Relation between elevation of boiling point (ΔT_b) and molal elevation constant (K_b). Prove ΔT_b ∝ m.

**Boiling Point Elevation:**
A solution boils at a **higher temperature** than the pure solvent because dissolved solute reduces the vapour pressure. The elevation is:

$$\Delta T_b = T_b(\text{solution}) - T_b(\text{pure solvent})$$

**Derivation:**

From Raoult's Law, the relative lowering of vapour pressure leads to:

$$\Delta T_b = K_b \cdot m$$

Where:
- K_b = molal elevation constant (ebullioscopic constant) — property of solvent only
- m = molality of solution = moles of solute per kg of solvent

**Proof that ΔT_b ∝ m:**

$$\Delta T_b = K_b \cdot m$$

At constant K_b (fixed solvent): ΔT_b ∝ m ✓

**K_b for water = 0.512°C·kg/mol**

$$K_b = \frac{RT_b^2 M_A}{1000 \times \Delta H_{vap}}$$

Where M_A = molar mass of solvent, T_b = normal boiling point, ΔH_vap = enthalpy of vaporization.

---

### Q: Calculate the molecular weight of a substance. 0.440g dissolved in 22.2g of C₆H₆ lowered the freezing point by 0.567°C (K_f = 5.12°C/mole).

**Given:**
- Mass of solute (w₂) = 0.440 g
- Mass of solvent (w₁) = 22.2 g benzene
- ΔT_f = 0.567°C
- K_f (benzene) = 5.12°C·kg/mol

**Formula:**

$$\Delta T_f = K_f \times m = K_f \times \frac{w_2 \times 1000}{M_2 \times w_1}$$

**Solving for M₂:**

$$M_2 = \frac{K_f \times w_2 \times 1000}{\Delta T_f \times w_1}$$

$$M_2 = \frac{5.12 \times 0.440 \times 1000}{0.567 \times 22.2}$$

$$M_2 = \frac{2252.8}{12.59} = \boxed{178.9 \approx 179 \text{ g/mol}}$$

### Q: Calculate the experimental molal elevation constant of water from 0.450g of urea in 22.5g of water giving boiling point elevation of 0.170°C.

**Given:**
- w₂ (urea) = 0.450 g
- w₁ (water) = 22.5 g
- ΔT_b = 0.170°C
- M₂ (urea) = 60 g/mol

**Molality:**

$$m = \frac{w_2 \times 1000}{M_2 \times w_1} = \frac{0.450 \times 1000}{60 \times 22.5} = \frac{450}{1350} = 0.333 \text{ mol/kg}$$

**K_b:**

$$K_b = \frac{\Delta T_b}{m} = \frac{0.170}{0.333} = \boxed{0.511 \approx 0.512 \text{ °C·kg/mol}}$$

This matches the standard value for water (K_b = 0.512°C·kg/mol). ✓

### Q: Calculate osmotic pressure of a 3% glucose solution at 25°C. / 2% glucose at 27°C.

**3% glucose at 25°C:**
- 3% w/v means 3g glucose in 100 mL solution = 30g per litre
- Molar mass of glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) = 180 g/mol
- C = 30/180 = 0.167 mol/L
- T = 25 + 273 = 298 K

$$\pi = CRT = 0.167 \times 0.0821 \times 298 = \boxed{4.08 \text{ atm}}$$

**2% glucose at 27°C:**
- C = 20g/L ÷ 180 = 0.111 mol/L
- T = 300 K

$$\pi = 0.111 \times 0.0821 \times 300 = \boxed{2.73 \text{ atm}}$$

### Q: Prove that osmotic pressure is directly proportional to the lowering of vapour pressure.

From Raoult's Law:
$$\frac{P^0 - P}{P^0} = x_B$$

For dilute solutions: $x_B \approx \frac{n_B}{n_A} = \frac{C \cdot M_A}{\rho \times 1000}$

Osmotic pressure: $\pi = CRT$

So: $C = \frac{\pi}{RT}$

Lowering of vapour pressure: $\Delta P = P^0 - P = P^0 \cdot x_B = P^0 \cdot \frac{C \cdot M_A}{\rho \times 1000}$

Since C is proportional to π:
$$\Delta P \propto \pi$$

Therefore, osmotic pressure is directly proportional to lowering of vapour pressure. ✓

### Q: Define isotonic solution and osmotic pressure. What is ideal and real solution?

**Isotonic Solution:**
Two solutions having **equal osmotic pressure** at the same temperature. No net osmotic flow occurs when separated by a semipermeable membrane.
Example: Normal saline (0.9% NaCl) is isotonic with blood plasma.

**Osmotic Pressure:** (See above)

**Ideal Solution:**
- Obeys Raoult's Law at all concentrations and temperatures.
- No volume change or heat change on mixing.
- Intermolecular forces between all components are equal.
- Example: Benzene-toluene, ethanol-water (approximately)

**Real Solution:**
- Deviates from Raoult's Law.
- **Positive deviation:** A-B interactions weaker than A-A and B-B (e.g., acetone-ethanol). Vapour pressure higher than ideal.
- **Negative deviation:** A-B interactions stronger than A-A and B-B (e.g., chloroform-acetone). Vapour pressure lower than ideal.

### Q: Why is the boiling point of a solution greater than that of pure solvent? Why does freezing point decrease?

**Higher Boiling Point:**
Dissolving a non-volatile solute **reduces the vapour pressure** of the solvent. For a liquid to boil, its vapour pressure must equal atmospheric pressure. The solution needs to be heated to a **higher temperature** to achieve this → elevated boiling point.

**Lower Freezing Point:**
At the freezing point, the vapour pressure of the liquid phase equals that of the solid phase. Since the solution has a **lower vapour pressure** than pure solvent, it must be cooled further for the vapour pressure to match that of ice → the freezing point is **depressed**.

## 6. CHEMICAL EQUILIBRIUM & THERMODYNAMICS

### Q: State the law of mass action. Derive the relationship between Kp, Kc, and Kx.

**Law of Mass Action:**
At constant temperature, the rate of a chemical reaction is directly proportional to the product of the molar concentrations of the reactants, each raised to the power of its stoichiometric coefficient.

For: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD

$$K_c = \frac{[C]^c [D]^d}{[A]^a [B]^b}$$

**Mathematical Formulation of Kp:**
$$K_p = \frac{(P_C)^c (P_D)^d}{(P_A)^a (P_B)^b}$$

**Derivation of K_p = K_c (RT)^Δn:**

Using ideal gas law: P = CRT, where C = [concentration] in mol/L

$$K_p = K_c \cdot (RT)^{\Delta n}$$

Where Δn = (c + d) − (a + b) = change in moles of gas

**Kx (in terms of mole fractions):**

$$K_x = K_p \cdot P^{-\Delta n} = K_c \cdot (RT)^{\Delta n} \cdot P^{-\Delta n}$$

**Summary:**

$$\boxed{K_p = K_c (RT)^{\Delta n}}$$

$$K_p = K_x \cdot P^{\Delta n}$$

When Δn = 0: K_p = K_c = K_x

---

### Q: Calculate K_p and K_c for the reaction N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃.

**Given reaction:** N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g)

**Δn = moles of products − moles of reactants = 2 − (1+3) = −2**

$$K_p = K_c (RT)^{\Delta n} = K_c (RT)^{-2}$$

**At 400°C (673 K), K_c = 5.8 × 10⁻² (from 2016 paper):**

$$K_p = 5.8 \times 10^{-2} \times (0.0821 \times 673)^{-2}$$
$$K_p = 5.8 \times 10^{-2} \times (55.23)^{-2}$$
$$K_p = 5.8 \times 10^{-2} \times 3.28 \times 10^{-4}$$
$$\boxed{K_p = 1.90 \times 10^{-5}}$$

**At 500°C (773 K), K_c = 6.0 × 10⁻² (from 2018 paper), R = 0.0821:**

$$K_p = 6.0 \times 10^{-2} \times (0.0821 \times 773)^{-2}$$
$$= 6.0 \times 10^{-2} \times (63.46)^{-2}$$
$$= 6.0 \times 10^{-2} \times 2.48 \times 10^{-4}$$
$$\boxed{K_p = 1.49 \times 10^{-5}}$$

### Q: Explain Le-Chatelier's Principle with examples.

**Le-Chatelier's Principle:**
When a system at equilibrium is subjected to an external stress (change in concentration, temperature, or pressure), the system will shift in the direction that tends to **counteract the stress** and restore equilibrium.

**Effect of Concentration Change:**
- Increase [reactant] → equilibrium shifts **forward**
- Increase [product] → equilibrium shifts **backward**
- Example: N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃ — adding N₂ shifts equilibrium right → more NH₃

**Effect of Pressure Change (for gaseous reactions):**
- Increase pressure → equilibrium shifts toward **fewer moles of gas**
- Example: N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃ (Δn = −2) — increasing pressure favors NH₃ production

**Effect of Temperature Change:**
- Increase temperature → equilibrium shifts in **endothermic direction**
- Example: N₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2NO − 43,200 cal (endothermic) — increasing T shifts right, more NO formed

**Effect of Catalyst:** Does not shift equilibrium; only speeds up reaching equilibrium.

---

### Q: Why is chemical equilibrium called a dynamic equilibrium?

At equilibrium, the **forward and reverse reactions continue to occur at equal rates** — the reaction does not stop. The concentrations of reactants and products remain constant because the rate of formation equals the rate of consumption of each species.

Evidence:
- Radioactive tracers show continued reaction even at equilibrium
- The rates are equal but non-zero

This is unlike **static equilibrium** (complete cessation of reaction), hence it is called **dynamic**.

## 7. CHEMICAL KINETICS

---

### Q: Define order and molecularity of a chemical reaction.

**Order of Reaction:**
The sum of the powers to which the concentration terms are raised in the **experimental rate law**.

$$\text{Rate} = k[A]^m [B]^n$$

Order = m + n (determined experimentally)

- **Zero order:** Rate is independent of concentration. Rate = k
- **First order:** Rate ∝ [A]. Rate = k[A]
- **Second order:** Rate ∝ [A]². Rate = k[A]²

**Molecularity:**
The **number of molecules** that actually collide and participate in the rate-determining elementary step.
- Unimolecular: 1 molecule (e.g., radioactive decay)
- Bimolecular: 2 molecules (most common)
- Termolecular: 3 molecules (rare)

| Feature | Order | Molecularity |
|---|---|---|
| Determined by | Experiment | Reaction mechanism (theory) |
| Can be | Zero, fractional, or integer | Only 1, 2, or 3 |
| Applies to | Overall or single-step | Elementary steps only |

---

### Q: What is pseudo-order reaction?

A **pseudo-order reaction** is one where the reaction appears to be of a lower order than its true molecularity because one or more reactants are present in **large excess** (their concentration remains effectively constant).

**Example:**
The hydrolysis of ethyl acetate (CH₃COOC₂H₅) in the presence of excess water:

$$\text{CH}_3\text{COOC}_2\text{H}_5 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{CH}_3\text{COOH} + \text{C}_2\text{H}_5\text{OH}$$

True order = 2 (one order each for ester and water)  
But since water is in huge excess, [H₂O] ≈ constant:

$$\text{Rate} = k[\text{ester}][\text{H}_2\text{O}] \approx k'[\text{ester}]$$

This behaves as a **first-order reaction** → called **pseudo-first-order** reaction.

---

### Q: Derive the mathematical expression for 1st order reaction. Why does 1st order reaction not complete?

**First Order Reaction:**

$$A \rightarrow \text{Products}$$

$$-\frac{d[A]}{dt} = k[A]$$

**Integrating:**

$$-\int_{[A]_0}^{[A]_t} \frac{d[A]}{[A]} = k \int_0^t dt$$

$$\ln \frac{[A]_0}{[A]_t} = kt$$

$$\boxed{k = \frac{2.303}{t} \log \frac{a}{a-x}}$$

Where: a = initial concentration, x = amount reacted, (a−x) = amount remaining

**Half-life:** 
$$t_{1/2} = \frac{0.693}{k}$$

**Why 1st order reaction never completes:**
The half-life (t₁/₂ = 0.693/k) is **constant** and independent of concentration. In each successive half-life, the concentration is halved — it approaches zero **asymptotically** but never actually reaches zero. Theoretically, it would take infinite time for all reactant to be consumed.

---

### Q: Show for a 2nd order reaction: K = x / [at(a−x)].

**Second Order Reaction (A → Products):**

$$-\frac{d[A]}{dt} = k[A]^2$$

Let initial concentration = a, concentration reacted = x at time t:  
[A] = a − x, and dx/dt = k(a−x)²

$$\int_0^x \frac{dx}{(a-x)^2} = k \int_0^t dt$$

$$\left[\frac{1}{a-x}\right]_0^x = kt$$

$$\frac{1}{a-x} - \frac{1}{a} = kt$$

$$\frac{a - (a-x)}{a(a-x)} = kt$$

$$\frac{x}{a(a-x)} = kt$$

$$\boxed{K = \frac{x}{at(a-x)}}$$ ✓

---

### Q: When does a 2nd order reaction become 1st order? Prove from 2nd order equation.

When one reactant (say B) is in **large excess**, its concentration remains essentially constant throughout the reaction:

For: A + B → Products

$$\text{Rate} = k[A][B]$$

If [B] >> [A], then [B] ≈ constant = b₀

$$\text{Rate} = k \cdot b_0 \cdot [A] = k'[A]$$

where k' = k·b₀ = pseudo-first-order rate constant.

**Proof from 2nd order equation:**

2nd order: $K = \frac{x}{at(a-x)}$

If initial concentration of B (= b) >> a, and b ≈ (b−x) throughout:

$$K_{2nd} = \frac{1}{bt} \ln\frac{a}{a-x} \rightarrow K_{1st} = \frac{1}{t}\ln\frac{a}{a-x}$$

→ This is exactly the 1st order integrated rate equation.

---

### Q: Explain the collision theory on the rate of reaction. Calculate velocity constant K.

**Collision Theory:**

For a reaction to occur, reactant molecules must:
1. **Collide** with each other (necessary but not sufficient condition)
2. Have **energy ≥ activation energy** (threshold energy Ea)
3. Have **proper orientation** at the time of collision

**Rate = Collision frequency × Fraction with E ≥ Ea × Steric factor**

$$k = A \cdot e^{-E_a/RT}$$

This is the **Arrhenius equation**, where:
- A = frequency factor (pre-exponential factor)
- Ea = activation energy
- R = 8.314 J/mol·K
- T = absolute temperature

**Only a small fraction** of collisions are effective — those with proper orientation AND sufficient energy.

**Calculate K for A + B → Product:**
$$k = A \cdot e^{-E_a/RT}$$

If two temperatures T₁ and T₂ are given:
$$\log \frac{k_2}{k_1} = \frac{E_a}{2.303R} \left(\frac{1}{T_1} - \frac{1}{T_2}\right)$$

---

### Q: What is activation energy? Calculate Ea for decomposition of N₂O₅ (k at 25°C = 3.46 × 10⁻⁵, at 65°C = 4.87 × 10⁻³).

**Activation Energy (Ea):** The minimum energy that colliding molecules must possess for the collision to result in a chemical reaction.

**Calculation:**

$$\log \frac{k_2}{k_1} = \frac{E_a}{2.303R} \left(\frac{T_2 - T_1}{T_1 T_2}\right)$$

Given:
- k₁ = 3.46 × 10⁻⁵ at T₁ = 25°C = 298 K
- k₂ = 4.87 × 10⁻³ at T₂ = 65°C = 338 K
- R = 8.314 J/mol·K

$$\log \frac{4.87 \times 10^{-3}}{3.46 \times 10^{-5}} = \frac{E_a}{2.303 \times 8.314} \times \frac{40}{298 \times 338}$$

$$\log(140.75) = \frac{E_a}{19.147} \times \frac{40}{100724}$$

$$2.1485 = \frac{E_a \times 3.97 \times 10^{-4}}{19.147}$$

$$E_a = \frac{2.1485 \times 19.147}{3.97 \times 10^{-4}} = \frac{41.14}{3.97 \times 10^{-4}}$$

$$\boxed{E_a \approx 103,600 \text{ J/mol} = 103.6 \text{ kJ/mol}}$$

---

### Q: Define the 'Gold Number'. Define velocity constant.

**Gold Number (Zsigmondy's Gold Number):**
The mass (in milligrams) of a **protective colloid** that just prevents the coagulation (color change from red to blue) of **10 mL of standard red gold sol** by the addition of 1 mL of 10% NaCl solution.

- **Lower gold number → better protective colloid**
- Examples: Gelatin (gold number = 0.005–0.01), Starch (gold number = 25)
- Used to compare the protective ability of different colloids.

**Velocity Constant (Rate Constant, k):**
The rate of reaction when the concentration of each reactant is **unity** (1 mol/L). It is the proportionality constant in the rate law:

$$\text{Rate} = k[A]^m[B]^n$$

When [A] = [B] = 1 mol/L, Rate = k

**Units of k:**
- Zero order: mol·L⁻¹·s⁻¹
- First order: s⁻¹
- Second order: L·mol⁻¹·s⁻¹

## 8. PHOTOCHEMISTRY

---

### Q: State the laws of photochemistry.

**First Law (Grotthus-Draper Law):**
Only the radiation that is **absorbed** by a substance can bring about a photochemical reaction. Radiation that passes through or is reflected has no photochemical effect.

**Second Law (Stark-Einstein Law / Law of Photochemical Equivalence):**
Each molecule (or atom) that participates in a photochemical reaction absorbs **one quantum (photon)** of radiation. Each photon activates one molecule.

$$E = h\nu = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$

For one mole of molecules (one einstein):
$$E = N_A \cdot h\nu$$

---

### Q: What is quantum efficiency/yield? Write down causes of low and high quantum yield.

**Quantum Yield (Φ):**
The number of molecules that react (or the number of molecules of product formed) per photon absorbed.

$$\Phi = \frac{\text{Number of molecules reacted (or formed)}}{\text{Number of photons absorbed}}$$

**High Quantum Yield (Φ >> 1):** One photon triggers a chain reaction → many molecules react per photon.
- Example: H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl (Φ = 10⁵ to 10⁶ due to chain mechanism)

**Low Quantum Yield (Φ << 1):** Many photons absorbed but few molecules react.
- Reasons for low quantum yield:
  1. **Deactivation** by collision (excited molecule loses energy before reaction)
  2. **Fluorescence/phosphorescence** (energy re-emitted as light, not used for reaction)
  3. **Quenching** by solvent or other molecules
  4. **Reverse reaction** (photoproducts recombine)
  5. **Non-reactive excited states**

---

### Q: Calculate the energy of a photon corresponding to wavelength 3600 Å (or 1000 Å).

**Energy of a single photon:**

$$E = h\nu = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$

**For λ = 3600 Å = 3600 × 10⁻¹⁰ m:**

$$E = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8}{3600 \times 10^{-10}}$$

$$E = \frac{1.988 \times 10^{-25}}{3.6 \times 10^{-7}} = 5.52 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$$

**Energy per mole (1 einstein) = E × N_A:**

$$E_{mole} = 5.52 \times 10^{-19} \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} = 3.32 \times 10^5 \text{ J/mol} = 332 \text{ kJ/mol}$$

**For λ = 1000 Å = 10⁻⁷ m:**

$$E = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8}{10^{-7}} = 1.988 \times 10^{-18} \text{ J/photon}$$

$$E_{mole} = 1.988 \times 10^{-18} \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} = 1.197 \times 10^6 \text{ J/mol} = 286 \text{ kcal/mol}$$

---

### Q: Distinguish between photochemical and thermal reactions.

| Feature | Photochemical Reaction | Thermal Reaction |
|---|---|---|
| Energy source | Light (UV/visible photons) | Heat |
| Initiation | Absorption of photon | Thermal activation (collisions) |
| Activation energy | Provided by photon energy (hν) | Provided by kinetic energy (k_BT) |
| Temperature dependence | Nearly independent of temperature | Strongly depends on temperature (Arrhenius) |
| Example | H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl (in light) | N₂ + O₂ → 2NO (at high T) |
| Quantum yield | Defined for photochemical | Not applicable |
| Control | Controlled by light intensity & wavelength | Controlled by temperature |

---

### Q: Write notes on fluorescence and phosphorescence. What is chemiluminescence?

**Fluorescence:**
- A molecule absorbs a photon and is excited to a higher electronic state.
- It immediately (within 10⁻⁸ s) emits a photon of **longer wavelength** (lower energy) as it returns to the ground state.
- **Ceases as soon as light source is removed.**
- Example: Quinine solution in sunlight, chlorophyll, fluorescent dyes

**Phosphorescence:**
- Similar to fluorescence but involves a **metastable triplet state** (spin-forbidden transition).
- Emission is **delayed** (milliseconds to hours) after removing the light source.
- The excited molecule gets "trapped" in a triplet state before returning to ground state.
- Example: Glow-in-the-dark materials, luminous paint

**Chemiluminescence:**
Light emitted as a result of a **chemical reaction** (not due to external light excitation or heat).
- Chemical energy is directly converted to light energy.
- Example: Luminol reaction (forensics), fireflies (bioluminescence), glow sticks
- The reaction produces an electronically excited product that emits light as it relaxes.

## 9. COLLOID CHEMISTRY

---

### Q: Write down the types of colloids. Distinguish between lyophilic and lyophobic colloids.

**Classification of Colloids:**

**Based on interaction between dispersed phase and dispersion medium:**

| Type | Dispersed Phase | Dispersion Medium | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sol | Solid | Liquid | Gold sol, starch in water |
| Gel | Liquid | Solid | Gelatin, butter, cheese |
| Aerosol | Solid or liquid | Gas | Smoke, fog, mist |
| Foam | Gas | Liquid | Soap suds, shaving cream |
| Emulsion | Liquid | Liquid | Milk, mayonnaise |

**Lyophilic vs Lyophobic Colloids:**

| Feature | Lyophilic ("solvent-loving") | Lyophobic ("solvent-hating") |
|---|---|---|
| Affinity | High affinity for dispersion medium | Low affinity |
| Stability | Very stable | Unstable, easily coagulated |
| Preparation | Easy — dissolve in solvent | Difficult — special methods needed |
| Reversibility | Reversible (re-dissolve after drying) | Irreversible |
| Viscosity | High | Low |
| Surface tension | Low | Normal |
| Tyndall effect | Weak | Strong |
| Examples | Gelatin, starch, protein in water | Gold sol, AgCl, Fe(OH)₃ sol |

---

### Q: Write down the preparation of colloids by Bredig's arc method and peptization.

**Bredig's Arc Method (for metal sols):**
1. The metal whose sol is to be prepared is made into **two electrodes**.
2. These electrodes are dipped in the **dispersion medium** (distilled water or ice-cold water).
3. An electric arc is struck between the electrodes.
4. The intense heat vaporizes the metal; the vapors immediately **condense in the cold liquid** to form colloidal particles.
5. A stabilizing agent (like KOH or traces of alkali) is added.
- **Example:** Gold sol, platinum sol, silver sol
- The metal particles formed are 1–100 nm in size.

**Peptization:**
- The process of **converting a freshly prepared precipitate** into a colloidal sol by adding a small amount of a suitable electrolyte (called the **peptizing agent**).
- Mechanism: The peptizing agent provides ions that are preferentially adsorbed on the precipitate → particles acquire charge → repel each other → disperse into colloidal solution.
- **Example:**
  - Fe(OH)₃ precipitate + FeCl₃ → Fe(OH)₃ sol (Fe³⁺ ions adsorbed)
  - AgCl precipitate + HCl or AgNO₃ → AgCl sol

---

### Q: Explain the process of 'Dialysis' for purification of colloids.

**Dialysis:**
A method for **purifying colloidal solutions** from dissolved crystalloids (ions and small molecules) by diffusion through a **semipermeable membrane** (e.g., parchment paper, cellophane, animal gut).

**Process:**
1. The impure colloidal solution is placed inside a **semipermeable membrane bag**.
2. The bag is suspended in **running/fresh water** (or solvent).
3. Small ions and molecules **diffuse outward** through the membrane into the surrounding water.
4. Colloidal particles are **too large to pass** through the membrane and remain inside.
5. The surrounding water is periodically changed until the sol is pure.

**Applications:**
- Purification of blood in **artificial kidney (hemodialysis)**
- Industrial purification of colloids
- Removal of ions from sols (e.g., removing Cl⁻ from As₂S₃ sol)

**Electrodialysis:** Application of electric field across the membrane speeds up the process of dialysis.

---

### Q: What happened when a beam of light is passed through a colloidal solution? (Tyndall Effect)

**Tyndall Effect:**
When a beam of light is passed through a colloidal solution, the **path of the light beam becomes visible** (as a bright cone) when viewed from the side. This is called the **Tyndall cone**.

**Explanation:**
- Colloidal particles (1–1000 nm) are large enough to **scatter light** in all directions.
- True solutions and pure solvents do not show this effect (particles too small).
- The scattered light makes the beam path visible.

**Application:**
- Used to **distinguish** between true solutions and colloidal solutions.
- Used in **ultramicroscope** — colloidal particles appear as bright spots on a dark background.

**When an electrolyte is added:** Coagulation/flocculation — particles aggregate and settle.
**Under electric field:** Electrophoresis — charged colloidal particles migrate toward oppositely charged electrode.

---

### Q: Discuss the method of preparation and purification of colloidal solution.

**Preparation Methods:**

**1. Dispersion Methods (breaking large particles into colloidal size):**
- **Mechanical dispersion:** Colloid mill — solid ground between two discs; liquid colloids passed through small gaps.
- **Bredig's arc method:** Electric arc between metal electrodes in liquid (for metal sols).
- **Peptization:** Freshly precipitated material dispersed by electrolyte addition.
- **Ultrasonic dispersion:** High-frequency sound waves break particles to colloidal size.

**2. Condensation Methods (building up from molecular size):**
- **Chemical methods:** Double decomposition, hydrolysis, reduction, oxidation
  - As₂O₃ + H₂S → As₂S₃ sol + H₂O (double decomposition)
  - FeCl₃ + 3H₂O → Fe(OH)₃ sol + 3HCl (hydrolysis)
  - AuCl₃ + HCHO (reducing agent) → Gold sol (reduction)
- **Solvent exchange:** Sulfur dissolved in alcohol, then water added → sulfur sol

**Purification Methods:**
- **Dialysis** (as explained above)
- **Electrodialysis** (faster, uses electric field)
- **Ultrafiltration** (special filter paper with colloidal-size pores; separates sol from true solution particles)

---

### Q: State and explain Beer-Lambert's Law.

**Beer-Lambert Law:**
The absorbance of a solution is directly proportional to both the **concentration** of the absorbing substance and the **path length** of light through it.

$$A = \varepsilon \cdot c \cdot l$$

Or equivalently:
$$\log \frac{I_0}{I} = \varepsilon \cdot c \cdot l$$

Where:
- A = absorbance (no unit)
- I₀ = intensity of incident light
- I = intensity of transmitted light
- ε = molar absorption coefficient (L·mol⁻¹·cm⁻¹)
- c = concentration (mol/L)
- l = path length (cm)

**Limitations:**
- Valid only at **low concentrations** (dilute solutions — ideal behavior)
- Light must be **monochromatic** (single wavelength)
- The absorbing species must not **associate, dissociate, or react** with solvent
- Does not apply to **highly concentrated solutions** due to solute-solute interactions

### Q: What do you mean by ionization potential and electron affinity?

**Ionization Potential (IP):**
The energy required to remove the outermost (most loosely bound) electron from a **neutral gaseous atom** in its ground state.

$$M(g) \rightarrow M^+(g) + e^- \quad \Delta H = IE$$

Factors affecting IP: atomic size, nuclear charge, shielding, orbital type

**Electron Affinity (EA):**
The energy **released** (or sometimes absorbed) when an electron is added to a **neutral gaseous atom** to form a negative ion.

$$X(g) + e^- \rightarrow X^-(g) + \text{energy (EA)}$$

- High EA → strong tendency to gain electrons → highly electronegative
- Cl has the highest EA; F has second highest (due to small size causing repulsion)

---

### Q: Compare the size of Fe with Fe²⁺ and Cl with Cl⁻.

**Fe vs Fe²⁺:**
- Fe (26e⁻) has more electrons → larger electron cloud
- Fe²⁺ has lost 2 electrons → fewer electron-electron repulsions → electron cloud contracts
- **Size: Fe > Fe²⁺**
- (Fe: ~126 pm; Fe²⁺: ~77 pm)

**Cl vs Cl⁻:**
- Cl⁻ has gained 1 electron → more electrons for same nuclear charge → increased shielding and repulsion → electron cloud expands
- **Size: Cl⁻ > Cl**
- (Cl: ~99 pm; Cl⁻: ~181 pm)

**General rule:**
- Cations are smaller than parent atoms
- Anions are larger than parent atoms

### Q: Define homogeneous and heterogeneous catalysis.

**Homogeneous Catalysis:**
The catalyst and reactants are in the **same phase** (all gas or all liquid).

- Example: Oxidation of SO₂ to SO₃ using NO as catalyst (all gases)
  $$2\text{SO}_2 + \text{O}_2 \xrightarrow{\text{NO(g)}} 2\text{SO}_3$$
- Mechanism: catalyst forms intermediate compound with reactant, then regenerated
- More efficient contact between reactants and catalyst

**Heterogeneous Catalysis:**
The catalyst and reactants are in **different phases**.

- Example: Haber process — N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃ (Fe catalyst, solid; reactants, gas)
- Example: Hydrogenation of oils — H₂ + oil → fat (Ni catalyst, solid; reactants, gas/liquid)
- Mechanism: Adsorption of reactants on catalyst surface → reaction → desorption of products

### Q: Explain activated complex and energy of activation with figure.

**Activation Energy (Ea):**
The minimum energy required for reactant molecules to transform into products. It is the energy needed to reach the **transition state (activated complex)**.

**Activated Complex:**
A short-lived, high-energy, unstable species formed at the top of the energy barrier during the collision of reactant molecules. It can either form products or revert to reactants.

**Energy Profile Diagram:**

```
        [Activated Complex]
              *
             /|\
            / | \
           /  |  \
Reactants /   |Ea \
   ___   /    |    \___  Products
         ←Ea→     ΔH →

Energy
  ↑
  |      ‡ (transition state, top of barrier)
  |     / \
  |    /   \
  |   /     \_____ Products (exothermic)
  |__/  Reactants
  └──────────────────→ Reaction coordinate
```

- Exothermic reaction: products at lower energy than reactants
- Endothermic reaction: products at higher energy than reactants
- Catalyst: **lowers Ea** → reaction proceeds faster (provides alternative pathway)

### Q: Write notes on emulsion.

**Emulsion:**
A colloidal system in which **one liquid is dispersed in another immiscible liquid** in the form of droplets.

**Types:**
1. **O/W (Oil in Water):** Oil droplets dispersed in water. Example: Milk, cold cream
2. **W/O (Water in Oil):** Water droplets dispersed in oil. Example: Butter, margarine

**Emulsifying Agent (Emulsifier):**
A substance that stabilizes emulsions by reducing interfacial tension.
- Examples: Soap, detergents, proteins, lecithin
- Mechanism: Emulsifier molecules arrange at the oil-water interface with hydrophilic end in water and hydrophobic end in oil → prevents coalescence of droplets

**Properties of Emulsions:**
- Show Tyndall effect
- Show Brownian motion
- Can be broken (demulsification) by heating, centrifugation, or adding electrolytes

**Applications:** Food industry (mayonnaise), pharmaceuticals (ointments), cosmetics

### Q: Define conjugate acid-base pairs.

In **Bronsted-Lowry theory**, when an acid donates a proton (H⁺), it becomes its **conjugate base**. When a base accepts a proton, it becomes its **conjugate acid**.

$$\text{Acid}_1 + \text{Base}_2 \rightleftharpoons \text{Conjugate Base}_1 + \text{Conjugate Acid}_2$$

**Examples:**

| Acid | Conjugate Base |
|---|---|
| HCl | Cl⁻ |
| H₂SO₄ | HSO₄⁻ |
| NH₄⁺ | NH₃ |
| H₂O | OH⁻ |

| Base | Conjugate Acid |
|---|---|
| Cl⁻ | HCl |
| NH₃ | NH₄⁺ |
| OH⁻ | H₂O |
| H₂O | H₃O⁺ |

**Strong acid → weak conjugate base** (and vice versa)

Example reaction:
$$\underbrace{\text{HCl}}_{\text{acid}_1} + \underbrace{\text{H}_2\text{O}}_{\text{base}_2} \rightleftharpoons \underbrace{\text{Cl}^-}_{\text{conj. base}_1} + \underbrace{\text{H}_3\text{O}^+}_{\text{conj. acid}_2}$$

### Q: Define Vant Hoff reaction isochore. What is zero-order reaction?

**Vant Hoff Reaction Isochore:**
An equation describing the effect of temperature on the equilibrium constant:

$$\frac{d(\ln K)}{dT} = \frac{\Delta H°}{RT^2}$$

Integrated form:
$$\ln \frac{K_2}{K_1} = \frac{\Delta H°}{R} \left(\frac{1}{T_1} - \frac{1}{T_2}\right)$$

- If ΔH° > 0 (endothermic): K increases with T
- If ΔH° < 0 (exothermic): K decreases with T

**Zero-Order Reaction:**
A reaction where the rate is **independent of the concentration** of reactants.

$$\text{Rate} = k[A]^0 = k$$

Integrated form: [A]₀ − [A]_t = kt → [A] decreases linearly with time.

Half-life: $t_{1/2} = \frac{[A]_0}{2k}$ (depends on initial concentration — unique to zero order)

Examples: Decomposition of N₂O on hot Pt surface; some enzyme-catalyzed reactions (substrate at saturation)
