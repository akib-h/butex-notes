## PART 1 — WHAT IS ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE?

### 🔷 Core Definition

**Artificial Intelligence (AI)** is the branch of computer science concerned with building systems that can perform tasks that would normally require human intelligence. These tasks include reasoning, learning from experience, understanding natural language, recognizing patterns, solving problems, and making decisions.

> 📌 **Simple Definition for Slide Use:**
> *"AI is the simulation of human intelligence by machines — systems that can learn, reason, and respond to the world the way humans do."*

---

### 🔷 Key Properties of an AI System

| Property | What It Means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Learning** | Improving from experience or data | Spam filters getting smarter |
| **Reasoning** | Drawing conclusions from information | Medical diagnosis systems |
| **Problem Solving** | Finding solutions to complex tasks | Chess engines, navigation apps |
| **Perception** | Understanding sensory input | Face recognition, speech-to-text |
| **Language Understanding** | Interpreting and generating human language | ChatGPT, Google Translate |
| **Adaptation** | Adjusting behavior based on context | Recommendation algorithms |

---

### 🔷 The Alan Turing Foundation (1950)

- In **1950**, mathematician and logician **Alan Turing** published a landmark paper titled *"Computing Machinery and Intelligence"*
- He posed the famous question: **"Can machines think?"**
- Turing proposed what he called the **Imitation Game** — now universally known as the **Turing Test**
- The test works as follows: if a human judge cannot reliably distinguish between a machine and a human in a text conversation, the machine has demonstrated intelligent behavior

> 📌 **Key Quote (paraphrased for slide):**
> *"Turing's 1950 question — 'Can machines think?' — is still the philosophical foundation of every AI system built today."*

---

### 🔷 The Birth of AI as a Field (1956)

- **1956 — The Dartmouth Conference:** Organized by **John McCarthy** at Dartmouth College, New Hampshire
- McCarthy formally coined the term **"Artificial Intelligence"**
- He defined it as: *"the science and engineering of making intelligent machines"*
- This conference is universally considered the **official founding event** of AI as an academic discipline
- Attendees included **Marvin Minsky**, **Claude Shannon**, and **Herbert Simon** — all figures who would go on to shape the field

## PART 2 — HISTORY OF AI: FULL TIMELINE

### 🕰️ Era 1: The Optimistic Beginnings (1950s–1960s)

| Year | Event | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| 1950 | Alan Turing publishes "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" | Introduces the Turing Test; launches philosophical debate on machine intelligence |
| 1956 | Dartmouth Conference | AI officially named as a field; founding figures assembled |
| 1957 | Frank Rosenblatt develops the **Perceptron** | First artificial neural network model; the ancestor of modern deep learning |
| 1958 | John McCarthy develops **LISP** programming language | First language specifically designed for AI research |
| 1965 | Joseph Weizenbaum creates **ELIZA** | First chatbot; simulated human conversation using pattern matching |
| 1969 | Stanford Research Institute builds **Shakey the Robot** | First mobile robot capable of reasoning about its own actions |

**Key Characteristic of This Era:**
- Enormous optimism — researchers believed human-level AI was 10–20 years away
- Foundational mathematical and logical frameworks were established
- Government and military funding was generous (especially from DARPA in the US)

---

### 🕰️ Era 2: The First AI Winter (1970s–1980s)

**What Is an AI Winter?**
An "AI Winter" is a period of sharply reduced interest, funding, and research activity in artificial intelligence, typically following a wave of unfulfilled promises.

**Causes of the First AI Winter (mid-1970s):**
- Early AI programs could only solve "toy problems" — highly simplified versions of real-world tasks
- Real-world complexity far exceeded what early hardware and algorithms could handle
- The **Lighthill Report** (1973) in the UK criticized AI research for its failure to deliver practical results
- US funding body **DARPA** drastically reduced AI research grants

**Notable Development Despite the Winter:**
- **1974–1980:** The Japanese **Fifth Generation Computer Project** attempted to build Prolog-based reasoning machines
- **1980:** **Expert Systems** emerge — AI programs that encode human expert knowledge as if-then rules
  - Example: **MYCIN** (1974, Stanford) — diagnosed blood diseases and recommended antibiotics with 69% accuracy, matching junior doctors
  - Example: **XCON** (1982, DEC) — configured computer systems for orders, saving the company ~$25 million/year by the mid-1980s

---

### 🕰️ Era 3: The Expert Systems Boom and Second AI Winter (1980s)

**The Rise of Expert Systems (Early 1980s):**
- Corporations invested heavily in expert systems — AI programs that encoded domain expertise
- By 1985, companies were spending over **$1 billion per year** on expert systems
- The AI hardware market reached **$400 million** annually

**The Second AI Winter (Late 1980s–Early 1990s):**
- Expert systems proved brittle: they could not handle exceptions, learn, or update themselves
- Maintenance was expensive — human experts had to manually update rules constantly
- The **LISP Machine market** collapsed in 1987 when cheaper alternatives appeared
- DARPA again cut funding after evaluating that progress was too slow

---

### 🕰️ Era 4: Statistical Learning and the Internet Revolution (1990s–2000s)

| Year | Event | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| 1997 | IBM **Deep Blue** defeats Garry Kasparov at chess | AI defeats the world champion; landmark cultural moment |
| 1998 | **Google** founded | Search algorithms develop early machine learning for ranking pages |
| 1998 | Yann LeCun develops **LeNet** (CNN for digit recognition) | Early demonstration of convolutional neural networks |
| 2002 | **iRobot Roomba** released | AI enters the consumer home |
| 2006 | Geoffrey Hinton re-introduces **deep neural network training** | Key breakthrough that would ignite the deep learning revolution |
| 2007 | **iPhone** released | Puts AI-capable hardware in billions of hands |
| 2009 | **ImageNet** dataset created | 14 million labeled images; becomes the training ground for modern vision AI |

**Key Shift in This Era:**
- AI moved from **rule-based systems** to **statistical methods** — letting data drive learning
- The internet explosion provided **unprecedented amounts of data** for AI to learn from
- Hardware improvements (especially GPUs for parallel computing) made larger models feasible

---

### 🕰️ Era 5: The Deep Learning Revolution (2010s)

| Year | Event | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| 2011 | IBM **Watson** defeats Jeopardy! champions | AI demonstrates natural language understanding at scale |
| 2011 | Apple releases **Siri** | First mainstream AI assistant reaches consumers |
| 2012 | **AlexNet** wins ImageNet competition | Deep CNN reduces image classification error by half; ignites deep learning boom |
| 2014 | **GANs (Generative Adversarial Networks)** invented by Ian Goodfellow | AI can now generate realistic images, video, and audio |
| 2016 | DeepMind's **AlphaGo** defeats Lee Sedol at Go | AI masters a game far more complex than chess; major symbolic milestone |
| 2017 | Google publishes **"Attention Is All You Need"** (Transformer paper) | Foundation for all modern large language models including GPT and BERT |
| 2018 | **BERT** (Google) and **GPT-1** (OpenAI) released | Language models begin to understand context at human-level depth |

---

### 🕰️ Era 6: The Age of Large Language Models and Generative AI (2020s–Present)

| Year | Event | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| 2020 | **GPT-3** released (OpenAI) | 175 billion parameters; demonstrates few-shot learning capability |
| 2021 | **DALL·E** released | AI generates images from text descriptions |
| 2022 | **ChatGPT** released (November 30, 2022) | Crosses 100M users in 2 months — fastest app growth in history |
| 2023 | **GPT-4**, **Gemini**, **Claude** released | Multimodal AI — models that understand text, images, and code simultaneously |
| 2023 | EU passes the **AI Act** | World's first comprehensive AI regulatory framework |
| 2024 | **AI agents** emerge as next frontier | AI systems that can autonomously plan, use tools, and execute multi-step tasks |

## PART 3 — KEY CONCEPTS AND DEFINITIONS (For Script Support)

### The Three Waves of AI (Summary Framework)

| Wave | Era | Core Technology | Key Limitation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Symbolic AI** | 1950s–1980s | Logic rules and expert knowledge | Could not learn; brittle; couldn't handle ambiguity |
| **Statistical ML** | 1990s–2000s | Probabilistic methods, neural nets | Required feature engineering; limited by hardware |
| **Deep Learning / LLMs** | 2010s–present | Multi-layer neural nets, transformers | Requires massive data and compute; opaque decisions |

---

### Why Did AI Progress Accelerate So Dramatically in the 2010s?

Three converging factors — often called the **"Three Ds"**:

1. **Data** — The internet, smartphones, and social media created an explosion of labeled and unlabeled data at a scale previously unimaginable
2. **Compute (Hardware)** — Graphics Processing Units (GPUs) from NVIDIA became repurposed for parallel AI training, reducing training times dramatically
3. **Deep Learning Algorithms** — Breakthroughs in network architecture (CNNs, RNNs, Transformers) unlocked capabilities that shallower methods couldn't reach
