## SECTION 2 — WHAT IS AI + HISTORY OF AI
### Slides 3–5 | Duration: ~3 minutes
## ═══════════════════════════════════════

`[SLIDE 3 — What is Artificial Intelligence?]`

*Step forward. Acknowledge the audience briefly. Establish eye contact before speaking.*

---

**"Thank you, [Leader's Name]."**

*Pause one beat.*

**"So — what exactly is Artificial Intelligence?"**

**"At its core, AI is the simulation of human intelligence by machines. It's the science of building systems that can perform tasks which, if done by a human, would require intelligence — things like recognizing speech, making decisions, translating languages, or identifying objects in an image."**

**"The term was formally coined in 1956 by John McCarthy at the Dartmouth Conference — where he defined AI as the science and engineering of making intelligent machines."**

**"But the dream is older than that."**

*Pause — let that line breathe. Then continue.*

---

`[SLIDE 4 — History of AI: Timeline Visual]`

**"Let's walk through AI's history — because understanding where it came from tells us a lot about where it's going."**

**"In 1950, mathematician Alan Turing published his famous paper asking the question: 'Can machines think?' He proposed what we now call the Turing Test — if a machine could converse so naturally that you couldn't tell it from a human, it could be considered intelligent."**

*Pause.*

**"Through the 1960s and 70s, researchers were optimistic — perhaps too optimistic. Early AI programs could solve math problems and play chess, but they hit a wall: the real world is far more complex than a chessboard."**

**"This led to what historians call the 'AI Winter' — periods in the 1970s and 1980s where funding dried up and interest collapsed, because AI couldn't deliver on its early promises."**

*Pause. Give the audience a moment to absorb the concept of repeated disappointment.*

**"Think of it like this: imagine you build a car that works perfectly on a perfectly flat empty road — and then someone asks you to drive it through Dhaka traffic. That's what early AI faced when it left the lab."**

*Slight pause — let the analogy land.*

---

`[SLIDE 5 — History Continued: The Revival]`

**"But then something changed."**

*Pause for effect.*

**"In the 1990s and 2000s, the rise of the internet gave AI something it had always lacked: data. Massive amounts of it."**

**"In 1997, IBM's Deep Blue defeated world chess champion Garry Kasparov — a landmark moment that showed the world AI could master tasks of immense complexity."**

**"Then came the deep learning revolution of the 2010s — neural networks with many layers, fueled by powerful GPUs and enormous datasets, began outperforming humans at tasks like image recognition and language translation."**

**"In 2017, researchers at Google published a paper that changed everything — introducing the Transformer architecture, which became the foundation of every modern AI language system you've heard of."**

**"And in 2022, the world met ChatGPT — a large language model that crossed 100 million users in just two months — the fastest-growing application in history."**

*Pause. Speak slowly and clearly.*

**"We are living in the most significant chapter of AI history — and it is still being written."**

*Take a brief pause. Nod toward Member 3.*

**"But to understand why AI has become so powerful, we need to understand the engine underneath it. I'll hand things over to [Member 3's Name] to explain how AI actually learns."**

- "The dream is older than that" — pause after this line; it creates narrative tension

**Slide 4:**

- The AI Winter concept: use the word "disappointed" or "unfulfilled" — it makes it more human
- The Dhaka traffic analogy is your own — it's local and relatable; own it

**Slide 5:**
- "But then something changed" — this is your narrative pivot; say it simply and clearly
- Don't rush the 1997 → 2017 → 2022 timeline — let each beat land
- "Still being written" — make eye contact with the room; let them feel included in the story
