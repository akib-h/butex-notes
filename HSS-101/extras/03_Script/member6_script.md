`[SLIDE 15 — AI Ethics: Bias and Fairness]`

*Step forward calmly. Take a breath. Your tone here is different from the others — quieter, more deliberate.*

---

**"Thank you, [Member 4's Name]."**

*Pause one full second.*

**"Everything you just heard sounds promising — and much of it genuinely is."**

*Pause.*

**"But AI is not neutral."**

*Let that sit for a beat.*

**"It reflects the data it's trained on, and that data reflects human society — with all of its biases and inequalities."**

**"A 2018 study by researchers at MIT Media Lab tested three major commercial facial recognition systems. The result: error rates for darker-skinned women were up to 34.7% higher than for lighter-skinned men — simply because the training data was overwhelmingly composed of lighter-skinned male faces."**

**"In 2019, a healthcare algorithm widely used across the United States was found to systematically disadvantage Black patients — not out of malice, but because the algorithm used historical healthcare spending as a proxy for health need. Since Black patients had historically received less care due to systemic inequity, the algorithm predicted they needed less care — and allocated resources accordingly."**

*Pause.*

**"When AI systems make consequential decisions — about who gets a loan, who gets a job interview, who gets flagged at a border — bias in the algorithm becomes bias at scale. Millions of people can be affected before anyone notices."**

*Speak the next sentence slowly.*

**"Fairness in AI is not a philosophical nicety. It is an engineering requirement."**

---

`[SLIDE 16 — AI Ethics: Privacy and Surveillance]`

**"Privacy is another major concern."**

**"AI-powered surveillance systems can now identify individuals in crowds from CCTV footage, track movements across a city, and predict behavior patterns from digital footprints. While this has legitimate law enforcement uses, it also enables control at unprecedented scale."**

**"And then there are deepfakes — AI-generated videos that make real people appear to say or do things they never did. In 2019, criminals used an AI voice clone to impersonate a CEO and successfully authorized a fraudulent wire transfer of $243,000."**

*Pause. The audience should feel this.*

**"That incident was in 2019. The technology has improved enormously since then."**

**"The question is not just 'can AI do this?' — it clearly can. The question is: 'should it, and who decides?'"**

---

`[SLIDE 17 — AI Ethics: Jobs and Society]`

**"Perhaps the most debated concern is economic disruption."**

**"A 2023 report by Goldman Sachs estimated that AI could automate tasks equivalent to 300 million full-time jobs globally. Roles involving repetitive cognitive tasks — data entry, basic customer service, elements of legal and financial analysis — are at highest risk."**

**"But history gives us some perspective. The Industrial Revolution looked catastrophic from the inside — and it eventually created more jobs than it destroyed. The electrification of industry, the rise of computers, the internet — each wave disrupted existing work and created entirely new categories of employment."**

*Pause.*

**"The key question with AI is not 'will there be change?' — there will be. The question is: 'will the transition be managed fairly? Will workers be supported? Will the productivity gains be broadly shared, or concentrated in few hands?'"**

**"We do not have those answers yet. Which is precisely why governance matters."**

---

`[SLIDE 18 — The Future of AI]`

**"So where is all of this going?"**

**"In the near term — the next five to ten years — AI will become more deeply embedded in every profession. Doctors will use AI co-pilots. Lawyers will use AI research assistants. Engineers will use AI code generators. Education will be more personalized than ever."**

**"In the longer term, researchers are working toward Artificial General Intelligence — AI that can match human cognitive flexibility across any domain. The timeline is deeply debated — some experts say within a decade, others say never. But the consensus is that the question is being taken more seriously than ever before."**

**"Geoffrey Hinton — widely known as the Godfather of Deep Learning — resigned from Google in 2023. His reason: he wanted to speak freely about the risks AI poses to humanity. When the person who helped invent modern deep learning says the risks deserve serious attention, that deserves serious attention."**

---

`[SLIDE 19 — The Future: Regulation and Hope]`

**"Regulation is beginning to catch up."**

**"The European Union passed the AI Act — the world's first comprehensive AI regulatory framework — establishing risk tiers and clear compliance requirements. Systems that could harm fundamental rights are banned. High-risk systems face strict oversight. It's not perfect — but it's a start."**

**"Countries like Bangladesh will need to develop their own governance frameworks — frameworks that protect citizens while enabling the innovation and economic opportunity that AI offers."**

*Pause. Shift tone — from concern to possibility.*

**"But amidst all of the concern, let us not lose sight of the opportunity."**

**"AI has the potential to help diagnose diseases earlier and more accurately. To educate every child as if they had a private tutor. To respond to climate change faster than any government committee could. To solve problems that have stumped humanity for generations."**

*Pause. Look slowly across the room.*

**"The future of AI is not written."**

**"We — engineers, policymakers, educators, students, citizens — are writing it right now."**

*Pause. Nod to the Leader.*

**"I'll hand it back to [Leader's Name] to bring everything together."**

## DELIVERY NOTES

**Slide 15 (Bias):**
- "But AI is not neutral" — three-word sentence, four-syllable idea. Say it quietly. Don't rush past it.
- The MIT study and healthcare algorithm: don't read these robotically — you're describing real people being harmed by code
- "Bias at scale" — this is your key concept. Return to it as needed.

**Slide 16 (Privacy):**
- The $243,000 wire transfer: say the number clearly — it's concrete and memorable
- "That was 2019. The technology has improved enormously since then." — understated, effective. Pause after.
- Don't be alarmist — your job is to make the audience think, not frighten them

**Slide 17 (Jobs):**
- 300 million — say it slowly. Give the audience a second to compute what that means.
- The historical analogy (Industrial Revolution) is important — you are providing balance, not dismissing the concern
- "We do not have those answers yet" — honest, not evasive

**Slide 18 (Future):**
- Geoffrey Hinton story: don't rush this. It has gravity.
- "When the person who helped invent modern deep learning says the risks deserve serious attention, that deserves serious attention." — you are not editorializing; you are reporting

**Slide 19 (Regulation + Hope):**
- The pivot from concern to hope must feel genuine — not like a required happy ending
- "The future of AI is not written" — pause before and after
- "We are writing it right now" — include yourself in "we." You are speaking to a room full of people who will shape this.

## BACKUP LINE — For Q&A on Existential AI Risk

> Use if asked about long-term AI safety concerns:

**"The concern isn't that AI will suddenly 'go evil.' The concern, as articulated by researchers at organizations like Anthropic and OpenAI, is more subtle: an advanced AI system optimizing hard for a goal we defined imprecisely might achieve that goal in ways we didn't intend — and might be difficult to stop once it's capable enough. It's less science fiction, more engineering safety. The same kind of careful thinking that goes into nuclear safety or aviation safety needs to go into AI. Not because catastrophe is certain — but because the stakes are high enough that we can't afford to be careless."**
