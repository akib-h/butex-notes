## SECTION 1 — HISTORY OF ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE

---

Most people think of AI as something born in Silicon Valley sometime around 2015. But the story is much older — and much more human — than that.

---

### The Philosophical Roots (Pre-1950s)

The dream of creating artificial minds goes back centuries. Ancient Greek myths told stories of Talos — a giant bronze automaton built to protect the island of Crete. Philosophers like Descartes wondered whether animals were simply biological machines. Leibniz dreamed of a "calculus of thought."

But the real story of AI as a scientific discipline begins with one question, asked by one man.

---

### Alan Turing and the Birth of the Field (1950)

In 1950, British mathematician **Alan Turing** published a paper titled *"Computing Machinery and Intelligence."* He opened it with a deceptively simple question:

> *"Can machines think?"*

He proposed what he called the **Imitation Game** — now known as the **Turing Test** — where a machine would be considered intelligent if it could hold a conversation indistinguishable from a human.

That paper planted the seed.

---

### The Dartmouth Conference (1956)

Six years later, in the summer of 1956, a small group of scientists gathered at **Dartmouth College** in the United States. It was here that **John McCarthy** officially coined the term **"Artificial Intelligence."**

The founding optimism was breathtaking. They believed that *"every aspect of learning or any feature of intelligence can in principle be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it."*

They thought they'd crack it in one summer.

They were off by about seventy years — and counting.

> 💬 Speaker note: A slight smile here works well. The audience will appreciate the irony.

---

### Early Enthusiasm and the First AI Winter (1956–1980s)

The 1960s and early 70s saw real progress. Early programs like **ELIZA** — one of the first chatbots, developed at MIT — could simulate conversation convincingly enough to fool people. Logic-based systems called **Expert Systems** were designed to replicate the decision-making of human specialists.

But progress plateaued. The problems turned out to be *far* harder than expected. Computers weren't powerful enough. Data was scarce. Funding dried up.

This period of reduced interest and investment is called the **AI Winter** — and the field went through it not once, but twice. The first winter hit in the late 1970s, and a second followed in the late 1980s.

---

### The Machine Learning Revolution (1990s–2000s)

Things began to shift in the 1990s. Instead of programming machines with explicit rules, researchers started letting machines *learn from data.*

A landmark moment came in **1997**, when **IBM's Deep Blue** defeated world chess champion Garry Kasparov. Not by thinking like a human — but by computing possibilities at incredible speed.

Then in **2006**, Geoffrey Hinton and his colleagues revived **deep learning** — a technique that had existed since the 80s but was finally becoming practical. This would become the engine of the modern AI era.

---

### The Deep Learning Explosion (2010s–Present)

The real turning point came in **2012**, when a deep learning model called **AlexNet** demolished the competition in a global image recognition challenge — cutting the error rate nearly in half.

The floodgates opened.

- **2016**: Google DeepMind's **AlphaGo** defeats the world Go champion — a game once thought to be beyond machine reach.
- **2017**: The **Transformer architecture** is introduced — the backbone of every modern language model.
- **2022–2023**: **ChatGPT** reaches 100 million users in two months — faster than any technology in history.

We are now in what many call the **Third AI Wave** — and unlike the two winters before it, this one shows no sign of stopping.

## SECTION 2 — TYPES OF AI

---

### Framing

Now that we know where AI came from, let's talk about what AI actually *is* — because "AI" is an umbrella term that covers very different things.

There are two major ways to classify AI: by **capability**, and by **functionality.**

---

### Classification by Capability

**1. Narrow AI (Weak AI)**
This is all the AI that exists today. Narrow AI is designed to do *one specific task* — and it can do that task extraordinarily well. Face recognition, language translation, spam detection, chess — these are all Narrow AI.

The key point: no matter how impressive it gets at its task, it has *no understanding* outside of it. Ask a chess AI to write a poem, and it's completely lost.

**2. General AI (AGI — Artificial General Intelligence)**
This is AI that can perform *any intellectual task a human can* — with the same flexibility and adaptability. It can reason across domains, learn from limited examples, and handle novel situations.

AGI does not exist yet. But it is the target that many of the most ambitious AI labs in the world are actively working toward.

**3. Super AI (Superintelligence)**
This is hypothetical AI that surpasses human intelligence in *every domain* — scientific creativity, social skills, strategic thinking, everything.

This is the domain of philosophy and speculation today — but it is taken seriously by researchers like Nick Bostrom and institutions like the **Future of Life Institute**.

---

### Classification by Functionality

AI can also be categorized by what it actually *does*:

- **Reactive Machines** — No memory, no learning. They respond to inputs with fixed logic. IBM's Deep Blue was this. Pure reaction.
- **Limited Memory AI** — Can use recent past data to inform decisions. Self-driving cars use this — they remember what just happened on the road.
- **Theory of Mind AI** — A future category: AI that understands human emotions, beliefs, and intentions. Not fully achieved yet.
- **Self-Aware AI** — Fully hypothetical. AI that has consciousness and self-understanding. The stuff of science fiction — for now.

---

### Closing and Handoff

So AI isn't one thing. It's a spectrum — from a narrow spell-checker on your phone to the theoretical dream of a machine that can think, feel, and reason as broadly as we do.
