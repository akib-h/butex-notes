**Are our jobs going to be taken by AI?**

Let's be honest about this. Let's not be dismissive, and let's not be alarmist. Let's look at what we actually know.

---

### The History of Automation Anxiety

First, some perspective.

Every major wave of technology has triggered the fear of mass unemployment. The printing press. The steam engine. The factory assembly line. The computer. The internet.

And each time, the reality was more complex than the fear. New jobs emerged. Some jobs disappeared. The workforce adapted — often painfully, often unevenly — but it adapted.

The question is: **Is this time different?**

The honest answer is: maybe. Possibly. And we should take that seriously.

---

### What the Research Says

The **McKinsey Global Institute** estimates that by 2030, **up to 30% of current job tasks** globally could be automated with current or near-future AI technology.

Note the word: *tasks*, not *jobs*. Most jobs are bundles of many tasks. AI may automate *some* tasks within a job without eliminating the job entirely — but it will fundamentally change what people spend their time doing.

The **World Economic Forum** estimates that AI and automation will displace around **85 million jobs** globally by 2025 — but also create **97 million new roles**. Net positive on paper. But those aren't the same people in the same places. Displacement is real even if the totals balance.

---

### Which Jobs Are Most Exposed?

Jobs involving **routine, predictable, information-based tasks** are most at risk:

- Data entry, bookkeeping, basic accounting
- Paralegal research and document review
- Basic customer service and call centers
- Certain types of financial analysis
- Radiology reading (partly — AI assists, doesn't replace entirely, yet)
- Content writing of simple, templated nature

Jobs that are **less exposed** — at least for now:

- Roles requiring genuine emotional intelligence: counseling, social work, nursing
- Roles requiring physical dexterity in unstructured environments: plumbing, construction
- Creative roles with true originality and cultural context
- Roles requiring complex judgment in high-stakes, novel situations: senior leadership, crisis management

---

### Jobs Being Created

AI is also *creating* jobs — some of which barely existed five years ago:

- **AI / ML Engineers and Researchers**
- **Prompt Engineers** — designing effective instructions for AI systems
- **AI Ethics Officers and Auditors**
- **Data Annotators and Curators** — the humans who label training data
- **AI Integration Specialists** — helping organizations deploy AI tools
- **Cybersecurity professionals** focused on AI-specific threats

---

### The Bangladesh Angle

For Bangladesh, the concern is specific. Our garments sector — 4 million workers, mostly women — performs tasks that are increasingly automatable with robotics and AI-driven quality control.

At the same time, our growing IT and freelancing sector — Bangladesh ranks among the top countries for online freelancers — is positioned to benefit directly from the AI economy if workers are equipped with the right skills.

The window is open. But it won't stay open forever.

The investment in **AI literacy, digital skills, and STEM education** is not optional for Bangladesh. It is existential.

## SECTION 2 — THE NEAR-TERM FUTURE OF AI (Next 5–10 Years)

---

### What's Coming Soon

Looking ahead at the next five to ten years, several trends are already clearly emerging:

**1. Multimodal AI Becomes Standard**
Current AI systems that handle text, image, audio, and video separately will increasingly merge. You'll have a single AI assistant that can watch a video, read a document, listen to a conversation, and synthesize all of it — simultaneously.

**2. AI Agents**
The next major frontier beyond chatbots is **AI agents** — AI systems that don't just answer questions but take *actions*. Book a flight. Write, test, and deploy code. Research a topic, synthesize findings, and produce a report. Browse the web autonomously. These "agentic" systems are already in early deployment and will become a dominant computing paradigm.

**3. AI in Scientific Discovery**
Beyond AlphaFold, AI is beginning to contribute to **materials science, climate modeling, mathematics, and drug discovery** at a pace no human team could match. We may be entering a period where AI dramatically accelerates the pace of scientific progress itself — compressing decades of research into years.

**4. Commoditization of AI**
AI capabilities that feel cutting-edge today will be commodity services within five years — available cheaply, embedded in every software product. The competitive advantage will shift from "having AI" to "using AI wisely."

**5. Regulation Begins in Earnest**
The **EU AI Act**, enacted in 2024, is the world's first comprehensive AI regulation. It classifies AI systems by risk level and imposes strict requirements on high-risk applications. Other jurisdictions — the UK, US, and eventually Bangladesh — will develop their own frameworks.

## SECTION 3 — LONG-TERM FUTURE & REGULATION

---

### The AGI Question

The most significant long-term question in AI is one that divides even the world's leading experts:

**When — and whether — will we achieve AGI?**

Some of the most serious researchers in the field — including those at OpenAI and Google DeepMind — believe AGI could arrive within the next **10–20 years**. Others believe it's decades away, or fundamentally misconceived as a goal.

What is clear: we are making progress on general capabilities faster than most predicted even five years ago. And the systems we build are increasingly able to generalize across domains in ways that would have seemed remarkable a decade ago.

---

### Existential Risks — Taking Them Seriously

A growing community of researchers — including Nobel laureates, former heads of state, and AI scientists — have signed public statements warning that AI represents one of the most consequential risks in human history if not managed carefully.

The concerns include:

- **Misalignment**: An AI system that pursues goals not fully aligned with human values — not from malice, but from misspecification of objectives. Teaching a system to maximize a metric without properly encoding what we truly want.
- **Concentration of Power**: Advanced AI controlled by a small number of actors — corporations or governments — could enable unprecedented concentration of economic and political power.
- **Bioweapons and Cyberweapons**: AI dramatically lowers the barrier to developing dangerous biological or cyber capabilities.

These are not science fiction. They are being actively researched by serious institutions including **Anthropic's Alignment Science team, the UK AI Safety Institute, and Oxford's Future of Humanity Institute.**

---

### What Good Regulation Looks Like

Regulation doesn't mean stifling innovation. It means *governing* innovation — ensuring that progress benefits everyone, not just the first movers.

Key elements of good AI governance:

- **Risk-tiered oversight**: Higher scrutiny for high-stakes applications (criminal justice, healthcare, critical infrastructure) than for low-stakes ones (playlist recommendations).
- **Transparency requirements**: When a major decision affecting a person is made by AI, they should have the right to know — and the right to appeal.
- **Safety evaluations**: Large models should be evaluated for dangerous capabilities before deployment — analogous to drug trials before a medication goes to market.
- **International coordination**: AI development doesn't stop at borders. Governance frameworks need to be global — similar to how nuclear non-proliferation is handled, though far more complex.
- **Inclusive governance**: The countries and communities *affected* by AI should have a voice in how it's governed — not just the countries building it.

---

### A Note of Measured Optimism

I want to end my section with this:

The same tools that raise these risks are tools of extraordinary promise. AI that cures diseases. AI that models climate solutions. AI that makes quality education available to every child on earth, regardless of where they were born.

The future is genuinely open. It is not written by algorithms — it is written by **choices.** Policy choices, engineering choices, educational choices, and ethical choices.

The generation in this room is going to make many of those choices. I hope we make them with care, with humility, and with the understanding that the technology is only as wise as the people who wield it.
