### 🖥️ The Name: Why "x86"?

> *"The name is not random. It is a statement."*

**x86** refers to a family of processor instruction set architectures that originated with Intel's **8086 microprocessor**, released in **1978**. Over the decades, the x86 architecture evolved into the backbone of personal computing — powering the desktops, laptops, and servers that run the modern digital world. Processors from Intel and AMD, both built on the x86 architecture, remain dominant to this day.

Choosing this name for our group was a deliberate and meaningful decision, grounded in four key reasons:

---

#### 1. 🏛️ Historical Significance in Computing
x86 architecture marked a pivotal turning point in the history of computation. It is one of the oldest and most successful processor architectures ever designed — and its legacy continues to shape software and hardware compatibility across the entire technology industry. This directly ties into AI: understanding the computational infrastructure that made AI possible is just as important as understanding AI itself.

#### 2. 🤖 Deep Connection to AI and Technology
AI is not software alone — it runs on hardware. The x86 architecture has powered the research workstations, data centers, and consumer machines where AI algorithms were written, tested, and deployed for decades. By naming ourselves x86, we acknowledge the physical foundation that makes artificial intelligence possible. Every model ever trained, every chatbot ever deployed, has at some point depended on x86-family processors.

#### 3. 🪨 Symbolizing Strength, Stability, and Reliability
Just as x86 has been a stable and consistent pillar of the technology world for over four decades — surviving every wave of disruption and emerging stronger — our group aims to deliver a **solid, dependable, and thoroughly researched** presentation. The name signals that we are grounded in established knowledge while reaching toward new frontiers.

#### 4. 🚀 Bridging the Past and the Future
Despite newer architectures emerging — ARM for mobile, RISC-V for open-source silicon, custom AI chips like Google's TPUs — x86 still holds a commanding presence. This mirrors our presentation's theme: honoring the **Basics** (the foundational knowledge) while pushing into the **Beyond** (the future, the ethics, the transformations ahead). The name captures the exact tension our presentation explores.

---

### 💬 In Summary

> *"x86 is where modern computing began. AI is where it is going. Our group stands at that intersection — and this presentation is our exploration of the journey between them."*

Choosing x86 as our group name was not just a nod to hardware history. It is a philosophical stance: that great futures are built on great foundations, and that understanding where technology came from is the clearest lens through which to understand where it is going.

## 6. Research Guidelines

### 📚 Recommended Sources
Use only **credible, citable sources.** Avoid random blogs or Wikipedia as a final source.

| Source Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Academic Papers | Google Scholar, ResearchGate, IEEE Xplore, arXiv |
| Reputable Tech Sites | MIT Technology Review, Wired, Nature, Science Daily |
| AI Organizations | OpenAI Blog, DeepMind, Google AI Blog, IBM Research |
| News | BBC Tech, Reuters Technology, The Verge |
| Books | "Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach" — Russell & Norvig |

### 📝 Research Note Format
Each member should submit research in this standard format:

```markdown
## [Your Name] — Research Notes: [Your Topic]

### Source 1
- Title:
- URL / DOI:
- Key Points:
  - Point 1
  - Point 2
  - Point 3

### Source 2
...

### Slide Content (Draft)
**Slide Title:**
- Bullet 1
- Bullet 2
- Bullet 3

**Speaker Note:**
"What I will say out loud for this slide..."
```

### ⚠️ Research Rules
- Every factual claim must have a source
- Statistics must include the year they were published
- Do not copy-paste paragraphs — paraphrase and cite
- Keep slide bullet points to **maximum 5 per slide**, each under 10 words
- Save all sources in a shared References document

## 7. PowerPoint Design Standards

> **Designer (Member 5)** must enforce these standards across all slides.

### 🎨 Visual Identity
| Element | Specification |
|---|---|
| **Theme** | Dark tech theme OR clean white — decide on Day 1 |
| **Primary Color** | Deep blue `#0D1B2A` or midnight `#1A1A2E` |
| **Accent Color** | Electric cyan `#00D4FF` or neon green `#39FF14` |
| **Font — Headings** | Montserrat Bold or Rajdhani Bold (Google Fonts) |
| **Font — Body** | Roboto Regular or Source Sans Pro |
| **Font Size — Title** | 36–44pt |
| **Font Size — Body** | 20–24pt |
| **Logo** | "x86" text logo in top-right corner of every slide |

### 📐 Slide Layout Rules
- **One idea per slide** — do not cram multiple concepts
- **Max 5 bullet points** per content slide
- **Use visuals** — every second slide should have an image, diagram, or chart
- **Consistent margins** — 0.5 inch from edges on all sides
- **Slide numbers** on every slide except cover
- **No clip art** — use flat vector icons (Flaticon, Icons8) or screenshots

### 🎞️ Animation & Transitions
- Use **one transition style** throughout (Fade or Morph — no wipes/flips)
- Bullet point animations: **Fade In** one at a time (for verbal delivery pacing)
- Do NOT use spinning, bouncing, or blinking animations
- Slide transitions should be **0.3–0.5 seconds** max


## 10. Practice & Rehearsal Plan

### Day 6 Rehearsal Schedule

| Time Slot | Activity |
|---|---|
| 30 min | Full read-through of final script together |
| 45 min | Each member presents their section out loud with slides |
| 15 min | Leader gives individual feedback |
| 20 min | Q&A practice — members ask each other tough questions |
| 10 min | Final timing check |

### 🎤 Presentation Tips for Each Member

**For All Members:**
- Speak to the audience, **not to the screen**
- Maintain eye contact — rotate among different audience members
- Use transition phrases: *"Building on that...", "Now, moving to...", "As Member X mentioned..."*
- Pause for 2 seconds after key points — let the audience absorb
- Do NOT read bullet points word-for-word — explain them in your own words

**For the Leader (Opening):**
- Start with an engaging hook — a statistic or question: *"Did you know that AI systems now diagnose cancer more accurately than doctors in certain cases?"*
- Introduce the group name "x86" and its significance briefly
- Set the roadmap: tell the audience what they'll learn

**For the Leader (Closing / Q&A):**
- Summarize key takeaways in 3 sentences max
- Thank the audience and faculty
- Lead the Q&A confidently: *"Great question. Let me address that..."*
- If you don't know an answer: *"That's beyond today's scope, but we'd be happy to research that."*
