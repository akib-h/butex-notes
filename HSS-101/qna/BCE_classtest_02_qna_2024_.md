### Q(a). What is **Memo**? Show the difference between memo and letter.

#### 📌 What is a Memo?

A **Memorandum** (plural: *Memoranda*), commonly called a **memo**, is a short, concise **internal written communication** used within an organization to inform, instruct, request, or remind employees or departments about a particular matter.

The term comes from the Latin *memorandum est* — meaning *"it must be remembered."*

#### Key Characteristics of a Memo:
- Used for **internal communication** only (within the same organization)
- Follows a **fixed, standard format** (To, From, Date, Subject)
- Usually **brief and to the point** — no greetings or closings
- Can be **formal or semi-formal** in tone
- May be **circulated to multiple recipients** simultaneously
- **No signature** is mandatory (initials may be added)

#### Standard Format of a Memo:

```
MEMORANDUM

TO:     [Recipient(s) Name and Designation]
FROM:   [Sender's Name and Designation]
DATE:   [Date of Issue]
SUB:    [Clear, concise subject line]

Body of the memo — written in clear, direct paragraphs.
Action required or decision made should be stated clearly.

[Initials of Sender]
```

#### 📊 Difference Between Memo and Letter

| Feature | **Memo** | **Letter** |
|---------|----------|------------|
| **Purpose** | Internal communication within an organization | External communication with outside parties |
| **Audience** | Employees, departments, colleagues | Clients, suppliers, government bodies, public |
| **Format** | To, From, Date, Subject (no salutation/closing) | Full formal format with salutation and closing |
| **Salutation** | ❌ Not used (e.g., "Dear Mr. X" is absent) | ✅ Required (e.g., "Dear Sir/Madam") |
| **Complimentary Close** | ❌ Not used (e.g., "Yours sincerely" absent) | ✅ Required (e.g., "Yours faithfully") |
| **Signature** | Optional (initials suffice) | Mandatory (full name + designation) |
| **Tone** | Direct, semi-formal, concise | Formal, polished, professional |
| **Length** | Usually short (1 page or less) | Can be longer, more detailed |
| **Envelope** | ❌ Not required (distributed internally) | ✅ Required (mailed or delivered) |
| **Letterhead** | Uses organization's memo pad | Uses official company letterhead |
| **Confidentiality** | Internal — assumed confidential | May be public or formal record |
| **Examples** | Policy update, meeting notice, task delegation | Job application, business proposal, complaint |

> 💡 **Memory Tip:** A memo stays *inside* the walls of an organization; a letter goes *outside.*

### Q(b). Structure of a **Notice** — Discuss its importance in Business Communication

#### 📌 What is a Notice?

A **notice** is a **formal written announcement** intended to inform a specific group of people (employees, members, students) about an event, meeting, policy change, decision, or important information. It is concise, authoritative, and factual.

#### 🏗️ Structure of a Notice

A well-written notice follows this standard structure:

```
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
              [NAME OF THE ORGANIZATION]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
NOTICE

Date: [DD/MM/YYYY]

Subject/Title: [Bold, clear heading — e.g., "Annual General Meeting"]

Body:
This is to inform all [members/employees/students] that...
[Main information: what, when, where, who is affected]
[Supporting details or agenda if needed]

All concerned are requested to [action required].

[Name of Issuing Authority]
[Designation]
[Organization Name]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
```

#### Components Explained:

| Part | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **1. Organization Name** | Name of the issuing institution at the top |
| **2. The Word "NOTICE"** | Written in bold/caps to identify the document type |
| **3. Date** | Date of issue — important for record-keeping |
| **4. Title/Subject** | A short, descriptive heading of the notice content |
| **5. Body** | The main message — clear, concise, factual (5W1H: Who, What, When, Where, Why, How) |
| **6. Action Required** | What the recipients must do in response |
| **7. Issuing Authority** | Name, designation, and signature of the person issuing the notice |

#### ✅ Sample Notice:

```
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

academic activities will remain suspended on 21st February 2026 in

Shaheed Minar.

Registrar
Gopalganj Textile Engineering College
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
```

#### 🎯 Importance of Notice in Business Communication

1. **Official Announcement:** Notices serve as **formal, documented records** of information — legally binding in many contexts (e.g., AGM notices, termination notices).

2. **Mass Communication Efficiency:** A single notice can **simultaneously inform** hundreds of employees, saving time compared to individual conversations.

3. **Transparency & Accountability:** Posting notices promotes **organizational transparency** — everyone receives the same information at the same time, reducing rumors.

4. **Legal Compliance:** Many business and legal processes **require formal notice** (e.g., shareholder meetings, policy changes, employment terms) by law.

5. **Record & Reference:** Notices are **archived for future reference**, providing evidence that information was communicated on a specific date.

6. **Policy Enforcement:** Notices are used to **communicate new rules, procedures, and policies** across departments consistently.

7. **Professional Image:** A well-structured notice reflects the **professionalism and organizational culture** of a company.

### Q(c). What is **Interpersonal Communication**? Show the difference between Intrapersonal and Interpersonal Communication.

#### 📌 What is Interpersonal Communication?

**Interpersonal communication** is the process of **exchanging information, feelings, meanings, and ideas between two or more people** through verbal (spoken/written) and non-verbal (gestures, expressions, body language) channels.

It is *interactive*, *relational*, and *transactional* — meaning both parties simultaneously send and receive messages and influence each other.

**Examples:**
- A manager discussing a project with a team member (face-to-face)
- Sending a professional email to a client
- A job interview
- A phone call between colleagues
- Group discussion in a meeting

#### Types of Interpersonal Communication:
1. **Dyadic** — between two people (one-on-one)
2. **Small Group** — among 3–12 people (team meetings)
3. **Public Communication** — one person to a large audience

#### Key Elements:
- **Sender** — initiates the message
- **Receiver** — receives and decodes the message
- **Message** — the content being communicated
- **Channel** — medium used (voice, text, phone, email)
- **Feedback** — response from receiver
- **Noise** — anything that disrupts communication

---

#### 📊 Difference Between Intrapersonal and Interpersonal Communication

| Feature | **Intrapersonal Communication** | **Interpersonal Communication** |
|---------|--------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Definition** | Communication *within oneself* (internal dialogue) | Communication *between two or more people* |
| **Number of People** | Only one person (the self) | Two or more people |
| **Nature** | Internal, private, personal | External, social, relational |
| **Medium** | Thoughts, self-talk, inner reflection, dreams | Speech, writing, gestures, expressions |
| **Feedback** | Self-generated (inner reflection) | Comes from the other person(s) |
| **Examples** | Thinking, daydreaming, meditation, journaling, self-evaluation | Conversation, meeting, interview, debate, email |
| **Purpose** | Self-understanding, decision making, emotional processing | Sharing information, building relationships, influencing others |
| **Visibility** | Cannot be observed by others | Observable and measurable |
| **Noise** | Internal (anxiety, biases, emotions) | External (physical noise) and internal both |
| **Channel** | Neural/cognitive (brain processes) | Verbal and non-verbal channels |
| **Business Relevance** | Self-motivation, stress management, planning | Negotiation, teamwork, leadership, conflict resolution |

> 💡 **Key Insight:** Intrapersonal communication is the **foundation** of interpersonal communication. How well you communicate with others often depends on how clearly you think and process information within yourself.

### Q(d). What is **Language Function**? Distinguish between Written Language and Spoken Language.

#### 📌 What is Language Function?

**Language function** refers to the **purpose or use** for which language is employed in a specific context of communication. It describes *what we do with language* — the intention or goal behind our words.

The British linguist **Michael Halliday** (1975) proposed seven key functions of language:

| # | Function | Description | Example |
|---|----------|-------------|---------|
| 1 | **Instrumental** | To get things done, express needs | "Please send me the report." |
| 2 | **Regulatory** | To control behavior of others | "You must follow the dress code." |
| 3 | **Interactional** | To establish/maintain social relationships | "How are you?" |
| 4 | **Personal** | To express feelings, opinions, identity | "I believe we should change the policy." |
| 5 | **Heuristic** | To explore and learn ("tell me why") | "Why did the production rate drop?" |
| 6 | **Imaginative** | To create fictional worlds, stories, humor | Writing a business case narrative |
| 7 | **Representational/Informative** | To convey facts and information | "The factory produced 500 units today." |

> In **business communication**, the most commonly used functions are **Instrumental, Regulatory, Interactional, and Representational.**

---

#### 📊 Difference Between Written Language and Spoken Language

| Feature | **Written Language** | **Spoken Language** |
|---------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Medium** | Uses written symbols (letters, text) on paper/screen | Uses sound waves; oral and auditory |
| **Permanence** | **Permanent** — can be stored, retrieved, referenced | **Temporary** — disappears unless recorded |
| **Feedback** | **Delayed** — response comes later | **Immediate** — instant feedback possible |
| **Planning** | Carefully **planned and edited** before delivery | Often **spontaneous**, less structured |
| **Grammar & Syntax** | Follows strict grammar rules; complete sentences | Can use incomplete sentences, contractions, slang |
| **Vocabulary** | Formal, precise, elaborate vocabulary | Informal, simpler vocabulary common |
| **Non-verbal Cues** | Absent (uses punctuation, formatting to compensate) | Rich in **tone, pitch, speed, gestures, facial expressions** |
| **Revision** | Can be **revised and corrected** before sending | Cannot be "unsaid" (though can be corrected mid-speech) |
| **Audience** | **Absent** — writer cannot see reader's reaction | **Present** (usually) — speaker sees listener's response |
| **Examples** | Letters, emails, reports, notices, memos, contracts | Conversations, meetings, presentations, phone calls |
| **Formality** | Generally more formal | Can range from very formal (speech) to very informal (chat) |
| **Legal Standing** | Usually has **stronger legal validity** (signed documents) | Oral agreements are harder to prove legally |
| **Dialect/Accent** | Not directly expressed | Strongly influenced by dialect and accent |
| **Business Use** | Contracts, reports, official correspondence | Negotiations, meetings, presentations, interviews |

> ✏️ **Note:** In modern business, the boundary is blurring — text messages and emails often adopt spoken language patterns, while formal speeches use carefully scripted written language.

### Q1(b). What is **Intrapersonal Communication**? Does it help to improve our condition in an institution? `[5 marks]`

#### 📌 What is Intrapersonal Communication?

**Intrapersonal communication** is the internal process of **communicating with oneself** — the constant stream of thoughts, feelings, beliefs, self-talk, and reflection occurring within a person's mind. It is the **most fundamental form of communication** from which all other forms emerge.

It includes:
- 💭 **Inner monologue / Self-talk** (positive or negative)
- 🔍 **Self-reflection and introspection**
- 🎯 **Goal setting and personal planning**
- 😌 **Emotional regulation and management**
- 🧠 **Problem-solving and decision making**
- 📓 **Journaling and personal writing**
- 🧘 **Meditation and mindfulness**

#### How Intrapersonal Communication Works:

```
[Experience / External Event]
        ↓
[Perception — how you interpret it]
        ↓
[Inner Dialogue — what you tell yourself about it]
        ↓
[Emotion — how it makes you feel]
        ↓
[Decision / Response — what you choose to do]
        ↓
[Action — interpersonal or behavioral outcome]
```

---

#### ✅ Does Intrapersonal Communication Help Improve Our Condition in an Institution?

**Yes, absolutely.** Here is how:

**1. Enhances Self-Awareness:**
Employees and students who practice intrapersonal communication regularly become more aware of their strengths, weaknesses, values, and behavioral patterns. This self-knowledge allows them to work on areas needing improvement — directly benefiting the institution.

**2. Improves Decision Making:**
Before communicating with others, people who engage in thoughtful inner dialogue make **better, more rational decisions**, reducing impulsive or poor choices in institutional settings (e.g., before confronting a colleague, a manager reflects internally — leading to calmer, more productive outcomes).

**3. Boosts Emotional Intelligence (EQ):**
Intrapersonal communication is the foundation of emotional regulation. Employees with high EQ create **more positive and productive work environments**, reduce conflicts, and collaborate more effectively.

**4. Increases Motivation and Goal Focus:**
Positive self-talk and personal goal-setting (forms of intrapersonal communication) drive **individual motivation and productivity**. A motivated workforce directly improves institutional performance.

**5. Reduces Miscommunication:**
A person who clearly understands their own intentions through internal reflection is **less likely to send confusing or contradictory messages** to others — improving overall communication quality in the institution.

**6. Stress Management:**
Institutions benefit when employees can internally manage workplace stress through mindfulness, journaling, and self-talk — reducing absenteeism, burnout, and turnover.

> 📣 **Conclusion:** Intrapersonal communication is the "silent engine" that powers interpersonal effectiveness. When individuals within an institution are mentally aware, emotionally regulated, and self-directed, the institution as a whole operates more harmoniously and productively.

### Q2(a). What is **Noise in Communication**? Discuss the main causes of noise in communication and the ways of reducing them. `[5 marks]`

#### 📌 What is Noise in Communication?

In communication theory, **noise** (also called *interference* or *barrier*) refers to **any factor that distorts, disrupts, or interferes with the accurate transmission or reception of a message**, preventing it from being fully understood as the sender intended.

The concept was first formalized by **Shannon and Weaver** in their **Mathematical Theory of Communication (1949)**, where noise was defined as any signal that distorts the intended message in the communication channel.

> 📢 **Key Point:** Noise doesn't just mean sound — it is any barrier that reduces communication effectiveness.

#### 🔊 Types and Main Causes of Noise in Communication

**1. Physical / External Noise**

Disturbances in the external environment.

| Cause | Example |
|-------|---------|
| Loud sounds | Traffic, machinery, construction near office |
| Poor infrastructure | Bad telephone line, slow internet, poor lighting |
| Uncomfortable environment | Extreme heat/cold, crowded room |
| Technical failures | Microphone malfunction during presentation |

**2. Psychological / Internal Noise**

Mental and emotional interference within the sender or receiver.

| Cause | Example |
|-------|---------|
| Preconceptions & biases | Dismissing a junior employee's idea without consideration |
| Emotional state | Sending an angry email without thinking |
| Stress and anxiety | A nervous job candidate missing key questions |
| Selective perception | Hearing only what we want to hear |
| Lack of attention/interest | Daydreaming during a meeting |

**3. Semantic / Language Noise**

Problems with words, meaning, or language used.

| Cause | Example |
|-------|---------|
| Jargon | Using technical textile terms with non-technical clients |
| Ambiguous language | "Handle this ASAP" — what is "ASAP" exactly? |
| Different connotations | "Cheap" means affordable to one, inferior to another |
| Language barrier | Communicating in a language not mastered by receiver |
| Poor grammar | Unclear or misleading sentences |

**4. Organizational / Structural Noise**

Problems arising from the structure of an organization.

| Cause | Example |
|-------|---------|
| Too many levels of hierarchy | Message distorted passing through many levels |
| Information overload | Receiving too many emails — important ones missed |
| Grapevine / rumors | Informal channels spreading wrong information |
| Filtering | Managers withholding/altering information |

**5. Cultural Noise**

Differences in cultural backgrounds, norms, and values.

| Cause | Example |
|-------|---------|
| Different norms | Direct communication (Western) vs. indirect (Asian) cultures |
| Non-verbal misinterpretation | A thumbs-up means different things in different cultures |
| Ethnocentrism | Assuming one's own cultural communication style is universal |

---

#### 🛠️ Ways to Reduce Noise in Communication

| Type of Noise | Solutions |
|---------------|-----------|
| **Physical** | Choose quiet meeting environments; use good quality equipment; improve infrastructure |
| **Psychological** | Practice active listening; be aware of biases; regulate emotions before communicating |
| **Semantic** | Use simple, clear language; define technical terms; confirm understanding |
| **Organizational** | Simplify communication channels; reduce unnecessary hierarchy; encourage open communication |
| **Cultural** | Promote cultural awareness training; use neutral, inclusive language; be patient |

**General Strategies:**
- ✅ **Use feedback** — always confirm the message was understood
- ✅ **Choose the right channel** — important messages deserve face-to-face, not just a text
- ✅ **Be concise and clear** — avoid ambiguity and unnecessary complexity
- ✅ **Repeat key information** — especially in oral communication
- ✅ **Practice empathy** — try to understand the receiver's perspective

### Q2(b). Draw a **Standard Communication Cycle** and describe its different components `[5 marks]`

#### 📌 The Standard Communication Cycle (Model)

The most widely accepted model is the **Shannon-Weaver Model (1949)**, later refined by **Schramm (1954)** to include feedback, creating what is commonly called the **Circular/Transactional Communication Model.**

#### 🔄 Diagram of the Communication Cycle:

```
                    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
                    │       COMMUNICATION CYCLE               │
                    └─────────────────────────────────────────┘

 ┌──────────┐    ┌──────────┐    ┌──────────┐    ┌──────────┐    ┌──────────┐
 │          │    │          │    │          │    │          │    │          │
 │  SENDER  │───▶│ ENCODING │───▶│ CHANNEL  │───▶│ DECODING │───▶│RECEIVER  │
 │(Source)  │    │(Message  │    │(Medium)  │    │(Message  │    │(Audience)│
 │          │    │ formed)  │    │          │    │ received)│    │          │
 └──────────┘    └──────────┘    └──────────┘    └──────────┘    └──────────┘
       ▲                               ▲                               │
       │                               │                               │
       │                           NOISE                               │
       │                        (Interference)                         │
       │                                                               │
       │                        FEEDBACK                               │
       └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

---

#### 📦 Components of the Communication Cycle (Explained)

**1. 📤 Sender (Source)**
- The person or entity who **initiates the communication**
- Has an idea, thought, feeling, or information to convey
- Responsible for choosing appropriate language, tone, and channel
- *Example: A factory manager who wants to inform workers about a shift change*

**2. ✍️ Encoding**
- The process of **converting the idea/thought into a communicable format** (words, symbols, images, gestures)
- Influenced by the sender's knowledge, culture, communication skills, and emotional state
- *Example: The manager writes a notice or speaks the message in clear Bengali/English*

**3. 📡 Channel (Medium)**
- The **route or medium through which the encoded message travels** from sender to receiver
- Types of channels:
  - **Verbal:** Face-to-face, telephone, radio, TV
  - **Written:** Email, letter, memo, notice, report
  - **Non-verbal:** Body language, facial expressions, visuals
- *Example: The manager posts a written notice on the bulletin board*

**4. 🔊 Noise (Interference)**
- Any **distortion or barrier** that affects the clarity of the message during transmission
- Can be physical, psychological, semantic, or cultural (see Q2a above)
- *Example: Workers can't read the notice because it's in English and they prefer Bengali*

**5. 📥 Decoding**
- The process by which the **receiver interprets and makes sense of the message**
- Success depends on the receiver's knowledge, language proficiency, attention, and cultural background
- *Example: Workers read the notice and understand the new shift timings*

**6. 👤 Receiver (Audience)**
- The person or group **for whom the message is intended**
- Actively decodes the message based on their own frame of reference
- *Example: Factory floor workers who receive the shift-change notification*

**7. 🔁 Feedback**
- The **receiver's response** to the sender's message — this completes the communication cycle
- Feedback shows whether the message was received, understood, and accepted
- Can be **verbal** (reply, question), **non-verbal** (nod, confused look), or **written** (reply email)
- Transforms communication from one-way (linear) to **two-way (interactive/circular)**
- *Example: Workers acknowledge the notice by signing an acknowledgment sheet, or ask the manager for clarification*

---

#### 🔁 Why is it Called a "Cycle"?

It is a **cycle** because communication does not end when the receiver gets the message. The receiver becomes a new sender when they give feedback, the original sender now becomes a receiver, and the whole process repeats — continuously, dynamically.

```
Sender → Encodes → Channel → Decodes → Receiver
   ↑                                        │
   └──────────── Feedback ──────────────────┘
              (Receiver becomes Sender)
```

> 📚 **Models to Know:** Shannon-Weaver (1949), Schramm (1954), Berlo's SMCR Model (1960), Transactional Model (Barnlund, 1970)
