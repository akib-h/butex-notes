### Q1. Why is silk known as **"the Queen of All Fibers"**? `[2 marks]`

Silk is universally referred to as the **"Queen of All Fibers"** for the following reasons:

- **Natural Luster & Beauty:** The silk filament has a triangular, prism-like cross-section that refracts incoming light at various angles, producing a unique, iridescent shimmer unmatched by any other natural fiber.
- **Exceptional Strength:** Despite being extremely fine (9–11 µm diameter), silk has a tensile strength of about **4.5 g/den**, making it stronger than steel wire of the same diameter by weight.
- **Smooth & Soft Texture:** Its fine, smooth surface gives it an incomparably luxurious feel against the skin.
- **Moisture Absorption:** Silk can absorb up to **11% of its weight** in moisture without feeling damp, making it comfortable in both warm and cool conditions.
- **Lightweight & Drapability:** Its low density (~1.34 g/cm³) and flexibility give garments exceptional drape and elegance.
- **Hypoallergenic & Biocompatible:** Silk fibroin is protein-based and gentle on sensitive skin; it is even used in medical sutures.
- **Historical & Cultural Prestige:** For millennia, silk was reserved for royalty and nobility across China, Persia, Rome, and the rest of the world — cementing its "royal" status.

> 💡 **Summary:** A combination of unparalleled natural beauty, superior mechanical properties, comfort, and a centuries-old association with luxury earns silk its title as the Queen of All Fibers.

### Q2. Define: **Sericulture** and **Degumming** `[2 marks]`

#### 🐛 Sericulture
**Sericulture** is the science and practice of **cultivating silkworms** (*Bombyx mori*) for the commercial production of raw silk. It involves:
1. Cultivation of mulberry trees (*Morus alba*) as silkworm food
2. Rearing silkworms from egg → larva → pupa stage
3. Harvesting cocoons before the adult moth emerges
4. Processing cocoons to extract continuous silk filament (reeling)

> The word derives from Latin *Sericum* (silk) + *cultura* (cultivation). Sericulture originated in China around **2700 BCE**.

#### 🧼 Degumming
**Degumming** is the process of **removing sericin** (the natural gummy protein coating) from raw silk filaments to expose the lustrous **fibroin** core underneath.

- **Method:** Raw silk is boiled in a mildly alkaline soap/water solution (e.g., Marseille soap, sodium carbonate) at ~90–100°C for 1–2 hours.
- **Weight Loss:** Degumming causes a **20–25% weight loss** in silk.
- **Result:** Silk becomes softer, smoother, more lustrous, and gains its characteristic sheen.
- **Weighted Silk (Fraud):** After degumming, metallic salts (tin, iron) are sometimes added back to restore lost weight — this weakens the fiber over time.

### Q3. List the **five amino acids** available in silk `[2 marks]`

Silk fibroin is a protein composed of **~18 amino acids**. The **five most abundant** are:

| # | Amino Acid | Approximate Content (%) |
|---|-----------|------------------------|
| 1 | **Glycine** (Gly) | ~44.5% |
| 2 | **Alanine** (Ala) | ~29.0% |
| 3 | **Serine** (Ser) | ~12.0% |
| 4 | **Tyrosine** (Tyr) | ~5.2% |
| 5 | **Valine** (Val) | ~2.4% |

> 📌 **Note:** These five amino acids together account for over **90%** of silk fibroin's composition. The repeating unit of silk fibroin is primarily: **(-Gly-Ala-Gly-Ala-Gly-Ser-)n**, giving silk its characteristic beta-sheet crystalline structure that imparts high strength and low extensibility.

Other minor amino acids present include: Glutamic acid, Aspartic acid, Leucine, Isoleucine, Arginine, and Phenylalanine.

### Q4. Which place is called **"the Silk City of Bangladesh"** & why? `[2 marks]`

**Rajshahi** is called the **"Silk City of Bangladesh"** (also known as *Resham Shahar*).

#### Why Rajshahi?

- **Historical Tradition:** Rajshahi has been the epicenter of silk production and weaving in Bengal for **several centuries**, particularly known since the Mughal period.
- **Mulberry Cultivation:** The fertile land and favorable climate of the Rajshahi region support large-scale cultivation of mulberry trees (*Morus alba*), the primary food source for silkworms.
- **Sericulture Hub:** The Bangladesh Sericulture Research and Training Institute (BSRTI) is located in Rajshahi, which leads R&D and training for silk production nationwide.
- **Famous Rajshahi Silk:** "Rajshahi Silk" fabric (especially *Garad* and *Murshidabad* varieties) is internationally recognized for its fine quality, lustrous appearance, and intricate designs.
- **Economic Importance:** The silk industry employs thousands of weavers and workers in the Rajshahi division, making it the backbone of the local economy.

> 🌐 Chapai Nawabganj (adjacent district) also contributes significantly to silk production in the region.

### Q5. Write down the **chemical composition of silk** `[2 marks]`

#### A. Compositional (by protein fraction):

| Component | Percentage |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Fibroin** (structural protein — the actual fiber) | 70–75% |
| **Sericin** (gum protein — the outer coating) | 20–25% |
| **Wax** | ~1.5% |
| **Mineral matter / Ash** | ~1.0% |
| **Fat & Oil** | ~0.5% |
| **Coloring matter** | Trace |

#### B. Elemental composition of Silk Fibroin:

| Element | Percentage |
|---------|-----------|
| **Carbon (C)** | ~49.0% |
| **Hydrogen (H)** | ~6.5% |
| **Nitrogen (N)** | ~17.0% |
| **Oxygen (O)** | ~27.5% |
| **Sulfur (S)** | Trace (~0.5%) |

> 💡 The **absence of significant sulfur** (unlike wool/keratin) means silk lacks disulfide bonds, contributing to its lower elasticity compared to wool but greater chemical stability in mild acids.

### Q1. Write down the **composition of keratin** `[2 marks]`

**Keratin** is the fibrous structural protein that forms the basis of **wool fiber** (and also hair, nails, feathers, and horns in animals).

#### A. Elemental Composition of Keratin:

| Element | Percentage |
|---------|-----------|
| **Carbon (C)** | 49–50% |
| **Hydrogen (H)** | 6–7% |
| **Nitrogen (N)** | 15–17% |
| **Oxygen (O)** | 21–24% |
| **Sulfur (S)** | **2–5%** *(key distinguishing element)* |

#### B. Amino Acid Composition:
Keratin contains **~18 amino acids**, the most significant being:

| Amino Acid | Significance |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Cystine** | Forms **disulfide (S–S) bonds** — responsible for wool's elasticity and crimp |
| Glutamic acid | Most abundant by percentage |
| Arginine | Key structural role |
| Serine | Common in soft keratin |
| Glycine | Structural component |

> 🔑 **Key fact:** The high sulfur content (from cystine) distinguishes keratin from silk fibroin and is responsible for wool's unique properties (elasticity, crimp, and reactivity to chemicals).

### Q2. Which property is responsible for the **elasticity of wool fiber**? What is **wool carbonizing**? `[2 marks]`

#### Elasticity of Wool:

The elasticity of wool fiber is primarily due to **two interconnected factors**:

1. **α-Helical Structure (Coiled-Coil):** The keratin protein chains in wool exist in an **alpha (α) helical** conformation — like a coiled spring. When stretched, the helix uncoils; when released, it recoils back. This gives wool up to **30–40% stretch recovery** before permanent deformation.

2. **Disulfide (S–S) Cross-links:** Cystine amino acid residues form **disulfide bonds** between adjacent protein chains (cross-links). These act as molecular springs, storing energy during stretching and releasing it on recovery.
   - Additional cross-links include: **hydrogen bonds**, **salt linkages (ionic bonds)**, and **hydrophobic interactions**.

> Wool can be stretched up to **30% of its length** and still return to its original length, making it far more elastic than cotton or silk.

#### Wool Carbonizing:

**Carbonizing** (also spelled *carbonising*) is a chemical process used to **remove vegetable impurities** (grass, seeds, burrs, twigs) from raw wool without damaging the protein fiber.

**Process Steps:**
1. Raw wool containing vegetable matter is **immersed in dilute sulfuric acid** (H₂SO₄, ~5–7%) or exposed to **hydrochloric acid (HCl) gas**.
2. The acid degrades (hydrolyses) the **cellulosic** vegetable material (cellulose is acid-sensitive), turning it into brittle, charred carbon.
3. The wool is then **dried and baked** at ~110–150°C (the acid concentrates as water evaporates, accelerating cellulose destruction).
4. Wool is then **crushed** and passed through rollers/beaters — the carbonized plant matter crumbles to dust and is removed by suction/beaters.
5. Finally, wool is **neutralized** (with ammonia or sodium bicarbonate) and washed.

> 💡 Wool (keratin/protein) is resistant to dilute acids, while cellulose (plant matter) is destroyed — this selectivity is the basis of carbonizing.

### Q3. Mention the **classification of wool by fleece** `[2 marks]`

Wool can be classified by **fleece type** (based on fiber diameter, staple length, and breed characteristics):

| Classification | Fiber Diameter | Breed Examples | Characteristics |
|---------------|---------------|----------------|-----------------|
| **1. Fine Wool (Merino type)** | <25 µm | Merino, Rambouillet | Softest, finest; used in luxury apparel |
| **2. Medium Wool (Down type)** | 25–35 µm | Dorset, Shropshire, Southdown | Good crimp; used in knitting yarns |
| **3. Long/Luster Wool** | 35–45 µm | Lincoln, Leicester, Cotswold | Long staple; high luster; used in worsted |
| **4. Cross-bred Wool** | 25–35 µm | Corriedale, Columbia | Between fine & long; versatile |
| **5. Carpet Wool (Coarse)** | >35–40 µm | Scottish Blackface, Cheviot | Coarse, strong; used in carpets/rugs |
| **6. Double-coated Wool** | Mixed | Karakul, Icelandic | Has both fine undercoat & coarse outer fiber |

> 🐑 The finest wool in the world is **Merino** (sometimes as fine as 14–15 µm), while the coarsest carpet wools can exceed 70 µm.

### Q5. Describe the **dew retting of jute** `[2 marks]`

**Retting** is the process of separating bast fibers from the surrounding woody stem through microbial action. **Dew retting** is one of the methods.

#### Dew Retting — Definition:
Dew retting is a method where **cut jute stalks are spread on grassy fields or meadows** and exposed to alternating **dew, rain, sun, and air** over several weeks.

#### Process:
1. Harvested jute plants are cut and spread in **thin layers on open fields**.
2. **Morning dew, rain, and atmospheric moisture** keep the stalks moist.
3. **Naturally occurring microorganisms** (bacteria and fungi — primarily *Clostridium* sp., *Bacillus* sp.) present in soil and moisture colonize the stalks.
4. These microbes produce **pectinase enzymes** that break down the **pectin and hemicellulose** binding the bast fibers to the woody core (*xylem*).
5. The process takes approximately **2–5 weeks** (longer than water retting).
6. After retting, fibers are **stripped, washed, and dried**.

#### Advantages of Dew Retting:
- ✅ **Environmentally friendly** — no polluted wastewater compared to water retting
- ✅ Suitable for water-scarce regions
- ✅ Produces **brighter, lighter-colored** fiber (less bacterial discoloration)

#### Disadvantages:
- ❌ **Slower** than water retting (takes longer)
- ❌ **Uneven retting** due to variable weather conditions
- ❌ Requires large open field space
- ❌ Fiber quality can vary; risk of **over-retting** causing damage

> 🌿 **Note:** Water retting is still the most common method in Bangladesh, while dew retting is more commonly practiced in Europe for flax. However, due to environmental concerns, dew retting and enzymatic retting are gaining renewed interest.
