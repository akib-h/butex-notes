## 1. What is Flax?

1. **Flax** is a **natural bast fibre** obtained from the inner bark (phloem) of the stem of the flax plant, *Linum usitatissimum*.
2. The name *Linum usitatissimum* is Latin for **"most useful"** — reflecting the plant's extraordinary versatility as a source of both fibre and oil.
3. The textile fibre derived from flax is commercially known as **linen**, which is one of the oldest and most valued textile materials in the world.
4. Flax belongs to the family **Linaceae** and is one of approximately **298 species** within the genus *Linum*, though *L. usitatissimum* is by far the most cultivated.
5. Unlike cotton (a seed fibre) or wool (an animal fibre), flax is a **multi-cellular** fibre — meaning each "fibre unit" is actually a bundle of many individual elongated plant cells bound together by pectic substances.

> **Example:** When you hold a linen shirt, the fibres you feel are not single cells but rather bundles of 10–40 spindle-shaped cells, each 2–3 cm long, which together form a fibre strand up to 100 cm in length.

## 2. Historical Background

1. Flax has the **longest documented textile history** of any plant fibre, with evidence of its use dating back approximately **30,000 years** in the Republic of Georgia (Nahal Hemar cave site).
2. Woven linen fabrics have been found in **ancient Egyptian tombs** (circa 5,000–3,000 BC); Egyptian mummies were wrapped in fine linen cloth.
3. *Linum angustifolium*, the **wild ancestor** of modern flax, is native to the region stretching from the Black Sea to the Canary Islands.
4. Flax cultivation spread from **Mesopotamia and Egypt** to the rest of the ancient world, reaching Europe through Roman trade routes.
5. By the **16th century**, laws in England required that a quarter-acre of flax be planted for every 60 acres of cultivated land — demonstrating its economic importance.
6. Linen was the **dominant textile material in Europe** from the Middle Ages until the early 19th century, when cotton began to challenge its position.
7. The **Industrial Revolution** mechanized flax processing (wet spinning was invented around 1825), making linen more affordable and widely available.
8. In the **20th century**, synthetic fibres gradually displaced linen for many uses, but renewed interest in sustainable and natural materials has led to a **revival of flax research** and production.
9. The art of producing linen from flax was known as early as **2500 BC**, but industrial-scale production was introduced in the **12th century**.

> **Example:** The Turin Shroud, one of the most studied textiles in history, is a piece of woven linen fabric, demonstrating flax's ancient religious and cultural significance.

## 3. Botanical Classification

1. **Kingdom:** Plantae
2. **Division:** Magnoliophyta (Angiosperms / Flowering plants)
3. **Class:** Magnoliopsida (Dicotyledons)
4. **Order:** Malpighiales
5. **Family:** Linaceae
6. **Genus:** *Linum*
7. **Species:** *Linum usitatissimum* L.
8. **Common Names:** Flax (fibre), Linseed (oil crop)

### Plant Morphology at a Glance

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Plant type | Annual herb |
| Height | 60–120 cm (fibre varieties tend taller) |
| Stem | Slender, erect, branching only near top |
| Leaves | Narrow, lanceolate, alternate |
| Flowers | 5-petalled; blue (*vulgare*) or white (*album*) |
| Root | Taproot system, shallow |
| Seeds | Small, flat, oval; rich in omega-3 fatty acids |
| Growing season | ~100 days from sowing to harvest |

> **Example:** Fibre varieties of flax (*L. usitatissimum* var. *vulgare*) are bred to be tall and unbranched, maximizing the length and quality of the bast fibres. Oil varieties (*L. usitatissimum* var. *humile*) are shorter and more branched, optimizing seed yield.

## 4. Types of Flax

1. **Textile Flax (Fibre Flax):**
   - Cultivated primarily for bast fibre production.
   - Plants are tall (90–120 cm), slender, with minimal branching.
   - Sown densely to discourage branching and promote long, straight stems.
   - *Example:* Varieties like 'Bolchoï', 'Escalina', 'Electra', commonly grown in France and Belgium.

2. **Oilseed Flax (Linseed):**
   - Cultivated for seeds rich in linseed oil (omega-3 fatty acids).
   - Plants are shorter (45–60 cm), more branched, with higher seed yield.
   - Grown primarily in Canada, the United States, Russia, and India.
   - *Example:* Varieties like 'Noralta', 'Bethune', grown extensively in Saskatchewan, Canada.

3. **Dual-Purpose Flax:**
   - Cultivated for both fibre and oil production.
   - Quality of both outputs may be slightly lower than specialised varieties.
   - Economic compromise crop used in some regions.
   - *Example:* Growing oleaginous varieties and extracting bast fibres as a secondary process to produce paper or composite reinforcements.

## 5. Fibre Classification

1. **By origin:** Natural fibre → Vegetable fibre → Bast fibre
2. **By chemical composition:** Cellulosic (primary component is cellulose)
3. **By cell structure:** Multi-cellular (each technical fibre consists of multiple individual cells)
4. **By length:** Staple fibre (definite, measurable length, unlike silk which is a continuous filament)
5. **Commercial classification:**
   - **Line (Long Fibre):** Longer, finer, parallel fibres obtained after combing/hackling; used for fine linen yarns.
   - **Tow (Short Fibre):** Shorter, tangled fibres and broken pieces removed during hackling; used for coarser yarns, canvas, rope.

> **Example:** A fine Irish linen bedsheet is made from *line* flax fibres (long, parallel, combed), whereas burlap-like linen canvas is made from *tow* fibres.

---

## 6. Why Study Flax?

1. **Sustainability:** Flax requires minimal irrigation, pesticides, and fertilizers compared to cotton. Every part of the plant is usable (fibre, oil, shive, seeds), making it a zero-waste crop.
2. **Mechanical performance:** Flax fibres have a specific tensile modulus comparable to glass fibre, at a fraction of the weight and environmental cost.
3. **Comfort and health:** Linen fabric has superior moisture management, antibacterial properties, and UV resistance.
4. **Industrial relevance:** Flax is increasingly used in automotive composite panels, aerospace components, biomedical materials, and geotextiles.
5. **Carbon sequestration:** Flax sequesters CO₂ during growth, contributing to climate change mitigation.
