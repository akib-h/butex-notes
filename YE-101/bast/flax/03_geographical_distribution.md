## 1. Global Overview

1. Flax (*Linum usitatissimum*) is cultivated in **over 50 countries** worldwide, on every inhabited continent except Antarctica.
2. Global flax fibre production represents approximately **1% of world plant fibre production**, estimated at **200,000–240,000 tonnes per year** of scutched fibre.
3. There is a sharp geographic split between **fibre flax** (grown primarily in Europe) and **linseed/oilseed flax** (grown primarily in North America, India, China, and Russia).
4. **Western Europe** dominates quality textile flax production, accounting for roughly **80% of the world's scutched bast fibre**.
5. **China** is the world's largest textile-clothing producer and also a major grower and processor of flax, particularly for the Asian market.
6. In 2015–2016, the total cultivated area of flax in Western Europe was approximately **96,870–104,000 hectares**, predominantly in France, Belgium, and the Netherlands.

> **Example:** A roll of premium Irish linen for shirting may contain fibres grown in France, spun in Ireland, and woven in Italy — reflecting the global, highly specialised supply chain of fine textile flax.

## 2. Major Fibre Flax Producing Countries

### 2.1 France 🇫🇷

1. France is the **world's leading producer of textile-grade flax fibre**, responsible for approximately **60–70% of global scutched fibre** production.
2. The primary growing regions are **Normandy** (Seine-Maritime, Calvados) and **Brittany** — areas with deep loamy soils, adequate rainfall, and mild temperatures ideal for fibre flax.
3. France hosts the majority of scutching mills and is the global reference point for flax fibre quality.
4. French flax is highly prized for its fineness, lustre, and consistent quality.

### 2.2 Belgium 🇧🇪

1. Belgium, particularly the **Flanders region**, has an unparalleled historical reputation for flax processing.
2. Belgian flax (especially water-retted Belgian linen from the Lys River valley) was historically considered the world's finest.
3. Belgium remains a significant producer and home to many **scutching mills and spinning facilities**.
4. The town of **Ghent** was historically a major centre of the linen trade.

### 2.3 Netherlands 🇳🇱

1. The Netherlands cultivates significant quantities of fibre flax, particularly in the **Zeeland** and **Flevoland** provinces.
2. The Dutch contribute substantially to European fibre production and play a major role in the **linen seed production** supply chain.

### 2.4 Russia & Belarus 🇷🇺 🇧🇾

1. Russia and Belarus were historically the **largest flax producers globally** — in the Soviet era, the USSR accounted for the majority of world fibre flax production.
2. Cultivation areas declined significantly after the fall of the Soviet Union, but both countries remain substantial producers.
3. Russian flax is generally coarser than Western European flax and is primarily used for **industrial and technical textile applications**.
4. **Belarus** has maintained and modernised its flax industry and exports significant quantities of scutched fibre.

### 2.5 China 🇨🇳

1. China is both a major grower and processor of flax, with large cultivation areas in **Heilongjiang, Inner Mongolia, and Gansu** provinces.
2. China has used flax for several thousand years and is a major manufacturer of linen fabric for both domestic consumption and export.
3. China is the **world leader in textile-clothing production**, accounting for 22–24% of global output, including much of the world's linen fabric manufacturing.

### 2.6 Egypt 🇪🇬

1. Egypt has one of the oldest flax-growing traditions in the world, dating back to ancient pharaonic civilisations.
2. Egyptian flax is cultivated in the **Nile Delta** region, benefiting from rich alluvial soils and warm, dry conditions.
3. Egyptian linen has a long-standing reputation for quality, particularly in the production of **fine linen for luxury household textiles**.

## 3. Major Linseed (Oil) Flax Producing Countries

1. **Canada:** The world's largest linseed producer since the 1990s, with the majority of production in **Saskatchewan, Manitoba, and Alberta**. Canada exports more than 90% of its flax to Europe, the United States, and Japan.
2. **Kazakhstan:** Major central Asian producer of linseed.
3. **Russia:** Both fibre and linseed varieties are grown.
4. **China:** Large-scale linseed production alongside fibre flax.
5. **India:** Major linseed producer; flax straw from linseed varieties is increasingly being processed for composite and industrial fibre.
6. **USA:** North and South Dakota, Montana, and Minnesota are the primary linseed-producing states.

> **Note:** Linseed flax in Canada and the USA is grown almost exclusively for **oil and seed** (human nutrition, industrial coatings), with fibre extraction being a secondary or by-product industry.

## 4. Historical Centres of Flax Production

1. **Ancient Egypt (5000–3000 BC):** The birthplace of high-quality linen production; Egyptian mummies were wrapped in finely woven linen.
2. **Mesopotamia (5000 BC):** Among the earliest archaeological evidence of flax cultivation for both fibre and oil.
3. **Flanders (Belgium), 13th–18th century:** The global centre of fine linen trade; Ghent and Bruges were the commercial hubs of the linen industry.
4. **Ireland, 17th–20th century:** Belfast became the "Linen Capital of the World" during the Industrial Revolution, with mechanised wet spinning mills producing the finest linen yarn.
5. **Russia (18th–20th century):** The dominant supplier of industrial flax fibre to the world until the Soviet era's decline.

> **Example:** "Belfast linen" and "Irish linen" remain internationally recognised quality marks for fine linen, even though Ireland today grows relatively little flax of its own — most fibre is sourced from France and Belgium and spun and woven in Ireland.

## 5. Global Trade & Market Share

1. The global linen market was valued at approximately **USD 1.2–1.5 billion** and is expected to grow at a CAGR of 5–7% through the late 2020s, driven by sustainable fashion trends.
2. **Exports:** France and Belgium dominate exports of scutched fibre and linen fabric. China dominates linen garment manufacturing.
3. **Imports:** Major importers of linen fabrics include the United States, the United Kingdom, Germany, and Japan.
4. Over **10,000 companies** in 14 European countries are involved in the European flax fibre production market.
5. Europe is responsible for **more than 80% of the world's scutched flax fibre** production.

## 6. Climate Zones Suitable for Flax

1. **Temperate oceanic climate** (Cfb in Köppen classification): Ideal — northern France, Belgium, Netherlands, Ireland, UK.
2. **Humid continental climate** (Dfb, Dfc): Suitable — Russia, Belarus, Poland, Canada.
3. **Mediterranean climate** (Csa, Csb): Possible with irrigation — Spain, parts of Egypt.
4. **Semi-arid / Steppe climate**: Suitable mainly for linseed varieties; poor for textile flax.
5. **Tropical and subtropical climates**: Generally unsuitable for textile flax due to excessive heat and humidity, though linseed may be grown in cooler highland zones.

> **Example:** Wisconsin (USA) has been identified as having climate conditions well-suited to flax cultivation for both fibre and seed, as its temperate continental climate provides adequate growing season temperatures and rainfall.
