## 1. Why Grading Matters

1. Flax fibre is a **natural agricultural product** and therefore inherently variable in quality — no two harvests are identical.
2. Grading provides a **standardised language** for buyers and sellers to agree on price and appropriate end use without physically inspecting every bale.
3. Factors affecting quality include: **variety, climate, soil, retting method, retting duration, breaking, scutching and hackling efficiency**.
4. Poor grading leads to **processing inefficiency** — using low-quality fibre in high-quality yarn production results in excessive breakages and rejections.
5. The international marketing of flax fibre has historically relied largely on **subjective (organoleptic) methods** — experienced classers assessing fibre by sight, touch, and feel — supplemented increasingly by objective instrumental tests.

> **Example:** Two bales of "scutched French flax" may look superficially similar but differ significantly in fineness, strength, and shive content. A skilled classer can distinguish Grade 1 from Grade 3 fibre within seconds by feel and visual inspection.

## 2. Commercial Classification: Line vs. Tow

The primary commercial distinction in flax fibre is between **long line fibre** and **short tow fibre**:

| Feature | Line (Long Fibre) | Tow (Short Fibre) |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Length | 25–150 cm (typically 50–90 cm) | 2–25 cm |
| Fineness | Finer, more uniform | Coarser, irregular |
| Alignment | Parallel, combed | Tangled, random |
| Origin in processing | Remains after hackling | Removed during hackling & scutching |
| Primary end use | Fine linen yarns, apparel, household textiles | Coarser yarn, canvas, rope, paper, composites |
| Commercial value | Higher | Lower |
| Spinning system | Wet spinning (ring spinning) | Dry spinning (open end, ring spinning) |

> **Example:** A fine Irish linen shirt (count ~80s) uses high-grade *line* fibre. A linen dish towel uses *line* fibre of medium grade. A flax paper used for banknotes uses *tow* fibre of specific quality.

## 3. Quality Parameters for Grading

Flax fibre is graded on the following key parameters:

### 3.1 Colour

1. The ideal colour of **scutched flax** ranges from **silver-grey to pale golden yellow**, depending on the retting method and variety.
2. **Dew-retted** fibres tend to be **grey-beige**.
3. **Water-retted** fibres tend to be **lighter grey or silver**, considered most desirable.
4. **Chemical-retted** fibres may be pale but can appear bleached.
5. Brown, dark, or reddish discolouration indicates **over-retting, mildew, or weather damage** — a defect that reduces grade.

### 3.2 Fineness (Tex or Decitex)

1. Fineness is a measure of how fine the individual fibre bundles are — the finer the fibre, the higher the quality and commercial value.
2. Measured in **tex** (mass in grams per 1000 metres of fibre) or expressed as a count (Nm — metric number).
3. Fine flax suitable for high-count linen yarn: **< 3–5 tex** (elementary fibre bundle).
4. Coarser flax suitable for canvas or rope: **> 10 tex**.

### 3.3 Strength (Tenacity)

1. Measured as **breaking force (Newtons)** or **specific strength (cN/tex)**.
2. High-quality textile flax should have a **tenacity of 6.5–8 g/denier** (approximately 58–72 cN/tex).
3. Over-retted or mechanically damaged fibres will show significantly lower strength.

### 3.4 Length

1. Longer fibres are generally more valuable — they produce stronger, more regular yarns.
2. **Premium line fibre:** 70–100 cm bundles.
3. **Standard line fibre:** 40–70 cm bundles.
4. **Short line:** 25–40 cm.
5. **Tow:** < 25 cm.

### 3.5 Shive Content (Cleanliness)

1. **Shive** = fragments of the woody core of the flax stem that contaminate the fibre bundles.
2. High shive content reduces yarn evenness and increases processing waste.
3. Assessed visually and by weight percentage: premium fibre should have **< 2–3% shive content**.

### 3.6 Uniformity & Evenness

1. Refers to how consistent the fibre bundles are in thickness, length, and alignment.
2. Highly uniform fibre produces more regular yarn with fewer breaks during spinning.

### 3.7 Moisture Content

1. Standard commercial moisture content for traded flax fibre is **12–14%** (conditioned weight basis).
2. Excessively wet fibre is heavier and can develop mould; excessively dry fibre becomes brittle.

## 4. European Grading System

European (primarily French and Belgian) flax is traded using a system of **numbered grades**:

| Grade | Description | Typical Use |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| Grade 1 (Extra / Superfine) | Finest, palest, most uniform; minimal shive; very long line | Luxury linen, damask, fine shirting, lace |
| Grade 2 (Superior) | Fine, good uniformity, low shive content | Fine apparel, household linen, sheeting |
| Grade 3 (Standard/Good) | Medium fineness, slightly more shive | Medium-weight apparel, industrial linen |
| Grade 4 (Medium) | Coarser, noticeable shive, less uniform | Canvas, towelling, industrial fabric |
| Grade 5 (Inferior/Lower) | Coarse, high shive, irregular | Rope, twine, packaging, composites |
| Tow (various sub-grades) | Short, tangled fibre from hackling/scutching | Paper, composites, coarser textiles |

> **Example:** The best grades of French scutched flax (Grade 1–2) command a market price of €2.50–€4.00 per kg, while lower grades (Grade 4–5) or tow may sell for €0.50–€1.20 per kg.

## 5. Grading by End Use

1. **Fine linen apparel and household textiles:**
   - Requires Grade 1–2 long line fibre.
   - Fineness: < 5 tex fibre bundles.
   - Colour: silver-grey or pale golden.
   - *Example:* Fine damask tablecloths, luxury shirting fabric.

2. **Industrial and technical textiles (canvas, towelling):**
   - Grades 3–4.
   - Strength more important than fineness.
   - *Example:* Linen canvas for artist painting grounds, heavy linen towelling.

3. **Paper production (currency, cigarette paper):**
   - Specific tow grades with controlled fibre length and cleanliness.
   - High alpha-cellulose content preferred.
   - *Example:* Euro and US Dollar banknotes contain a percentage of flax/linen fibre for durability and security.

4. **Composite reinforcement (automotive, aerospace):**
   - Composite industry uses grades 3–5 and tow.
   - Strength, surface quality, and chemical compatibility with resin are key.
   - *Example:* BMW i3 door panels contain flax fibre-reinforced composite panels.

5. **Medical and non-woven applications:**
   - Cottonized flax (see [10_cottonized_flax.md](./10_cottonized_flax.md)) of high purity.
   - *Example:* Medical bandages and wound dressings made from bleached flax tow.

## 6. Belgian & Irish Grading Practices

1. **Belgian grades** traditionally used a **number system from 1 to 12**, with 1 being the coarsest and 12 being the finest (inverse of some other systems).
2. **Irish/UK** grading historically used **descriptive quality terms**: Extra, Super, Fine, Good, Middling, Ordinary, Rough.
3. Both systems rely heavily on **experienced human classers** using organoleptic assessment — the same approach used for centuries.
4. Modern grading is increasingly supplemented by **objective instrumental analysis** (OFDA fibre diameter analyser, tensile testing, digital image analysis).

---

## 7. International Standards

1. **ISO 3065:1999** — Determination of fineness of flax fibre bundles.
2. **ISO 6658:1985** — Determination of moisture content of flax.
3. **ASTM Standards (Flax-PP):** ASTM International, following pressure from the composite industry, has developed standardised test methods for flax fibre including fineness, strength, length, and surface characteristics.
4. **EN 13232 (European Standard):** Covers textile fibre classification methodologies.
5. **FAO/UNIDO Standards:** International standards developed for global flax fibre trade.

> **Note:** One of the ongoing challenges in the flax industry is that **no single universally accepted international grading standard** yet exists for fibre flax comparable to the Upland Cotton grading system (USDA). This limits price transparency in international trade.
