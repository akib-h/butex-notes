## 1. Overview of Flax Stem Anatomy

The flax stem (straw) is a composite structure consisting of several distinct tissue layers, from outermost to innermost:

1. **Cuticle:** The outermost waxy, protective layer of the stem. Composed primarily of lipids (waxes and cutin), it provides a barrier against water loss and pathogen ingress. The cuticle and the adjacent single-cell epidermis layer together comprise **13–24% (by weight)** of the bast fraction.

2. **Epidermis:** A single layer of thin-walled cells just below the cuticle. Closely connected to the cuticle and generally detaches as a unit with it during processing.

3. **Cortex (Bast / Phloem region):** This is where the **valuable bast fibres** are located. The bast fibres are arranged in **20–50 discrete bundles**, each bundle encircling the central woody core.

4. **Cambium:** A thin layer of actively dividing cells between the phloem and xylem.

5. **Xylem (Woody Core / Shive):** The central lignified core tissue of the stem. Rich in lignin (26–32%), this is the **non-fibre** woody material that must be removed during retting, breaking, and scutching. It becomes "shive" waste during processing.

6. **Pith (Medulla):** The very centre of the stem; a small, soft parenchyma zone.

> **Example:** When you look at a cross-section of a flax stem under a microscope, you can clearly see the ring of bright, birefringent (shiny) fibre bundles surrounding the dark, heavily lignified woody core — like a wreath of fibres around a wooden core.

## 2. Macroscopic Structure of the Fibre

1. **Flax is a "technical fibre"** — the commercially traded unit is not a single cell but a **bundle of 10–40 ultimate (elementary) fibres** bound together by the middle lamella (pectin-rich cement).
2. **Technical fibre length:** 25–150 cm (line fibre); averaging ~50 cm for commercially traded scutched flax.
3. **Technical fibre diameter (bundle):** 40–80 μm, depending on the number of individual cells in the cross-section.
4. **Fibre-to-length ratio:** In the finest and longest flax, the length-to-breadth ratio can reach **15,000:1**; in short flax, this drops to **1,500:1**.
5. **Appearance:** Flax fibre has a **subdued, silky lustre**, a smooth surface, and a regular, cylindrical form. It is cream, ivory, grey, or pale golden in colour.
6. **Texture:** Smooth to touch (unlike jute or hemp which feel coarser); nodes along the length give a characteristic tactile texture.
7. **Flexibility:** Moderate — stiffer than cotton because of the high cellulose crystallinity and fibre bundle structure, but flexible enough to be woven and knitted.

## 3. Hierarchical Fibre Organisation

Flax fibre is organised in **4 hierarchical levels**, from macro to nano scale:

```
Plant Stem (cm scale)
        ↓
Technical Fibre Bundle (mm–cm scale; 10–40 ultimate fibres)
        ↓
Ultimate (Elementary) Fibre Cell (mm scale; 1 single cell)
        ↓
Cell Wall Layers & Microfibrils (nm–μm scale)
```

### Level 1: Technical Fibre Bundle

1. The commercially traded fibre unit; consists of **10–40 ultimate fibre cells** cemented together by pectin-rich middle lamella.
2. Length: up to 150 cm; diameter: 40–80 μm.
3. After retting, bundles are partially separated from one another.

### Level 2: Ultimate (Elementary) Fibre Cell

1. A single, spindle-shaped plant cell with pointed (tapered) ends.
2. Length: **2–3 cm** (20–40 mm); some sources cite 6–65 mm.
3. Diameter: **10–25 μm** (average ~15–20 μm).
4. This is the true fibre unit — what is measured for tensile properties of individual fibres.
5. Each ultimate fibre is bounded by its own **primary cell wall** and contains a thick **secondary cell wall** (the main structural element).

### Level 3: Cell Wall Layers

The cell wall of each ultimate fibre consists of:

1. **Middle lamella (ML):** Between adjacent cells; pectin-rich; acts as adhesive binding cells together.
2. **Primary Cell Wall (PCW):** Thin outer wall deposited during cell elongation; contains cellulose, pectins, hemicelluloses.
3. **Secondary Cell Wall (SCW):** The thick, main structural wall deposited during cell maturation; three layers:
   - **S1 layer:** Outer secondary wall; cellulose, hemicelluloses, pectins, lignin.
   - **S2 layer (G-layer):** Thickest layer; composed almost entirely of highly crystalline cellulose and pectins. This is the main load-bearing layer.
   - **S3 layer (Gn-layer):** Innermost secondary wall layer; highly crystalline cellulose.
4. **Lumen:** The central hollow channel of the cell; narrow in flax (unlike cotton which has a wide lumen). The lumen nearly disappears in mature fibres as the S2 and S3 layers fill the interior.

### Level 4: Cellulose Microfibrils

1. The S2 layer consists of **crystalline cellulose microfibrils** arranged in a helical pattern.
2. **Microfibril Angle (MFA):** The angle at which microfibrils are wound relative to the fibre axis. For flax, MFA = **approximately 10°** (almost parallel to the fibre axis).
3. A low MFA means the microfibrils are nearly parallel to the fibre axis → **high stiffness and tensile strength**.
4. *Compare with cotton MFA: ~25–30° → cotton is much more elastic/flexible.*
5. Cellulose chain length (degree of polymerisation, DP): **~18,000** glucose units in flax (much higher than cotton's DP of ~5,000), contributing to flax's superior strength.

> **Example:** The low microfibril angle in flax is why linen fabric feels stiffer and crisper than cotton but is 2–3× stronger in tensile tests.

## 4. Cell Wall Architecture

```
Cell Structure (from outside to inside):
────────────────────────────────
Middle Lamella (ML)         ← Pectin, lignin (between cells)
Primary Cell Wall (PCW)     ← Cellulose, pectin, hemicellulose
Secondary Cell Wall S1      ← Cellulose, hemicellulose, pectin, lignin
Secondary Cell Wall S2 (G)  ← Crystalline cellulose + pectin (MAIN LAYER)
Secondary Cell Wall S3 (Gn) ← Crystalline cellulose
Lumen (central channel)     ← Narrow in mature flax
────────────────────────────────
```

Key structural metrics:

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Cell wall thickness | 5–10 μm (in mature ultimate fibre) |
| Lumen diameter | 2–5 μm (narrow) |
| S2 layer proportion | ~80% of total cell wall |
| Microfibril angle (MFA) | ~10° from fibre axis |
| Degree of polymerisation (DP) | ~18,000 |
| Crystallinity of cellulose | 65–90% |

## 5. Key Morphological Features

### 5.1 Nodes

1. Flax fibres exhibit characteristic transverse markings called **nodes** (also called "fibernodes" or "dislocation bands") visible at regular intervals along the length.
2. Nodes are cross-marks or slight swellings perpendicular to the fibre axis.
3. They arise from **compression or bending stresses** during growth, harvesting, or processing.
4. Nodes add to the **tactile texture** of linen fabric and contribute to its spinning character (fibres interlock during twisting due to nodes).
5. Nodes are also **weak points** — sites of preferential fracture, dye uptake, and enzyme attack.

### 5.2 Lustre

1. Flax fibre has a characteristic **natural lustre** — smooth surface + semi-transparency allows incident light to reflect evenly.
2. Lustre is enhanced by **wet finishing** (calendering, mangling) and is a hallmark quality of fine linen fabric.

### 5.3 Pointed Ends

1. Individual ultimate fibre cells taper to **sharp, pointed ends**, which help fibres interlock during spinning (contributing to yarn cohesion).

### 5.4 Surface Smoothness

1. Compared to jute or hemp, flax fibres are relatively **smooth on the surface**, contributing to their lustre and comfort in apparel applications.

## 6. Comparison with Other Bast Fibres

| Feature | Flax | Jute | Hemp | Ramie |
|---------|------|------|------|-------|
| Ultimate fibre length | 20–40 mm | 2–5 mm | 20–25 mm | 40–250 mm |
| Technical fibre length | 25–150 cm | 150–300 cm | 50–200 cm | 50–150 cm |
| Diameter (ultimate) | 10–25 μm | 10–20 μm | 16–50 μm | 20–80 μm |
| Cellulose % | 64–80% | 61–71% | 70–77% | 76–80% |
| MFA | ~10° | ~8° | ~6° | — |
| Lumen | Narrow | Moderate | Narrow | Narrow |
| Node structure | Yes | Faint | Less prominent | Prominent |
| Lustre | Silky | Silky | Dull | Silky |
