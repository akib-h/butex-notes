## 1. Introduction to Microscopy of Flax

1. Microscopic examination is an essential tool in **fibre identification**, quality assessment, and structural research for flax fibres.
2. Two primary modes of examination are used in standard textile analysis:
   - **Longitudinal Section (L.S.):** Viewing the fibre along its length (side view).
   - **Cross-Section (C.S.):** Viewing the fibre cut perpendicular to its axis (end view).
3. In addition, **polarised light microscopy** and **scanning electron microscopy (SEM)** provide detailed structural and surface information.
4. The microscopic appearance of flax is distinctive and well-documented, allowing trained analysts to **identify flax fibres confidently** in blended fabrics or archaeological textiles.

> **Example:** A forensic textile analyst examining a piece of ancient fabric can determine it is linen (flax) rather than cotton or hemp by observing the characteristic polygonal cross-section with a small lumen and the node-bearing longitudinal structure.

## 2. Longitudinal Section (L.S.) Microscopic View

When viewed longitudinally (side-on) under a light microscope, flax fibres display the following characteristic features:

### 2.1 Overall Appearance

1. The fibres appear as **long, cylindrical, slightly irregular cylinders** with a smooth surface.
2. Unlike cotton, flax fibres have **no convolutions** (twists or ribbon-like appearance) along their length.
3. The fibre has a **slightly irregular width** along its length — it is not perfectly uniform in diameter.

### 2.2 Nodes

1. **Nodes** (also called crossmarks, dislocations, or fibernodes) are the most distinctive longitudinal feature of flax.
2. They appear as **transverse marks, swellings, or kink bands** crossing the fibre perpendicularly.
3. Nodes occur at **irregular intervals** along the fibre length.
4. They arise during growth (as joints between fibre cells) and are accentuated by mechanical processing.
5. Under normal light: nodes appear as slight constrictions or crossings.
6. Under polarised light: nodes appear as **dark bands** interrupting the otherwise bright (birefringent) fibre.
7. Nodes are **diagnostically important** — they help distinguish flax from ramie and hemp.

### 2.3 Lumen

1. A **narrow central lumen** (the original cell cavity) runs along the centre of each ultimate fibre cell.
2. The lumen is much narrower in flax than in cotton — sometimes barely visible in mature fibres where the thick secondary cell wall nearly fills the interior.
3. The lumen appears as a **fine, slightly irregular dark line** running along the fibre axis.

### 2.4 Cell Junctions

1. At the junctions between adjacent ultimate fibre cells (the pointed end-to-end connections), a slight swelling or **taper** is visible, because individual cells overlap.
2. This gives the longitudinal view a characteristic **segmented or jointed appearance** when very finely resolved.

### 2.5 Surface Texture

1. The fibre surface is **smooth** compared to jute or hemp.
2. Fine **surface striations** (parallel lines along the length) may be visible at very high magnification, corresponding to underlying cellulose microfibrils.

### Summary of Longitudinal Features

| Feature | Appearance |
|---------|-----------|
| Width uniformity | Slightly irregular |
| Convolutions | **Absent** (diagnostic — distinguishes from cotton) |
| Surface | Smooth, striated at high magnification |
| Nodes | Present; transverse crossmarks at irregular intervals |
| Lumen | Narrow, continuous, slightly irregular |
| Ends | Tapered/pointed (ultimate fibre cells) |
| Lustre under light | Bright, semi-transparent |

> **Example:** In a polarised light micrograph of flax, the main body of the fibre glows bright white (due to high cellulose crystallinity and birefringence), while fibernodes appear as dark bands — the alternating bright/dark pattern is characteristic of flax.

## 3. Cross-Sectional (C.S.) Microscopic View

When cut across its width and viewed from the end (cross-sectional view), flax fibres show very distinctive features:

### 3.1 Shape of the Ultimate Fibre

1. Individual ultimate fibre cells have an **irregular polygonal shape** in cross-section — typically **5–6 sided** (pentagonal or hexagonal) with slightly rounded corners.
2. This polygonal shape is caused by the **mutual compression of neighbouring fibre cells** as they pack tightly together in the bundle.
3. The corners may be rounded or angular depending on maturity and the degree of secondary wall deposition.

### 3.2 The Lumen

1. The **lumen** (central canal) is clearly visible as a **small, approximately circular to oval opening** at the centre of each ultimate fibre cell.
2. The lumen is **small relative to the cell size** — the lumen-to-cell-diameter ratio is approximately **1:6 to 1:8**.
3. *Compare with cotton:* Cotton has a large, kidney-shaped lumen (the main lumen-to-cell ratio is ~1:2); this makes flax fibres **denser and heavier** than cotton.

### 3.3 Cell Wall Thickness

1. The **thick secondary cell wall** dominates the cross-section, occupying 80–90% of the total cell cross-sectional area.
2. Cell wall thickness in mature flax ultimate fibres: **5–10 μm** (compared to lumen diameter of ~2–5 μm).

### 3.4 Bundle Cross-Section

1. A **technical fibre bundle** in cross-section appears as a cluster of **10–40 polygonal cells** packed closely together.
2. The bundle is outlined by the middle lamella (pectin-rich boundary between cells).
3. The cross-sectional area of a full bundle: typically 40–80 μm in diameter.

### 3.5 Stem Cross-Section

In a full flax stem cross-section:

1. **Outermost ring:** Thin-walled epidermal cells (cuticle/epidermis).
2. **Cortex ring:** Multiple discrete fibre bundles (bright, birefringent polygonal cells).
3. **Inner zone:** Thin-walled parenchyma.
4. **Woody core:** Large, lignified xylem vessels and fibres (dark with phloroglucinol staining).
5. **Central pith:** Small soft parenchyma zone.

### Summary of Cross-Sectional Features

| Feature | Appearance |
|---------|-----------|
| Ultimate fibre shape | Polygonal (5–6 sided), rounded corners |
| Lumen | Small, circular-oval; narrow |
| Cell wall | Thick; occupies >80% of cross-section |
| Bundle shape | Irregular cluster of polygonal cells |
| Bundle diameter | 40–80 μm |
| Cell size | 10–25 μm width |

> **Example:** In a forensic or historical textile lab, a transverse cut through a suspect linen fabric is mounted on a glass slide. Under the microscope, the polygonal, thick-walled cells with small central lumens confirm the fibre is flax — not hemp (which shows larger, more rounded cells) or ramie (which shows flattened cells with very fine, irregular walls).

## 4. Polarised Light Microscopy

1. Under **polarised light** (cross-polarisers), crystalline materials display **birefringence** — they appear bright against a dark background.
2. Flax fibres are **highly birefringent** due to the high crystallinity of their cellulose content and the near-parallel alignment of microfibrils (~10° MFA).
3. Under polarised light:
   - Healthy fibre body: **Bright white or yellow-white** (strongly birefringent).
   - Fibernodes / dislocations: **Dark transverse bands** (disrupted crystalline alignment).
   - Lumen: **Dark line**.
4. The distinctive bright/dark alternating pattern along the length of flax under polarised light is a **key identification feature**.
5. **Extinction angles:** The fibre goes dark (extinction) when its axis is aligned 45° to the polariser — this behaviour can be used to calculate MFA.

> **Example:** Conservation scientists examining a fragile Egyptian linen archaeological specimen use polarised light microscopy to assess fibre condition — the density of dark fibernodes (dislocations) indicates the degree of degradation, without physically stressing the artefact.

## 5. Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)

SEM provides **high-resolution surface and cross-sectional images** of flax fibres:

1. **Surface view (SEM):** Shows the smooth, slightly striated surface of ultimate fibres; surface wax deposits visible as globules; nodes appear as compression bands or ridges.
2. **Cross-section (SEM):** Clearly shows the polygonal cell shapes, thick secondary cell walls, and narrow lumens.
3. **Fractured ends (SEM):** When flax fibres are broken in tension, fracture surfaces show **fibril pullout** — individual cellulose microfibrils pull free from the cell wall, giving a characteristic brushy fracture morphology.
4. **After retting (SEM):** The middle lamella between cells is visibly degraded; cells are more separated; surface appears cleaner.
5. **After cottonisation (SEM):** Individual cells are clearly separated; surface shows removed pectin and hemicellulose layers.

## 6. Comparison with Other Fibres Under Microscope

| Feature | Flax | Cotton | Jute | Hemp | Ramie |
|---------|------|--------|------|------|-------|
| L.S. — convolutions | ❌ None | ✅ Present (ribbon-like) | ❌ None | ❌ None | ❌ None |
| L.S. — nodes | ✅ Clear | ❌ None | Faint | ✅ Present | ✅ Very pronounced |
| L.S. — surface | Smooth, slightly striated | Twisted ribbon | Striated | Rough | Striated, ridged |
| C.S. — shape | Polygonal, rounded corners | Kidney-shaped, flat | Polygonal | Polygonal | Flattened, irregular |
| C.S. — lumen | Small, circular | Large, kidney | Moderate | Small | Very small |
| Birefringence | Very high | Moderate | Moderate | High | Very high |

## 7. How to Identify Flax Under the Microscope

A trained analyst identifies flax by checking for these features in order:

1. ✅ **No convolutions** (rules out cotton).
2. ✅ **Nodes visible** along length (differentiates from ramie which has more pronounced ridges; hemp which has less regular nodes).
3. ✅ **Polygonal cross-section** with small central lumen (eliminates cotton with its kidney/flat shape).
4. ✅ **Strong birefringence** under polarised light with dark fibernode bands.
5. ✅ **Thick cell walls** — lumen diameter is <20% of cell diameter.
