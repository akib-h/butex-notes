## 1. Overview of Chemical Composition

1. Flax fibre is a **lignocellulosic composite material** — its properties arise from the complex interplay of its chemical components.
2. The dominant component is **cellulose**, which constitutes approximately **64–80% of the dry fibre mass** after retting.
3. The remaining non-cellulosic components include **hemicellulose, pectin, lignin, waxes, proteins, ash, and water-soluble compounds**.
4. These non-cellulosic components significantly influence **fibre characteristics** including colour, moisture absorption, mechanical properties, and processability.
5. The exact composition varies with **variety, cultivation conditions, retting method, and processing stage**.

### Average Chemical Composition of Flax Fibre (Retted)

| Component | % Content (by dry weight) |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| Cellulose | 64–80% (avg. ~71%) |
| Hemicellulose | 12–17% |
| Pectin | 1.8–5% |
| Lignin | 2–5% |
| Wax/Fat | 1.5–2% |
| Ash (minerals) | 1–2% |
| Moisture (air-dry) | 7–12% |
| Proteins & other | trace |

> **Source:** Compiled from Morrison et al. (2000), ScienceDirect, and ResearchGate comparative studies.

> **Example:** If you take 100 g of dried retted flax fibre, approximately 71 g is pure cellulose — the rest (29 g) is a mixture of hemicellulose, pectin, wax, lignin, ash, and other minor components that must be removed during scouring and bleaching to obtain pure linen yarn.

## 2. Cellulose — Primary Component

### 2.1 Structure of Cellulose

1. **Cellulose** is a linear (unbranched), high-molecular-weight **polysaccharide** composed of D-glucose units.
2. Glucose monomers are linked by **β-1,4-glycosidic bonds**, forming a long-chain polymer called **cellobiose** (repeating unit = two glucose units).
3. The chemical formula of cellulose: **(C₆H₁₀O₅)ₙ**
4. Each glucose unit contains three **hydroxyl groups (–OH)** at C-2, C-3, and C-6 positions; these –OH groups are the reactive sites for:
   - Hydrogen bonding with adjacent chains.
   - Chemical reactions (dye uptake, etherification, esterification).
5. In flax, the **degree of polymerisation (DP)** averages approximately **18,000** glucose units per chain — significantly higher than cotton (DP ~5,000), which contributes to flax's greater tensile strength.

### 2.2 Cellulose Organisation

```
Cellulose Hierarchy:
Glucose monomer (0.5 nm)
        ↓
Cellobiose unit (2 glucose units)
        ↓
Cellulose chain (β-1,4-glucan, DP ~18,000)
        ↓
Crystallite / microfibril (hydrogen-bonded chains)
        ↓
Macrofibril (packed microfibrils)
        ↓
Cell wall (laminate of oriented macrofibrils)
```

### 2.3 Crystalline vs. Amorphous Cellulose

1. Cellulose in flax fibres exists in both **crystalline** and **amorphous** regions.
2. **Crystallinity index:** 65–90% (highly crystalline compared to most cellulosic fibres).
3. Crystalline cellulose (cellulose I): Chains are parallel and densely hydrogen bonded; very high strength and stiffness; resistant to chemical attack.
4. Amorphous cellulose: Randomly arranged chains with fewer hydrogen bonds; more accessible to moisture, dyes, and chemicals.

> **Example:** The high crystallinity of flax cellulose is why linen fabric is so strong and wrinkle-prone — the highly ordered, rigid cellulose structure resists bending and recovering from deformation.

## 3. Hemicellulose

1. **Hemicellulose** content in flax: **12–17%** (varies with retting and processing stage).
2. Hemicelluloses are **heterogeneous branched polysaccharides** — unlike cellulose, they are not composed of a single sugar type.
3. Major hemicelluloses in flax: **Xylans** (xylose-based), **glucomannans**, and **xyloglucans**.
4. Hemicellulose chains are **shorter** than cellulose (DP ~100–200) and are **water-insoluble**.
5. Hemicellulose forms **strong complexes with both cellulose and lignin** through hydrogen bonds and covalent ester linkages.
6. Functions:
   - Acts as a **matrix** surrounding cellulose microfibrils.
   - Contributes to cell wall **stiffness** and **cohesion**.
   - The ratio of structural hemicellulose to pectin is correlated with **fibre tensile properties**.
7. Removal of hemicellulose during scouring/bleaching **increases pore size** and improves accessibility for dyes and finishing chemicals.
8. Like cellulose, hemicellulose contains **hydroxyl groups (–OH)** making it hydrophilic, but it is water-insoluble.

---

## 4. Pectin

1. **Pectin** content in flax: **1.8–5%** (range varies with maturity and retting).
2. Pectin is a complex polysaccharide with an **acidic main chain of polygalacturonic acid** (galacturonic acid units linked by α-1,4-glycosidic bonds).
3. Side chains contain **neutral sugars** (arabinose, galactose).
4. The **middle lamella** between adjacent ultimate fibre cells is primarily composed of **calcium pectate** — a calcium salt of pectic acid that forms strong gels, cementing the fibre bundle together.
5. **Pectin is the target of the retting process** — microbial pectinases (pectin-degrading enzymes) break down the pectin in the middle lamella, allowing fibre separation from the woody core and from neighbouring cells.
6. In cottonised flax, most pectin must be removed to separate ultimate fibre cells.
7. **De-methylesterification of pectin** (by pectinase enzymes) converts methyl-esterified pectin to a more ionisable form, facilitating crosslinking with calcium ions and cell wall stiffening.

---

## 5. Lignin

1. **Lignin** content in flax bast fibres: **2–5%** (much lower than in woody plants where lignin can reach 20–35%).
2. Lignin is a **complex, three-dimensional, amorphous phenylpropanoid polymer** — the most abundant aromatic polymer in nature.
3. **Monolignol units:** p-coumaryl alcohol (H), coniferyl alcohol (G), and sinapyl alcohol (S) are the building blocks. They are linked by **ether bonds (β-O-4)** and **carbon-carbon bonds**.
4. Lignin is found primarily in the **middle lamella and S1 layer** (outer secondary wall) of flax fibres; the S2 and S3 layers (main load-bearing layers) contain very little lignin.
5. Functions:
   - Provides **rigidity and compressive resistance** to the stem and fibre.
   - Acts as an **adhesive** between the woody core and bast fibres.
   - **Hydrophobic** — resists water penetration.
6. Lignin is responsible for the **brown/yellow colour** of incompletely processed flax fibre; it must be removed during bleaching for bright white linen.
7. Removal of lignin by alkali or oxidative bleaching **improves fibre softness and dye uptake** but must be controlled to avoid cellulose degradation.

---

## 6. Waxes & Fats

1. **Wax/fat** content in flax: **1.5–2%**.
2. Present as a natural protective coating on the fibre surface and within the cuticle layer.
3. Composition: Long-chain aliphatic hydrocarbons, esters, alcohols, aldehydes, and ketones.
4. Functions:
   - **Lubricant** during processing — reduces inter-fibre friction.
   - **Water resistance** — hydrophobic coating reduces initial moisture absorption.
   - Contributes to the characteristic **smoothness and lustre** of flax fibre.
5. Waxes are removed during scouring (alkali treatment) and bleaching, which is necessary before dyeing and finishing.
6. Removal of wax during normal laundering causes **cell ends to project above the fabric surface** — a phenomenon sometimes called "cottonising of linen" (see [10_cottonized_flax.md](./10_cottonized_flax.md)).

---

## 7. Minor Components

1. **Ash (minerals):** 1–2%; mainly calcium, potassium, magnesium, silicon, and iron compounds absorbed from soil.
2. **Proteins:** Trace amounts of structural proteins associated with cell wall assembly.
3. **Phenolic compounds/Flavonoids (Lignans):** *Linum usitatissimum* is particularly rich in **secoisolariciresinol diglucoside (SDG)** — a plant lignan with demonstrated antioxidant and health-promoting properties. These are found primarily in seeds but trace amounts are present in fibres.
4. **Water-soluble compounds:** Sugars, organic acids, colouring matter.
5. **Natural colouring matter:** Resides largely in the cuticle and woody shive fragments; responsible for difficulty bleaching linen to a bright white.

## 8. Bonding in Flax Fibre

The properties of flax are determined not just by its chemical components but by the **types and strengths of bonds** between and within these components:

### 8.1 Intramolecular Hydrogen Bonds (within a cellulose chain)

1. Within a single cellulose chain, **intramolecular hydrogen bonds** form between the hydroxyl group at C-3 of one glucose ring and the ring oxygen of the adjacent glucose unit (O3–H···O5').
2. Also: O2–H···O6' bond.
3. These bonds maintain the **rigid, linear conformation** of the cellulose chain, preventing it from coiling (unlike starch, which forms helices due to α-1,4 linkages).

### 8.2 Intermolecular Hydrogen Bonds (between cellulose chains)

1. Between adjacent cellulose chains, **intermolecular hydrogen bonds** form (particularly O6–H···O3' and O2–H···O6').
2. These bonds cause cellulose chains to pack together in **crystalline arrays** (microfibrils).
3. The dense network of intermolecular H-bonds gives cellulose its **insolubility in water** (despite having many hydroxyl groups) and its **mechanical strength**.

### 8.3 Van der Waals Forces

1. Between cellulose chains and between cellulose and other matrix polymers, **weak van der Waals interactions** contribute to overall cohesion.

### 8.4 Ester Bonds (Hemicellulose–Lignin Cross-linking)

1. Hemicellulose is cross-linked to lignin via **ester bonds** (between phenolic hydroxyl groups of lignin and carboxyl groups of hemicellulose).
2. These bonds are broken by **alkali treatment** (scouring/mercerisation), which explains why alkaline conditions are used to remove non-cellulosic components.

### 8.5 Ionic Bonds (Calcium Pectate)

1. In the middle lamella, **calcium ions (Ca²⁺)** cross-link pectin chains through **ionic bonds** to form calcium pectate gels.
2. This is the primary adhesive holding fibre bundles together.
3. **Sequestering agents (EDTA)** and **acidic conditions** disrupt these ionic bonds, enabling enzymatic retting to penetrate the middle lamella.

### Summary of Bonds

| Bond Type | Location | Function |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| β-1,4-glycosidic bonds | Cellulose backbone | Structural; links glucose units |
| Intramolecular H-bonds | Within cellulose chain | Maintains linear conformation |
| Intermolecular H-bonds | Between cellulose chains | Crystallinity, strength, insolubility |
| Van der Waals forces | Between all polymers | Cohesion of cell wall |
| Ester bonds | Hemicellulose–Lignin | Cross-linking; alkali-cleavable |
| Ionic bonds (Ca²⁺) | Pectin middle lamella | Cell-to-cell adhesion; retting target |
| α-1,4-glycosidic bonds | Pectin backbone | Pectin structural backbone |
