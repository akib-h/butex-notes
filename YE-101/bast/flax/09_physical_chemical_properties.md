## 1. Physical Properties

### 1.1 Length

1. **Technical fibre (bundle) length:** 25–150 cm (line fibre); average ~50 cm.
2. **Ultimate (elementary) fibre cell length:** 6–65 mm; average ~20–40 mm.
3. Long fibres are more valuable — they produce stronger, more regular yarns with fewer splice points.
4. *Example:* A high-grade scutched Belgian flax bundle used for fine shirting may be 70–90 cm long, enabling very fine, consistent yarn to be spun.

### 1.2 Diameter (Fineness)

1. **Ultimate fibre cell diameter:** 10–25 μm (average ~15–20 μm).
2. **Technical fibre bundle diameter:** 40–80 μm (depending on number of cells in bundle).
3. Fineness is expressed in **tex** (grams per 1,000 m) or **dtex** in scientific literature.
4. *Example:* A high-quality French fibre bundle suitable for 60-count wet-spun yarn will have a linear density of ~3–5 tex.

### 1.3 Colour

1. **Natural colour:** Ranges from **silver-grey** (dew-retted) to **pale golden-ivory** (water-retted) to **light beige/brownish** (partially processed).
2. Colour depends on: retting method, variety, growing conditions, and age of the fibre.
3. **Bleached flax:** White to bright white (after scouring, bleaching, and finishing).
4. *Example:* Undyed, unbleached linen fabric ("ecru linen") has a characteristic warm, slightly golden-beige colour from the natural flax pigments.

### 1.4 Lustre

1. Flax fibre has a characteristic **natural silky lustre** — the highest of any plant (cellulosic) fibre.
2. Lustre arises from:
   - **Smooth fibre surface** (minimal surface irregularity).
   - **Semi-transparency** of the fibre (light penetrates and reflects from multiple layers).
   - **Parallel alignment** of microfibrils at low MFA angle.
3. Lustre is enhanced by:
   - Wet processing (wet spinning).
   - Calendering (hot pressing of fabric).
   - Mangling (rolling under pressure).
4. *Example:* Fine damask linen tablecloths have an unmistakable lustrous sheen that is impossible to replicate exactly in cotton or synthetic fabrics.

### 1.5 Density

1. **Density of flax fibre:** 1.45–1.54 g/cm³ (typically quoted as ~1.50 g/cm³).
2. Higher density than cotton (1.54 g/cm³) due to the narrower lumen (less air space in fibre).
3. This means linen fabrics feel **heavier and denser** than equivalent cotton fabrics.
4. *Example:* A 200 g/m² linen fabric will feel noticeably heavier and cooler than a 200 g/m² cotton fabric of equivalent construction.

### 1.6 Electrical Properties

1. Flax is a **poor conductor of electricity** (an insulator under dry conditions) like all natural cellulosic fibres.
2. Under humid conditions, the moisture absorbed by flax increases its electrical conductivity and reduces static buildup.
3. *Example:* Linen clothing rarely suffers from static cling in humid climates (e.g., summer), but can build up static in very dry conditions.

## 2. Thermal Properties

1. **Good thermal conductor:** Flax conducts heat away from the skin efficiently — hence linen feels **cool to the touch** and is ideal for hot-weather clothing.
2. **Decomposition temperature:** Flax begins to discolour above **120°C** and decomposes (carbonises) above **200°C**.
3. **Flammability:** Flax is **flammable** — it ignites readily and burns quickly with a clean, bright flame (similar to paper), leaving a grey ash.
4. **Safe ironing temperature:** **215–232°C** (the highest of any commonly ironed fibre) — linen can be ironed at high heat without damage.
5. **Heat conductance:** Good; the thermal conductivity of linen fabric is significantly higher than wool or synthetic fibres.

> **Example:** The "linen test" — experienced launderers know that linen requires the hottest iron setting. Linen at 230°C irons smoothly and crisply; cotton at the same temperature may begin to scorch.

## 3. Moisture & Absorption Properties

1. **Moisture regain (MR):** **12–14% at standard conditions** (65% RH, 20°C). This is higher than cotton (~8.5%) — flax absorbs more moisture from the environment.
2. **Moisture absorption capacity:** Flax can absorb up to **20% of its own weight** in moisture without feeling wet (due to rapid wicking away from skin).
3. **Rapid absorption:** Linen absorbs perspiration and moisture **very quickly** — due to the capillary action between individual microfibrils (as described by Ulster Linen's microscopic analogy of fine glass tubes in water).
4. **Wicking speed:** Faster than cotton.
5. **Wet strength:** Flax is **stronger when wet** — wet tenacity increases approximately **20% above dry strength**. This is the opposite of many synthetic fibres and is unusual among natural fibres.
6. **Swelling:** Flax swells in both longitudinal and transverse directions when wet, but transverse swelling is much greater.
7. **Drying rate:** Linen dries faster than cotton due to lower fibre wall porosity and better wicking.

> **Example:** Historically, linen was the preferred fabric for **surgical dressings** precisely because of its high moisture absorption, rapid wicking, and excellent wet strength — it remained intact and functional even when saturated.

## 4. Mechanical Properties

1. **Tensile strength (Tenacity):**
   - Dry: **5.5–8 g/denier** (≈ 49–71 cN/tex); some sources cite 6.5–8 g/denier.
   - Wet: **Approximately 20% higher** than dry strength.
   - *Comparison:* Flax is approximately **2–3× stronger** than cotton in tensile tests.

2. **Elongation at break:**
   - Dry: **1.8%** (extremely low — flax is one of the least extensible natural fibres).
   - Wet: **2.2%**.
   - *Comparison:* Cotton: 3–7%; Wool: 25–35%; Flax is very rigid with minimal stretch.

3. **Elastic recovery:**
   - Flax has **low elastic recovery** — once stretched even slightly, it does not fully recover its original length.
   - This means linen fabric **wrinkles (creases) very easily** — a well-known characteristic of linen.

4. **Stiffness / Flexural rigidity:**
   - Flax fibre is **stiffer** than cotton (higher Young's modulus: ~50–80 GPa for individual fibres).
   - This is due to the high cellulose crystallinity, low MFA (~10°), and high DP.

5. **Abrasion resistance:**
   - Moderate to good — linen fabric becomes **softer with washing and use** as individual fibre cells separate gradually.
   - *Example:* Antique linen sheets that have been washed hundreds of times become extraordinarily soft and lustrous ("well-worn linen").

6. **Pilling resistance:**
   - Excellent — the relatively long, smooth, stiff fibres do not easily pill under abrasion.

7. **Dimensional stability:**
   - Good in woven form; however, linen garments can **shrink** if not preshrunk or if washed improperly.

## 5. Chemical Properties

### 5.1 Effect of Acids

1. **Dilute acids:** Flax is **relatively resistant** to dilute acids at room temperature if washed off promptly. Short exposure to dilute hydrochloric acid (HCl) or sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) causes minimal damage.
2. **Concentrated acids:** Flax is **severely damaged and destroyed** by concentrated mineral acids — they **hydrolyse the β-1,4-glycosidic bonds** in cellulose chains at the glycoside oxygen atom.
3. **Mechanism:** Acid protonates the glycosidic oxygen → ring opening → chain cleavage → formation of reducing sugars → loss of tensile strength and fibre dissolution.
4. **Organic acids:** Less damaging than inorganic acids; acetic acid (vinegar) at normal concentrations causes no measurable damage.
5. *Example:* Battery acid (sulfuric acid) spilled on linen will destroy it completely. A mild vinegar soak used in laundering causes no damage.

### 5.2 Effect of Alkalis

1. Flax has **excellent resistance to alkalis** — much better than wool (which is destroyed by strong alkalis) and better than cotton.
2. **Dilute alkalis:** No damage; commonly used in laundry detergents.
3. **Strong alkalis (NaOH, KOH):** Flax can withstand concentrated NaOH solutions much better than cotton — concentrated NaOH causes **mercerisation** in cotton (swelling and improved lustre), but flax does not mercerize in the same way.
4. **Mechanism:** Alkali hydrolyses the **waxes and gums** (ester linkages) binding flax cells, which is actually exploited in scouring to improve fibre separation and dye uptake.
5. **Normal laundering effect:** Alkaline detergents gradually hydrolyse the waxy gum (pectin/wax matrix) holding fibre cell bundles together → causes **cell ends to project above fabric surface** ("cottonising" effect over repeated washes — see [10_cottonized_flax.md](./10_cottonized_flax.md)).
6. *Example:* Linen can safely be washed with strongly alkaline laundry detergents and even boiled (traditional linen boiling was common until the 20th century) without fibre damage.

### 5.3 Effect of Bleaching Agents

1. **Oxidising bleaches (sodium hypochlorite — NaOCl, hydrogen peroxide — H₂O₂, sodium perborate — NaBO₃):**
   - These are the most effective and commonly used bleaches for linen.
   - Act by liberating active oxygen which oxidises coloured chromophore groups in lignin and natural pigments → bleaching.
   - Must be controlled — excessive oxidative bleaching degrades cellulose chains (oxycellulose formation → strength loss).

2. **Reducing bleaches (sodium bisulphite — NaHSO₃):**
   - Less effective on linen; mainly used for temporary whitening or in combination treatments.

3. **Chlorine bleaches:**
   - Effective; cool hypochlorite solutions do not damage flax fibres.
   - However, excessive chlorine bleaching causes **chlorination of cellulose** → brittleness.

4. **Linen is more difficult to bleach than cotton** because:
   - Its natural lignin content is higher.
   - Woody shive fragments (if present) are very resistant to bleaching.
   - The natural colouring matter (from cuticle and woody fragments) is tenacious.
   - *Example:* Cotton fabric can be bleached to brilliant white in a single peroxide treatment; linen requires multiple treatments or more aggressive conditions to achieve comparable whiteness.

### 5.4 Effect of Organic Solvents

1. Flax fibre has **excellent resistance to organic solvents** — it is insoluble in and unaffected by:
   - Acetone, benzene, alcohols, ether, chlorinated solvents.
2. This makes linen garments **safe for dry cleaning** with conventional solvents.
3. *Example:* Perchloroethylene (the standard dry-cleaning solvent) does not damage linen fabrics.

### 5.5 Effect of Dyes

1. Linen does **not dye easily** compared to wool or even cotton.
2. **Direct dyes and vat dyes** give the most satisfactory results on flax.
3. **Reactive dyes** can also be used with appropriate alkaline fixation conditions.
4. **Mordant dyes** (used with metallic salts) were the traditional method for dyeing linen.
5. Linen's resistance to dyeing is due to:
   - High crystallinity (dye molecules cannot penetrate crystal lattice).
   - Residual wax and pectin coating blocking dye access.
   - Lower accessibility of hydroxyl groups compared to cotton.
6. Bleaching and scouring **before dyeing** is essential to achieve level, bright colours.
7. *Example:* Pure white linen table linen takes months to achieve its characteristic bright white through traditional sun bleaching on grass (dew bleaching or "grassing").

### 5.6 Effect of Biological Agents

1. **Mildew/Fungi:** Flax is **attacked by mildew (fungi)** under humid conditions — cellulose is degraded enzymatically by cellulases.
   - Dry linen has reasonable mildew resistance.
   - Wet/damp linen stored in poor conditions will develop mildew quickly.

2. **Bacteria:** Bacterial cellulases also degrade flax cellulose.

3. **Insects:**
   - Flax is **NOT attacked by moth or carpet beetles** (these attack protein fibres only).
   - However, **silverfish (*Lepisma saccharina*)** do feed on cellulosic materials including linen.

4. **Sunlight / UV:**
   - Prolonged UV exposure causes **photooxidation** of cellulose → yellowing, strength loss.
   - Linen is generally more resistant to UV degradation than cotton.
   - *Example:* Linen awnings and curtains yellow more slowly than cotton equivalents when exposed to direct sunlight.

## 6. Other Notable Properties

1. **Antistatic:** In humid conditions, linen does not build up static electricity.
2. **Antibacterial:** Flax fibres have inherent mild antibacterial properties (attributed to phenolic compounds/lignans and the physical structure), making linen suitable for healthcare products.
3. **UV protective:** Linen fabric has higher UPF (Ultraviolet Protection Factor) than cotton of equivalent weight.
4. **Biodegradable:** Linen is fully biodegradable under natural conditions.
5. **Recyclable:** Linen can be mechanically recycled into lower-grade fibre.
