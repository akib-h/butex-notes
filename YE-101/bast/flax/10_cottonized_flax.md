## 1. What is Cottonized Flax?

1. **Cottonized flax** is flax fibre that has been processed to separate the **technical fibre bundles** into individual **ultimate (elementary) fibre cells** — each approximately **25–40 mm in length** and **10–25 μm in diameter**.
2. The name "cottonized" refers to the fact that the resulting individual short fibres **resemble cotton fibres** in their dimensions, processability, and behaviour on cotton spinning machinery.
3. In the natural state, flax fibres exist as **bundles of 10–40 cells** cemented by pectin. Cottonisation breaks these bundles apart to release single cells.
4. The process removes or disrupts the **pectin, hemicellulose, waxes, and residual lignin** that hold the fibre bundle together.
5. Cottonized flax is an **emerging and commercially important** form of flax fibre, enabling flax to be blended with cotton, wool, silk, and synthetic fibres on standard ring-spinning or air-jet spinning machinery.

> **Example:** A blended T-shirt fabric labelled "50% cotton / 50% linen" may be made from cottonized flax fibres mixed with cotton and spun on cotton-system machinery — this would not be possible with long, stiff, conventional flax line fibre.

---

## 2. Why Cottonize Flax?

1. **Blending capability:** Conventional flax line fibre is too long and stiff to blend uniformly with short cotton fibres. Cottonization reduces fibre length to the cotton staple range (25–40 mm), enabling intimate blending.
2. **Machinery compatibility:** Cottonized flax can be processed on **cotton short-staple spinning systems** (ring spinning, open-end, air-jet), whereas conventional flax requires specialised linen wet-spinning machinery.
3. **Softness improvement:** Individual ultimate fibres are much softer and finer than thick fibre bundles — cottonized flax fabrics feel significantly softer and less stiff than standard linen.
4. **Cost reduction:** Blending cottonized flax with cotton allows the manufacturing sector to benefit from flax's moisture-management and eco-credentials at lower cost than pure linen.
5. **Sustainability:** Cottonization enables use of **flax tow** (short fibre by-product of linen processing) and **linseed flax straw** (previously wasted) as raw material for textile production.
6. **New application areas:** Cottonized flax can be used in non-woven fabrics, medical textiles, geotextiles, and automotive sound-absorbing panels.

## 3. Methods of Cottonisation

### 3.1 Mechanical Cottonisation

1. **Process:** The fibre bundles are subjected to intensive **mechanical beating, hammering, or roller compression** — similar to traditional breaking and scutching but applied more aggressively or repeatedly.
2. The mechanical action physically fractures the middle lamella (pectin cement) between cells, separating the bundle into smaller groups or individual cells.
3. **Advantages:** No chemicals required; simple equipment; lowest cost.
4. **Disadvantages:** Cell wall damage (creates micro-cracks); fibre strength reduced; complete separation difficult (some bundles remain); irregular, non-uniform fibres.
5. *Example:* Pilot-scale flax cottonization studies at the Institute of Natural Fibres (Poland) used a modified scutching line to achieve a linear mass reduction from 6.5 tex (bundle) to ~0.6 tex (elementary fibre) through mechanical cottonisation after wet degumming.

### 3.2 Chemical Cottonisation

1. **Process:** Fibre bundles are treated with **alkaline solutions** (NaOH, Na₂CO₃, or Na₂SO₃) which hydrolyse the ester bonds cross-linking hemicellulose to lignin and dissolve pectin.
2. After chemical treatment, a **mechanical step** (washing, beating) completes the separation.
3. **Reagents commonly used:**
   - **Sodium hydroxide (NaOH):** 0.5–5% solutions; saponifies waxes; dissolves pectin and hemicellulose.
   - **Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂):** Bleaches and partially degrades non-cellulosic components.
   - **Sodium carbonate (Na₂CO₃):** Milder alkali; better selectivity.
4. **Advantages:** More complete bundle separation than mechanical alone; improved colour and softness.
5. **Disadvantages:** Chemical waste; potential cellulose degradation if conditions are too harsh; requires wastewater treatment.
6. *Example:* Treatment with 2–4% H₂O₂ at 50–80°C for 60 minutes followed by mechanical action achieved 2–4× finer fibres in one study, with no significant loss of mechanical strength.

### 3.3 Enzymatic Cottonisation

1. **Process:** **Pectinase** and/or **hemicellulase** enzymes are used in aqueous solution to selectively degrade the pectin and hemicellulose in the middle lamella without attacking cellulose.
2. After enzymatic treatment, the bundles are mechanically separated by beating/washing.
3. **Enzymes used:**
   - Pectinases (polygalacturonase, pectin methylesterase).
   - Hemicellulases (xylanase, mannanase).
   - Sometimes combined with cellulase (careful concentration control to avoid cellulose damage).
4. **Advantages:** Highly selective; preserves cellulose integrity and fibre strength; environmentally friendly; minimal wastewater issues.
5. **Disadvantages:** Slower process; higher enzyme cost; sensitive to pH, temperature, and inhibitors.
6. *Example:* In the DEMETER EU research project, enzymatic retting followed by cottonisation produced cottonized flax indistinguishable from cotton on short-staple spinning systems, with better moisture-management properties.

### 3.4 Steam Explosion

1. **Process:** Retted flax straw or bundles are treated in a pressure vessel with **high-pressure steam** (150–200°C, 0.5–2 MPa), then suddenly released to atmospheric pressure.
2. The sudden depressurisation causes explosive decompression inside the fibre structure, **physically rupturing** the middle lamella and separating cells.
3. **Advantages:** Very effective; no chemicals; fast.
4. **Disadvantages:** High energy input; risk of thermal degradation of cellulose; specialised equipment required; colour may be affected.
5. *Example:* The "Duralin" process (Netherlands) uses a steam explosion-like autoclave treatment on green (unretted) flax followed by mild conditioning, producing cottonized fibres with reduced moisture sensitivity — useful for composite applications.

## 4. Properties of Cottonized Flax

1. **Fibre length:** 25–40 mm (cotton staple range) — processable on cotton spinning systems.
2. **Fineness:** Significantly reduced compared to raw bundles; typically 0.5–1.0 tex per elementary fibre (comparable to coarse cotton).
3. **Softness:** Dramatically increased — the thick, stiff bundle is separated into thin, flexible individual cells.
4. **Colour:** Improved (whiter) because non-cellulosic surface components are removed.
5. **Moisture absorption:** Maintained or improved — more surface area is exposed for moisture uptake.
6. **Tensile strength:** Some reduction due to removal of supporting hemicellulose matrix and potential mechanical damage.
7. **Blendability:** Excellent with cotton, wool, silk, and synthetic fibres.
8. **Spinning system:** Compatible with ring spinning, open-end (rotor) spinning, air-jet spinning.

> **Example:** Cottonized flax fibres produced from dew-retted French flax after enzymatic treatment and mechanical separation achieved a linear density of 0.6 tex — comparable to coarse cotton — and were successfully spun into 30/1 ring-spun yarn on a standard cotton frame.

## 5. Natural Cottonisation (in Laundering)

1. A natural form of cottonisation occurs gradually during **repeated washing of linen fabrics**.
2. Each wash with alkaline detergent **slowly hydrolyses** the waxes and gums (pectin, hemicellulose matrix) that bind individual fibre cells together in the technical fibre bundle.
3. Over time (many washes), **individual cell ends begin to project above the fabric surface**, creating a **softer hand and slightly fuzzier texture**.
4. This explains why **old, well-washed linen becomes progressively softer** — a quality prized in antique linen.
5. However, excessive cottonisation through repeated washing **weakens the fabric** over time, as the inter-cell bonding that contributes to linen's strength is progressively disrupted.
6. The process is referred to in older textile literature as **"cottonising of linen"** (naturally occurring) vs. the industrial **"cottonisation"** (deliberate, commercial process).

> **Example:** A linen tablecloth used and washed weekly for 20+ years feels dramatically softer and more supple than a new piece of linen — this is natural cottonisation in action. Many collectors prize "well-washed vintage linen" for precisely this property.

## 6. Applications of Cottonized Flax

1. **Blended apparel textiles:**
   - Cotton/linen blends (25–50% cottonized flax + 50–75% cotton): T-shirts, knit tops, casual wear.
   - Linen/wool blends: Suiting fabric with improved comfort and moisture management.
   - Linen/silk blends: Luxury outerwear.
   - *Example:* Many "linen-cotton blend" shirts on the market use cottonized flax spun with cotton on conventional ring-spinning systems.

2. **Non-woven fabrics:**
   - Cottonized flax can be air-laid or wet-laid into non-woven structures.
   - Applications: Geotextiles, filtration media, horticultural mats.

3. **Automotive industry:**
   - Car interior panels use **flax fibre reinforced composites** for sound dampening and structural panels.
   - BMW, Mercedes-Benz, and Audi have used natural fibre composites (including cottonized flax) in door panels, parcel shelves, and trunk liners.
   - *Example:* BMW i3 uses kenaf and flax composite panels to reduce weight and improve sustainability metrics.

4. **Medical and hygiene textiles:**
   - Wound dressings and bandages benefiting from linen's absorbency and mild antibacterial properties.
   - Surgical gauze blends.
   - *Example:* Flax-based non-woven wound dressings are used in European medical facilities as sustainable alternatives to synthetic dressings.

5. **Technical composites:**
   - Cottonized tow used as reinforcement fibre in bio-based polymer matrices (PLA, PHB).
   - Aerospace and sporting goods applications research ongoing.

## 7. Comparison: Raw Flax vs. Cottonized Flax

| Feature | Raw Flax (Line Fibre) | Cottonized Flax |
|---------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Form | Bundle of 10–40 cells | Individual ultimate cells |
| Length | 25–150 cm | 25–40 mm |
| Diameter | 40–80 μm (bundle) | 10–25 μm (single cell) |
| Fineness | 5–10+ tex | 0.5–1.0 tex |
| Processing system | Wet spinning (linen) | Cotton ring/OE spinning |
| Blendability | Limited (with long fibres only) | Excellent (cotton, wool, synthetics) |
| Softness | Stiff, crisp | Soft, cotton-like |
| Cellulose purity | 64–80% | 80–90% (cleaned) |
| Non-cellulosic components | High (pectin, wax, hemicellulose) | Low (mostly removed) |
| End use | Fine linen fabric, industrial | Blended apparel, composites, non-wovens |
