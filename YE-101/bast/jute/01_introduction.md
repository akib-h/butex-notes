## 1.1 Definition

Jute is a **ligno-cellulosic**, natural, **bast** and **composite** fibre obtained from the bark (bast) of the jute plant (*Corchorus* species). Many **ultimate fibres** (1–4 mm each) are cemented together laterally and longitudinally by **lignin** (the natural cementing agent) to build a long technical fibre. It is therefore classified as:

- **Natural fibre** — grown from plants
- **Bast fibre** — extracted from the stem/bark of the plant
- **Cellulosic fibre** — primary component is cellulose
- **Ligno-cellulosic fibre** — also contains significant lignin (~10–13%)
- **Composite fibre** — multiple components (cellulose microfibrils + hemicellulose + lignin matrix) with different physical/chemical properties

> **Why "composite"?**  
> A composite material is made from two or more constituent materials with significantly different physical or chemical properties, which when combined produce a material with different characteristics from the individual components.  
> Jute consists of:  
> - **Material 1:** Ultrafine cellulosic fibrils  
> - **Material 2:** A matrix of hemicellulose and lignin  
> These two materials have different properties individually, but when combined they form the long, strong, stiff jute fibre — just like concrete (aggregate + cement). Hence, jute is called a **composite fibre**.

## 1.3 Why is Jute Called the "Golden Fibre"?

Jute is called the **Golden Fibre** for two primary reasons:

1. **Golden colour** — The raw fibre has a characteristic silky golden/brownish shine.
2. **High cash value** — Jute is one of the most affordable yet economically significant natural fibres in world trade.

Additional reasons:
- It is 100% biodegradable and environment-friendly.
- It plays a crucial role in environment protection — it does not emit toxic fumes or residue on combustion.
- It is one of the cheapest, strongest, and most versatile of all natural fibres.
- Considered the **"fibre of the future"** due to growing demand as a replacement for synthetic packaging.

## 1.4 Botanical Species (Origin / Source)

The principal two botanical species of jute are:

### i) *Corchorus olitorius* — **Tossa Jute** (Dark Jute)

- Also called **Bangla Tossa (BT)**
- Plant height: 5–15 feet
- Waterlogging: Normally **cannot** withstand
- Duration: 4–5 months
- Stem: Cylindrical; Branches: Branched but less vigorous
- Capsule: Elongated, 5–6 locules, seeds 25–40 in a single row in locule, with transverse partitions; 125–200 seeds/pod
- Seed weight: ~500 seeds/g
- Fibre quality: **Superior quality** — golden/reddish colour, finer texture

### ii) *Corchorus capsularis* — **White Jute**

- Also called **Bangla White (BW)**
- Plant height: 5–12 feet tall
- Waterlogging: **Can withstand**
- Duration: 3–5 months
- Stem: Cylindrical; Branches: Branched or unbranched
- Capsule: Rounded, 5 locular, seeds 7–10 in two rows in each locule, without transverse partitions; 35–40 in each fruit
- Seed weight: ~300 seeds/g

### Comparison Table: *Capsularis* vs *Olitorius*

| Character | *Capsularis* (White) | *Olitorius* (Tossa) |
|-----------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Height | 5–12 feet | 5–15 feet |
| Waterlogging | Can withstand | Normally cannot |
| Duration | 3–5 months | 4–5 months |
| Stem | Cylindrical | Cylindrical |
| Branches | Branched or unbranched | Branched, less vigorous |
| Capsule | Rounded, 5 locular | Elongated, 5–6 locular |
| Seeds/pod | 35–40 | 125–200 |
| Seed weight | 300 seeds/g | 500 seeds/g |
| Fibre quality | Good | Superior |

## 1.6 Why is Jute Called a Bast Fibre?

Fibres obtained from the **stem or bark** of certain vegetable plants are called **bast fibres**.

Examples: Jute, Flax, Hemp, Ramie, Kenaf

They occur in **dicotyledonous plants** between the outer bark and the woody central cylinder. Each fibre consists of bast or sclerenchymous cells overlapped in such a way that they produce continuous length of fibre. They provide **strength as well as flexibility** to the stem of the plant.

**Jute fibre** is obtained from the bark of the jute plant and also possesses the above qualities. Therefore, jute is called a **bast fibre**.

## 1.7 Environmental Significance

Jute is highly eco-friendly:

- **100% biodegradable** — decomposes within **1–2 years** after disposal (vs. hundreds of years for plastics)
- **Carbon sink** — one hectare of jute plants can consume approximately **15 tonnes of CO₂** from the atmosphere and release about **11 tonnes of O₂** during the 100-day jute growing season
- Cultivation **improves soil fertility** by shedding leaves in the field
- **Renewable** — annual crop, grows within 4–6 months
- Requires **less water** compared to other cash crops like cotton
- No toxic residue on combustion
