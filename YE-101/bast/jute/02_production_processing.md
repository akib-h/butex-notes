## 2.2 Requirements for Successful Cultivation

| Requirement | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| **Temperature** | High temperature up to **95°F** (35°C), minimum **80°F** (27°C) during growth |
| **Ideal range** | 24°C – 37°C |
| **Rainfall** | Over **40 inches** (160–200 cm); distributed so young plants have sufficient moisture, bulk of fall when crop is mature |
| **Soil type** | Well-penetrative, alluvial/loamy soils; river basins best; pH **4.8–5.8** |
| **Soil condition** | Well-preventive soil of fairly fine texture |
| **Seeds** | Suitable quality seeds |
| **Water supply** | Sufficient for retting plants and washing stripped fibres |
| **Labour** | Sufficient skilled labour to handle the crop at the proper time |
| **Market** | Facilities for placing fibres in the market |

---

## 2.3 Stage 1 — Land Preparation

Jute seeds are **very small**, so a very fine preparation of land is necessary.

**Process:**
1. Land is **ploughed and cross-ploughed**:
   - 1st ploughing: North–South direction
   - 2nd ploughing: East–West direction
2. This ploughing is repeated **6 to 8 times**
3. Between ploughings, the land is **laddered** to break down lumps of earth and level the ground

This ensures fine, uniform seedbed required for germination of tiny jute seeds.

---

## 2.4 Stage 2 — Sowing

**Time:** Sowing starts with the showers in **March or April** and continues till early June. Optimum time depends on onset of pre-monsoon rain and the variety.

- In Bangladesh, India, Myanmar, Nepal: sowing ranges from **late February to May**
- Sowing should be done preferably **in lines**

**Fertilisers used:**
- **Phosphorus (P₂O₅)**
- **Potassium/Potash (K₂O)**
- **Nitrogen (N)**

### Nutrient Requirements (per hectare for good crop)

For **Tossa jute** (yielding 3.1 t ha⁻¹ dry fibre):

| Nutrient | Amount |
|----------|--------|
| Nitrogen (N) | 65 kg |
| P₂O₅ | 52 kg |
| K₂O | 163 kg |
| CaO | 128 kg |
| MgO | 33 kg |

For **White jute** (yielding 2.0 t ha⁻¹ dry fibre):

| Nutrient | Amount |
|----------|--------|
| Nitrogen (N) | 84 kg |
| P₂O₅ | 37 kg |
| K₂O | 117 kg |
| CaO | 120 kg |
| MgO | 49 kg |

> **Note:** Nitrogen remains the main contributing nutrient. Nearly **18–21 kg extra jute fibre** may be obtained by the application of 1 kg ha⁻¹ of Sulphur (S).

### Sowing Methods

| Method | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Line Sowing** | Sowing done in lines — **latest/modern method** |
| **Broadcasting Sowing** | Sowing done randomly — **traditional method** |

### Seed Rate and Spacing

| Jute Type | Line Sowing (kg/ha) | Broadcast Casting (kg/ha) | Spacing (cm) | Plants/sq.m |
|-----------|--------------------|-----------------------------|--------------|-------------|
| *Olitorius* | 5 | 7 | 25 × 5 | 80 |
| *Capsularis* | 7 | 10 | 30 × 5 | 67 |

## 2.5 Stage 3 — Interculture

Interculture operations help manage the field after sowing:

### i) Raking
- Helps **uprooting the weeds** (আগাছা তোলা)
- Brings plant population to **30–45 plants per sq. m**
- Usually one raking between **two to three weeks** following sowing

### ii) Thinning
- Done to **remove excess jute plants** for better growing
- Followed by two to three weedings at an interval of 15–20 days

### iii) Weeding (আগাছা পরিষ্কার করা)
- Raking is followed by **hand weeding**
- Small hand-held wheel hoes or scrappers may also be used
- With subsequent crop growth and canopy development, weed growth is checked

### Irrigation
- One pre-sowing irrigation (if necessary) + **2–3 additional irrigations** at later stages
- Jute crop requires **500 mm of water**
- First irrigation after sowing; life irrigation on **4th day** after sowing
- Afterwards, irrigation once in **15 days**

### Drainage
- Appropriate drainage must be arranged to avoid stagnation of water during high rainfall

### Disease and Pest Management
- Jute is subject to attack by diseases and pests at different stages
- Losses: **10% by plant diseases** and **12% by insects** (recorded in Bangladesh, India, Nepal)
- Crop rotation practices minimise losses

---

## 2.6 Stage 4 — Harvesting

**Timing:**
- Jute is generally harvested when plants are at **early pod stage**
- Harvest index remains constant over **60–105 days** following sowing
- Reaches peak during **110–130 days** following sowing
- Optimal: harvested any time between **120 to 150 days** when the flowers have been shed

> **If harvested before flowering** → fibre remains **weak**  
> **If harvesting delayed beyond 120 days** → fibre becomes **coarse and lacks luster**

**Early harvesting gives good healthy fibres.**

**Process:**
1. Plants are usually cut **close to the ground by hand**
2. A curved, **sickle-type knife** is used
3. Plants are sorted by height and diameter
4. Assorted plants are tied into **bundles** (each bundle: **8–10 kg**)
5. Bundles are kept standing in the field for **3–4 days** (some sources say 3–7 days) for **defoliation** (leaves to shed/dry up)

---

## 2.7 Stage 5 — Retting

**Definition:** Retting is the process of **extracting fibre from the long-lasting life stem or bast** of the bast fibre plants. Fibres get loosened due to **decomposition of hard cell walls by the action of bacteria**.

### Types of Retting

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Water/Microbial Retting** | Most popular; century-old method; bundles submerged in soft running water |
| **Chemical Retting** | Boiling & applying chemicals |
| **Mechanical Retting** | Hammering |
| **Steam/Vapor/Dew Retting** | Steam or dew exposure |

**Water Retting (Most Common):**

1. After harvesting, jute stalks are tied into bundles and **submerged in soft running water**
2. Bundles steeped in water at least **60 cm to 90 cm depth**
3. Stalk stays submerged for **15–20 days** (complete; may require less time if jute quality is better)
4. At lower temperature and in running water, retting process may take about **one month**
5. Favourable temperature: **80°F to 85°F**

**To judge when retting is complete:**
- Make periodic checks on samples from different parts of the pond
- When fibres can be **separated from the stem at ease**, stripping, washing, and drying should be done as quickly as possible

> **Note:** The fibre extraction process of bast fibres in water retting is done by the farmers while standing under water.

---

## 2.8 Stage 6 — Stripping / Extraction (ছুড়ে তোলা)

**Definition:** Stripping is the process of **removing the fibres from the stalk** after the completion of retting.

### Two Methods of Fibre Stripping

**Method 1 — Individual Stem Stripping:**
- Bundles of stems are removed from water, allowed to drain off
- Each stem is stripped separately
- Fibres are made up into handfuls and then washed

**Method 2 — Bunch Stripping:**
- Worker stands in water
- Takes some stems in the left hand and **beats the roots of the stems with a wooden mallet**
- When root ends are sufficiently crushed, they are broken off
- Loose fibres are then drawn to permit easy separation from the rest of the stems
- The stripped fibres are then washed

---

## 2.9 Stage 7 — Washing

**Process:**
1. Extracted fibres are **washed in clean water**
2. The washing process consists of holding the extracted fibre bundle by the butt end and **jerking it through the water**
3. Entire dirt, gum, extraneous plant materials, and retting residues are removed thoroughly
4. The dark colour of fibres can be removed by **dipping them in tamarind water for 15 to 20 minutes** and again washing in clean water

---

## 2.10 Stage 8 — Drying

**Process:**
1. After extraction and washing, fibre is **spread over bamboo perch or bar** (bamboo railings) for drying
2. **Drying under mild sun** is recommended
3. Drying on bare ground is **discouraged** — causes contamination by dirt and other particles
4. Drying may require **2–7 days** depending on intensity of sunlight and relative humidity
5. Fibre needs thorough drying before storage

**Moisture content of dried fibres:**
- Ranges between **10–19%** in major jute growing countries
- **Lower moisture content** → higher storage life, better maintenance of colour and strength, and **higher price** for the farmer

---

## 2.11 Stage 9 — Bailing & Packing

**Process:**
1. The bailing of jute fibre is done according to **grading system**
2. Fibre is graded into: **Top, Middle, B, C, and X-Bottoms**
3. Packed into **Kutcha bales** of about **250 pounds (~113 kg)** for use in mills or jute market

**Storage:**
- Farmers generally store jute fibre in their **dwelling houses** on the floor with other crops
- In most cases these floors are **dampened grounds**
- Sometimes split bamboos are spread on the floors to prevent fibre from coming into contact with damp ground
- Small farmers sell fibre as soon as it is ready
- Intermediate dealers (middlemen) do not store for long
- **Jute balers** hold jute for longer time
