## 4.1 Overview — Physical Structure

Jute fibre structure can be studied at three levels:

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Macro-structure** | Visible features of the fibre (technical fibre, bundles) |
| **Micro-structure** | Ultimate cell structure (under microscope) |
| **Morphological structure** | Internal cell wall layers and molecular arrangement |

---

## 4.2 Anatomy of the Jute Stem

The jute plant stem has the following cross-sectional layers from outside to inside:

```
         [Outer surface]
              ↓
         EPIDERMIS (outer skin)
              ↓
         CORTEX (contains fibre bundles)
              ↓
         CAMBIUM
              ↓
         XYLEM (Central woody cylinder)
              ↓
         CENTRAL CANAL
```

- **Fibre bundles** are located in the **cortex** (between the outer bark and the woody central cylinder)
- The fibre bundles form a cylindrical sheath made with single fibres joined together by a **three-dimensional network** from top to bottom of the stem, known as **"meshy structure"**

## 4.3 Macro-Structure

### 4.3.1 Definition

Each fibre element visible by naked eye, of a raw jute reed available commercially, is basically a **group of about 5–15 ultimate cells**, cemented together laterally and longitudinally by means of inter-cellular materials (primarily non-cellulosic in composition).

### 4.3.2 Key Features

- **Multicellular fibre** — jute fibre is multicellular (unlike cotton, which is unicellular)
- Due to this, jute fibre is much **coarser than cotton**
- Due to random disintegration of cell bundles caused by microbial attack during retting, the number of ultimate cells per cross-section of a jute fibre **varies much along its length**
- This mechanism makes commercially available fibres not only coarser but also **highly irregular in diameter** along the fibre length
- These fibre features severely **limit the use of jute** in manufacturing fine yarns and lighter fabrics

### 4.3.3 Ultimate Cell Dimensions

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Length (L) | 0.8–6 mm; **Average: 2.5 mm** |
| Width/Breadth (B) | 10–25 µm; **Average: 18 µm** |
| L/B Ratio | Average: **110** |
| Number of ultimate cells in cross-section | **5–15** |
| Fibre length (Reed length) | **5–12 feet** |

### 4.3.4 Cross-sectional Shape

- The cross-section of the ultimate cells is found to be **polygonal with rounded corners**
- The layer of natural cement present between the ultimate cells is known as **middle lamella**
- Each ultimate cell has **thick cell wall** and **lumen** (the central canal) with more or less **oval cross-section**

## 4.4 Micro-Structure

### 4.4.1 Cell Wall Composition

The cell wall of each ultimate cell is composed of:

1. **Outer thin Primary Wall** — fibres lying in **criss-cross manner**
2. **Inner thick Secondary Wall** — fibres almost **parallelly arranged as right-hand spirals**

Both walls are composed of **ultra-fine microfibrils** (Figure-2), differing from each other in molecular architecture.

```
Micro-structure of a Jute Fibre Ultimate Cell:

         Lumen (central canal)
              ↑
         S₂ (Secondary wall, inner)
              ↑
         S₁ (Secondary wall, outer)
              ↑
         Lignin layer
              ↑
         Primary Wall
              ↑
         Middle Lamella (between cells)
              ↑
         Cuticle (outermost)
```

### 4.4.2 Microfibril Arrangement

| Wall Layer | Microfibril Arrangement |
|-----------|------------------------|
| **Primary Wall** | Lying in **criss-cross (random)** manner |
| **Secondary Wall** | Almost **parallelly arranged** as right-hand spirals |
| **Orientation angle** | **7–9°** with reference to the cell axis |

> **Mathematical Significance of Orientation Angle:**
> The orientation angle (θ = 7–9°) determines the mechanical properties of the fibre.
> 
> If a fibril is oriented at angle θ to the fibre axis, its contribution to axial stiffness can be expressed as:
> 
> ```
> E_axial = E_fibril × cos⁴(θ)
> ```
> 
> For θ = 8° (average):
> ```
> cos(8°) = 0.9903
> cos⁴(8°) = 0.9903⁴ ≈ 0.961
> ```
> So jute microfibrils contribute about **96.1%** of their intrinsic stiffness to the fibre axis — a high efficiency, making jute stiff and strong despite its low elongation.

### 4.4.3 Within Ultimate Cells

- The **ultrafine fibrils**, being purely cellulosic, are the **highly ordered crystalline region**
- The **inter-fibrillar locations** are less ordered regions — these accommodate:
  - Short chain hemicellulose molecules (to a large extent)
  - Bulky lignin molecules (to a little extent)
- Formation of cell walls and infiltration of hemicelluloses, lignin, and other incrusting materials takes place **in stages during plant growth**

## 4.5 Morphological Structure

### 4.5.1 Cell Wall Layers (Morphological)

The cell wall is made up of the following layers:

| Layer | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Primary Wall (P)** | First layer deposited during cell development; thin; composed of cellulose, pectin, and lignin |
| **Secondary Wall S1** | Outer secondary layer |
| **Secondary Wall S2** | Middle secondary layer — **cellulose attains highest concentration** here |
| **Secondary Wall S3** | Inner secondary layer |
| **Middle Lamella** | Region between cells — **lignin is most concentrated** here; acts as the cementing agent |

> As in all ligno-cellulosic fibres, these layers mainly contain cellulose, hemicelluloses, and lignin in varying amounts. The individual fibres are bonded together by a **lignin-rich region known as the middle lamella**.

### 4.5.2 Hemicellulose Role

- Microfibrils are **cross-linked together** by hemicellulose homopolymers
- Lignin **assists and strengthens** the attachment of hemicelluloses to microfibrils
- Two main types of hemicellulose in jute: **Xylans** and **Glucomannans**

---

## 4.6 Longitudinal and Cross-Sectional View

### Longitudinal View

- Commercial jute varies from **yellow to brown to greyish** in colour
- The bundle of fibres is held together by gummy material (**lignin**)
- By contrast with the regular lumen of flax, that of jute is **irregular** — it becomes narrow in places quite suddenly

### Cross-Sectional View

- **Polygonal** shaped cells with rounded corners
- Multiple ultimate cells visible
- Lumen (central oval/round canal) in each ultimate cell
- Middle lamella visible between cells

## 4.7 Jute vs Cotton — Structural Differences

| Feature | Jute Fibre | Cotton Fibre |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| **Cell structure** | Multicellular (5–15 ultimate cells) | Unicellular (single cell) |
| **Cellulose Content** | Moderate (~60–65%) | High (~90–95%) |
| **Lignin Content** | High (~12–13%); contributes to stiffness | Negligible; makes it soft and flexible |
| **Hemicellulose** | High (~14–20%); binds cellulose and lignin | Low; minor matrix material |
| **Primary Wall** | Thin; cellulose, pectin, lignin mix | Thin; mostly cellulose with pectin/waxes |
| **Secondary Wall** | Thicker; cellulose, lignin, hemicellulose | Thick; mostly highly crystalline cellulose |
| **Tertiary Wall** | Present; lignin + hemicellulose | Absent |
| **Microfibril Arrangement** | Less regular; random in primary/tertiary walls | Well-organized; helical in secondary wall |
| **Lumen** | Smaller, more rigid (thick walls) | Wider, collapsible; characteristic twisted ribbon |
| **Crystallinity** | Lower (~45–55%) | Higher (~65–70%) |
| **Flexibility** | Lower (lignin-rich) | High (no lignin) |
| **Water Absorption** | Lower (lignin reduces hydrophilicity) | Higher (pure cellulose is hydrophilic) |
| **Surface Texture** | Coarse and rough | Smooth and soft |
