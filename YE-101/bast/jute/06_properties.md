## 6.2 Physical Properties — Detailed Explanation

### 6.2.1 Strength (Tenacity)

- **Dry Tenacity:** 3.5–5 gm/denier (some sources: 3–4 gm/den)
- **Wet condition:** Lower than dry
- Jute loses strength when wet

> **Why jute fibre strength first increases then decreases in water?**
>
> **Initial Increase:**  
> Water absorption causes the fibres to **swell**, tightening the microfibrils and increasing inter-fibre bonding, which **temporarily boosts strength**.
>
> **Later Decrease:**  
> Prolonged soaking **breaks down binding agents** like hemicellulose and lignin, weakening the fibre structure and causing a **loss of strength**.

### 6.2.2 Elasticity

- **Breaking extension:** 1.7–1.8%
- **Elastic recovery:** Very low
- Less extension, high stiffness
- Jute fibre is **brittle** and can hold less twist
- Therefore, **emulsion** (batching oil) is added to make it soft and workable

### 6.2.3 Moisture Properties

```
Moisture Regain (MR) = (Weight of water in fibre / Oven dry weight) × 100

Standard MR = 13.75%

Under high humidity: Can absorb up to 23% water

Moisture Content (MC) = (Weight of water in fibre / Wet weight) × 100

Standard MC = 12.1%
```

**Relationship between MR and MC:**
```
MC = MR / (100 + MR) × 100

For jute: MC = 13.75 / (100 + 13.75) × 100
              = 13.75 / 113.75 × 100
              = 12.09% ≈ 12.1%  ✓
```

### 6.2.4 Specific Gravity

```
Specific Gravity of jute = 1.48–1.5

This means jute is 1.48–1.5 times denser than water.

Volume of 1 kg jute fibre = Mass / (SG × density of water)
                           = 1 kg / (1.5 × 1000 kg/m³)
                           = 0.000667 m³ = 667 cm³
```

Compared to: Cotton (1.54), Wool (1.31), Nylon (1.14)

### 6.2.5 Resiliency

- **Resiliency: BAD** — jute does not recover well from deformation
- Reason: High lignin content makes fibres stiff and rigid; low elastic recovery
- This is a major **disadvantage** for apparel applications

### 6.2.6 Why Jute is a Hard and Rough Fibre

1. **High Lignin Content (12–14%):** Lignin makes the fibres **rigid and stiff**
2. **Thick Cell Walls:** The ultimate cells of jute have thick walls and a small lumen, contributing to its **coarse texture**
3. **Low Elasticity:** Jute fibres have low flexibility, making them **less soft and more rigid**
4. **Processing Methods:** The retting process leaves natural resins, **adding to the roughness**

## 6.5 Chemical Properties

### 6.5.1 Effect of Acids

- Easily **damaged by hot dilute** or **cold concentrated acids**
- Cold weak acids do **not affect** it
- Example: Dissolved in **58% H₂SO₄** in warm condition (chemical identification test)

### 6.5.2 Effect of Alkalis

- **Resistant to alkalis** (cold dilute alkalis)
- Strong alkali (caustic soda/NaOH) degrades the strength
- Fibres lose weight when heated with caustic soda
- Treatment with strong NaOH converts jute into a **wool-like fibre**

### 6.5.3 Effect of Bleaches

- **Not affected by (resistant to) oxidizing and reducing agents** (general statement from some sources)
- However, jute **can be bleached but loses strength**
- Bleaching agents: H₂O₂, NaOCl, NaClO₂, Na₂O₂, CH₃COOH, KMnO₄, etc.
- Due to lignin content, **cannot be bleached to pure white**

### 6.5.4 Effect of Organic Solvents

- **Resistant to organic solvents**

### 6.5.5 Effect of Sunlight

- **Poor sunlight resistance** — scorches at high temperature
- Lignin undergoes photo-oxidation → **yellowing**
- UV light degrades both cellulose and lignin components

### 6.5.6 Dyeability

- **Good affinity for basic (cationic) dyes** — due to acidic hemicellulose side chains
- Light fastness: **Poor**
- Wash fastness: **Poor**
- Reason: Basic dyes are not strongly anchored to the cellulosic substrate

### 6.5.7 Effect of Micro-organisms

- **Good to excellent resistance** — better than cotton and linen
- **Not affected by moth and mildew**
- Soured (acidic) jute has good resistance to micro-organisms and insects
- Biological degradation is primarily by fungi and bacteria affecting the lignin component

### 6.5.8 Summary — Chemical Properties Table

| Chemical Agent | Effect |
|---------------|--------|
| Hot dilute acids | **Damaged easily** |
| Cold weak acids | **Not affected** |
| Strong alkalis (NaOH) | **Degrades strength** |
| Cold dilute alkalis | **Resistant** |
| Oxidizing bleaches | Bleachable but **loses strength** |
| Reducing bleaches | Resistant |
| Organic solvents | **Resistant** |
| Sunlight (UV) | **Poor resistance** — yellows |
| Heat | Burns rapidly |
| Basic dyes | **Good affinity** (poor fastness) |
| Mildew/moth | **Good resistance** |

## 6.6 Favourable (Advantageous) Properties

1. **Great antistatic properties**
2. **Good insulating properties**
3. **Low thermal conductivity**
4. **100% Biodegradable** — environment-friendly like cotton
5. **Cheap** in market
6. **Good acoustic insulating properties**
7. **Moderate moisture regain** (13.75%)
8. Can be **widely used** — Agriculture, Textile, Woven, Nonwoven sectors
9. Can be **blended** with natural and synthetic fibres
10. **Good dimensional stability**

---

## 6.7 Disfavourable (Disadvantageous) Properties

1. **Very low crease resistance**
2. **Poor drapeability**
3. Creates **shade effect** and becomes **yellowish** if sunlight is used
4. **Loses strength** when wetted
5. **Bad resiliency**
6. **Becomes subject to microbial attack** in humid climates
7. **Cannot be bleached to pure white**
8. **Poor light fastness and wash fastness** of dyes
9. **Brittle** nature
10. **Rough surface** — not suitable for apparel without treatment

## 6.8 Mathematical Examples — Property Calculations

### Example 1: Moisture Regain Calculation

> A sample of jute weighs **115 g** in standard atmospheric conditions and **100 g** when oven-dried.
> Calculate the Moisture Regain (MR).

```
MR = (Wet weight − Dry weight) / Dry weight × 100
MR = (115 − 100) / 100 × 100
MR = 15 / 100 × 100
MR = 15%

Standard MR of jute = 13.75%
This sample has slightly higher moisture than standard.
```

### Example 2: Tenacity vs Load

> A jute fibre of **fineness 2 tex** breaks at a load of **7 mN**. Calculate the tenacity in mN/tex and gm/denier.

```
Tenacity (mN/tex) = Breaking load (mN) / Linear density (tex)
                  = 7 mN / 2 tex
                  = 3.5 mN/tex

Converting to gm/den:
1 mN/tex = 0.1133 gf/den (conversion factor)
Tenacity = 3.5 × 0.1133 = 0.397 gf/den ≈ 0.4 gf/den

OR using direct conversion:
Tenacity (gf/den) = Tenacity (mN/tex) / 8.83
= 3.5 / 8.83 = 0.396 gf/den
```

### Example 3: Elongation and Elastic Recovery

> Jute has **1.8% elongation at break** and **poor elastic recovery (≈20%)**.  
> If a jute fibre is stretched by 1.5% of its length, what permanent deformation remains after removing the load?

```
Applied extension = 1.5% of length L → extension = 0.015L

Elastic recovery = 20% (of applied extension)
Recoverable extension = 20% × 0.015L = 0.003L

Permanent deformation = 0.015L − 0.003L = 0.012L
                      = 1.2% of original length

This means even at 80% of break extension, 1.2% permanent set remains — showing poor resiliency.
```
