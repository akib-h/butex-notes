## 1. Introduction

**Hemp** (*Cannabis sativa* L.) is one of the **oldest cultivated plants** in human history, with documented use spanning over **10,000 years**. It is a **bast fibre** crop — meaning the useful fibres are extracted from the **inner bark (phloem/bast)** of the plant's stem.

> **Key Definition:**
> Hemp fibre is a **natural cellulosic bast fibre** obtained from the **stems of the Cannabis sativa plant**, known for its exceptional strength, durability, biodegradability, and antimicrobial properties.

Hemp is also considered the **world's oldest and strongest natural textile fibre**. It was the dominant textile fibre in Europe until cotton became widespread in the 19th century.

### Hemp vs. Marijuana

| Feature | Industrial Hemp | Marijuana |
|---------|----------------|-----------|
| **THC Content** | < 0.3% (legal limit) | 3–15% |
| **Purpose** | Fibre, seeds, oil | Psychoactive use |
| **Plant appearance** | Similar | Similar |
| **Legal status** | Legal in most countries | Restricted in many |

> ⚠️ Industrial hemp for textile use has negligible psychoactive content. THC (tetrahydrocannabinol) levels are intentionally kept very low through selective breeding.

|-----------|---------|
| **Scientific name** | *Cannabis sativa* L. |

| **Soil requirement** | Well-drained, nitrogen-rich, non-acidic soil |

## 3. Structure of the Hemp Plant & Fibre

### 3.1 Cross-Section of Hemp Stem

```
Hemp Stem Cross-Section:
┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  Epidermis (outer skin)                 │
│  ────────────────────────────────────── │
│  Cortex / Bast Layer (contains fibres)  │  ← Bast fibres (long, strong)
│  ────────────────────────────────────── │
│  Cambium                                │
│  ────────────────────────────────────── │
│  Woody Core / Hurd (Shives)             │  ← Core fibres (short, woody)
│  ────────────────────────────────────── │
│  Pith (centre)                          │
└─────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### 3.2 Microscopic Structure of Hemp Fibre

**Longitudinal view:**
- The fibre is **uneven in diameter**
- Shows **joints and fractures (cracks)** on the surface
- Irregular node-like structures visible

**Cross-sectional view:**
- Polygonal cross-section (similar to linen)
- **Thick cell wall** with a **narrow central lumen**
- Multiple ultimate fibres (elementary fibres) grouped into fibre bundles

### 3.3 Cell Wall Architecture

The hemp fibre cell wall has a **layered structure**:
```
Primary cell wall (P)
    └── Secondary cell wall:
         ├── S1 (outer) — thin, spiral angle ~70°
         ├── S2 (middle) — thickest, spiral angle ~6°  ← main load-bearing layer
         └── S3 (inner) — thin
```

The **microfibril angle in S2** is very low (~6°), which contributes to hemp's high tensile strength.

## 4. Types of Hemp Fibre

### 4.1 Bast Fibres (Long Fibres)
- Located in the **outer layer (phloem)** of the stem
- **Length:** 5–55 mm (primary fibres: ~20 mm; technical fibres up to several metres)
- **Diameter:** 16–50 µm
- **Properties:** Strong, flexible, suitable for textiles and technical applications
- **Uses:** Rope, canvas, clothing, composites, industrial fabrics

### 4.2 Hurd Fibres / Core Fibres (Short Fibres)
- Located in the **woody inner core** of the stem
- **Short and brittle** — similar to wood fibres
- **Chemical composition:** Similar to hardwood (~40% cellulose, ~20% hemicellulose, ~20% lignin)
- **Uses:** Paper production, insulation, animal bedding, hempcrete, composite boards

> **Ratio:** Bast fibres make up approximately **20–30%** of the stem; the woody core makes up the remaining **70–80%**.

## 5. Chemical Composition

Hemp bast fibre is primarily **lignocellulosic** — composed of cellulose, hemicellulose, lignin, and pectin:

| Component | Percentage in Bast Fibre |
|-----------|------------------------|
| **Cellulose** | 53–91% |
| **Hemicellulose** | 4–18% |
| **Lignin** | 1–21% |
| **Pectin** | 1–17% |
| **Moisture/Wax** | ~8% |
| **Ash** | ~1% |

> ⚠️ **Note:** These values vary considerably depending on hemp variety (cultivar), growing conditions, retting method, and processing. For example, water-retted hemp may have higher cellulose content than dew-retted hemp.

### Role of Each Component:

1. **Cellulose:** Provides tensile strength and structural integrity; the primary load-bearing polymer
2. **Hemicellulose:** Acts as a matrix between cellulose microfibrils; contributes to moisture absorption
3. **Lignin:** Gives rigidity and stiffness; responsible for the coarse, scratchy texture — **removed for softer textiles**
4. **Pectin:** Acts as a cementing agent binding fibres together; broken down during retting

## 7. Mechanical Properties

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| **Tensile strength** | 270–900 MPa |
| **Young's modulus (stiffness)** | 23.5–90 GPa |
| **Elongation at break** | 1–3% |
| **Specific strength** | High (strong relative to weight) |
| **Comparison with cotton** | Hemp is 8× stronger than cotton |
| **Comparison with steel** | Hemp technical fibre is stronger than glass fibre |

> Hemp is described as the **strongest natural textile fibre** — longer, stronger, more lustrous, and more absorbent than cotton.

## 8. Fibre Extraction & Processing

### 8.1 Harvesting
- Hemp is harvested when the plant reaches **early flowering stage** (optimal fibre quality)
- **Mechanical harvesting** is standard using specialized machines (similar to flax harvesters)
- After cutting, the stems are left in the field for **initial drying**

### 8.2 Processing Value Chain

```
Cultivation → Harvesting → Retting → Breaking → Scutching → Hackling → Spinning → Weaving
```

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Retting** | Decomposition of pectin binding fibres to the stem (microbial or chemical) |
| **Breaking** | Mechanical crushing of the woody core |
| **Scutching** | Beating out the woody core (shives) from the retted fibres |
| **Hackling** | Combing fibres to align them and remove short fibres/impurities |
| **Spinning** | Twisting fibres into yarn |
| **Weaving/Knitting** | Forming fabric |

## 9. Retting Methods

**Retting** is the controlled microbial or chemical decomposition of pectin and other non-cellulosic components to separate fibres from the stalk.

### 9.1 Dew Retting (Field Retting)
- Hemp stems spread on field for **3–5 weeks**
- **Moisture from rain, dew, and snow** facilitates microbial action
- **Advantages:** Low cost, no equipment needed
- **Disadvantages:** Weather-dependent; uneven quality; slow (up to 5 weeks); produces coarse, light-brown fibre
- **Most common method** globally

### 9.2 Water Retting
- Stems submerged in **ponds, rivers, or tanks** of water
- Anaerobic bacteria break down pectin in **1–4 weeks**
- **Advantages:** Higher quality, more uniform fibre; lighter colour
- **Disadvantages:** Creates **water pollution** (foul-smelling effluent); now largely banned in many countries

### 9.3 Enzymatic Retting
- Uses specific enzymes (pectinases, hemicellulases) to break down pectin
- **Advantages:** Controlled process; high fibre quality; suitable for composites
- **Disadvantages:** High enzyme cost; not widely commercialized for hemp yet
- **Best for:** Technical applications requiring high cellulose content

### 9.4 Steam Explosion
- High-pressure steam applied to stems, then rapidly released
- The pressure differential physically disrupts the fibre-hurd bond
- **Advantages:** Fast, no chemicals needed
- **Disadvantages:** Can damage fibre quality; high energy cost

### 9.5 Osmotic Degumming
- Uses salt solutions for fibre separation
- **Advantages:** Controlled environment
- **Disadvantages:** Requires post-processing washing

### 9.6 Mechanical Decortication
- Purely mechanical separation using rollers and beaters
- **Advantages:** No water use; fast; can be done without retting
- **Disadvantages:** Lower fibre quality; more shive contamination

## 10. Spinning & Yarn Production

Hemp fibres can be processed into yarn using three main spinning systems:

### 10.1 Traditional Wet Spinning (Flax/Hemp System)
- Long bast fibres are hackling-combed and wet-spun (soaked in warm water during spinning)
- Produces **fine, strong linen-type yarn**
- **High quality but labour-intensive**

### 10.2 Cotton Spinning (After Decortication/Cottonization)
- Hemp fibres treated to make them shorter and softer (cottonization)
- Processed on standard cotton ring-spinning equipment
- **More accessible** — can use existing cotton industry infrastructure

### 10.3 Wool Spinning System
- Shorter hemp fibres (tow) processed similarly to wool
- Suitable for **coarser, bulkier yarns**

## 11. Industrial & Textile Applications

### 11.1 Textiles & Apparel
1. T-shirts, jeans, dresses, jackets
2. Sports clothing and activewear
3. Underwear and socks
4. Bed sheets, towels, pillowcases
5. Hats, shoes, bags, and luggage

> **Blending:** Hemp is commonly blended with cotton, linen, and silk to improve softness and texture.

### 11.2 Industrial & Technical Applications
1. **Rope and twine** — marine, agricultural, and construction use
2. **Canvas** — sails, tarpaulins, tents
3. **Composites** — automotive interiors, building panels
4. **Geotextiles** — erosion control mats
5. **Insulation boards** — eco-friendly building insulation

### 11.3 Paper Production
1. Hemp is used for paper in Europe due to its **low lignin content** (less bleaching required)
2. High-quality paper: banknotes, cigarette paper, bible paper
3. **Hemp paper lasts much longer** than wood-based paper

### 11.4 Construction
1. **Hempcrete:** Hemp hurd + lime binder → strong, lightweight, insulating building material
2. Fibreboard and composite panels

### 11.5 Automotive Industry
1. Hemp fibre-reinforced thermoplastics for car interior panels, door liners, dashboards
2. Replaces fibreglass in some composite applications

## 12. Advantages & Disadvantages

### ✅ Advantages
1. **Extremely strong** — stronger than cotton, linen, and most natural fibres
2. **Naturally antimicrobial** — resists mould, mildew, and bacteria
3. **UV protection** — blocks UV radiation
4. **Breathable and moisture-wicking**
5. **Biodegradable** — fully breaks down in the environment
6. **Sustainable crop** — requires no pesticides, enriches soil, grows rapidly
7. **Durable** — fabrics last longer than cotton
8. **Hypoallergenic** — suitable for sensitive skin
9. **Versatile** — used across 25,000+ product categories (food, fibre, fuel, medicine)

### ❌ Disadvantages
1. **Coarse texture** — natural hemp fabric can feel rough; lignin removal required for softness
2. **Wrinkles easily** — prone to creasing
3. **Slow dyeing** — less affinity for dyes than cotton; requires more dye
4. **Processing complexity** — retting is time-consuming and can pollute water
5. **Legal complications** — confusion with marijuana leads to regulatory barriers in some countries
6. **Higher cost** — currently more expensive than cotton due to lower production volumes
7. **Limited commercial processing infrastructure**

## 14. Examples & Case Studies

### Example 1: Hemp in Marine Applications
- Hemp rope has been used for centuries in sailing ships due to its strength, saltwater resistance, and natural rot-resistance
- **Example:** Viking longships, British naval ships (HMS Victory used hemp rope in its rigging)
- Today, hemp composite panels are used in yacht hulls as an alternative to fibreglass

### Example 2: Hemp in Automotive Industry (Mercedes-Benz)
- Mercedes-Benz has been using **hemp-reinforced composite panels** for car interiors since the 1990s
- Hemp fibre provides similar strength to fibreglass at lower weight and environmental cost
- Other manufacturers (BMW, Audi, Ford) have followed suit

### Example 3: Hempcrete Building (France)
- The oldest known hempcrete building exists in **Nogent-sur-Seine, France** (built in the 6th century)
- Modern hempcrete buildings are being constructed across Europe for their superior insulation and CO₂ absorption
- **Hempcrete is carbon negative** — absorbs more CO₂ than is produced in its manufacture

### Example 4: Hemp Paper (USA Founding Documents)
- The first drafts of the **US Declaration of Independence** and Constitution were written on hemp paper
- Hemp paper is more durable than wood paper — the 1976 US Bicentennial edition used hemp paper

### Example 5: Traditional Bangladeshi Hemp Industry
- Bangladesh is one of the traditional cultivators of hemp for jute-like coarse textile applications
- Local artisans use dew-retted hemp to make **gunny bags, ropes, and mats**

> 📌 **Key Takeaway for Exams:**
> Hemp is a **bast fibre** from *Cannabis sativa*, consisting of **53–91% cellulose**. It is the **strongest natural textile fibre**, with unique antimicrobial, UV-protective, and biodegradable properties. The two main fibre types are **bast fibres (long, strong, textile-grade)** and **hurd fibres (short, woody, used for paper and construction)**. **Retting** is the critical fibre extraction step.
