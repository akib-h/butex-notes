## 1. Introduction

**Ramie** (*Boehmeria nivea*) is one of the **oldest natural textile fibres**, with a history of use in China dating back over **6,000 years**. It is also known as **China grass**, **rhea** (in India and Philippines), or **grass cloth** (in the trade). Ramie belongs to the **nettle family (Urticaceae)** and is a **bast fibre** — extracted from the inner bark of the plant stem.

> **Key Definition:**
> Ramie is a **natural cellulosic bast fibre** obtained from the phloem (inner bark) of *Boehmeria nivea*, distinguished by its extraordinary whiteness, brightness, silky lustre, and being among the **strongest and stiffest natural fibres** known.

Ramie is sometimes called the **"textile of kings"** due to its silky appearance and association with fine fabric production in ancient China and Egypt.

## 2. Botanical Origin & Cultivation

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Scientific name** | *Boehmeria nivea* (White ramie) / *Boehmeria nivea* var. *tenacissima* (Green ramie) |
| **Family** | Urticaceae (Nettle family) |
| **Origin** | East Asia (China, Southeast Asia) |
| **Plant type** | Perennial shrub |
| **Plant height** | 1–2.5 metres |
| **Leaf type** | Heart-shaped, white/silver underside (White ramie) or green underside (Green ramie) |
| **Crop cycle** | Perennial — can be harvested **3–6 times per year** |
| **Climate** | Tropical and subtropical; warm and humid |
| **Soil** | Well-drained, fertile soils; sensitive to waterlogging |
| **Top producers** | China (>90% of world production), Philippines, Brazil, India, South Korea |
| **Productive life** | A single plantation can yield fibres for **6–20 years** |

### Two Varieties of Ramie:
1. **White Ramie** (*B. nivea*) — more commercially important; white hairy underside on leaves
2. **Green Ramie** (*B. tenacissima*) — green underside; grown more in tropical Southeast Asia; also called "rhea"

## 3. Structure of Ramie Fibre

### 3.1 Microscopic Structure

**Longitudinal view:**
- The fibre shows a **cylindrical** shape with a **smooth surface**
- Visible **cross-markings** (nodes) at irregular intervals
- **No twist** (unlike cotton)
- The fibre ends are **blunt and rounded**

**Cross-sectional view:**
- **Irregular polygonal shape** — wider and flatter than linen
- Contains a very **large, prominent lumen** (central hollow channel)
- **Thick cell walls**
- The lumen may collapse in dry conditions, giving the fibre a folded or irregular appearance

### 3.2 Structural Characteristics

```
Ramie Fibre Structure:
┌────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  Primary Cell Wall (P)                 │
│  Secondary Cell Wall:                  │
│    S1 — outer thin layer               │
│    S2 — thick main layer (cellulose)   │ ← Highly crystalline cellulose
│    S3 — inner thin layer               │
│  Lumen (hollow centre — large)         │
└────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Degree of Polymerisation (DP):** Ramie cellulose has a very high DP (~6,000–8,000), which contributes to its outstanding strength and stiffness.

## 4. Chemical Composition

| Component | Percentage |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Cellulose** | 68–76% |
| **Hemicellulose** | 13–16% |
| **Lignin** | 0.6–1.0% (very low) |
| **Pectin** | 1.9–2.0% |
| **Wax/Fat** | 0.3% |
| **Moisture** | ~8–10% |
| **Ash** | ~2% |

### Key Chemical Facts:

1. **Very low lignin content (~0.6–1%)** — This makes ramie naturally very white and bright, and easier to bleach than hemp or jute
2. **High crystallinity (~70%)** — The cellulose chains are highly ordered, contributing to exceptional stiffness
3. **Very high α-cellulose content** — purer form of cellulose than most other natural fibres
4. **Requires degumming** — The raw fibre contains significant gums and pectins that must be removed for textile use

## 5. Physical Properties

| Property | Value/Description |
|----------|------------------|
| **Colour** | Naturally white (whitest of all natural fibres) |
| **Lustre** | High silky lustre — resembles silk |
| **Density** | 1.5 g/cm³ |
| **Fibre length** | 40–250 mm (individual ultimate fibre) |
| **Fibre diameter** | 40–60 µm |
| **Moisture regain** | 7–8% |
| **Thermal properties** | Excellent conductor of heat; cool to touch |
| **UV resistance** | Good |
| **Resistance to rot** | Excellent — does not rot or mildew easily |
| **Dye affinity** | Good |
| **Lustre on wetting** | Increases (unlike cotton) |
| **Biodegradability** | Fully biodegradable |

### Notable Physical Characteristics:
1. **Naturally white** — No bleaching required for white fabric (lowest lignin = whitest fibre)
2. **Silky appearance** — Often compared to silk in appearance and lustre
3. **Stiff** — Stiffer than other bast fibres (this limits its drapability)
4. **Does not wrinkle** as badly as linen
5. **Gets stronger when wet** (like linen)

## 6. Mechanical Properties

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| **Tensile strength** | 400–1000 MPa |
| **Young's modulus** | 44–128 GPa |
| **Elongation at break** | 1.2–3.8% |
| **Stiffness** | **Highest among all natural cellulosic fibres** |
| **Wet strength** | Increases by ~25% when wet |
| **Comparison** | Stronger and stiffer than flax; 3× stronger than cotton |

> Ramie has an average fibre **length of 40–250 mm**, making it **longer, stronger, and stiffer than flax fibres** — a remarkable quality among natural fibres.

## 7. Fibre Extraction & Processing

The extraction of ramie fibre is complex and requires several stages:

### 7.1 Harvesting
- Stems are harvested when the plant begins to **flower** (maximum cellulose content)
- Cut close to the ground; **3–6 harvests per year** possible from a single plantation
- Harvested using hand tools or mechanical cutters

### 7.2 Decortication
- The **outer green bark** is removed from the stem mechanically
- A **decorticator machine** scrapes and separates the ribbons of inner bast from the outer bark and woody core
- This produces **raw ramie ribbons** (green/wet) containing:
  - Cellulosic fibres
  - Gums, pectins, and waxes (must be removed)

### 7.3 Degumming (Critical Step)
Ramie contains significant gums that must be removed — this is the **most important and challenging step** in ramie processing (see Section 8).

### 7.4 Washing & Drying
- After degumming, fibres are washed thoroughly to remove chemicals
- Dried gently to preserve fibre quality

### 7.5 Hackling & Combing
- Dried fibres are hackled (combed) to align them and remove short fibres and impurities
- Produces **long-line combed ramie** for fine yarns and **ramie tow** for coarser applications

## 8. Degumming Process

**Degumming** is the removal of non-cellulosic materials (gums, pectins, waxes, lignin) from raw ramie fibres. It is the most critical and technically demanding step because ramie's gum content is very high (~35% of raw fibre by weight).

### 8.1 Chemical Degumming
```
Alkali treatment (NaOH) → Bleaching → Neutralization → Washing → Drying
```
- **NaOH (caustic soda)** at high temperature dissolves gums and pectins
- Most commonly used industrial method
- Can damage fibres if conditions are not carefully controlled

### 8.2 Biological Degumming (Enzymatic)
- Uses **pectinase, hemicellulase, and other enzymes** to selectively remove gums
- **Advantages:** Gentler on fibres; more environmentally friendly
- **Disadvantages:** Slower and more expensive

### 8.3 Steam Explosion
- Similar to hemp processing — high-pressure steam disrupts gum-fibre bonds
- **Advantages:** No chemicals; fast
- **Disadvantages:** May reduce fibre length

### 8.4 Retting
- Microbial action breaks down gums (similar to hemp/flax retting)
- **Advantages:** Natural, low cost
- **Disadvantages:** Inconsistent quality; slow

> ⚠️ **Degumming is the primary reason why ramie processing is expensive** — it requires significant energy, chemicals, and water, limiting large-scale commercialization.

## 9. Spinning & Fabric Formation

### 9.1 Spinning
- Degummed ramie can be spun using:
  - **Wet spinning** (flax spinning system) — for fine yarns
  - **Cotton ring spinning** — after cottonization/shortening
  - **Open-end spinning** — for coarser applications

### 9.2 Blending
- Ramie is commonly blended with:
  - **Cotton** — improves softness and drape; reduces cost
  - **Polyester** — improves wrinkle resistance and dimensional stability
  - **Wool** — adds warmth; improves handle
  - **Silk** — produces luxurious, high-strength fabrics

### 9.3 Fabric Characteristics
- **Ramie fabric is:**
  - Crisp and smooth
  - Slightly stiff (higher stiffness than linen)
  - Bright white (naturally)
  - Strong and durable
  - Cool and absorbent — excellent for summer wear

## 10. Industrial & Textile Applications

### 10.1 Clothing & Apparel
1. Summer clothing — shirts, blouses, dresses (breathable, cool)
2. Sweaters and knitwear (often blended with wool or acrylic)
3. Ethnic and traditional garments (China, Philippines: "piña-ramie" blends)

### 10.2 Home Textiles
1. Tablecloths, napkins, and runners
2. Bed linens and pillow covers
3. Curtains and upholstery fabric
4. Handkerchiefs

### 10.3 Industrial Applications
1. **Fishing nets** — excellent strength; resists saltwater degradation
2. **Packaging materials** — ropes, sacks, twine
3. **Industrial belting and hose covers**
4. **Paper production** — specialty paper (bank notes, cigarette paper)
5. **Fibre-reinforced composites** — ramie/PP and ramie/PLA composites for automotive and construction

### 10.4 Medical & Technical Uses
1. Surgical sutures (historical use)
2. Filter paper and technical fabrics
3. Geotextiles

## 11. Advantages & Disadvantages

### ✅ Advantages
1. **Exceptionally strong** — strongest and stiffest natural cellulosic fibre
2. **Naturally white** — lowest lignin content means natural brightness; easy to bleach
3. **Resistant to bacteria, mold, and mildew**
4. **Lustrous appearance** — resembles silk; premium look
5. **Stronger when wet** — maintains strength even when damp
6. **Excellent dimensional stability**
7. **Breathable and cool** — ideal for hot climates
8. **Perennial crop** — 3–6 harvests/year; no replanting needed for years
9. **High cellulose content** — excellent for industrial uses

### ❌ Disadvantages
1. **Stiff and brittle** — limited flexibility; breaks with repeated folding
2. **Difficult and expensive to process** — degumming is chemically intensive
3. **Poor elasticity** — low elongation at break; not stretchy
4. **Wrinkle-prone** in blended fabrics
5. **Lower drape** compared to cotton and silk
6. **Labour-intensive harvesting** — decortication is not fully mechanized
7. **Limited dyeing affinity** in some colour ranges
8. **Expensive** — processing costs make it pricier than cotton and jute

## 13. Examples & Case Studies

### Example 1: Ancient Egyptian Mummy Wrappings
- **Ramie cloth** (grass cloth) has been found in Egyptian mummy wrappings dating to approximately **5000–3000 BCE**
- The fibre's exceptional resistance to microbial degradation and moisture made it ideal for preservation
- Modern analysis confirmed the fibres were ramie, not linen as initially assumed

### Example 2: Traditional Philippine Textiles
- The Philippines produces **"piña-ramie"** fabric — a blend of pineapple fibre (piña) and ramie
- Used for traditional **Barong Tagalog** (national formal wear) and **Terno** dresses
- Highly prized for its lightweight, semi-transparent, and lustrous quality

### Example 3: Ramie in Chinese History
- **"Grass cloth"** made from ramie was documented in Chinese records as early as **4000 BCE**
- Ancient Chinese silk roads exported ramie fabric to the Middle East and Europe
- Ramie was used for **imperial robes** in ancient China due to its silky appearance

### Example 4: Ramie-Reinforced Composites (Modern Research)
- Studies on **PP/ramie** and **PLA/ramie** composites show excellent mechanical properties
- Ramie fibre composites are being explored for **automotive interior panels, furniture components**, and **packaging**
- Research by Scielo shows ramie composites can achieve tensile modulus of up to **25 GPa** in aligned configurations

### Example 5: Ramie in Bangladesh & India
- Locally called **"China grass"** or **"Boehmeria"**
- Used traditionally for making **coarse cloth, ropes, and fishing nets**
- Small-scale cultivation continues in North Bengal and Assam (India)
