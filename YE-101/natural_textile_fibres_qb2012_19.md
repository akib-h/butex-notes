## General Fiber Concepts

### 1. What is Fiber Morphology?

**Fiber morphology** refers to the form and structure of fibers, including both external appearance and internal organization.

**Key Aspects:**
- Cross-sectional shape (circular, kidney-shaped, polygonal, etc.)
- Longitudinal features (convolutions, striations, nodes)
- Surface characteristics (scales, grooves, smoothness)
- Size dimensions (diameter, length)
- Internal structure visible under microscopy

### 3. "All Textile Fibres are Fibres, but All Fibres are Not Textile Fibres"

**Requirements for Textile Fibers:**

| Criterion | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Length-to-Diameter Ratio** | > 1000:1 (minimum) |
| **Strength** | Sufficient to withstand processing |
| **Flexibility** | Bend without breaking |
| **Cohesiveness** | Ability to stick together |
| **Uniformity** | Reasonable consistency |
| **Elongation** | 5-50% elastic recovery |
| **Spinability** | Can be converted into yarn |

**Examples:**
- ✅ Cotton, wool, silk, polyester = Textile fibers

### 5. Staple Fiber vs Filament

| Feature | Staple Fiber | Filament |
|---------|-------------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Short, discrete fibers | Continuous, very long fibers |
| **Length** | Few cm to meters | Kilometers (virtually unlimited) |
| **Natural Examples** | Cotton, wool, jute, flax | Silk |
| **Man-made Examples** | Cut polyester, cut rayon | Polyester filament, nylon |
| **Processing** | Must be spun into yarn | Can be used directly or twisted |
| **Yarn Surface** | Hairy, fuzzy | Smooth |
| **Fabric Feel** | Warm, soft, breathable | Smooth, cool, lustrous |
| **Cost** | Generally lower | Varies |

### 6. Degree of Polymerization (DP)

**Definition**: The number of repeating structural units (monomers) in a polymer chain.

**Formula:**
```
DP = Molecular Weight of Polymer / Molecular Weight of Repeating Unit
```

**Examples:**

| Fiber | Typical DP |
|-------|-----------|
| Cotton cellulose | 10,000-15,000 |
| Flax cellulose | 8,000-10,000 |
| Jute cellulose | 5,000-7,000 |
| Wool (keratin) | 500-3,000 |
| Nylon 6,6 | 50-100 |
| Silk fibroin | 200-500 |

**Characteristics of Fiber-Forming Polymers:**

1. **Linear Structure** - Chains must be linear, not branched
2. **High Molecular Weight** - DP typically > 200
3. **Regular Structure** - Repeating units allow crystallization
4. **Strong Intermolecular Forces** - H-bonds, van der Waals forces
5. **Functional Groups** - Enable dyeing and chemical reactions
6. **Chain Flexibility** - Balance between rigidity and flexibility
7. **Thermal Stability** - Withstand processing temperatures
8. **Chemical Stability** - Resist degradation

### 7. Moisture Regain

**Definition**: Percentage of moisture in material relative to oven-dry weight.

**Formula:**
```
Moisture Regain (%) = [(Wet Weight - Dry Weight) / Dry Weight] × 100
```

**Standard Values (at 65% RH, 20°C):**

| Fiber | Moisture Regain (%) |
|-------|---------------------|
| Cotton | 8.5 |
| Flax | 12.0 |
| Jute | 13.75 |
| Wool | 16-18 |
| Silk | 11.0 |
| Polyester | 0.4 |
| Nylon 6,6 | 4.5 |
| Acrylic | 1-2 |

**Importance:**
- Affects comfort and wearability
- Influences static electricity
- Impacts dimensional stability
- Determines dyeing properties

| Aspect | Natural Fibers | Man-Made Fibers |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| **Source** | Plants, animals, minerals | Synthetic polymers or regenerated cellulose |

## Cotton Fiber

### 1. Matured Cotton Fiber

**Definition**: Cotton fiber that has completed its growth cycle with full development of cell wall and optimal physical properties.

**Classification by Maturity:**

| Type | Cell Wall | Lumen | Characteristics |
|------|-----------|-------|----------------|
| **Mature** | Thick (>0.75 ratio) | Small | Good strength, normal convolutions, good dyeability |
| **Semi-Mature** | Medium (0.50-0.75) | Medium | Intermediate properties |
| **Immature/Dead** | Thin (<0.50) | Large | Poor strength, ribbon-like, poor dye uptake, transparent |

**Maturity Ratio:**
```
Maturity Ratio = (Cell Wall Thickness / Fiber Diameter) × 100
```

### 2. Convolution Development Stages

**Growth Timeline:**

```
DAY 0-20: PRIMARY WALL FORMATION
┌─────────┐
│    ○    │  ← Large lumen
│  ┌───┐  │     Thin primary wall
│  │   │  │     Cell elongation
│  └───┘  │
└─────────┘

DAY 20-45: SECONDARY WALL DEPOSITION
┌─────────┐
│  ████   │  ← Secondary wall thickens
│  ┌─┐    │     Cellulose layers form
│  │ │    │     Lumen shrinks
│  └─┘    │
└─────────┘

DAY 45-50: MATURATION & DRYING
┌─────────┐
│ ∿∿∿∿∿   │  ← Fiber collapses
│  ∽∽∽    │     Convolutions form
│   ∿∿    │     Spiral twisting
└─────────┘
```

**Mechanism:**
1. **Elongation Phase** (0-20 days)
   - Fiber grows in length
   - Primary wall forms
   - Cytoplasm fills cell

2. **Thickening Phase** (20-45 days)
   - Daily cellulose deposition
   - Secondary wall builds up
   - 20-30 concentric layers

3. **Maturation** (45-50 days)
   - Water loss from fiber
   - Collapse and flattening
   - Twisting due to:
     - Non-uniform wall thickness
     - Differential drying stress
     - Spiral fibril arrangement

**Result:** 150-300 convolution reversals per inch

### 4. Chemical Properties of Cotton

**Chemical Composition:**

| Component | Percentage |
|-----------|------------|
| Cellulose | 88-96% |
| Water | 6-8% |
| Protein | 1.0-1.9% |
| Pectin | 0.4-1.2% |
| Wax | 0.4-1.2% |
| Ash | 0.7-1.6% |
| Organic acids | 0.5-1.0% |

**Reactions:**

1. **Acids**
   - Dilute/cold: Minimal effect
   - Concentrated/cold: Swelling, strength loss
   - Hot concentrated: Dissolution (hydrolyzes to glucose)

2. **Alkalis**
   - Dilute: Minimal damage
   - Concentrated/cold: **Mercerization** (increases luster, strength, dye affinity)
   - Hot concentrated: Degradation

3. **Oxidizing Agents**
   - Controlled: Bleaching (H₂O₂, NaClO)
   - Excessive: Yellowing, degradation

4. **Organic Solvents**
   - Generally resistant
   - Dissolves in special solvents (Schweizer's reagent, cuprammonium hydroxide)

5. **Biodegradation**
   - Susceptible to mildew in damp conditions
   - Attacked by cellulolytic enzymes

### 5. Ginning

**Definition**: Mechanical separation of cotton lint (fibers) from seeds after harvesting.

**Objectives:**
- Separate fibers from seeds efficiently
- Remove trash and foreign matter
- Maintain fiber quality
- Minimize damage
- Produce clean, spinnable lint

**Types of Ginning:**

#### A. Saw Ginning

```
SAWGIN PROCESS

   Feed Hopper
        ↓
   ┌─────────────────┐
   │  Spike Rollers  │ ← Regulate flow
   └────────┬────────┘
            ↓
   ┌─────────────────────┐
   │  ╔═══════════════╗ │
   │  ║  Saw Cylinder ║ │
   │  ║  60-100 Saws  ║ │← 400-600 RPM
   │  ╚═══════════════╝ │
   │         ↕           │
   │  Grid Bars ═══════ │← Narrow spacing
   └────┬─────────┬─────┘
        ↓         ↓
    Lint Out  Seeds Out
```

**Characteristics:**
- **Speed**: 120-150 kg/hour
- **Best For**: Short & medium staple (Upland cotton)
- **Advantages**: High capacity, efficient
- **Disadvantages**: More fiber damage, nep formation

#### B. Roller Ginning (McCarthy)

```
ROLLER GIN PROCESS

   Feed System
        ↓
   ┌────────────────────┐
   │  Leather Roller    │← 350-450 RPM
   │        +           │
   │  Stationary Knife  │← Oscillating motion
   │        │           │
   │   Grid Bars ═══    │
   └───┬──────────┬─────┘
       ↓          ↓
   Lint Out  Seeds Out
```

**Characteristics:**
- **Speed**: 30-40 kg/hour
- **Best For**: Long staple (Egyptian, Pima, Sea Island)
- **Advantages**: Minimal damage, excellent quality
- **Disadvantages**: Slow, expensive

**Comparison:**

| Feature | Saw Gin | Roller Gin |
|---------|---------|------------|
| Speed | Fast (4-5 bales/hr) | Slow (0.5-1 bale/hr) |
| Fiber Damage | Higher | Minimal |
| Staple Length | Short-Medium | Long |
| Neps | More | Fewer |
| Cost | Lower | Higher |
| Cotton Type | Upland | Egyptian, Pima |

### 6. Why Cotton is Stronger When Wet

Cotton exhibits 10-20% strength increase when wet due to:

1. **Hydrogen Bond Rearrangement**
   - Water molecules penetrate between cellulose chains
   - Break existing weak H-bonds
   - Form new, stronger cellulose-water-cellulose bonds
   - More efficient bonding arrangement

2. **Molecular Swelling**
   - Water absorption causes fiber expansion
   - Better molecular alignment
   - Increased crystalline order

3. **Stress Distribution**
   - Water acts as lubricant
   - Better load distribution
   - Reduced stress concentration

4. **Plasticization**
   - Water plasticizes amorphous regions
   - Molecular chains slide and realign
   - Better stress transfer

**Practical Implications:**
- Safe for vigorous washing
- Suitable for marine applications
- No damage from wet processing

### 7. Cotton Fiber Identification

**Non-Technical Tests:**

1. **Visual**
   - Soft, dull, creamy-white
   - Staple fiber form

2. **Touch**
   - Soft, warm hand feel
   - Natural waviness

3. **Burning**
   - Burns readily, yellow flame
   - Smell: Burning paper
   - Ash: Soft, gray, feathery
   - Continues burning after flame removed

**Technical Tests:**

1. **Microscopy**
   - **Longitudinal**: Ribbon-like with convolutions
   - **Cross-section**: Kidney-shaped/collapsed tube with lumen

2. **Chemical Staining**
   - Iodine + H₂SO₄ → Blue color
   - Chlor-zinc-iodine → Red-violet

3. **Solubility**
   - 70% H₂SO₄ → Dissolves
   - Cuprammonium hydroxide → Dissolves
   - Caustic soda → Swells, doesn't dissolve

4. **Physical**
   - Moisture regain: 8.5%
   - Density: 1.52 g/cm³

### 8. Organic Cotton

**Definition**: Cotton grown without synthetic pesticides, fertilizers, or GMOs using organic methods.

**Requirements:**
- No synthetic chemicals
- Natural pest control
- Crop rotation
- Organic fertilizers (compost, manure)
- 3-year land transition period
- Certification (GOTS, USDA Organic)

**Advantages:**

1. **Environmental**
   - Reduced pollution
   - Improved soil health
   - Lower carbon footprint
   - Biodiversity protection

2. **Health**
   - No pesticide residues
   - Safer for farmers
   - Hypoallergenic
   - Better for sensitive skin

3. **Quality**
   - Often hand-picked (less damage)
   - Softer feel
   - No chemical residues

4. **Social**
   - Fair trade practices
   - Better working conditions
   - Community development

**Disadvantages:**
- Higher cost (20-40% premium)
- Lower initial yields
- More labor-intensive
- Limited supply

4. **Lumen**
   - Central collapsed tube
   - Dried protoplasm residues
   - Size varies with maturity

### 2. Lignin and Its Effects

**What is Lignin?**

**Chemical Nature:**
- Complex aromatic heteropolymer
- Composition: Phenylpropane units (p-coumaryl, coniferyl, sinapyl alcohols)
- Structure: 3D, amorphous, highly branched
- Molecular Weight: 1,000-20,000 Daltons

**Location:**
- Middle lamella (intercellular cement)
- Primary and secondary cell walls
- Acts as binding agent

**Function:**
- Provides rigidity and strength
- Water impermeability
- Microbial resistance
- Binds cellulose fibrils

**Effects on Jute Fiber:**

**Positive Effects:**
1. Structural strength and stiffness
2. Dimensional stability
3. Protection against degradation
4. Natural bonding between cells

**Negative Effects:**
1. **Processing:**
   - Makes fiber coarse and harsh
   - Reduces flexibility
   - Difficult to spin fine yarn
   - Hard to blend with cotton

2. **Dyeing:**
   - Poor dye affinity
   - Uneven dye uptake
   - Limited color brightness
   - Yellowing tendency

3. **Quality:**
   - Low elasticity (1-2%)
   - Brittle nature
   - Reduced softness
   - Limited textile use

4. **Degradation:**
   - UV exposure → yellowing
   - Oxidative degradation
   - Strength loss over time

**Lignin Content:**
- Jute: 12-16%
- Cotton: 0%
- Flax: 2-3%
- Hemp: 3-5%

### 7. Jute Fiber Defects

- Reduces quality and processing efficiency

- Improper harvesting
- Causes processing problems

**3. Dazed Fiber**
- Over-retted fiber
- Weak and brittle
- Dark brown color
- Reduced strength

**4. Heart-Damaged Fiber**
- Damage to fiber core
- Microbial or insect attack
- Weak spots in fiber

**5. Mossy Fiber**
- Green moss growth on fiber
- Improper drying or storage
- Moisture damage

**6. Barky Fiber**
- Bark residue not removed
- Poor stripping
- Reduces softness

## Flax Fiber

### 1. Chemical Composition

| Component | Percentage |
|-----------|------------|
| Cellulose | 62-72% |
| Hemicellulose | 18-20% |
| Lignin | 2-3% |
| Pectin | 1.5-3% |
| Wax & Fats | 1.5-2% |
| Ash | 1% |
| Moisture | 8-12% |

**Key Features:**
- Highest cellulose content among bast fibers
- Lowest lignin (makes it softest bast fiber)
- High crystallinity (70-80%)

### 2. Dressing of Flax

**Definition:** Final processing to prepare fiber for spinning.

**Steps:**

1. **Breaking**
   - Passes through fluted rollers
   - Breaks woody core (shive) into pieces
   - Fiber bundles stay intact

2. **Scutching**
   - Rotating blades beat the fiber
   - Removes broken woody parts
   - Separates fiber bundles
   - Removes short tow fibers

3. **Hackling/Combing**
   - Drawn through pins of increasing fineness
   - Aligns fibers parallel
   - Separates by length:
     - **Line fiber**: Long (50-90 cm) for fine yarn
     - **Tow**: Short (<30 cm) for coarse yarn

### 3. Why Flax is Least Extensible

**Elongation:** Only 2.5-3.5% at break

**Reasons:**

1. **High Cellulose (62-72%)**
   - Rigid polymer structure
   - Extensive crystalline regions

2. **High Crystallinity (70-80%)**
   - Ordered molecular arrangement
   - Limited molecular mobility
   - Restricted chain slippage

3. **High DP (8,000-10,000)**
   - Long, rigid cellulose chains
   - Minimal flexibility

4. **Low Spiral Angle (5-10°)**
   - Nearly parallel fibril alignment
   - Maximum strength, minimum elongation

5. **Strong H-Bonding**
   - Extensive hydrogen bonds
   - Rigid crystalline structure

**Comparison:**
- Flax: 2.5-3.5%
- Cotton: 5-7%
- Wool: 25-35%
- Silk: 20-25%

### 4. Cottonization of Flax

**Definition:** Converting long flax fibers to cotton-like short staple.

**Process:**
1. **Mechanical cutting/breaking**
2. **Chemical treatment:** 2-5% NaOH or enzymes
3. **Temperature:** 60-90°C
4. **Time:** 30-120 minutes
5. **Final length:** 25-40 mm

**Why Done:**

1. **Process on cotton machinery**
2. **Higher production speeds**
3. **Lower investment cost**
4. **Use of flax tow (waste)**
5. **Blending opportunities**
6. **Softer fabrics**

**Applications:**
- Cotton-flax blends (50:50)
- Knitwear
- Nonwovens
- Home textiles

## Wool Fiber

### 1. How Wool Keeps Warm

**Mechanisms:**

1. **Crimp Structure**
   - 8-15 crimps/cm creates 3D structure
   - Traps air pockets (excellent insulator)

2. **Scale Structure**
   - Overlapping scales prevent close packing
   - More air entrapment

3. **Low Thermal Conductivity**
   - Wool: 0.04-0.05 W/mK
   - Slower heat transfer than cotton/synthetics

4. **Hygroscopic Nature**
   - Absorbs up to 30% moisture
   - Exothermic reaction releases heat (25 kJ/mol)
   - Heat of sorption warms body

5. **Low Density** (1.31 g/cm³)
   - Lightweight for volume
   - More air space per weight

6. **Bulkiness**
   - Springy, resilient fibers
   - Maintains loft/thickness
   - Doesn't compress easily

### 2. Chemical Structure of Silk

Silk consists of two primary proteins: fibroin (approximately 75%) and sericin (approximately 25%). Fibroin forms the structural core with a semicrystalline structure, while sericin is a water-soluble glue-like protein that binds fibroin threads together.

**Fibroin Structure:**

```
Amino Acid Composition:
- Glycine (Gly): 45% (R = H)
- Alanine (Ala): 29% (R = CH₃)
- Serine (Ser): 12% (R = CH₂OH)
- Others: 14% (Tyr, Val, Asp, Glu)

Sequence Pattern:
Gly-Ser-Gly-Ala-Gly-Ala (repeating)

Structure:
β-pleated sheet (antiparallel)
- H-bonds between chains
- Tight packing (50% glycine, no side chain)
- Side chains above/below plane
```

**Molecular Components:**
- Heavy chain: ~390 kDa
- Light chain: ~26 kDa
- Linked by disulfide bond
- Glycoprotein P25: ~25 kDa
- Assembly ratio: 6H:6L:1P25

**Sericin:**
- Water-soluble glycoprotein
- Amorphous structure
- Contains serine, threonine, aspartic acid
- 20-30% of cocoon weight

### 3. Sericulture Process

Sericulture involves rearing silkworms and producing cocoons through a controlled process.

**Steps:**

```
1. EGG PRODUCTION
   ├─ Moth mating
   ├─ Egg laying (300-500 eggs/moth)
   ├─ Egg disinfection (2% formalin)
   └─ Egg incubation (10 days at 30°C)
   ↓
2. LARVA REARING (35 days)
   ├─ Hatching (caterpillars emerge)
   ├─ Feeding on mulberry leaves
   ├─ Four molting stages
   ├─ Weight increase: 10,000x
   └─ Preparation for spinning
   ↓
3. COCOON SPINNING (2-3 days)
   ├─ Fibroin secreted by salivary glands
   ├─ Sericin from second glands
   ├─ Extrusion through spinnerets
   ├─ Solidification on air contact
   └─ 1.5 km filament per cocoon
   ↓
4. HARVESTING
   ├─ Cocoon collection (8-10 days after spinning)
   ├─ Sorting by quality
   └─ Some kept for breeding
   ↓
5. STIFLING
   ├─ Hot air, steam, or boiling water
   ├─ Kills pupa inside cocoon
   └─ Prevents moth emergence
   ↓
6. REELING
   ├─ Cocoons soaked in hot water (15 min)
   ├─ Sericin softens
   ├─ Filaments unwound
   ├─ 4-20 filaments twisted together
   └─ Wound onto reels
   ↓
7. RAW SILK
   └─ One thread contains ~50 filaments
      ~900-1500m from one cocoon
```

### 4. Weighting of Silk

**Definition:** Adding metallic salts to silk to increase weight and body.

**Process:**

1. **Metallic Salt Treatment**
   - Tin salts (most common): SnCl₂, SnCl₄
   - Weighting agents: Na₂HPO₄, Na₂SiO₃
   - Silk immersed in salt solution

2. **Precipitation**
   - Metallic compounds precipitate in fiber
   - Increases weight by 10-400%

**Why Done:**
- Compensate for sericin loss during degumming (25% weight loss)
- Improve drape and handle
- Increase body/fullness
- Enhanced luster
- Better appearance

**Effects:**

**Positive:**
- Fuller, heavier fabric
- Better drape
- Improved luster
- Scroop (rustling sound)

**Negative:**
- Reduced strength (up to 50% loss)
- Decreased flexibility
- Poor durability
- Splits and cracks develop
- Not suitable for long-term use

**Modern Status:** Less common due to negative effects on fiber properties.

### 5. Wool Collection & Processing

**Collection:**

1. **Shearing**
   - Annual shearing (spring/early summer)
   - Electric or manual clippers
   - Fleece removed in one piece
   - Yield: 2-10 kg per sheep

2. **Grading**
   - Quality assessment at farm
   - Separation by fineness and length
   - Color assessment

**Processing Steps:**

```
1. SORTING
   ├─ Separate fleece by quality
   ├─ Remove stained/damaged parts
   └─ Grade by fineness
   ↓
2. SCOURING
   ├─ Washing in warm water + detergent
   ├─ Remove grease (lanolin), dirt, sweat
   ├─ Multiple wash baths
   └─ Yield: 50-70% clean wool
   ↓
3. CARBONIZING (if needed)
   ├─ Treatment with dilute H₂SO₄ (4-7%)
   ├─ Destroys vegetable matter
   ├─ Dried and baked (90-100°C)
   └─ Vegetable matter crumbles away
   ↓
4. CARDING
   ├─ Disentangle and align fibers
   ├─ Remove remaining impurities
   └─ Form into sliver
   ↓
5. GILLING & COMBING (for worsted)
   ├─ Parallel alignment
   ├─ Remove short fibers (noils)
   └─ Create uniform sliver
   ↓
6. SPINNING
   ├─ Woolen: carded only, fuzzy yarn
   └─ Worsted: combed, smooth yarn
```

### 6. Chemical Properties of Wool

**Composition:**
- Protein: 97-100% (keratin)
- Lipids: 0.5-2%
- Moisture: 16-18%

**Reactions:**

1. **Acids**
   - Dilute acids: Generally resistant
   - Concentrated acids: Damage and dissolution
   - Mineral acids (HCl, H₂SO₄): Hydrolysis of peptide bonds

2. **Alkalis**
   - Dilute cold: Minimal effect
   - Concentrated/hot: Severe damage, dissolution
   - pH > 9: Disulfide bond breakage
   - Used in carbonizing process

3. **Oxidizing Agents**
   - Chlorine bleaches: Damage (yellowing)
   - H₂O₂: Can be used carefully
   - Reduces disulfide bonds

4. **Reducing Agents**
   - Break disulfide bonds
   - Cause felting, shrinkage control
   - Used in permanent setting

### 7. Wool Polymer

**Structure:**

```
KERATIN POLYPEPTIDE CHAIN

NH₂-[AA₁]-[AA₂]-[AA₃]-...-[AAn]-COOH

Key Amino Acids:
- Cystine: 10-14% (disulfide bonds)
- Glutamic acid: 11-15%
- Serine: 10-12%
- Glycine: 5-8%
- 18 different amino acids total
```

**Bonds in Wool:**

1. **Peptide Bonds** (Primary structure)
   - Covalent bonds linking amino acids
   - Form polypeptide chain backbone

2. **Disulfide Bonds** (-S-S-)
   - Covalent crosslinks between cystine residues
   - Strongest bonds
   - Provide strength and elasticity
   - Heat-resistant
   - Permanent set potential

3. **Hydrogen Bonds**
   - Between C=O and N-H groups
   - Moderate strength
   - Broken by water/heat
   - Reform on drying
   - Responsible for temporary set

4. **Salt Linkages** (Ionic bonds)
   - Between -COO⁻ and -NH₃⁺ groups
   - Moderate strength
   - pH sensitive
   - Broken by acids/alkalis

5. **Van der Waals Forces**
   - Weak attractions
   - Between non-polar side chains
   - Contribute to fiber cohesion

**Structure Levels:**
- α-helix structure (springy)
- Crystalline and amorphous regions
- Two-phase cortex (ortho- and para-)

## Silk Fiber

### 1. Fibroin and Sericin

**Fibroin:** Structural protein (~75% of silk) that forms the core fiber. It's a semicrystalline fibrous protein composed of heavy chain (~390 kDa) and light chain (~26 kDa) linked by a disulfide bond. It's insoluble in water and provides mechanical strength.

**Sericin:** Glue-like amorphous protein (~25% of silk) that binds two fibroin fibers together. It's water-soluble and can be removed through degumming.

### 2. Physical Properties of Silk

| Property | Value |
|----------|--------|
| **Length** | 300-1500 meters (continuous filament) |
| **Diameter** | 10-13 microns |
| **Fineness** | 1.1 dtex (standard for denier) |
| **Tenacity (Dry)** | 35-45 cN/tex |
| **Tenacity (Wet)** | 28-36 cN/tex (20% decrease) |
| **Elongation** | 20-25% |
| **Elastic Recovery** | Good (92% at 2% extension) |
| **Density** | 1.33-1.40 g/cm³ |
| **Moisture Regain** | 11% |
| **Luster** | High (natural sheen) |
| **Crimp** | None (smooth) |
| **Resilience** | Good |

### 3. Degumming of Silk

**Definition:** Removal of sericin coating from raw silk.

**Purpose:**
- Remove gummy sericin (20-30% weight)
- Improve softness and luster
- Enhance dyeability
- Prepare for textile use

**Methods:**

1. **Soap Degumming** (Traditional)
   - Boil in soap solution (mild alkali)
   - Temperature: 95-98°C
   - Time: 1.5-2 hours
   - Gentle on fiber

2. **Acid Degumming**
   - Dilute acid + soap
   - Better luster
   - More delicate

3. **Enzymatic Degumming** (Modern)
   - Protease enzymes
   - Lower temperature (50-60°C)
   - Eco-friendly
   - Better fiber properties

4. **Fermentation**
   - Natural bacterial action
   - Traditional Asian method
   - Time-consuming

**Effect:** Silk becomes softer, more lustrous, better drape

### 4. Silk Identification

**Non-Technical:**

1. **Burning Test**
   - Burns slowly
   - Smell: Burning hair/feathers
   - Flame: Small, sputtering
   - Ash: Black, crushable bead
   - Self-extinguishing

2. **Touch**
   - Smooth, cool to touch
   - Lustrous
   - Good drape

**Technical:**

1. **Microscopy**
   - **Longitudinal:** Smooth, no markings
   - **Cross-section:** Triangular with rounded corners

2. **Chemical Tests**
   - **Solubility:** Dissolves in concentrated acids
   - **Staining:** Specific protein stains
   - **Alkali:** Dissolves in 5% NaOH

3. **Physical:**
   - Density: 1.33-1.40 g/cm³
   - Birefringence under polarized light

### 5. Types of Silk

**Cultivated Silk:**

1. **Mulberry Silk** (Bombyx mori)
   - Most common (90% production)
   - White/cream color
   - Finest quality
   - Smooth, uniform
   - Fed on mulberry leaves

**Wild Silks:**

1. **Tussah/Tasar** (Antheraea mylitta)
   - Brown/tan color
   - Coarser texture
   - Feeds on oak leaves
   - Strong, durable

2. **Eri Silk** (Samia ricini)
   - White/brick red
   - Coarse, wool-like
   - Feeds on castor plants
   - "Peace silk" (moth emerges)

3. **Muga Silk** (Antheraea assamensis)
   - Golden yellow
   - Very strong
   - Feeds on som/soalu plants
   - Found in Assam, India

4. **Spider Silk**
   - Extremely strong
   - Not commercially viable
   - Research material
