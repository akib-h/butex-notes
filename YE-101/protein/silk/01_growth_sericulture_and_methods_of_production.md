## 1. What is Silk?

Silk is a **natural protein filament fibre** produced by the larvae of certain insects — most notably the domesticated mulberry silkworm *Bombyx mori* — during the construction of their cocoons. It is the **only natural fibre that occurs as a continuous filament**, making it unique among all textile fibres.

Key identifiers of silk:
1. It is a **protein fibre**, primarily composed of fibroin (~75–80%) and sericin (~20–25%).
2. It is classified as an **animal fibre** under natural textile fibres.
3. It is often called the **"Queen of Fibres"** for its lustre, luxury, and comfort.
4. A single cocoon can yield a silk filament of **600–1,500 metres** in length.
5. Silk is the **only natural filament** that does not need to be spun before it can be used for textile fabrics.

> **Example:** A single *Bombyx mori* cocoon unwound continuously yields roughly 1,000–1,300 metres of usable filament — comparable in length to 10+ football fields.

## 2. History and Origin of Silk

1. Silk was first discovered in **China around 2700–2650 BC**, attributed in legend to Empress **Lei Zu** (Hsi-Ling-Shih), consort of the Yellow Emperor.
2. According to Chinese records, silk fabric was in use as far back as the **27th century BC** (Fan Shengzhi shu, Western Han Dynasty).
3. For approximately **3,000 years**, the production method was a **closely guarded Chinese state secret**, punishable by death if revealed.
4. The earliest physical evidence of long-distance silk trade is silk found in **Egyptian mummy hair (c. 1070 BC)**, from the 21st Dynasty.
5. By **300 AD**, Japan and India had penetrated the secrecy and begun their own sericulture industries.
6. The famous **Silk Road** — the vast trade network connecting East Asia to Europe — owes its name and existence to this single fibre.
7. In **550 AD**, Byzantine Emperor Justinian reportedly had silk worm eggs smuggled from China by monks, ending China's monopoly in the West.

> **Example:** The Silk Road stretched over 6,400 km, facilitating trade in silk, spices, and ideas between China, Central Asia, the Middle East, and Europe.

## 3. Types of Silk

Silk is broadly classified based on the species of silkworm and cultivation method:

1. **Mulberry Silk** (*Bombyx mori*) — Most widely produced, finest quality, white/cream colour. Cultivated on mulberry leaves. Accounts for ~90% of world production.
2. **Tasar (Tussar) Silk** (*Antheraea mylitta*) — Wild silk from India. Coarser, tan-brown in colour. Fed on oak (Terminalia) leaves. Used for furnishing fabrics.
3. **Muga Silk** (*Antheraea assamensis*) — Produced exclusively in Assam, India. Golden-yellow colour with natural sheen. Highly durable.
4. **Eri (Endi) Silk** (*Samia cynthia ricini*) — Known as "Peace Silk" because the moth is allowed to emerge before reeling. Fed on castor leaves. Heavier and less lustrous than mulberry.
5. **Spider Silk** — Highly experimental; stronger than steel but commercial production is not viable at scale.

> **Example:** Muga silk from Assam has such remarkable durability that a single Muga silk garment can last over 100 years and actually becomes more lustrous with age and washing.

## 4. The Silkworm — *Bombyx mori*

*Bombyx mori* (Latin: "silkworm of the mulberry tree") is a **fully domesticated moth** that has co-evolved with humans over millennia.

Key biological facts:
1. It is **not technically a worm** but the **larva (caterpillar) of a moth**.
2. After thousands of years of selective breeding, it is **completely dependent on humans** — it cannot survive in the wild.
3. The adult moth is **blind and cannot fly**; it lives only a few days, during which it mates and lays eggs.
4. A female deposits approximately **400–500 eggs** per cycle before dying.
5. The silkworm has **two large silk glands** (the posterior silk glands) that produce fibroin and **two smaller glands** that produce sericin.
6. Liquid silk is secreted through a **single spinneret** on the head, hardening upon exposure to air.
7. Male silkworms produce **more silk of higher quality** than females.

> **Example:** The mulberry silkworm evolved into a blind, flightless moth — a direct result of over 5,000 years of human selective breeding prioritising silk yield over the moth's survival instincts.

## 5. Life Cycle of the Silkworm

The silkworm undergoes **complete metamorphosis** (holometabolism) in four distinct stages:

```
EGG ──► LARVA (Silkworm) ──► PUPA (Chrysalis) ──► ADULT MOTH
```

### Stage 1 — Egg (Ovum)
1. Eggs are small, oval, and pinhead-sized (~1 mm).
2. Newly laid eggs are pale yellow, darkening to gray-blue as the embryo develops.
3. Incubation period: **10–14 days** at 24–28°C.
4. One female lays ~400–500 eggs in a single laying.
5. Eggs are laid on specially prepared paper cards and stored in controlled conditions.

### Stage 2 — Larva (Silkworm / Caterpillar)
1. The larva hatches as a tiny **"ant" (kego)** about 3 mm in length.
2. The larval stage lasts approximately **25–30 days** through **five instars** (moultings).
3. The silkworm eats **almost continuously**, consuming chopped fresh mulberry leaves.
4. During the 5th (final) instar, the silkworm becomes translucent and its body becomes slightly yellow — indicating readiness to spin.
5. Each larva can increase its weight by **10,000 times** during this stage.
6. **To produce 1 kg of silk**, approximately **3,000 silkworms** must eat **104 kg of mulberry leaves**.

### Stage 3 — Pupa (Cocoon formation)
1. The spinning process (cocoon formation) takes **3–8 days**.
2. The larva secretes liquid silk in a figure-8 pattern, rotating its head ~300,000 times.
3. The two fibroin filaments from the glands are cemented together by sericin as they exit the spinneret.
4. The finished cocoon is a **continuous single filament** of 600–1,500 metres.
5. The standard filament thickness averages **2.2–2.5 tex**.

### Stage 4 — Adult Moth
1. If allowed to emerge naturally, the moth secretes an alkaline fluid that dissolves the cocoon end, **breaking the filament**.
2. To preserve the continuous filament, pupae are **killed** before emergence by steam, hot air, or boiling water at the chrysalis stage.
3. The adult moths used for **breeding** are allowed to emerge, mate, and lay eggs.
4. Adult moths do not eat; they live only **4–5 days** after emerging.

> **Example:** In a typical sericulture farm, for every 100 female moths allowed to emerge for breeding, approximately 40,000 eggs can be collected — enough to eventually produce significant quantities of raw silk.

## 6. Mulberry Cultivation (Moriculture)

The cultivation of mulberry trees to feed silkworms is called **Moriculture** and is an integral part of sericulture.

1. The preferred species is **Morus alba** (White Mulberry), though *Morus multicaulis* is also used.
2. Mulberry plants are propagated by **stem cuttings** or seedlings.
3. They are grown in well-drained, fertile soils with moderate rainfall.
4. Mulberry leaves are **harvested year-round** in tropical climates, allowing 4–5 silkworm crops per year.
5. Leaf quality directly affects cocoon quality — fresh, tender, young leaves are preferred for younger larvae, while mature leaves are given to older larvae.
6. Mulberry cultivation is considered **relatively eco-friendly** as the trees require minimal pesticides or fertilizers.

> **Example:** One hectare of mulberry plantation can produce enough leaves to support approximately 100 kg of raw silk production per year in favourable conditions.

## 7. Sericulture — Step-by-Step Process

**Sericulture** is defined as the agricultural activity associated with the rearing of silkworms and the production of raw silk cocoons. The complete production process involves the following sequential steps:

### Step 1 — Egg Hatching (Incubation)
1. Silkworm eggs are obtained from authorised grainage (egg-producing) centres.
2. Eggs are stored in controlled humidity (~75–80%) and temperature (~25°C).
3. After 10–14 days, the eggs hatch into tiny larvae.

### Step 2 — Larval Rearing (Worm Cultivation)
1. Newly hatched larvae are placed in clean, ventilated rearing trays.
2. They are fed chopped mulberry leaves **4–5 times per day**.
3. As larvae grow through 5 instars, they are spread to larger trays to prevent overcrowding.
4. Rearing conditions are carefully maintained: temperature 24–28°C, humidity 70–85%.
5. The rearing house is kept clean to prevent diseases like flacherie (bacterial) and muscardine (fungal).

### Step 3 — Mounting (Channaka/Mountage)
1. When silkworms are ready to spin (~28 days), they are transferred to **mountage frames** — twig structures or purpose-made plastic frames.
2. The worms attach themselves and begin spinning their cocoons.
3. Spinning takes 3–8 days to complete.

### Step 4 — Cocoon Harvest
1. Completed cocoons are harvested **5–7 days after spinning begins**.
2. Cocoons are graded and sorted by colour, shape, size, and texture.
3. Defective cocoons (double cocoons, pierced cocoons, stained cocoons) are set aside for spun silk production.

### Step 5 — Stifling (Killing the Pupa)
1. To prevent the moth from emerging and breaking the filament, the pupae inside the cocoons are killed by:
   - **Steam stifling** (most common industrial method)
   - **Hot air drying** at 80–100°C for 2–3 hours
   - **Sun drying** (traditional method)
   - **Cold storage** at -10°C
2. Dead cocoons can be stored for several months before reeling.

### Step 6 — Reeling
1. Stifled cocoons are immersed in **hot water (85–95°C)** to soften the sericin and locate the filament end.
2. Filament ends from **8–15 cocoons** are drawn together and wound onto a reel simultaneously — this creates a usable raw silk thread called a **bave** (a pair of fibroin filaments bonded by sericin).
3. The process of taking up the filament is called **reeling**.
4. Advanced countries like China use **fully automatic reeling machines**; traditional methods use hand reeling (charkha).
5. The diameter of a single filament (brin) is approximately **10–14 μm** — too fine for single-cocoon reeling.
6. The product of reeling is called **raw silk** or **grège**.

### Step 7 — Throwing
1. Raw silk from the reel is then converted into usable yarn through **throwing** — adding twist to the reeled filaments.
2. Throwing gives the yarn strength, coherence, and desired characteristics.
3. Types of thrown silk yarn include:
   - **Greige (Singles):** Two or more raw silk threads reeled together with slight twist.
   - **Tram:** Low-twist thread used for weft in woven fabrics.
   - **Organzine:** High-twist thread used for warp.
   - **Crêpe:** Very high-twist thread (2,000–3,000 turns/metre) creating crêpe fabrics.

### Step 8 — Degumming
1. Raw silk still contains **sericin gum** (~20–30% weight) which gives it stiffness.
2. Degumming removes sericin by boiling the silk in **soap and water** (or mild alkali), reducing weight by up to 30%.
3. After degumming, silk is soft, lustrous, and semi-transparent.
4. Enzymes like Trypsin and Papain can also be used for enzymatic degumming.

### Step 9 — Dyeing and Finishing
1. Degummed silk is dyed, woven, and finished into fabric.
2. Silk has **excellent dyeing properties** due to its protein (amphoteric) nature.
3. Finishing treatments include **weighting** (adding metallic salts like tin to increase weight and drape).

> **Example (Full Process):** The production of one pure silk kimono requires the cocoons of approximately **5,000 silkworms**, which in turn requires the consumption of over **500 kg of mulberry leaves**. This illustrates the labour and resource intensity of silk production.

## 8. Methods of Silk Production

### 8.1 Traditional Hand Reeling
1. Cocoons are softened in warm water in a basin.
2. A brush is used to locate the filament ends.
3. Threads from multiple cocoons are drawn manually and wound on a hand reel.
4. Produces: **2–3 kg of raw silk per day per worker**.
5. Still practised in rural India and parts of China and Japan.

### 8.2 Machine Reeling (Cottage Machine)
1. Semi-automatic machines improve speed and consistency.
2. A croissure (crossing point) ensures even distribution of sericin bonding.
3. Output: **5–10 kg per day**.

### 8.3 Multi-End Reeling (Industrial Automatic)
1. Fully automatic machines (used in China's modern industry) simultaneously reel from 400+ cocoons.
2. Computerised controls maintain consistent filament tension, speed, and thickness.
3. Produces silk thread of consistent **2.2–2.5 tex** denier.

### 8.4 Spun Silk Production
1. Made from **short silk fibres** (floret, waste, broken filaments from damaged or pierced cocoons).
2. Short silk fibres are combed, drawn, and spun like wool or cotton.
3. Spun silk is **coarser and less lustrous** than reeled silk but is more economical.

## 9. Types of Silk Yarn

| Yarn Type | Description | Use |
|-----------|-------------|-----|
| **Raw silk (Grège)** | Reeled but not degummed, stiff with sericin intact | Base material for throwing |
| **Organzine** | Two or more greige threads, each twisted separately then doubled | Strong warp yarn for weaving |
| **Tram** | Two or more threads twisted together lightly | Soft weft yarn for weaving |
| **Crêpe** | Very high twist (2,000–3,000 TPM) | Crêpe fabric, georgette |
| **Spun silk** | Short silk fibres spun like wool | Budget silk fabrics |
| **Thrown silk (Greige)** | Reeled silk with a slight twist | Base for various fabrics |
| **Chappell silk** | Spun while the gum is still green | Has a characteristic lustre |
| **Organdie** | Produced from the best cocoons | Finest woven fabrics |

> **Example:** Organzine is used as the warp in the production of silk satin, while tram provides the soft, heavy weft that creates satin's characteristic smooth face.

## 10. Peace Silk (Ahimsa Silk)

1. In conventional sericulture, silkworms are **killed** inside cocoons to obtain continuous filaments.
2. **Peace silk** (Ahimsa = non-violence in Sanskrit) allows the moth to emerge naturally before the broken cocoons are collected.
3. The broken filaments are then **spun** (not reeled) into silk yarn.
4. Peace silk is **softer and finer** in texture but is **weaker** and more costly because it is time-consuming to produce.
5. The alkaline fluid the moth uses to exit the cocoon can also slightly **discolour** the silk.
6. Mahatma Gandhi promoted peace silk as an ethical alternative.
7. Peace silk has a **growing niche market** in ethical and sustainable fashion.

> **Example:** Peace silk is commercially produced in several Indian states (particularly Andhra Pradesh and West Bengal) and certified by organisations like the Global Organic Textile Standard (GOTS) for cruelty-free practices.
