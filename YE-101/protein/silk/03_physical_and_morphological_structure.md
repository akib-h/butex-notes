## 1. Overview of Silk Fibre Morphology

Silk fibre possesses a distinctive and complex morphology arising from its **biological origin as a spun protein secretion**. Unlike most natural fibres that are cut or plucked, silk is extruded as a continuous liquid that solidifies into a filament.

Key morphological facts:
1. Silk is the **only natural continuous filament fibre** — a single cocoon yields 600–1,500 m of usable thread.
2. In its raw form, silk consists of **two parallel fibroin filaments** (brins) bound together by a sericin coating.
3. The dual filament system is called a **bave** — elliptical in cross-section when raw (undegummed).
4. Upon degumming, the individual **brins** are revealed as approximately **triangular with rounded corners**.
5. Silk fibroin possesses a **semi-crystalline hierarchical structure** — microfibrils grouped into fibrils, fibrils into fibres.
6. The triangular prism-like cross-section is responsible for silk's famous **iridescent shimmer**, refracting incoming light at different angles.

> **Example:** If you look at a piece of pure silk under natural light and tilt it, you'll see it shift between different colours — this is not due to dye, but the triangular prism structure of each individual fibre bending light like a microscopic prism.

## 2. Hierarchical Structure of Silk

Silk fibre is organised in a **hierarchical multi-scale structure** from the molecular to the macroscopic level:

```
Amino Acids (Gly, Ala, Ser, Tyr...)
        │
        ▼
  Polypeptide Chains (H-chain + L-chain)
        │
        ▼
  β-sheet Crystallites (antiparallel β-sheets)
        │
        ▼
  Nanofibrils (20–30 nm diameter)
        │
        ▼
  Microfibrils (bundles of nanofibrils)
        │
        ▼
  Fibril Bundles
        │
        ▼
  Single Fibroin Filament / Brin (10–14 μm)
        │
        ▼
  Bave = 2 Brins + Sericin (20–30 μm, elliptical)
        │
        ▼
  Raw Silk Thread (8–15 cocoons combined during reeling)
        │
        ▼
  Silk Yarn / Fabric
```

Each level of organisation contributes to the final mechanical and optical properties of the silk textile.

> **Example:** The nanofibrils of silk (20–30 nm diameter) are comparable in scale to many synthetic nanocomposite materials, which is why silk is studied extensively in materials science and biomedical engineering.

## 3. The Bave and the Brin

### The Bave (Raw Silk Filament)
1. The **bave** is the dual-filament structure as it exits the silkworm's spinneret.
2. It consists of **two fibroin filaments** (brins) that are extruded simultaneously from the two silk glands.
3. The brins are cemented together by **sericin** secreted from separate glands.
4. The bave is **elliptical in cross-section** when undegummed.
5. A **longitudinal view** of the bave shows a rough, cracked surface with many striations — this roughness comes from the sericin layer.
6. Diameter of a bave: approximately **20–30 μm**.

### The Brin (Individual Fibroin Filament)
1. Each brin is a **single silk filament** of pure fibroin.
2. When degummed, the brin appears as a **triangular rod with rounded corners** under the microscope.
3. Diameter: approximately **10–14 μm** for mulberry silk.
4. The brin consists of bundles of **fibrils**, which are in turn composed of **nanofibrils**.
5. When a strand of fibroin is enlarged under SEM, it appears as a **large bundle of fibrils**.

> **Example:** The word "brin" comes from French, meaning "strand" or "thread." Understanding that each visible silk thread you see is actually made of multiple brins (filaments from multiple cocoons reeled together) helps explain why raw silk is stronger than a single cocoon filament.

## 4. Fibroin — The Structural Core

Fibroin is the **primary structural protein** of silk, forming the central core of each brin.

Morphological characteristics:
1. Fibroin constitutes **70–80%** of silk's total mass.
2. It forms a **fibrous (not globular) protein** — its chains extend rather than fold compactly.
3. The fibroin structure has **three crystalline phases**:

   | Phase | Content | Composition | Properties |
   |-------|---------|-------------|------------|
   | Phase I (Crystalline) | ~60% | Gly, Ala, Ser, Tyr | Highly crystalline, provides rigidity |
   | Phase II (Semi-crystalline) | ~30% | Gly, Ala, Tyr, Val | Transition zone |
   | Phase III (Amorphous) | ~10% | Bulky/ionic side-group amino acids | Provides elasticity |

4. The **long polypeptide chains** are oriented **parallel to the fibre axis**.
5. Fibroin forms **antiparallel β-pleated sheets** (Silk II structure) — a flat, planar arrangement.
6. Unlike keratin (wool), fibroin does **not** form α-helices in its native state.
7. The planar β-sheet structure makes fibroin **less extensible than wool**, as its chains are nearly fully extended already.

> **Example:** Fibroin is sometimes described as having a "zig-zag" backbone orientation — like a pleated sheet of paper. This rigid arrangement is why silk fabric feels smooth and flat rather than crimped or springy like wool.

## 5. Sericin — The Gum Layer

Sericin is the **adhesive, amorphous protein** that surrounds and binds the two fibroin brins in a bave.

Morphological characteristics:
1. Sericin constitutes **20–30%** of raw silk mass (by weight of the cocoon).
2. It is **hydrophilic** (water-attracting) — this is why it can be removed by boiling in water/soap.
3. Sericin is a **glue-like, gel-forming glycoprotein** with high serine content (~40% of amino acids are serine).
4. The molecular weight of sericin ranges from **20,000 to 200,000 Da** — much lower than fibroin.
5. There are **four types of sericin** (Sericin A, B, C, D) based on molecular weight and solubility.
6. Sericin is **amorphous** (non-crystalline) — it does not have the ordered β-sheet structure of fibroin.
7. Under SEM, sericin appears as a smooth, thick **outer coating** on the bave surface.
8. Sericin provides **biological protection** to the fibroin core inside the cocoon.
9. Sericin contains impurities — waxes, carbohydrates, mineral salts, and pigments.

Pigments in sericin:
- **Golden silk:** Contains xanthophylls.
- **Yellow silk:** Contains carotene and vialoxanthin.
- **Green silk:** Contains bombycin and bombycetin.

> **Example:** Raw silk (with sericin) has a rough, sticky feel — similar to fishing line. Degummed silk is soft, lustrous, and smooth. The 20–30% weight loss during degumming explains why silk fabric can feel "lighter" than expected relative to the amount of raw cocoon used.

## 6. Fibrillar Morphology — Fringed Fibrillar Model

The internal morphology of the silk fibroin filament is described by the **Fringed Fibrillar Model**:

1. The silk fibre consists of **bundles of fibrils** arranged along the fibre axis.
2. Each fibril is composed of **nanofibrils** (20–30 nm diameter) that are intertwined with each other — like a rope, where the twisting of smaller strands increases overall tensile strength.
3. The nanofibrils are composed of **β-sheet crystallites** stacked in antiparallel configuration.
4. Between crystalline regions are **amorphous regions** — disordered polymer chains that contribute to the fibre's flexibility and moisture absorption.
5. **High crystallinity + high molecular orientation** along the fibre axis → high tensile strength, low elongation.
6. The **fringed micelle model** explains the co-existence of crystalline and amorphous domains and accounts for silk's combination of rigidity and elasticity.
7. The crystalline regions are held together by **hydrogen bonds and van der Waals forces** between adjacent β-sheets.

> **Example:** The nanofibrillar structure of silk is analogous to reinforced concrete — the crystalline β-sheet "rods" (like steel rebar) are embedded in the amorphous matrix (like concrete), giving silk both stiffness and some capacity to deform under load.

## 7. Physical Dimensions and Parameters

| Parameter | Mulberry Silk | Tasar Silk | Muga Silk | Eri Silk |
|-----------|--------------|------------|-----------|----------|
| Filament length (m) | 600–1,500 | 400–1,200 | 300–500 | (broken — spun) |
| Filament diameter (brin, μm) | 10–14 | 15–24 | 13–20 | 12–16 |
| Density (kg/m³) with sericin | 1,320–1,400 | ~1,330 | ~1,330 | ~1,320 |
| Density (kg/m³) without sericin | 1,300–1,380 | ~1,310 | ~1,310 | ~1,300 |
| Linear density (denier) | 1–3 | 2–6 | 2–5 | 3–5 |
| Cross-section | Triangular (rounded) | Wedge/elongated triangular | Elongated triangular | Flat wedge |
| Surface | Smooth | Striated | Striated | Striated |

> **Note:** Denier = grams of weight per 9,000 metres. A 1-denier thread is finer than a human hair (~70 denier).

> **Example:** A single brin of mulberry silk with a diameter of 10–14 μm is approximately **5 times thinner than a human hair** (average hair diameter: ~70 μm), yet it possesses greater tensile strength than a steel wire of the same diameter.

## 8. Crystalline and Amorphous Regions

Silk fibre is a **semi-crystalline polymer** with distinct crystalline and amorphous zones:

### Crystalline Regions
1. Composed of tightly packed **antiparallel β-pleated sheets** (Silk II structure).
2. The dominant amino acids in crystalline regions: **Glycine, Alanine, Serine**.
3. Small side-chains (especially Gly with H; Ala with CH₃) allow **tight molecular packing**.
4. Responsible for: high tensile strength, rigidity, resistance to dissolution, chemical inertness.
5. Degree of crystallinity in mulberry silk: **~60–65%**.
6. Nanocrystallite dimensions: approximately **6 nm × 21 nm × 2 nm** (width × length × height).

### Amorphous Regions
1. Composed of disordered, randomly coiled polypeptide chains.
2. Contain amino acids with **bulky, ionic, or large side chains** (Tyr, Val, Glu, Asp, Arg).
3. Responsible for: flexibility, elasticity, moisture absorption, dye uptake.
4. The amorphous zones allow one β-sheet layer to slide over adjacent ones, permitting **limited fibre extension**.
5. Silk's moisture regain (11%) is largely attributed to water absorption in amorphous regions.

### Silk I vs Silk II Structures
| Property | Silk I | Silk II |
|---------|--------|--------|
| Location | Inside silk gland (pre-spinning) | Final extruded fibre |
| Structure | Metastable, type II β-turn (crankshaft/S-zigzag) | Stable antiparallel β-sheet |
| Crystal system | Orthorhombic | Monoclinic |
| Stability | Unstable — converts to Silk II on spinning | Stable |
| Conversion | Converts to Silk II by methanol or spinning forces | Cannot easily revert |

> **Example:** Before the silkworm spins, the liquid silk in the gland is in the **Silk I** (β-turn) conformation — it can flow as a liquid. The mechanical shear force and pH change during spinning through the spinneret triggers **Silk I → Silk II** transformation, instantly converting the liquid to a solid fibre. This is nature's version of on-demand solidification.

## 9. Morphological Differences Among Silk Types

The cross-sectional and longitudinal morphology varies between different silk varieties:

### Mulberry Silk (*Bombyx mori*)
1. **Cross-section:** Approximately **triangular** with rounded corners.
2. **Longitudinal view:** Cylindrical, smooth surface with no striations (after degumming).
3. Fibroin brins are **fine** (10–14 μm) and tightly packed.
4. Sericin layer is thin and uniform.
5. Cocoons are **white to cream** in colour.

### Tasar Silk (*Antheraea mylitta*)
1. **Cross-section:** **Elongated rectangular or wedge-shaped**.
2. **Longitudinal view:** Flat, with fine **longitudinal striations** on the surface.
3. Coarser than mulberry — brin diameter 15–24 μm.
4. Brown/tan colour from pigments in sericin.
5. Striations contribute to its characteristic **harsh feel**.

### Muga Silk (*Antheraea assamensis*)
1. **Cross-section:** Elongated triangular — between mulberry and tasar shapes.
2. **Longitudinal view:** Flat, with fine striations (similar to tasar but finer).
3. **Natural golden-yellow colour** from pigments.
4. Extremely durable — high crystallinity and fibrillar packing density.

### Eri Silk (*Samia cynthia ricini*)
1. **Cross-section:** Flat, wedge or spindle-shaped.
2. **Longitudinal view:** Flat with striations.
3. Wider and less uniform than mulberry.
4. White or brick-red in colour.
5. Less lustrous due to irregular fibre surface.

> **Example:** The difference in cross-section between mulberry (triangular, smooth) and tasar (wedge, striated) silk explains their very different feel in fabric — mulberry silk feels smooth and cool, while tasar has a slightly rough, matte texture that is valued in furnishing fabrics.

## 10. Effect of Degumming on Morphology

Degumming (removal of sericin) profoundly affects silk's morphology:

| Property | Before Degumming (Raw Silk) | After Degumming (Pure Fibroin) |
|----------|---------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Cross-section shape | Elliptical (bave = 2 brins + sericin) | Triangular, rounded corners (individual brin) |
| Surface texture | Rough, striated, cracked | Smooth, almost structureless |
| Weight | 100% (base) | 70–80% (20–30% weight loss) |
| Colour | Cream/yellow (pigmented sericin) | Off-white to white |
| Lustre | Dull / waxy | High, brilliant sheen |
| Feel | Stiff, rough | Soft, smooth, luxurious |
| Transparency | Opaque | Semi-transparent |

1. After degumming, individual brins are clearly visible as separate, triangular filaments.
2. The residual yellowness after degumming comes from the fibroin itself (some pigment remains bound to fibroin).
3. **Over-degumming** can damage the fibroin structure, reducing tensile strength.
4. Degumming at **100°C** for 30 minutes with Na₂CO₃ is optimal for preserving mechanical properties.

> **Example:** When you purchase silk marked as "raw silk" versus "degummed silk," you are essentially buying the same fibre — but the raw version retains the sericin coating that adds body and stiffness, while degummed silk has been cleaned to reveal the pure, lustrous fibroin filaments underneath.
