## 1. Introduction to Fibre Microscopy

Microscopic examination is one of the **primary identification methods** for textile fibres, particularly natural fibres. It uses magnification of **100× or greater** to reveal structural features invisible to the naked eye.

Types of microscopy used for silk:
1. **Light (Optical) Microscopy** — Standard laboratory method; reveals longitudinal and cross-sectional outline.
2. **Polarised Light Microscopy** — Reveals birefringence and crystalline orientation.
3. **Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)** — High-resolution surface and cross-sectional imaging.
4. **Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM)** — Reveals nanofibrillar internal structure.
5. **Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM)** — Nanoscale surface topography.

Why microscopy is important for silk:
1. Identifies silk from other protein fibres (wool, hair).
2. Distinguishes **mulberry silk** from **non-mulberry silks** (tasar, muga, eri).
3. Detects adulteration or blending.
4. Assesses quality and fibre damage.
5. Confirms degumming status (raw vs degummed).

> **Example:** In forensic textile analysis, a single fibre recovered from a crime scene can be confirmed as silk — and even as mulberry vs. tasar — from its microscopic cross-section shape alone, using established AATCC Test Method 20-2011.

## 2. Microscopic Identification Method

### Preparation of Longitudinal Slide
1. A small number of fibres are teased apart and placed on a glass slide.
2. A drop of **mounting medium** (water, glycerine, or immersion oil) is applied to increase clarity.
3. A cover slip is placed, and the slide is examined under **transmitted light** at 100–400× magnification.

### Preparation of Cross-Section Slide (Hardy Microtome Method)
1. A bundle of fibres is embedded in a **polystyrene or PMMA matrix**.
2. The embedded bundle is sectioned transversely using a **microtome** (a precision cutting instrument).
3. Sections (1–5 μm thick) are placed on glass slides and examined under a microscope at **400–500× magnification**.
4. Cross-sectional surface areas can be measured using image analysis software (e.g., ImageJ).

### SEM Preparation
1. Fibres are mounted on aluminium stubs with conductive tape.
2. Sputter-coated with **gold** for 1 minute (to make surface conductive).
3. Examined at **5 kV accelerating voltage** using an SEM (e.g., JEOL JCM-6000).

> **Example:** The AATCC Test Method 20-2011 (Fibre Analysis: Qualitative) specifies that identification of silk type is based on cross-sectional shape after confirming it is silk via chemical dissolution — a systematic two-step approach used in quality control laboratories worldwide.

## 3. Longitudinal View — Undegummed Silk (Raw Bave)

The microscopic longitudinal view of **undegummed** (raw) silk reveals:

1. **Dual filament structure:** Two fibroin brins are visible, bonded together by sericin gum.
2. **Elliptical/oval overall profile:** The bave (dual strand) appears elliptical due to the sericin sheath.
3. **Rough, cracked surface:** The sericin outer layer appears irregular — showing a rough, cracked, striated texture under the microscope.
4. **Surface striations:** Many fine longitudinal striations (cracks in the sericin) run parallel to the fibre axis.
5. **The roughness is entirely in the sericin layer** — the underlying fibroin is smooth.
6. **Opaque appearance:** Due to the sericin, undegummed silk is less transparent under transmitted light.
7. **Non-uniform surface:** Natural irregularities and thickness variations in sericin coating are visible.

```
LONGITUDINAL VIEW — UNDEGUMMED SILK (Schematic):

  ══════════════════════════════════════   ← Rough sericin surface (cracked/striated)
  ─────────────────────────────────────   ← Fibroin Brin 1
  ─────────────────────────────────────   ← Fibroin Brin 2
  ══════════════════════════════════════   ← Sericin outer coating

Width: ~20–30 μm (bave)
```

> **Example:** Under a standard laboratory light microscope at 200×, an undegummed silk fibre resembles a pair of railroad tracks running parallel inside a rough-walled tunnel — you can see the two brins inside the opaque sericin sheath.

## 4. Longitudinal View — Degummed Silk (Brin)

After degumming, the microscopic longitudinal view changes dramatically:

### Mulberry Silk (Degummed)
1. **Smooth, featureless surface** — the removal of sericin reveals the perfectly smooth fibroin brin.
2. **Structureless, transparent rod** — appears as a transparent, glassy cylinder under transmitted light.
3. **Uniform diameter** — no irregularities along the length (unlike cotton or wool).
4. **Triangular cross-section profile** is hinted at in longitudinal view by the slight prismatic outline.
5. **High transparency** — semi-transparent, allowing light to pass through and refract.
6. **No striations** on the brin surface (unlike non-mulberry silks).

### Tasar Silk (Degummed)
1. Broader fibre than mulberry silk.
2. **Fine longitudinal lines** (striations) are visible along the filament surface.
3. These striations run parallel to the fibre axis.
4. Surface is not as smooth as mulberry silk.

### Muga Silk (Degummed)
1. Similar to tasar in having longitudinal striations.
2. Finer and more regular striations than tasar.
3. Golden natural colour visible even after degumming.

### Eri Silk (Degummed)
1. Irregular surface with striations.
2. Wider and less uniform than mulberry.
3. Flat, ribbon-like appearance due to wedge cross-section.

```
LONGITUDINAL VIEW COMPARISON (Schematic):

Mulberry:  ════════════════════════════  (smooth, structureless)
Tasar:     ══╤═╤═╤═╤═╤═╤═╤═╤═╤═╤═╤══  (striations / fine lines)
Muga:      ══╤═╤═╤═╤═╤═╤═╤═╤═╤═╤═╤══  (fine striations, golden tint)
Eri:       ══╤═╤═╤═╤═╤═╤═╤═╤═╤═╤═╤══  (striations, flat/irregular)
```

> **Example:** In forensic and quality control labs, the presence or absence of longitudinal striations under a microscope is the **first visual cue** to distinguish mulberry silk (smooth, no striations) from tasar or muga silk (striated).

## 5. Cross-Sectional View — Mulberry Silk

The cross-section of degummed mulberry silk (*Bombyx mori*) is one of the most diagnostic features:

1. **Shape:** **Approximately triangular** with rounded corners — like a rounded triangle or a "plano-convex lens" shape.
2. **Two distinct triangular brins:** The two fibroin filaments appear as **two separate triangular or subtriangular profiles** side by side.
3. **Smooth cross-sectional outline** — no irregularities or indentations on the perimeter.
4. **Absence of striations** in the cross-section profile.
5. **Typical dimensions:** ~10–14 μm per brin.
6. **Sericin layer** (if undegummed): Visible as an amorphous, granular coating around the two triangular brins.
7. **The triangular prism shape** is responsible for silk's shimmering appearance — it refracts light at different angles.

```
CROSS-SECTION — MULBERRY SILK:

    Undegummed:                  Degummed:
  ┌──────────────┐             △  ←  Brin 1 (triangular)
  │  ╱▲╲  ╱▲╲  │             △  ←  Brin 2 (triangular)
  │ /    \/   \ │
  │[Sericin   ]│
  └──────────────┘
  Elliptical bave              Two separate triangular brins
  (two brins inside sericin)
```

> **Example:** The triangular cross-section of silk was first documented by Antoni van Leeuwenhoek in the 17th century using one of the earliest microscopes. He noted that silk's cross-section differed from every other fibre he had examined.

## 6. Cross-Sectional View — Non-Mulberry Silks

Non-mulberry silks show significantly different cross-sectional morphologies:

### Tasar Silk (*Antheraea mylitta*)
1. **Shape:** **Elongated rectangular** or flat **wedge-shaped**.
2. Much wider than mulberry brins (15–24 μm vs 10–14 μm).
3. Irregular perimeter — not as geometrically regular as mulberry.
4. Striations are visible on the surface.
5. Cross-sections of all non-mulberry silks are **more or less rectangular**.

### Muga Silk (*Antheraea assamensis*)
1. **Shape:** **Elongated triangular** — between mulberry (triangular) and tasar (rectangular).
2. More uniform than tasar but still shows elongation.
3. Some surface striations visible.
4. Characteristic golden tint in cross-section pigmentation.

### Eri Silk (*Samia cynthia ricini*)
1. **Shape:** **Flat, wedge or spindle-shaped** — more irregular than all others.
2. Flatter and wider cross-section.
3. Striations on surface.
4. Less crystalline packing — hence less lustrous and weaker.

```
CROSS-SECTIONAL SHAPES — COMPARATIVE:

  Mulberry (B. mori):     ▲     ← Near-triangular, smooth, fine (~10–14 μm)
  Tasar (A. mylitta):     ▬     ← Elongated rectangle/wedge (~15–24 μm)
  Muga (A. assamensis):   △     ← Elongated triangle (~13–20 μm)
  Eri (S. c. ricini):     ◇     ← Flat spindle/wedge (~12–16 μm)
```

> **Example:** A forensic textile scientist can definitively identify Muga silk from tasar by comparing their cross-sections under SEM — Muga shows an elongated triangular shape with more uniform dimensions, while tasar shows a broader, more irregular rectangular profile.

## 7. SEM (Scanning Electron Microscope) Analysis

SEM provides the most detailed morphological information about silk fibres:

### Longitudinal SEM Views

**Undegummed silk (SEM — longitudinal):**
1. Shows thick, irregular sericin coating with surface cracks.
2. Protruding sericin bridges between adjacent fibres visible.
3. Some sericin-free patches may reveal the smooth fibroin beneath.

**Degummed silk (SEM — longitudinal):**
1. Mulberry: Perfectly smooth surface, no features.
2. Tasar and Muga: Clear longitudinal striations (ridges and valleys along the fibre axis).
3. The harsh feel of tasar/muga silk is directly linked to these surface striations.

### Cross-Sectional SEM Views

1. **Mulberry:** Shows two clean, smooth triangular brins — no pores, smooth perimeter.
2. **Tasar:** Two broad, rectangular filaments; surface striations extend through the cross-section.
3. **Muga:** Intermediate morphology; elongated triangular, moderate striations.
4. **Eri:** Flat spindle profile; some internal fibrillation visible.
5. SEM can also reveal: partial fibrillation from over-degumming, tin-weighting damage, and natural defects.

### Key SEM Findings for Mulberry Silk
1. The sericin layer completely surrounds both fibroin brins in an intact cocoon filament.
2. The fibroin brins are separated from each other within the sericin matrix.
3. After degumming, fibroin shows no surface texture at all — perfectly smooth.
4. Internal fibrillar structure: visible at high magnification (>5,000×) as parallel nanofibrils (20–30 nm diameter).
5. Cross-sectional area of mulberry brins: typically 80–150 μm² (variable between individual cocoons).

> **Example:** SEM analysis has been used to quantify the **weighting process** in historical silk museum pieces — tin-phosphate nanoparticles deposited on silk surfaces during 19th-century weighting treatments are clearly visible as bright specks on SEM images, and their distribution correlates with fabric deterioration patterns.

| Feature | Mulberry | Tasar | Muga | Eri |

| **Cross-section (degummed)** | Triangular, rounded | Elongated rectangular/wedge | Elongated triangular | Flat spindle/wedge |
| **Surface texture** | Smooth | Striated, rough | Striated | Striated |

| **Uniformity** | Very uniform | Less uniform | Moderate | Less uniform |
| **Lustre under microscope** | High | Moderate | High (golden) | Low |

## 9. Microscopic Identification vs Other Fibres

Under the microscope, silk can be differentiated from other natural fibres:

| Fibre | Longitudinal View | Cross-Section |
|-------|------------------|---------------|
| **Mulberry Silk** | Smooth, structureless rod | Triangular (rounded corners) |
| **Wool** | Overlapping scales (cuticle), crimp | Circular with cortex/medulla |
| **Cotton** | Twisted ribbon, bean-shaped, convolutions | Kidney-shaped (collapsed flat tube) |
| **Linen (Flax)** | Bamboo-like nodes, polygonal | Polygonal, lumen visible |
| **Human Hair** | Smooth to scaly (cuticle), medulla | Circular, dark medulla |
| **Cashmere** | Faint scales, smooth | Circular, smaller than wool |
| **Viscose Rayon** | Smooth, serrated (striations) | Serrated/lobed or circular |
| **Nylon** | Smooth | Circular |

Key distinguishing features of silk specifically:
1. **No scales** (unlike wool and hair).
2. **Triangular cross-section** (unlike all round-section fibres).
3. **Structureless smooth surface** when degummed (unlike wool, cotton, linen).
4. **Paired filament structure** (bave) when undegummed — unique to silk.
5. **Very high transparency** under transmitted light.

> **Example:** A classic mistake in textile identification is confusing fine wool fibres with silk under low magnification. The definitive test: look at the cross-section. Wool is circular; silk is triangular. And look for scales on the surface — wool has them, silk does not.

## 10. Burn Test for Identification

While not a microscopic test, the burn test is used alongside microscopy for silk identification:

### Silk Burn Characteristics
1. **Ignition:** Burns slowly; difficult to maintain flame once the source is removed.
2. **Flame behaviour:** Steady but the flame self-extinguishes when removed from heat source.
3. **Smell:** Smells like **burning hair or feathers** (protein burning).
4. **Ash:** Leaves a **black, crunchy ash** that can be **crushed to a powder** with fingers.
5. **Residue:** Irregular, non-beaded black char (as opposed to synthetic fibres which form hard, round, plastic beads).

### Comparison with Wool
| Feature | Silk | Wool |
|---------|------|------|
| Smell | Burning hair (similar) | Burning hair (similar) |
| Ash | Black, powdery, crushable | Black, powdery (similar) |
| Self-extinguishes | Yes | Yes |
| Distinctive | Slightly more disagreeable odour | Slightly milder |

Chemical test to distinguish silk from wool:
1. Treat with **concentrated cold hydrochloric acid**: Silk dissolves; wool swells.
2. Treat with **concentrated sodium hydroxide**: Silk dissolves; wool also dissolves (more slowly).
3. **Biuret test**: Both silk and wool give positive results (both proteins).
4. The key chemical distinction: wool contains **disulfide (cystine) bonds** (due to sulfur); silk does **not** — confirmed by sulfur test.

> **Example:** In a fabric quality lab, the quickest way to distinguish a silk–wool blend from pure silk is the **Biuret + sulfur test combination**: positive Biuret (protein present in both) + positive sulfur test = wool present. If no sulfur is detected, the sample is pure silk.
