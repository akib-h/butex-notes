## 1. Overview — Silk as a Protein Polymer

Silk is fundamentally a **natural protein biopolymer** — a material made entirely of amino acid building blocks organised into polypeptide chains. Understanding its chemistry explains all of its physical and chemical properties.

Key chemical facts:
1. Silk is composed primarily of **two proteins**: fibroin (~75–80%) and sericin (~20–25%).
2. Both proteins are **polypeptides** — long chains of amino acids linked by peptide bonds.
3. Silk fibroin is classified as a **fibrous (structural) protein**; sericin is a **globular (adhesive) protein**.
4. The empirical formula of silk fibroin is approximately **(C₁₅H₂₃N₅O₆)ₙ**.
5. Silk is **amphoteric** — it carries both acidic and basic functional groups, allowing it to react with both acids and alkalis.
6. The **Biuret test** (positive with purple colour) confirms the protein nature of silk.
7. Silk contains the chemical elements: **Carbon (C), Hydrogen (H), Nitrogen (N), and Oxygen (O)** — notably **no Sulfur (S)**, which distinguishes it from wool/keratin.

> **Example:** The absence of sulfur in silk means it has **no disulfide bonds** (–S–S–) — a key difference from wool. While wool gains its springy, elastic character partly from disulfide crosslinks between polypeptide chains, silk's chains are held together by hydrogen bonds and van der Waals forces instead.

## 2. Overall Chemical Composition of Raw Silk

| Component | Percentage by Weight | Nature |
|-----------|---------------------|--------|
| **Fibroin** | 70–80% | Fibrous structural protein |
| **Sericin** | 20–30% | Amorphous adhesive glycoprotein |
| **Wax** | ~1.5% | Fatty esters and alcohols |
| **Carbohydrate** | ~1.2% | Sugars (linked to sericin) |
| **Inorganic matter** | ~1.0% | Mineral salts (K, Na, Ca, Mg) |
| **Pigments** | trace | Carotenoids, flavonoids |

Additional notes:
1. The **sericin-to-fibroin ratio** varies by silkworm species and rearing conditions.
2. **Cocoon quality** is influenced directly by this ratio.
3. Waxes and fats are found mainly in the **sericin layer** — they provide water repellency to the cocoon surface.
4. Inorganic salts include potassium, calcium, magnesium, and sodium ions.

> **Example:** Commercial degumming removes sericin, wax, and most inorganic matter — leaving nearly pure fibroin. This is why degummed silk weighs 20–30% less than raw silk, and why some historical "weighted silk" was artificially loaded with metallic salts (tin) to make up for this weight loss.

## 3. Fibroin — Structure and Composition

Fibroin is the primary structural protein of silk — the fibre's "skeleton."

### Molecular Architecture
Silk fibroin is composed of **three protein chains** assembled in a specific ratio:

1. **Heavy (H) Chain:**
   - Molecular weight: **~390 kDa** (350–400 kDa).
   - Very long polypeptide — the dominant structural component.
   - Composed of **repetitive hydrophobic domains** alternating with short hydrophilic linkers.
   - The repeating unit is the **hexapeptide: Gly-Ser-Gly-Ala-Gly-Ala**.
   - The hydrophobic domains form **antiparallel β-sheets** — the crystalline core.

2. **Light (L) Chain:**
   - Molecular weight: **~26 kDa**.
   - Non-repetitive amino acid sequence (unlike H-chain).
   - More **hydrophilic** and relatively elastic.
   - Linked to the H-chain by a **single disulfide bond** at the C-terminus of the H-chain.

3. **P25 Glycoprotein:**
   - Molecular weight: **~25–30 kDa**.
   - Associated with the H–L complex through **non-covalent hydrophobic interactions** (not disulfide bonds).
   - Contains **N-linked oligosaccharide chains** — making it a glycoprotein.
   - Plays a key role in maintaining the **structural integrity** of the complex.

### Assembly Ratio
The three components assemble in a **6:6:1 molar ratio**:
- 6 H-chains : 6 L-chains : 1 P25 molecule.

```
Fibroin Complex Structure:

H–chain ──(S–S)── L–chain     ×6 pairs
         |
    Non-covalent ── P25  ×1 molecule
```

> **Example:** The H–L disulfide bond is essential for fibroin assembly — without it, the chains cannot properly combine to form the ordered β-sheet structure. This is analogous to a key-and-lock mechanism where the disulfide bond serves as the "lock" that holds the two chains together before β-sheet formation.

## 4. Sericin — Structure and Composition

Sericin is the **glue protein** that holds the two fibroin brins together and forms the protective outer layer of the cocoon.

### Composition
1. Molecular weight range: **20,000–200,000 Da** (heterogeneous — multiple forms).
2. Sericin is a **hydrophilic glycoprotein** — its chemical composition is quite different from fibroin.
3. The amino acid **serine** constitutes approximately **~40%** of sericin's residues — far higher than in fibroin.
4. Other major amino acids: aspartic acid, glycine, threonine, valine.
5. The serine-rich domains contain **–OH groups** that interact with water molecules, making sericin sticky and gel-like.

### Types of Sericin
| Type | Molecular Weight | Solubility |
|------|-----------------|------------|
| Sericin A | Highest | Least soluble |
| Sericin B | High | Moderately soluble |
| Sericin C | Low | More soluble |
| Sericin D | Lowest | Most soluble |

### Structure
1. Sericin is **amorphous** — it does not form the ordered β-sheets of fibroin.
2. It forms a **gel-like structure** around the fibroin brins.
3. Sericin can form **gels, films, and scaffolds** when isolated and processed — useful in biomedicine.
4. The hydrogen bonds between sericin and fibroin retard β-sheet crystallisation of fibroin during cocooning.

> **Example:** The hydrophilic (-OH rich) nature of sericin is what makes raw silk stiff and somewhat water-resistant on the outside — the sericin absorbs surface moisture, swells, and forms a gel that then seals the cocoon surface. This is nature's waterproofing mechanism.

## 5. Amino Acid Composition

The amino acid composition of **B. mori silk fibroin** is dominated by three small amino acids:

| Amino Acid | Symbol | Side Chain (R) | % in Fibroin | Function in Fibre |
|------------|--------|----------------|-------------|------------------|
| **Glycine** | Gly (G) | –H | ~43–45% | Smallest AA; allows tight packing; appears on one side of β-sheet |
| **Alanine** | Ala (A) | –CH₃ | ~29–30% | Small methyl group; appears on opposite side of β-sheet |
| **Serine** | Ser (S) | –CH₂OH | ~12% | Hydroxyl group; hydrogen bonding; moisture absorption |
| Tyrosine | Tyr (Y) | –CH₂C₆H₄OH | ~5% | Aromatic ring; UV absorption; some crystallinity |
| Valine | Val (V) | –CH(CH₃)₂ | ~2% | Bulky; in amorphous regions |
| Glutamic acid | Glu (E) | –CH₂CH₂COOH | ~1% | Charged; in amorphous regions |
| Aspartic acid | Asp (D) | –CH₂COOH | ~1% | Charged; in amorphous regions |
| Other | — | Various | ~5% | Amorphous regions |

Key facts:
1. Gly + Ala + Ser together constitute approximately **85%** of all fibroin residues.
2. The **molar ratio of Gly:Ala:Ser ≈ 3:2:1** in the crystalline domains.
3. **Glycine (45%)** — its tiny H side chain allows adjacent polypeptide chains to pack together extremely tightly → high crystallinity → high strength.
4. **No sulfur-containing amino acids** (no cysteine, no methionine) — therefore NO disulfide bonds.
5. **No proline** — proline would disrupt β-sheet formation (unlike collagen which relies on proline).

### Sericin Amino Acid Composition (for comparison)
| Amino Acid | % in Sericin |
|------------|-------------|
| Serine | ~38–40% |
| Aspartic acid | ~16–18% |
| Glycine | ~13% |
| Threonine | ~8% |
| Valine | ~4% |
| Others | ~20% |

> **Example:** Glycine is the **smallest amino acid possible** — its "side chain" is just a single hydrogen atom. In the β-sheet structure of silk, glycine residues occupy one face of the sheet while alanine residues occupy the other. This alternating pattern (Gly-Ala-Gly-Ala...) is like interlocking the teeth of two zippers — creating an incredibly compact, dense, and strong crystalline structure.

## 6. Primary Structure — Polypeptide Chain

The **primary structure** of a protein is its amino acid sequence — the order in which amino acids are connected by **peptide bonds**.

### Peptide Bond
```
    H    R₁   O        H    R₂   O
    |    |    ‖        |    |    ‖
  –N – CH – C–OH  +  H–N – CH – C–OH
                ↓
    H    R₁   O    H    R₂   O
    |    |    ‖    |    |    ‖
  –N – CH – C – N – CH – C–OH  +  H₂O
              Peptide Bond
```

1. Each amino acid is joined to the next by a **peptide bond** (–CO–NH–) — a covalent amide linkage.
2. The reaction is a **condensation reaction** releasing water.
3. Peptide bonds are **planar** (rigid due to partial double bond character of C–N) — this constraint forces the polypeptide chain to adopt specific secondary structures.
4. The **Bombyx mori H-chain** repeating unit is the hexapeptide: **Gly–Ser–Gly–Ala–Gly–Ala** (in the crystalline domains).
5. This can also be written as: **(–Gly–Ala–)ₙ** with occasional serine substitutions for glycine.
6. The L-chain has a **non-repetitive** sequence — making it more hydrophilic and less prone to crystallisation.

### Backbone Notation
```
Gly – Ser – Gly – Ala – Gly – Ala – Gly – Ser – Gly – Ala – Gly – Ala ...
 |                |                |
(small)         (–OH)           (small)
```

> **Example:** The repeating hexapeptide sequence (Gly-Ser-Gly-Ala-Gly-Ala)ₙ in silk fibroin is analogous to a repeating bar pattern in a barcode — it creates a highly regular, predictable structure that nature has optimised over millions of years of evolution for maximum mechanical efficiency.

## 7. Secondary Structure — β-Pleated Sheet

The **secondary structure** describes how the polypeptide chain is folded in space. In silk fibroin, the dominant secondary structure is the **antiparallel β-pleated sheet** (also called β-sheet or Silk II structure).

### β-Pleated Sheet Formation
1. The polypeptide chains **extend** fully (unlike in α-helices where they coil).
2. Adjacent chains run in **opposite directions** (antiparallel arrangement) — this is thermodynamically more stable.
3. The chains are held together side-by-side by **inter-chain hydrogen bonds** between NH and C=O groups.
4. The result is a **flat, pleated (zig-zag) sheet** structure.
5. The **glycine** side chains (–H) project to **one side** of the sheet plane.
6. The **alanine** side chains (–CH₃) project to the **other side** of the sheet plane.
7. Sheets then **stack** on top of each other — held by **van der Waals forces** between layers.

### Antiparallel vs Parallel β-sheets in Silk
| Property | Antiparallel β-sheet (dominant) | Parallel β-sheet |
|----------|--------------------------------|-----------------|
| Chain direction | Alternating opposite | All same direction |
| Tensile strength | 3.8 GPa | 2.5 GPa |
| Elongation at break | 4% | 9% |
| H-bond geometry | More linear, stronger | Slightly bent, weaker |
| Stability | More stable | Less stable |

### Diagrammatic Representation
```
Antiparallel β-Sheet (Silk II):

→  Gly–Ala–Gly–Ala–Gly–Ala →
              H–bonds (||)
←  Ala–Gly–Ala–Gly–Ala–Gly ←
              H–bonds (||)
→  Gly–Ala–Gly–Ala–Gly–Ala →
              H–bonds (||)
←  Ala–Gly–Ala–Gly–Ala–Gly ←

Gly side chains (–H) project downward
Ala side chains (–CH₃) project upward
Van der Waals forces hold stacked sheets
```

> **Example:** The β-pleated sheet is nature's version of corrugated cardboard — flat layers stacked on each other. Just as corrugated cardboard is strong and stiff when compressed along the corrugations but flexible when bent, β-sheets give silk its strength along the fibre axis while the sliding between sheets provides limited extensibility.

## 8. Tertiary and Quaternary Structure

### Tertiary Structure
1. The tertiary structure describes the **3D folding** of the polypeptide chain.
2. In silk fibroin, the tertiary structure is dominated by the **extended β-sheet conformation** — there is minimal coiling or compact globular folding.
3. The crystalline (β-sheet) and amorphous regions alternate along the chain length — forming a **fringed fibrillar** organisation.
4. The amorphous regions contain amino acids with bulky side chains (Tyr, Val, Glu, Asp) that cannot pack into tight β-sheets.
5. Unlike globular proteins (enzymes, antibodies), fibroin's tertiary structure is **highly oriented along a single axis** — the fibre axis.

### Quaternary Structure
1. Silk fibroin's quaternary structure = the assembly of multiple polypeptide chains.
2. The **H-chain + L-chain** are linked by a **disulfide bond** → forming one H-L pair.
3. Six H-L pairs associate with one P25 glycoprotein via **non-covalent interactions**.
4. Multiple fibroin elementary units aggregate via **hydrophobic interactions** during spinning.
5. This quaternary organisation creates the **nanofibrillar structure** visible under TEM.

> **Example:** The H-L chain disulfide bond in silk fibroin is the only covalent inter-chain bond. If this bond is broken (by reducing agents), the quaternary structure disassembles — this is the basis of chemical dissolution of silk in certain solvents like LiBr/water systems used in biomedical applications.

## 9. Types of Bonds in Silk

Silk's structural integrity relies on **multiple types of intermolecular and intramolecular bonds**:

### 9.1 Peptide Bonds (Covalent)
1. **Type:** Covalent amide bond (–CO–NH–).
2. **Location:** Within each polypeptide chain — the primary backbone bond.
3. **Strength:** Very strong (bond energy ~350 kJ/mol).
4. **Breakage:** Broken by **acid hydrolysis** (concentrated H₂SO₄) or proteolytic enzymes.
5. **Example in silk:** Every amino acid in the fibroin chain is connected to the next via peptide bonds.

### 9.2 Hydrogen Bonds
1. **Type:** Electrostatic dipole-dipole interaction between H (donor) and electronegative atom (acceptor, typically O or N).
2. **Location:** Between **C=O** and **N–H** groups on adjacent antiparallel β-sheet chains.
3. **Strength:** Individually weak (~8–40 kJ/mol) but **collectively very strong** — thousands per fibre.
4. **Role:** Primary force holding β-sheets together laterally → confers tensile strength.
5. **Effect of water:** Water competes for H-bonds → silk loses 15–20% strength when wet.
6. **Inter-chain H-bond:** –NH··· O=C– between adjacent chains in the β-sheet plane.
7. **Example:** When silk fibre is stretched, the applied force is distributed across millions of H-bonds simultaneously — this is why silk is strong without being brittle.

### 9.3 Disulfide Bonds (–S–S–)
1. **Type:** Covalent bond between two cysteine sulfur atoms.
2. **Location:** **Only at the C-terminus** of the H-chain — linking H-chain to L-chain.
3. **Quantity:** One disulfide bond per H-L pair (very limited compared to wool).
4. **Role:** Maintains quaternary structure of the fibroin complex.
5. **Contrast with wool:** Wool keratin has **many** disulfide bonds (cystine crosslinks) throughout the structure, giving wool its elastic springback. Silk has only **one** disulfide bond per fibroin complex — hence silk **does not** have wool's elastic springback.
6. **Consequence:** Because silk lacks disulfide crosslinks, **wrinkles in silk do not hang out** as quickly as in wool.

### 9.4 Van der Waals Forces
1. **Type:** Weak induced dipole-dipole interactions.
2. **Location:** Between stacked β-sheet layers (between sheet planes).
3. **Strength:** Very weak individually (~1–10 kJ/mol) but significant in aggregate.
4. **Role:** Holds adjacent β-sheets together in the fibre stacking direction.
5. **Effect:** Allows sheets to slide over each other (limited extensibility) → silk can elongate up to 20–25%.

### 9.5 Hydrophobic Interactions
1. **Type:** Entropy-driven association of nonpolar groups in aqueous environments.
2. **Location:** Between hydrophobic amino acid residues (Ala, Val, Tyr methylene groups) in the core of β-sheet crystallites.
3. **Role:** Drives aggregation of fibroin chains during spinning; contributes to crystallite stability.
4. **P25 glycoprotein** is associated with the H-L complex primarily via hydrophobic interactions.

### 9.6 Ionic (Salt) Bonds
1. **Type:** Electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged groups.
2. **Location:** Between cationic (–NH₃⁺) and anionic (–COO⁻) groups on amino acid side chains.
3. **Role:** Minor contribution in the amorphous regions; affects dye-binding sites.
4. **pH dependence:** At the **isoelectric point** of silk fibroin (pH ~3.8–4.3), ionic bonds are maximised.

### Bond Summary Table

| Bond Type | Location | Strength | Broken By | Role |
|-----------|---------|---------|-----------|------|
| Peptide (covalent) | Backbone | Very strong | Acid/enzyme | Primary chain structure |
| Disulfide (covalent) | H–L chain junction | Strong | Reducing agents | Quaternary structure |
| Hydrogen bonds | β-sheet lateral | Moderate (cumulative strong) | Water, heat | Tensile strength, β-sheet formation |
| Van der Waals | Between β-sheet layers | Weak (cumulative) | Solvents, heat | Sheet stacking, extensibility |
| Hydrophobic | Core crystallite | Moderate | Non-polar solvents | Fibre aggregation |
| Ionic bonds | Amorphous regions | Moderate | pH change | Dye binding |

> **Example:** The superiority of silk's mechanical properties over most polymers comes from the **synergy of bonds**: the covalent peptide backbone carries primary load, hydrogen bonds distribute stress laterally across the β-sheet, van der Waals forces allow controlled sliding between sheets (preventing brittle fracture), and hydrophobic interactions drive tight crystallite packing. No single bond type alone could achieve this performance.

## 10. Silk I and Silk II Crystal Structures

Two main crystalline polymorphs of silk fibroin exist:

### Silk I (Pre-spinning structure in gland)
1. Found in the **posterior silk gland** before spinning.
2. A **metastable, soluble** structure — the silk is in liquid form.
3. Structure: **Type II β-turn** conformation (crankshaft or S-zigzag pattern).
4. Also described as an **orthorhombic crystal system**.
5. Contains intramolecular hydrogen bonds.
6. The torsion angles (φ, ψ) determined by NMR are consistent with repeated type II β-turn structure.
7. **Critical property:** Silk I can flow as a viscous liquid — enabling the silkworm to store it in the gland and extrude it on demand.

### Silk II (Final fibre structure)
1. Found in the **extruded silk fibre** (cocoon and textile fibre).
2. **Stable, insoluble** structure.
3. Structure: **Antiparallel β-pleated sheet** — the dominant form.
4. **Monoclinic crystal system.**
5. Strong inter-chain hydrogen bonds between adjacent chains in the β-sheet.
6. High crystallinity (~60–65%).
7. **Irreversible** — once Silk II forms, it does not revert to Silk I under normal conditions.

### Silk III
1. A rare, unstable structure found at **air/water interfaces** in regenerated fibroin solutions.
2. Characterised by a **3-fold helical structure**.
3. Not present in native silk fibre.

### Silk I → Silk II Conversion
The transformation occurs during the spinning process:
- **Triggers:** Shear forces during extrusion through the spinneret; pH change (from ~6.9 in gland to ~4.8 at spinneret tip); ionic concentration change (K⁺ replaces Na⁺).
- **Mechanism:** Type II β-turn structure unfolds and reorganises into antiparallel β-sheets.
- **Artificial conversion:** Methanol treatment or potassium phosphate treatment can induce Silk I → Silk II in regenerated fibroin solutions.

> **Example:** The Silk I → Silk II conversion is what makes silk fibre permanent. When a silkworm spins its cocoon, the silk in its gland (Silk I, liquid) is instantaneously and irreversibly converted to solid fibre (Silk II) as it exits the spinneret — a transformation driven purely by mechanical and pH forces, with no heat or external processing needed.

## 11. Chemical Formula

The empirical formula of **silk fibroin** is:

```
(C₁₅H₂₃N₅O₆)ₙ
```

Where:
- C = Carbon
- H = Hydrogen
- N = Nitrogen
- O = Oxygen
- n = degree of polymerisation (very large number)
- **No sulfur (S)** — key distinguishing fact from wool (keratin).

The molecular weight of the **H-chain** alone is ~390,000 Da (390 kDa).

Elemental composition (approximate):
| Element | % by mass |
|---------|---------|
| Carbon (C) | ~48–50% |
| Hydrogen (H) | ~6–7% |
| Nitrogen (N) | ~17–18% |
| Oxygen (O) | ~25–27% |
| Sulfur (S) | ~0% (absent) |

> **Example:** An inorganic chemistry test for sulfur (lead acetate test — turns brown/black in the presence of S²⁻ ions) gives a **negative result** for silk but **positive** for wool. This single test result can immediately confirm: "No sulfur → not wool → possibly silk." Combined with microscopy (triangular cross-section), silk is confirmed.

## 12. Minor Chemical Components

### Waxes and Fats
1. Located in the **sericin layer** (outer coating).
2. Composed of fatty acid esters and long-chain alcohols.
3. Provide **hydrophobicity** to the cocoon surface (water-resistance).
4. Removed during degumming.

### Carbohydrates
1. Primarily associated with sericin (sericin is a glycoprotein).
2. N-linked oligosaccharide chains on P25 glycoprotein.
3. Sugars include mannose, glucosamine, galactose.
4. Quantity: ~1.2% by weight.

### Inorganic Salts
1. Potassium (K⁺), calcium (Ca²⁺), magnesium (Mg²⁺), sodium (Na⁺), phosphate (PO₄³⁻).
2. Calcium and potassium ions play a role in stabilising sericin structure.
3. Removed during degumming and washing.

### Pigments
1. **Xanthophylls** → golden cocoon colour.
2. **Carotene and vialoxanthin** → yellow colour.
3. **Bombycin and bombycetin** → green colour (rare).
4. The innate yellowness of raw silk largely comes from pigments in the sericin; degumming reduces but does not eliminate all colour.
