## 1. Overview

Silk's unique combination of properties — luminous sheen, extraordinary strength, moisture management, thermal regulation, and bio-compatibility — makes it unrivalled among natural fibres. These properties arise directly from the **β-sheet crystalline structure of fibroin** and the **amphoteric protein chemistry** of both fibroin and sericin.

> **Example:** Silk is sometimes described as having a "paradoxical" character — it feels cool in summer and warm in winter, it is strong yet smooth, it is biodegradable yet resistant to mould. Each of these paradoxes has a scientific explanation rooted in its molecular structure.

---

## 2. Physical Properties

### 2.1 Lustre

1. Silk possesses a **natural, brilliant, pearly lustre** — a property unique among natural fibres.
2. The lustre arises from the **triangular prism-like cross-section** of the individual brin, which refracts and reflects incoming light at multiple angles — producing different colours depending on the viewing angle (iridescence).
3. This is a **structural colour** effect, not a pigment effect.
4. The degree of lustre depends on fibre fineness, degumming quality, and processing.
5. Weighted silk (with metallic salts) has enhanced lustre and drape.
6. **Types of silk lustre:**
   - *Bright (Bave/Raw silk):* Moderate lustre due to sericin covering.
   - *Brilliant (Degummed):* Maximum natural lustre — highly reflective.

> **Example:** The pearly sheen of cultured pearls is aesthetically similar to silk's lustre — both arise from the interaction of light with thin, layered structures that act as diffraction gratings at a microscopic level.

---

### 2.2 Colour

1. **Cultivated (mulberry) silk:** Naturally **off-white to cream** in colour.
2. **Tasar silk:** Naturally **tan to medium brown**.
3. **Muga silk:** Naturally **golden yellow** — and this golden colour intensifies with washing and age.
4. **Eri silk:** Naturally **white or brick-red**.
5. Colour originates from **sericin pigments** (carotenoids, xanthophylls) and to a lesser extent fibroin.
6. After degumming, silk retains a slight **residual yellowness** from fibroin-bound pigments.
7. Silk has **excellent dyeability** — the widest colour gamut of any natural fibre.

---

### 2.3 Density

1. Silk is the **lightest among natural fibres**.
2. Density **with sericin:** 1,320–1,400 kg/m³.
3. Density **without sericin (pure fibroin):** 1,300–1,380 kg/m³.
4. For comparison: cotton ~1,540 kg/m³; wool ~1,310 kg/m³; nylon ~1,140 kg/m³.
5. The relatively low density is why silk fabrics feel lightweight yet provide excellent cover and drape.

> **Example:** A 1-metre square of silk satin weighing only 60 g can cover the same area as a cotton fabric weighing 120 g — silk provides half the weight for the same area. This is why silk has been prized for parachutes and military silk maps.

---

### 2.4 Length and Fineness

1. **Filament length per cocoon:** 600–1,500 metres (mulberry silk).
2. **Denier:** 1–3 denier per brin (mulberry); up to 6 denier for tasar.
3. **Diameter of brin:** 10–14 μm (mulberry) — approximately 5× thinner than a human hair.
4. Silk is finer than any other natural fibre and comparable to the finest synthetic fibres.
5. The thickness of silk filament yarn is expressed in **denier** (g per 9,000 m of length).

> **Example:** A single mulberry silk brin at 10 μm diameter is so fine it is barely visible to the naked eye. Yet if you stretch this same brin to its breaking point, it can support a weight of approximately 4 grams — strength-to-weight ratio comparable to high-tensile steel.

---

### 2.5 Strength (Tenacity)

1. Silk is the **strongest natural fibre** — stronger than many synthetic fibres.
2. **Dry tenacity:** 2.4–5.1 g/denier (3.5 g/d typical for mulberry silk).
3. **Wet tenacity:** 15–25% weaker than dry — silk loses significant strength when wet.
4. **Tensile strength:** Approximately 3.5–5.0 GPa (gigapascals) for individual fibroin filaments.
5. **Breaking force:** Higher than cotton, Kevlar, and nylon fibres at equivalent cross-sections.
6. A silk filament of the same diameter as a steel wire is **comparably strong** (iron wire: ~3.5 GPa; silk fibroin: ~3.8–5.0 GPa).
7. The high strength is attributed to: the tightly packed antiparallel β-sheet structure, the small side chains of Gly and Ala allowing tight molecular packing, and the vast number of inter-chain hydrogen bonds.

> **Example:** Historical Mongolian warriors wore silk undergarments beneath their leather armour — not for comfort, but because a silk-wrapped arrow wound was far easier to remove from the body than a barbed arrow through conventional material. The silk would wrap around the arrow tip and resist tearing, reducing internal damage.

---

### 2.6 Elongation and Elasticity

1. **Elongation at break (dry):** 10–25% (typical ~20%).
2. **Elongation at break (wet):** Slightly higher than dry (silk swells with water).
3. Silk has **moderate to poor elasticity (resilience)** compared to wool.
4. At deformations below 2%, silk shows **full elastic recovery** (completely springs back).
5. At deformations above ~5%, silk shows **permanent deformation** — it does not spring back fully.
6. Silk lacks the cystine crosslinks of wool — so stretched silk fibres tend to remain stretched.
7. **Flexibility:** Silk can withstand deformation of 20–25% of its initial length without breaking.
8. **Toughness:** Comparable to nylon and Kevlar.

> **Example:** If you stretch a wool garment slightly and release it, it springs back to its original shape (due to disulfide crosslinks acting as springs). If you stretch a silk garment similarly, it will remain slightly deformed — a practical difference to remember when ironing or blocking silk garments.

---

### 2.7 Moisture Absorption (Regain)

1. **Moisture regain:** Approximately **11%** at standard conditions (65% RH, 20°C) — this means silk can absorb 11% of its dry weight in moisture.
2. Silk can absorb up to **30% of its weight** in moisture without feeling damp.
3. This is due to the **hydrophilic amorphous regions** and polar groups (–NH, –OH, –COOH) in fibroin.
4. Silk is **highly absorbent** — superior to cotton and cellulose acetate in hygroscopicity.
5. Moisture absorption enables silk to wick perspiration away from the skin.
6. **Wet silk is weaker** (15–20% strength reduction) — the water molecules compete with H-bonds.
7. **Hygral expansion:** Silk swells when wet, altering dimensions — important for care and washing.

> **Example:** Silk surgical sutures were historically preferred in moist body environments (ocular, cardiovascular surgery) partly because of silk's excellent moisture management — it absorbs wound exudate and maintains knot strength even when saturated with moisture.

---

### 2.8 Thermal Properties

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Glass transition temperature (Tg) | ~175°C |
| Thermal degradation onset | ~275°C |
| Thermal decomposition (full) | >300°C |
| Stability (continuous use) | Up to ~140°C |
| Decomposes at | ~170–175°C |

1. Silk is **more heat-resistant than wool** (wool decomposes at ~130°C; silk at ~170°C).
2. Silk is a **good thermal insulator** — low thermal conductivity coefficient.
3. Silk fabrics feel **cool to the touch** in summer (due to high moisture absorption and thin fibre) and **warm in winter** (due to insulating properties of multiple silk layers).
4. Silk is **not thermoplastic** — it does not melt like synthetic fibres; it chars and decomposes.
5. **Ironing:** Silk requires a cool iron (140–150°C) — excessive heat causes yellowing and weakening.
6. **Flame:** Silk burns slowly and self-extinguishes (as a protein) — discussed in Section 10 of File 04.

> **Example:** The "cool in summer, warm in winter" paradox of silk is explained by two separate mechanisms: the thin (low-density) fibre allows good air circulation and heat transfer away from the body in summer; while in winter, silk's protein structure traps air and provides insulation similar to wool, albeit less effectively.

---

### 2.9 Electrical Properties

1. Silk is a **poor conductor of electricity** — it is an electrical insulator.
2. This makes it **susceptible to static electricity buildup**, especially in low humidity.
3. Friction imparts a **static charge** to silk — causing fabric to cling.
4. The crackling sound when removing silk from the body on a dry day is a familiar demonstration of static buildup.
5. Antistatic treatments are applied industrially to reduce cling.

> **Example:** In the early computing era (1950s–1960s), silk was used as electrical insulation in some precision instruments and capacitors precisely because of its excellent electrical insulation properties, fine gauge, and dimensional uniformity.

---

### 2.10 Resilience and Flexibility

1. **Resilience:** Moderate — better than cotton, inferior to wool.
2. Silk has **high resistance to creasing** due to good resilience of fibres and their rapid recovery from deformation.
3. The silk fibre recovers rapidly from bending deformations (flexural recovery) because van der Waals forces between β-sheets allow sheets to slide and re-stack.
4. **Flexural rigidity:** Relatively high — greater than wool and acetate fibres.
5. **Torsional rigidity:** Similar to wool and cotton.
6. **Wear resistance (abrasion):** Between that of wool and cotton — better than cotton, slightly less than wool.

---

### 2.11 Drape and Handle

1. Silk has **exceptional drape** — the ability to fall gracefully and flow according to gravity and body movement.
2. This arises from silk's low density, fine diameter, and smooth surface.
3. Silk has a characteristic **soft, smooth, cool handle** (feel) that is considered the gold standard in luxury textiles.
4. The **soft, pliable** nature of degummed silk is used to describe the "hand" of luxury fabrics.
5. Silk blends well with other plant and animal fibres.

---

### 2.12 Acoustic Properties (Scroop)

1. **Scroop** is the distinctive rustling or crunching sound produced by crisp silk fabrics when rubbed or moved.
2. Scroop is **not a natural property** of the silk fibre — it is an imparted property from processing (typically acid treatment).
3. Produced by acids that slightly degrade sericin or fibroin surface, creating micro-roughness.
4. Scroop does **not indicate quality** — it is a finishing effect.
5. The sound is produced by friction between fibres with micro-rough surfaces.

> **Example:** Luxury silk taffeta is known for its distinctive "swishing" scroop sound when a silk taffeta ballgown moves. This sound is actually the result of acid finishing treatment — not inherent to the silk molecule — yet it has become synonymous with luxury and high-fashion silk in popular perception.

## 3. Chemical Properties

### 3.1 Amphoteric Nature

1. Silk (like all proteins) is **amphoteric** — it has both **acidic (–COOH)** and **basic (–NH₂)** functional groups.
2. In acidic solution, silk becomes **cationic** (positively charged, –NH₃⁺ dominant).
3. In alkaline solution, silk becomes **anionic** (negatively charged, –COO⁻ dominant).
4. The **isoelectric point** (pI) of silk fibroin is approximately **pH 3.8–4.3** — at this pH, silk has zero net charge.
5. The amphoteric nature allows silk to bind both **acid dyes** (in acidic conditions) and **direct/reactive dyes** (in neutral/alkaline conditions).

---

### 3.2 Action of Acids

1. **Dilute acids (cold):** Mild effect — slight swelling, minor surface hydrolysis. Silk retains most of its strength.
2. **Dilute acids (hot):** Gradual hydrolysis of peptide bonds → silk weakens and eventually dissolves.
3. **Concentrated acids:**
   - **H₂SO₄ (concentrated):** Dissolves silk rapidly by hydrolysing peptide bonds.
   - **HCl:** Silk swells in concentrated cold HCl but dissolves in hot concentrated HCl.
4. **Mild acids** are used in silk processing — particularly for scroop development and certain dye fixation processes.
5. Silk is **more sensitive to acids** than wool — acids hydrolyse peptide bonds progressively.
6. **Key test:** Silk dissolves in concentrated H₂SO₄; wool swells (due to more crosslinks).

> **Example:** The deliberate use of dilute acetic acid (white vinegar) to rinse silk garments is a traditional care method — the mild acid helps maintain the slight natural acidity of silk, brighten colours, and increase lustre. The pH of silk naturally (~pH 4–5) is matched by this mildly acidic rinse.

---

### 3.3 Action of Alkalis

1. Alkalis (bases) are **severely damaging to silk** — more so than to wool.
2. **Dilute alkalis (cold):** Minor hydrolysis; may cause yellowing.
3. **Dilute alkalis (hot):** Significant hydrolysis and degradation.
4. **Concentrated NaOH:** Rapidly dissolves silk completely.
5. Silk is **not harmed by mild alkaline solutions** in brief contact — domestic detergents (pH ~8) cause slow damage.
6. Degumming itself uses mild alkali (sodium carbonate, 0.02%) — carefully controlled to remove sericin without attacking fibroin.
7. **Never use bleach or strong detergents** on silk — they will irreversibly damage the fibre.

> **Example:** Dry cleaning solvents (perchloroethylene, hydrocarbon solvents) do **not** damage silk — this is why silk garments are recommended for dry cleaning, not hot-water washing with strong detergents. The neutral, non-aqueous nature of dry cleaning solvents is compatible with silk's chemistry.

---

### 3.4 Action of Oxidising Agents

1. Oxidising agents (bleaches, H₂O₂, NaOCl) **damage silk** by oxidising amino acid side chains.
2. **Chlorine bleach (hypochlorite):** Particularly destructive — attacks tyrosine, tryptophan, and methionine residues; yellows and weakens silk irreversibly.
3. **Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂):** Less destructive than chlorine; used at low concentration for mild bleaching of raw silk (controlled conditions).
4. Silk loses strength and becomes brittle upon excessive oxidation.
5. **Perspiration** (which is slightly acidic and contains metabolic oxidants) causes yellowing over time — hence the common yellowing of silk underarms.
6. Antioxidants and UV-protective finishing agents are applied to extend silk lifespan.

---

### 3.5 Action of Reducing Agents

1. Reducing agents (Na₂SO₄, thiourea) can break the **disulfide bond** linking the H-chain and L-chain in fibroin.
2. This causes dissolution of the quaternary structure.
3. **Mercaptoethanol** (a strong reducing agent) is used in laboratory silk dissolution protocols.
4. Unlike wool (which is much more affected by reducing agents due to many disulfide bonds), silk has only one disulfide bond per fibroin complex — so reduction alone is insufficient to completely dissolve silk.
5. Full dissolution of silk fibroin requires a **combination** of reduction + chaotropic salts (e.g., 8M LiBr + mercaptoethanol).

---

### 3.6 Action of Enzymes

1. Silk is **biodegradable** — it is attacked by proteolytic enzymes (proteinases) which hydrolyse peptide bonds.
2. **Protease (trypsin, papain, subtilisin):** Degrade fibroin over time; used industrially for gentle degumming.
3. **Serrapeptase and other silk-specific proteases:** Used in biomedical applications to control the degradation rate of silk scaffolds in the body.
4. In textile use, **protease treatments** can be used to soften silk surface and reduce pilling.
5. Silk biodegrades in soil within months to a few years — unlike synthetic fibres which persist for centuries.

> **Example:** Silk sutures placed in the body are slowly degraded by the body's own protease enzymes over 6–12 months — a controlled, predictable degradation that has been exploited in timed drug delivery systems, where silk scaffolds loaded with drugs release them as they degrade in vivo.

---

### 3.7 Action of Sunlight (Photodegradation)

1. Silk **weakens upon prolonged exposure to sunlight** (UV radiation).
2. UV light causes **photooxidation** of aromatic amino acids (tyrosine, phenylalanine) → free radical chain reactions → chain scission.
3. Visible signs: yellowing, embrittlement, loss of lustre.
4. Silk degrades **faster in sunlight** than wool or cotton.
5. **Historical silk textiles** (museum pieces) often show severe photodegradation — a major conservation challenge.
6. **UV-protective finishing** (UV absorbers like benzophenones) is applied to silk garments for outdoor use.
7. Silk used as **window drapery** should be lined to prevent UV exposure.

> **Example:** The reason antique silk kimonos in Japanese museum collections are so carefully stored in dark, controlled environments is that even a few hours of direct sunlight can cause irreversible UV damage. Museums use UV-filtering glass and fibre optic lighting to illuminate silk textiles without causing degradation.

---

### 3.8 Action of Organic Solvents

1. Most common **dry cleaning solvents** (perchloroethylene, petroleum hydrocarbon, HFE solvents): **No damage** to silk.
2. **Acetone:** Mild effect; may affect some dyes but generally safe for short exposures.
3. **Alcohol (ethanol, methanol):**
   - Methanol induces **Silk I → Silk II conversion** in regenerated fibroin solutions.
   - May cause slight shrinkage or stiffening of silk fabrics.
4. **Formic acid:** Dissolves silk (used in laboratory analysis).
5. **Lithium bromide (LiBr) aqueous:** Dissolves fibroin (industrial/laboratory).
6. **HFIP (hexafluoroisopropanol):** Dissolves fibroin completely — used for biomedical film casting.
7. Silk is resistant to **petroleum-based solvents** — hence safe for dry cleaning.

---

### 3.9 Action of Salts (Weighting)

1. **Silk weighting** is the treatment of degummed silk with **metallic salts** (historically tin chloride/phosphate) to:
   - Restore weight lost during degumming (20–30% weight loss).
   - Improve drape, density, and lustre.
   - Enhance handle characteristics.
2. Weighting can increase silk weight by **10–100%** of the original fibre weight.
3. **Over-weighting weakens silk severely** — it disrupts the β-sheet crystallites and intermolecular packing.
4. X-ray diffraction reveals that tin-phosphate weighting disrupts the amorphous regions of fibroin.
5. **Modern weighted silk** often uses **organic weighting agents** (gum, starch, tannins) that are less damaging.
6. Heavily weighted historical silks (antique garments, museum pieces) often show severe degradation — the tin salt causes acid hydrolysis over time.

> **Example:** Many 19th-century silk museum garments are literally disintegrating due to historical tin-weighting practices — the tin compounds act as catalysts for hydrolysis, slowly breaking down the fibroin chains over decades. This is a major focus of textile conservation science.

---

### 3.10 Dyeability

1. Silk has **excellent dyeability** — one of the best of all natural fibres.
2. It accepts a wide range of dye classes: **acid dyes, direct dyes, reactive dyes, basic dyes, natural dyes, metallic mordant dyes**.
3. The best results are obtained with **acid dyes** at pH 4–5 (slightly acidic) — where NH₃⁺ groups on silk attract anionic dye molecules.
4. Silk shows **brilliant, deep, vibrant colours** — saturation levels unachievable on cotton or wool.
5. Silk requires **mild dyeing conditions** (lower temperature, neutral-to-acidic pH) to avoid damage.
6. The wide range of **polar groups** (–NH₂, –COOH, –OH from serine) provides numerous dye-bonding sites.
7. **Natural dyes** (turmeric, indigo, madder, saffron) have been used on silk for millennia with excellent results.

> **Example:** The famous **Hermès silk scarves** achieve their iconic vibrant colours through a multi-step screen-printing process using acid dyes on pre-degummed silk twill. Each scarf may involve 30+ separate colour screenings. The intensity and clarity of colour achievable on silk cannot be replicated on any other fabric with the same vibrancy.

## 4. Biological Properties

1. **Biodegradability:** Silk is fully biodegradable — degrades in natural environments within months to years by microbial protease action.
2. **Biocompatibility:** Degummed silk fibroin is **non-toxic, non-allergenic, and biocompatible** — accepted by the human body without immune rejection (widely used in medical sutures).
3. **Antimicrobial properties:** Silk naturally inhibits bacterial and fungal growth — relevant for medical applications and clothing.
4. **Hypoallergenic:** Degummed silk (fibroin only) is the most **hypoallergenic** natural fibre known.
   - **Note:** Sericin can trigger allergic reactions in some individuals — hence the importance of complete degumming for medical-grade silk.
5. **Resistance to mould and mildew:** Silk is rarely attacked by mildew under normal storage conditions.
6. **Insect resistance:** Silk is resistant to moths under clean conditions but may be attacked if stored dirty (perspiration/oils attract insects).

> **Example:** Silk sutures are still used in ophthalmic (eye) surgery because the human eye is extremely sensitive — the biocompatibility of degummed silk fibroin is so high that it causes virtually no inflammatory response in the delicate tissue around the eye, unlike some synthetic materials.
