## 1. Introduction — Animal Protein Fibres

Wool, silk, and hair fibres are all classified as **natural animal protein fibres**. They are composed of **polypeptide chains** and share the common chemical elements: Carbon (C), Hydrogen (H), Nitrogen (N), and Oxygen (O). However, they differ dramatically in their source, structure, molecular organisation, and resulting properties.

Classification:
1. **Silk** — Produced by the **silkworm** (*Bombyx mori* and other species) as a secreted **continuous filament**.
2. **Wool** — Produced by **sheep** as a **staple (short) fibre** growing from follicles in the skin.
3. **Hair fibres** — Produced by various mammals (**goat, rabbit, camel, alpaca, vicuña, etc.**) as staple fibres from follicles; includes cashmere, mohair, angora, and human hair.

The fundamental distinction:
- Silk: **extruded filament protein (fibroin)** — insect gland secretion.
- Wool & Hair: **hair (keratin)** — mammalian skin follicle growth product.

> **Example:** While silk, wool, and cashmere are all used to make luxury scarves, the sensation of each is distinct — silk is smooth and cool, wool is slightly rough and warm, cashmere is supremely soft but more delicate. These differences trace back to differences in molecular protein structure, fibre shape, and surface morphology.

## 3. Protein Chemistry

### Silk: Fibroin
1. Fibroin is a **fibrous protein** — extended, oriented polypeptide chains.
2. Chemical elements: **C, H, N, O** — **no sulfur**.
3. Molecular weight of H-chain: ~390 kDa.
4. Composed of 16 amino acids (simpler subset than keratin).
5. Three primary amino acids: **Glycine (43%), Alanine (30%), Serine (12%)**.
6. **No disulfide bonds** in the main chain (one only at H-L chain junction).
7. Empirical formula: approximately **(C₁₅H₂₃N₅O₆)ₙ**.

### Wool and Hair: Keratin
1. Keratin is an **α-helical fibrous protein** (intermediate filament protein).
2. Chemical elements: **C, H, N, O, and S** — notably contains **sulfur** (cysteine amino acid).
3. Molecular weight: ~45–68 kDa per keratin chain.
4. Contains **20 amino acids** (more complex than silk fibroin).
5. Contains significant amounts of **cysteine** — which forms **disulfide bonds (–S–S–)** between chains.
6. Sulfur content: ~3–4% by weight in wool keratin.

> **Example:** The "burning hair" smell when wool or silk burns comes from the same source — protein combustion releasing nitrogen compounds. However, wool burns with a stronger sulfur smell (from disulfide bonds in keratin). A trained textile technician can distinguish silk and wool by burning a small sample and carefully noting whether a distinct sulfur smell is present (wool) or not (silk).

## 4. Molecular Structure

### Silk Fibroin — β-sheet Structure

1. The dominant secondary structure in fibroin is the **antiparallel β-pleated sheet** (Silk II).
2. Polypeptide chains are **fully extended** in a flat, planar zig-zag conformation.
3. Chains are oriented **parallel to the fibre axis**.
4. Adjacent chains are held together by **inter-chain hydrogen bonds** between C=O and N-H groups.
5. β-sheets stack on each other → crystallite.
6. Small side chains (Gly: –H on one side; Ala: –CH₃ on the other) allow extremely **tight packing**.
7. Result: **High crystallinity (~60–65%)**, high orientation, high strength, low extensibility.

```
SILK (β-sheet):
→ Gly–Ala–Gly–Ala–Gly–Ala →   (flat, extended chain)
      |    |    |    |
← Ala–Gly–Ala–Gly–Ala–Gly ←   (antiparallel)
Interlocked hydrogen bonds (─ H···O ─)
Stacked sheets (van der Waals between layers)
```

### Wool/Hair Keratin — α-helix Structure

1. The dominant secondary structure in keratin is the **α-helix** (right-handed coil).
2. The polypeptide chain **coils** around itself — hydrogen bonds between amino acids 4 positions apart hold the helix.
3. **Multiple α-helices** coil around each other to form a **coiled-coil** (protofilament).
4. Protofilaments → microfibrils → macrofibrils → cortical cells → wool fibre.
5. **Disulfide bonds (–S–S–)** between cysteine residues on adjacent helices provide **crosslinks** — giving wool its elastic springback.
6. The coiled structure is more **extensible** (20–50% elongation) and **springy** than silk's extended structure.

```
WOOL/HAIR (α-helix):
      α-helix
  ╔═══╗
 ═╝   ╚═══╗
          ╚═══╗   ← coiled polypeptide chain
              ╚═══╗
                  ...
H-bonds within the helix (every 4th amino acid)
–S–S– bonds BETWEEN separate helix chains (crosslinks)
```

### Key Structural Contrast
| Property | Silk (β-sheet) | Wool/Hair (α-helix) |
|---------|----------------|---------------------|
| Secondary structure | Antiparallel β-pleated sheet | α-helix |
| Chain conformation | Extended, planar | Coiled |
| Main stabilising bonds | H-bonds between chains | H-bonds within helix; disulfide between chains |
| Extensibility | Low (~20%) | High (~30–50%) |
| Elasticity | Moderate | High |
| Effect of water on structure | Some H-bond disruption; slight weakening | Swells; H-bonds reform on drying (good recovery) |

> **Example:** When you stretch wool and release it, it springs back — the disulfide crosslinks act like rubber bands holding the coiled α-helices in place. When you stretch silk and release it, it remains slightly stretched — there are no disulfide crosslinks to pull it back, only the weaker stack-to-stack van der Waals forces between β-sheets.

## 5. Amino Acid Composition

### Silk Fibroin (B. mori)
| Amino Acid | % in Fibroin | Key Note |
|------------|-------------|---------|
| Glycine | 43–45% | Smallest AA; enables tight β-sheet packing |
| Alanine | 29–30% | Small side chain; tight packing |
| Serine | ~12% | –OH group; moisture absorption |
| Tyrosine | ~5% | Aromatic; some UV absorption |
| Others | ~9% | Amorphous regions |
| **Cysteine/Sulfur** | **0% (absent)** | **No disulfide bonds in main chain** |

Silk polymer contains **16 amino acids** (simpler composition).

### Wool Keratin
| Amino Acid | % in Wool | Key Note |
|------------|----------|---------|
| Glycine | ~8% | Less dominant than in silk |
| Alanine | ~5% | Less dominant than in silk |
| Serine | ~9% | Hydroxyl-containing |
| Glutamic acid | ~13% | Major — acidic side chain |
| Leucine | ~8% | Bulky, hydrophobic |
| **Cysteine** | **~11%** | **Forms –S–S– crosslinks → disulfide bonds** |
| Others | ~47% | 20 total amino acids |

Wool polymer contains **20 amino acids** (more complex composition, including sulfur-containing amino acids).

> **Example:** The most functionally important difference in amino acid composition is the presence of **cysteine** in wool (11%) and its **complete absence** in silk. Cysteine forms disulfide bonds that crosslink adjacent polypeptide chains in wool — giving it elasticity, springback, and resistance to alkalis. Silk, without cysteine, lacks this crosslinking — it is stronger in a straight line but less elastic.

## 6. Bond Types

| Bond Type | Silk | Wool | Hair |
|-----------|------|------|------|
| **Peptide bonds** | Yes (main backbone) | Yes (main backbone) | Yes (main backbone) |
| **Disulfide bonds (–S–S–)** | **One per fibroin complex (at H-L junction only)** | **Many (throughout)** | Many (throughout) |
| **Hydrogen bonds** | **Inter-chain** (between β-sheets) | **Intra-chain** (within α-helix); inter-chain | Intra-chain (within helix) |
| **Van der Waals** | Between stacked β-sheet layers | Between coiled-coil bundles | Between bundles |
| **Ionic bonds** | In amorphous regions | Present | Present |
| **Hydrophobic interactions** | Between H-chain hydrophobic domains | Between hydrophobic core of helices | Between hydrophobic core |

Key functional implications:
1. **More disulfide bonds (wool)** → higher elasticity, more crosslinks → better shape retention, but also harder to dissolve.
2. **Fewer disulfide bonds (silk)** → less elasticity, no springback → silks wrinkle and stay wrinkled unless steamed.
3. **Hydrogen bonds between chains (silk)** → lateral strength in β-sheet plane.
4. **Hydrogen bonds within chains (wool)** → maintain helix shape; broken by moisture → wool curls/crimps.

> **Example:** A **permanent wave** in human hair exploits disulfide bonds in keratin — the reducing agent breaks the –S–S– bonds, the hair is shaped (curled), then the oxidising agent reforms the bonds in the new curved position. This process works on silk **would not** create a permanent wave because silk has insufficient disulfide bonds to be reformed in a new position. This illustrates a critical practical consequence of the bond difference.

## 7. Microscopic Appearance

### Longitudinal View

| Fibre | Microscopic Feature |
|-------|-------------------|
| **Silk (mulberry, degummed)** | Smooth, structureless, transparent rod — no surface features |
| **Silk (undegummed/raw)** | Rough, cracked sericin surface; paired filaments visible |
| **Silk (tasar, muga)** | Fine longitudinal striations on surface |
| **Wool** | **Overlapping scales (cuticle)** clearly visible; crimp (waviness) present |
| **Cashmere** | Faint scales; smoother than wool; fine crimp |
| **Mohair** | Very few, flat scales; highly lustrous; near-smooth |
| **Human hair** | Cuticle scales visible; medulla (core) sometimes visible; larger diameter |
| **Camel hair** | Scales visible; more irregular than wool |

### Cross-Sectional View

| Fibre | Cross-section |
|-------|--------------|
| **Silk (mulberry)** | **Triangular with rounded corners** |
| **Silk (tasar)** | Elongated rectangular / wedge |
| **Silk (muga)** | Elongated triangular |
| **Wool** | **Circular or near-circular** with cortex and medulla visible |
| **Cashmere** | Circular (small diameter, ~14–18 μm) |
| **Mohair** | Circular (larger, ~25–40 μm) |
| **Human hair** | Circular (large, ~70 μm) with distinct medulla |
| **Camel hair** | Circular with medulla |

### Key Microscopic Distinction:
- **Scales present = Wool or Hair** (keratin-based fibre)
- **No scales + Triangular cross-section = Silk** (fibroin-based fibre)

> **Example:** Microscopic examination is the standard first step in fibre fraud detection — determining if a fabric sold as "100% silk" actually contains wool or synthetic fibre. Scales = wool; no scales + triangular = silk; no scales + circular = synthetic. This simple visual key resolves the most common cases quickly and inexpensively.

## 9. Chemical Properties — Comparison

| Property | Silk | Wool | Hair |
|----------|------|------|------|
| **Main protein** | Fibroin (+ sericin) | Keratin | Keratin |
| **Sulfur content** | **0%** (trace only) | ~3–4% | ~3–5% |
| **Disulfide bonds** | One (at H-L junction) | Many throughout | Many throughout |
| **Acid resistance** | Poor (peptide bond hydrolysis) | Moderate | Moderate |
| **Alkali resistance** | Poor | Poor (disulfides cleave) | Poor |
| **Chlorine resistance** | Poor (yellowing/weakening) | Very poor (machine wash shrinks wool) | Poor |
| **Enzyme sensitivity** | Protease: yes (biodegradable) | Protease: yes | Protease: yes |
| **Solubility in H₂SO₄** | Dissolves | Dissolves (slower) | Dissolves |
| **Solubility in NaOH** | Dissolves | Dissolves | Dissolves |
| **Solubility in HCl (cold conc.)** | **Dissolves** | **Swells** (does not dissolve) | Swells |
| **Isoelectric point** | ~pH 3.8–4.3 | ~pH 4.5–5.0 | ~pH 5.5 |
| **Burns** | Self-extinguishing, black powdery ash | Self-extinguishing, black powdery ash | Self-extinguishing |
| **Burn smell** | Burning hair/feathers | Burning hair (with sulfur note) | Burning hair |
| **Biuret test** | Positive (purple) | Positive (purple) | Positive (purple) |
| **Sulfur test** | **Negative** | **Positive** | Positive |

> **Example — Chemical Test for Silk vs Wool:**
> 1. Place both fibres in **concentrated cold hydrochloric acid (HCl)** for 5 minutes.
> 2. **Silk dissolves** completely.
> 3. **Wool swells** but remains largely intact.
> This single test result definitively identifies silk vs wool — the greater number of disulfide crosslinks in wool physically prevents dissolution in cold concentrated HCl, while silk's fewer crosslinks (and different structure) allows dissolution.

## 10. Morphological Structure — Detailed Comparison

### Silk Fibre Internal Organisation

```
Silk Fibre Cross-Section:
  Outer: Sericin (amorphous, sticky, removed by degumming)
  Inner: Fibroin (crystalline β-sheets)
  Organisation: Nanofibrils → Fibrils → Single Filament (Brin)
  Crystal: Antiparallel β-pleated sheets (Silk II)
  Crystallinity: ~60–65%
```

### Wool Fibre Internal Organisation

```
Wool Fibre Cross-Section:
  Outer: Cuticle (overlapping scales → imbricate structure)
  Middle: Cortex (main body = two types: ortho-cortex + para-cortex)
          [Cortex contains: macrofibrils → microfibrils → α-helices]
  Inner: Medulla (air-filled porous core, present in coarser wool)
  Crystal: α-helix → coiled coil (low crystallinity)
  Crystallinity: ~25–35%
```

### Hair Fibre Internal Organisation (Human Hair / Cashmere)

```
Hair Fibre Cross-Section:
  Outer: Cuticle (overlapping scales, fewer in cashmere)
  Middle: Cortex (macrofibrils, bilateral for crimp)
  Inner: Medulla (varies: absent in fine cashmere/human hair; present in coarser hair)
  Crystal: α-helix (same as wool keratin)
  Crystallinity: ~25–35%
```

### Three-Layer Comparison:

| Layer | Silk | Wool | Cashmere / Human Hair |
|-------|------|------|----------------------|
| **Outer** | Sericin (sticky, removable) | Cuticle scales (pointed toward tip) | Cuticle scales (fewer/flatter) |
| **Middle** | Fibroin fibrils (β-sheets) | Cortex (α-helix macrofibrils) | Cortex (α-helix) |
| **Inner** | None (solid) | Medulla (air core, in coarser wool) | Medulla (minimal/absent in fine hair) |

> **Example:** Wool's bilateral cortex (ortho-cortex on one side; para-cortex on the other) is the origin of **natural crimp** — the two halves absorb moisture differently, causing the fibre to curl. This is why freshly washed wool fabric shrinks and the fibres felt together. Silk has no such bilateral structure — and no natural crimp — which is why silk fabrics retain their shape and drape even after washing (with appropriate care).

## 11. Thermal Behaviour

| Property | Silk | Wool | Cashmere | Human Hair |
|----------|------|------|---------|-----------|
| **Glass transition (Tg)** | ~175°C | ~132°C | ~130°C | ~120°C |
| **Decomposition onset** | ~275°C | ~200°C | ~200°C | ~200°C |
| **Melting point** | Does not melt | Does not melt | Does not melt | Does not melt |
| **Flame behaviour** | Burns slowly, self-extinguishes | Burns slowly, self-extinguishes | Burns slowly, self-extinguishes | Burns slowly, self-extinguishes |
| **Ironing temperature** | 140–150°C (cool-warm iron) | 120–140°C (cool iron, damp cloth) | 100–120°C (cool iron) | Not ironed |
| **Thermal insulation** | Moderate | Excellent | Excellent | Moderate |
| **Thermoplastic** | No | No | No | No |

Key observations:
1. **Silk is more thermally stable** than wool/hair — its glass transition and decomposition temperatures are significantly higher.
2. All protein fibres are **non-thermoplastic** — they char and decompose rather than melting.
3. Wool's scaly structure traps more air than silk → better thermal insulation in wool.

## 12. Mechanical Properties

| Property | Silk | Wool | Cashmere | Human Hair |
|----------|------|------|---------|-----------|
| **Tenacity (g/d)** | 2.4–5.1 | 1.0–1.7 | ~1.0–1.5 | ~1.8 |
| **Wet tenacity (% of dry)** | 75–85% | 85–100% (wool is stronger wet) | ~75–85% | ~80–85% |
| **Elongation at break (%)** | 10–25 | 25–35 | 20–30 | 20–40 |
| **Elastic recovery (2% ext.)** | ~100% | ~100% | ~100% | Good |
| **Elastic recovery (5% ext.)** | ~52% | ~95–99% | ~85–95% | Moderate |
| **Stiffness (flexural rigidity)** | High | Moderate | Lower | High |
| **Abrasion resistance** | Between wool and cotton | Good | Good | High |

Key observations:
1. **Silk is stronger** than wool and cashmere in tensile strength (tenacity).
2. **Wool is more elastic and resilient** — can be stretched repeatedly and returns to original shape (due to disulfide crosslinks and coiled α-helix spring mechanism).
3. **Silk loses strength when wet** (15–20% loss); interestingly, **wool can be slightly stronger when wet** (water lubricates chain slipping, distributing stress more evenly).
4. Silk's high stiffness (flexural rigidity) is why silk fabric holds its shape in tailored garments.

> **Example:** A simple demonstration of the wool vs silk elasticity difference: pull a knitted wool fabric and a silk chiffon garment to 10% of their original length and release. The wool immediately springs back to shape; the silk remains slightly stretched. This is the disulfide crosslink difference made tangible.

## 13. Identification and Testing

### Microscopic Tests
| Test | Silk | Wool | Hair |
|------|------|------|------|
| **Longitudinal (400×)** | Smooth, structureless rod | Overlapping scales clearly visible | Scales (fewer than wool) |
| **Cross-section (500×)** | **Triangular (rounded corners)** | **Circular** | Circular |
| **Crimp visible** | No | Yes | Variable |

### Chemical Tests
| Test | Silk | Wool | Hair |
|------|------|------|------|
| **Concentrated cold HCl** | Dissolves | Swells (does not dissolve) | Swells |
| **Concentrated NaOH** | Dissolves | Dissolves | Dissolves |
| **Lead acetate test (sulfur)** | **Negative** | **Positive** | Positive |
| **Biuret test (protein)** | Positive (purple) | Positive (purple) | Positive (purple) |
| **Millon's test** | Positive (phenolic AA = Tyr) | Positive | Positive |

### Burn Tests
| Property | Silk | Wool | Cashmere/Hair |
|----------|------|------|--------------|
| Burn smell | Burning hair/protein | Burning hair (stronger sulfur) | Burning hair |
| Ash | Black, powdery, crushable | Black, powdery | Black, powdery |
| Self-extinguishes | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Residue | Non-beaded char | Non-beaded char | Non-beaded char |
| Distinction | Milder/cleaner smell | Distinct sulfur note | Similar to wool |

### Physical Tests
1. **Smooth/Cool feel:** Silk vs wool — silk feels cooler and smoother; wool feels slightly scratchy.
2. **Crimp:** Wool and hair have natural crimp/waviness; silk has none.
3. **Lustre:** Silk has brilliant sheen; wool has a dull, waxy sheen.
4. **Static test:** All three can develop static; silk is more prone in low humidity.
