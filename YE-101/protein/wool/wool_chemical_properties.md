## 1. Chemical Properties of Wool

### i. Effect of Bleach

- If bleach is used, the **di-sulphide (S–S) bonds will be broken**, causing damage to the colour of the fibre
- **Chlorine (Cl⁻) containing bleach** should NOT be used on wool fibres — it damages wool fibre
- Should be avoided
- For bleaching, **hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂)** is commonly used as the oxidizing agent (gentle bleaching)

**Mechanism:**
```
Oxidising agents → attack cystine linkage (S–S bond) preferentially
H₂O₂ (gentle) → safe for wool bleaching
Chlorine bleach → aggressive oxidation → destroys fibre
```

**Reducing agents for bleaching:**
- Wool is **most strongly attacked** by reducing agents in alkaline solution
- They attack preferentially on the cystine linkage

---

### ii. Effect of Alkalis

- Wool **dissolves readily in alkaline solutions** such as **5% solution of NaOH**
- The chemical nature of wool keratin is **particularly sensitive to alkaline substances**
- Strong alkalis (NaOH, KOH) → **dissolve wool completely**
- Mild alkalis → minimum effect:
  - Ammonium carbonate (NH₄)₂CO₃
  - Borax (Na₂B₄O₇)
  - Sodium phosphate (Na₃PO₄)
- **Wool is MORE sensitive to alkalis than acids** (opposite to cotton)

**Reaction (simplified):**
```
Wool protein + NaOH (strong, hot) → Hydrolysis of peptide bonds → Wool dissolves

Wool (keratin) + dilute NaOH → Swelling, weakening (partial hydrolysis)
Wool (keratin) + conc. NaOH → Complete dissolution
```

**pH tolerance:** Wool begins to show significant damage at pH > 9.5

---

### iii. Effect of Acids

- Wool is **more resistant to acids than alkalis**
- **Hot concentrated H₂SO₄** → wool is attacked and decomposes completely
- Wool is generally **resistant to other mineral acids** of all strengths, even at high temperatures
- **Exception: HNO₃ (nitric acid)** → tends to cause damage by oxidation (xanthoproteic reaction → yellow colour)
- The polymer system of wool is weakened in acidic solution, but the fibre **doesn't dissolve** (in dilute acids)
- This is why **carbonizing (H₂SO₄)** works: it destroys cellulose but leaves wool intact at controlled concentrations

**Chemical Test for Wool (Acid):**
```
Wool slowly dissolves in 70% HNO₃
Wool dissolves in concentrated NaOH
Wool swells but does not dissolve in dilute acids
```

---

### iv. Effect of Sunlight

- The keratin of wool **decomposes under the action of sunlight** (photodegradation)
- This process begins **before the wool is removed from the sheep**
- The sulphur in wool is converted into **sulphuric acid**
- The fibre becomes **discoloured** and develops a **harsh feel**
- Wool **loses strength** when exposed to sunlight for long periods
- Dyeing properties are also affected

**Mechanism:**
```
UV light + Wool keratin + O₂ → Photo-oxidation of cystine (S–S) bonds
→ Formation of sulphonic acid groups
→ Chain scission (breaking of polymer backbone)
→ Loss of strength + discolouration
```

---

### v. Colour Fastness / Dyeability

- Wool is **very easy to dye**
- **Acid dyes**, basic dyes, etc. are used to dye wool fibre
- Good colour fastness when properly dyed

**Dye types for wool:**
- Acid dyes (most common)
- Mordant dyes
- Chrome dyes (mordant with potassium dichromate)
- Pre-metallised dyes
- Reactive dyes

---

### vi. Effect of Organic Solvents

- Wool has a **good resistance to dry-cleaning** and other common organic solvents
- Safe for use with most dry-cleaning solvents (perchloroethylene, etc.)

---

### vii. Effect of Insects and Microorganisms

**Insects:**
- Wool is attacked by **moth-grubs** and other insects
- Moths lay eggs in wool; larvae feed on keratin
- Anti-moth treatment is commonly applied

**Microorganisms:**
- Wool has **poor resistance to mildews and bacteria**
- Not advisable to leave wool in a **damp condition** for too long → mould growth
- Antibacterial finishing is used for some wool products

## 2. Thermal Properties of Wool

| Property | Value/Behaviour |
|---|---|
| Heat conductivity | **Poor** (good insulator) |
| Heat resistance | **Low** |
| Burn temperature | 200–300°C |
| Decomposition | Turns yellow at 130°C |
| Charring | At 300°C |
| Scorching | Progressive from 200°C |
| Self-extinguishing | YES — wool does **NOT** continue to burn when removed from flame |

**Detailed Thermal Behaviour:**

```
Temperature    | Behaviour
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
< 100°C        | Moisture removed; no structural damage
100–130°C      | Beginning of polymer fragmentation
~130°C         | Decomposes; turns yellow
200–300°C      | Burns; polymer fragmentation and surface discolouration
300°C+         | Chars
```

**Burning Smell:** When wool burns/chars, it gives off a **characteristic smell similar to burning feathers** (or burning hair) — this is a key identification test.

**Why wool doesn't continue to burn:**
- High nitrogen and sulphur content in keratin → **forms char barrier** that inhibits combustion
- Wool is inherently **flame-resistant** — a natural property, no chemical treatment needed for basic applications

## 3. Identification of Wool Fibre

(QB 18, 13 from handnotes)

### i. Physical Test (Burning Test)

| Test | Wool Behaviour |
|---|---|
| Flame | Steady but **difficult to keep burning** |
| Smell | **Like burning hair** (strong, pungent) |
| Ash | Forms **crushable, brittle black ash** (char) |
| Self-extinguishing | **YES** — stops burning when removed from flame |

---

### ii. Chemical Test

- Wool fibre **dissolves in concentrated 5% Sodium Hydroxide (NaOH)**
- Wool fibre dissolves in **Sodium Hypochlorite (NaOCl)**
- Wool **slowly dissolves in 70% Nitric Acid (HNO₃)**
- But wool **does NOT dissolve in HOOC-COOH** (oxalic acid)

**Summary Chemical Test:**
```
Dissolves in: NaOH (5%), NaOCl, HNO₃ (70%, slowly)
Does NOT dissolve in: HOOC-COOH (oxalic acid), dilute acids
```

---

### iii. Microscopic Test

- **Cross-section:** Round or **oval** shape
- **Longitudinal view:** **Serrated / scaly surface** (overlapping epithelial cells pointing toward tip)

---

### iv. Distinguishing Wool from Silk

Both are protein fibres — they can be confused. Key distinction:

| Feature | Wool | Silk |
|---|---|---|
| Cystine (S–S bond) | **PRESENT** | ABSENT |
| Burning smell | Like burning hair | Like burning hair |
| Chemical test | Dissolves in NaOH | Dissolves in H₂SO₄, HNO₃, and HOOC-COOH |

**Practical test to distinguish wool from silk:**
```
Add concentrated cold HCl (hydrochloric acid):
  - Silk: DISSOLVES
  - Wool: SWELLS but does not dissolve

OR:
Supply concentrated HCOOH (98–99% formic acid):
  - Silk: Contains no cystine bond → cystine bond will be damaged in HCl
  - Test: Wool contains 'S–S' cystine bond; if HCOOH is applied which 
          doesn't contain cystine bond, that will be damaged → then we 
          can identify which is silk or wool

Alternatively (from handnotes):
Use concentrated cold hydrochloric acid + sodium hydroxide solution:
  - Silk: dissolves
  - Wool: swells
```

## 4. End Uses of Wool

Wool is extensively used in textile applications where **comfort and aesthetics** are important:

### Garments and Apparel:
1. **Oven-coats / Overcoats**
2. **Sweaters** (knitwear)
3. **Jackets**
4. **Blankets**
5. **Hats**
6. **Gloves**
7. **Socks**
8. **Suits, dress materials** (worsted)
9. **Cold weather clothing**
10. **Baby wear** (fine Merino)

### Technical/Industrial Uses:
7. **Carpets** (coarse wool)
8. **Felts** (non-woven — heavily felted wool)
9. **Table cloths and bags**
10. Upholstery

### Specialty Fibres:
- **Alpaca fibres** → hats, mitts, scarves, gloves, jumpers, rugs, toys; luxury textiles
- **Llama fibres** → expensive knitted fabrics, jackets, overcoats, blankets
- **Camel hair** → outerwear and underlinings
- **Cashmere** → luxury applications where soft, warm, fine fibre with beautiful drape is desired
- **Mohair** → outer-wear

> Wool is also often used in **blends** with cellulosic and man-made fibres.
