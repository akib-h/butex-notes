## 1. Definition of Wool

> **Wool** is a **natural, highly crimped, protein (keratin) hair fibre** derived from the fibrous covering (fleece) of sheep and other animals such as camels, goats, alpacas, and rabbits.

**From handnotes:**
- Natural textile fibre ✅
- Animal fibre → Protein → **Keratin** (20 types of α-amino acid) ✅
- Medium staple fibre (5–12 cm) ✅
- Multi-cellular fibre ✅
- Natural waveness / crimped fibre ✅
- Countless cortical cells available ✅

**Key formula insight:**
```
Wool = Protein fibre = Keratin polymer = Polypeptide chain
       made of α-amino acids linked by peptide bonds (-CO-NH-)
```

### Example
A standard Merino fleece has fibres averaging **20 µm** in diameter and **80 mm** in staple length — fine enough for next-to-skin garments.

## 2. Historical Significance

- Wool is possibly the **oldest fibre known to humans**
- It was the **first fibre to be spun into yarn** and woven into fabric
- Archaeological evidence suggests wool was used in **Mesopotamia around 3500 BCE**
- Merino sheep were so prized in medieval Spain that exporting them was punishable by death

## 3. Key Characteristics at a Glance

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Fibre type | Natural, staple, animal |
| Polymer | α-Keratin (protein) |
| Length | 5–12 cm (common); up to 35 cm coarse |
| Diameter | 14 µm (fine) to 45 µm (coarse) |
| Cross-section | Round or oval |
| Surface | Serrated / scaly (epithelial scales) |
| Colour | Off-white to creamy |
| Moisture regain | 16–18% |
| Specific gravity | 1.32 |
| Crystallinity | 25–30% crystalline; 70–75% amorphous |

## 4. Sources of Wool

Wool and wool-type fibres come from multiple animals:

| Animal | Fibre Name | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Sheep (Merino) | Merino Wool | Finest quality, softest |
| Sheep (Lincoln, Cheviot) | Long/Coarse Wool | Used for carpets |
| Angora Goat | Mohair | Long, silky, lustrous |
| Cashmere Goat | Cashmere | Extremely soft, luxury |
| Angora Rabbit | Angora | Very fine, light, fluffy |
| Camel | Camel Hair | Soft, beige, outerwear |
| Alpaca | Alpaca | Silky, durable, water-repellent |
| Llama / Vicuna / Guanaco | Llama / Vicuna | Andes animals, fine & warm |

> **Note:** Among all protein fibres, **wool accounts for ~90%** of global protein fibre consumption.

## 6. Production Statistics

- There are approximately **1.5 billion sheep** in the world
- From **1 sheep**, we get approximately **7 pounds (3.5 kg)** of wool per shearing
- **7%** of all fibres come from animal sources
- Of all animal fibres, **90% are wool fibres**
- The entire protein fibre range accounts for only **~6%** of world fibre consumption

### Mathematical Example: World Wool Output Estimate

```
Estimated wool-bearing sheep ≈ 1.0 billion (out of 1.5 billion total)
Average yield per sheep       ≈ 3.5 kg

Estimated annual production   = 1.0 × 10⁹ sheep × 3.5 kg/sheep
                              = 3.5 × 10⁹ kg
                              = 3.5 million metric tonnes
```
*(Actual global production is ~1–1.5 million metric tonnes of clean wool due to scouring losses of ~40–50%)*

## 7. Wool vs Hair — Key Distinction

Both wool and hair are protein fibres from the fibrous covering of sheep, goat, and camel — but they differ significantly:

| Feature | Wool | Hair |
|---|---|---|
| Length | Short to medium | Long |
| Texture | Fine, soft | Coarse, rough |
| Crimp | Highly crimped (natural waviness) | Straight or slightly wavy |
| Elasticity | Excellent | Less elastic |
| Surface | Scaly (serrated epithelial cells) | Smooth (no/few scales) |
| Stiffness | Less stiff | Stiffer |

> **Key:** The **scaly surface** of wool (overlapping epithelial cells) is the single most distinguishing structural feature — it causes felting and gives wool its unique textile properties.
