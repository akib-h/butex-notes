## 1. Overview of Process Flow

```
SHEEP
  │
  ▼ Shearing
FLEECE
  │
  ▼ Skirting
CLEAN FLEECE
  │
  ▼ Sorting / Grading
SORTED FLEECE
  │
  ▼ Scouring
WASHED FLEECE
  │
  ▼ Oiling
  │
  ▼ Carbonizing
  │
  ▼ Drying
  │
  ▼ Bailing
  │
  ▼ Garnetting (for recycled wool)
  │
  ▼ Carding
  │
  ▼ Spinning
YARN
  │
  ├──── Knitting ────► SWEATER
  └──── Weaving  ────► WOOLLEN FABRIC
```

From handnotes: **Shearing → Sorting → Scouring → Oiling → Carbonizing → Drying → Bailing → Garnetting**

## 2. Step 1: Shearing / Trimming / Clipping / Cutting

**Definition:** Shearing is the process of **cutting the woollen fleece of a sheep** using manual tools like trimmers, large scissors, etc.

**Key points:**
- The person who removes the sheep's wool is called a **shearer**
- The shearer must be careful that the sheep doesn't get hurt
- The fleece should be removed **intact as a single sheet**
- Each adult sheep is shorn **once each year** (usually in spring/early summer, before it gets too hot)
- From 1 sheep, we get up to **3.5 kg or 7 pounds** of wool fibre per year
- Shearing is done during **hot season** so the sheep can re-grow hair by winter

**Tools used:**
- Manual scissors
- Hand blades
- Electric shears

> **Example:** A professional shearer can shear up to 200 sheep per day using electric shears.

---

## 3. Step 2: Skirting

**Definition:** Skirting refers to the process of **removing unwanted parts of the fleece** after shearing.

- Includes stained, matted, or coarse sections such as:
  - Belly wool
  - Leg wool
  - Heavily soiled parts

---

## 4. Step 3: Sorting / Grading

**Definition:** Grading is the process of **sorting wool based on its quality, fibre diameter, length, strength, colour, fineness, and other characteristics**.

- Done by **skilled workers**
- Wool is sorted into:
  - Different grades by **fineness**
  - Different grades by **length**
  - Different grades by **strength**
  - Different grades by **colour**

> **Industrial Grading Parameters:** Fibre diameter (µm), Mean Fibre Diameter (MFD), Coefficient of Variation (CV%), staple length, staple strength (N/ktex).

---

## 5. Step 4: Scouring

**Definition:** Scouring involves **washing in warm soapy water several times** to remove natural grease or gum from the fibre and also remove dirt and dust.

**Raw wool composition (approximate):**

| Component | % |
|---|---|
| Wool keratin | 45 |
| Wool wax (lanolin) | 18 |
| Suint (dried sweat) | 10 |
| Water | 12 |
| Debris (vegetable matter, dust) | 15 |
| **Total non-fibre** | **~55%** |

> Impurities may comprise **up to 60%** of raw wool mass.

**Scouring process:**
- Solution used: **5% mild alkali or soap solution**
- The impurities — fat, oil, gum — **dissolve in the alkali**
- Repeated washings in warm water
- Nowadays done by machine (mechanized scouring bowls)

**Mathematical Calculation — Scouring Loss:**

```
Example:
  Raw wool input     = 100 kg
  Impurities removed = ~45–55 kg (lanolin, suint, dirt, water)
  Clean wool output  = 45–55 kg

  Scouring yield (%) = (Clean weight / Raw weight) × 100
                     = (50 / 100) × 100
                     = 50%

  (Typical scouring yield: 45–65% depending on breed and contamination)
```

---

## 6. Step 5: Oiling

**Definition:** After scouring, the wool fibre becomes **unmanageable (brittle, static-prone)**. So the fibre is treated with various selective oils — animal, vegetable, or mineral.

- Oils used: Mineral oil, olive oil, spinning oil emulsions
- Adds lubrication needed for carding and spinning
- Typically 0.5–2% oil on weight of fibre (owf)

---

## 7. Step 6: Carbonizing

**Definition:** Carbonizing is the **chemical process employed for removal of cellulosic/vegetable matter** from protein wool.

**Why needed?** Raw fleece often contains seeds, burrs, grass fragments (vegetable matter). These are not removed by scouring.

**How it works:**
```
Cellulose (vegetable matter) + H₂SO₄ → Carbon particles (destroyed)
Wool keratin (protein)       + H₂SO₄ → NOT destroyed (resistant)
```

**Step-by-step process:**
```
Scouring
   │
   ▼ Acidizing (soaking/spraying with dilute H₂SO₄ solution ~5–7%)
   │
   ▼ Moisture Removal
   │
   ▼ Drying and Baking (at controlled temperature, usually 65–90°C)
      Heat activates the acid which disintegrates the cellulose
   │
   ▼ Burr Crushing (mechanical rollers crush the now-brittle vegetable matter)
   │
   ▼ Dedusting (dust/carbon particles removed)
   │
   ▼ Neutralizing (washing with Na₂CO₃ or NaHCO₃ to remove residual acid)
   │
   ▼ Re-scouring (final wash)
```

> **Note:** About 12–16% moisture is left after drying to enable handling.

**Chemical reaction:**
```
(C₆H₁₀O₅)n  +  H₂SO₄  →  6nC  +  5nH₂O  (simplified carbonization)
  Cellulose     dilute     Carbon   Water
```

The acid converts the cellulose into easily crushable carbon, while wool protein remains largely unaffected at these concentrations.

---

## 8. Step 7: Drying

**Definition:** After scouring and carbonizing, wool fibre is dried.

- Wool fibre is given in a **woven rack** and heat is given
- Also dried by passing through **continuous flow heated air dryers**
- About **12–16% of the moisture is left** to enable handling

---

## 9. Step 8: Bailing

**Definition:** Bailing is the process of **compressing graded and sorted wool** by mechanical means into dense bales.

- Beneficial for **transportation and storage**
- Standard bale weight: approximately **330 kg = 1 bale**

---

## 10. Step 9: Garnetting

**Definition:** Garnetting is the **picking and shredding process of unused or recycled wool fibre**.

- Recovered (recycled) wool goes through garnetting
- Breaks down old woollen fabric into fibre form
- Used for shoddy and mungo wool production

**Fibre Damage in Mungo vs Shoddy:**

| Type | Cause of Damage | Recovery Difficulty |
|---|---|---|
| Shoddy | From fabric NOT excessively felted | Less damage; easier to reuse |
| Mungo | From milled/felted cloth | Interlocked fibres; hard to disentangle |

> In Mungo: fibres are tightly interlocked due to felting. Recovery difficulty is **proportional to fibre damage** — as fibres become more damaged, recovery becomes nearly impossible.

---

## 11. Step 10: Carding

**Definition:** Before wool can be used for making fabric, it is **disentangled and cleaned**. The intermixed fibres are separated to form continuous fibres.

- Wool fibres are passed through a **series of metal teeth** to straighten the fibres
- Output of carding: a continuous web of fibres called a **sliver**
- Carding produces **carded (woollen) sliver** — for woollen yarn

---

## 12. Step 11: Making Yarn (Spinning)

**Definition:** Carded wool is twisted into a rope called a **sliver**. The sliver is stretched and twisted into a thin yarn.

- Spinning for **woollen yarns** is typically done on a **mule spinning machine**
- After combing (for worsted): combed sliver → drawing → spinning

---

## 13. Complete Process Flow Diagram

```
SHEEP
  │
  ├─ Shearing (remove fleece as one piece)
  │
  ├─ Skirting (remove soiled/coarse edges)
  │
  ├─ Sorting/Grading (by fineness, length, colour)
  │
  ├─ Scouring (warm soapy water, remove lanolin, suint, dirt)
  │
  ├─ Oiling (restore manageability)
  │
  ├─ Carbonizing (H₂SO₄ → destroy vegetable matter)
  │
  ├─ Drying (controlled heat, retain 12–16% moisture)
  │
  ├─ Bailing (330 kg per bale, for transport/storage)
  │
  ├─ Garnetting (for recycled wool only)
  │
  ├─ Carding (straighten, disentangle fibres → sliver)
  │
  ├─ (Combing — for worsted yarn only)
  │
  └─ Spinning → YARN
                  │
                  ├── Knitting  → Sweaters, knitwear
                  └── Weaving   → Woollen fabrics, suits
```

## 14. Woollen vs Worsted Yarn

| Feature | Woollen Yarn | Worsted Yarn |
|---|---|---|
| Fibre length | **Short** staple fibre | **Long** staple fibre |
| Processing | **Only carded** | **Carded AND combed** |
| Twist | **Slightly** twisted | **Higher** twisted |
| Yarn count | **Coarser** yarn | **Finer** yarn |
| Strength | **Weaker** | **Stronger** |
| Feel | Softer | Harder |
| Produced from | Carded wool | Combed wool |

**Count Systems for Wool:**

| System | Definition | Formula |
|---|---|---|
| English Cotton Count (Ne) | No. of hanks of 840 yards per pound | `Ne = L / (840 × w)` |
| Woollen Count | No. of hanks of 256 yards per pound | `Woollen = L / (256 × w)` |
| Worsted Count | No. of hanks of 560 yards per pound | `Worsted = L / (560 × w)` |

Where:
- `L` = length in yards
- `w` = weight in pounds
- `1 kg = 2.2046 lbs`
- `1 yard = 0.9144 m`

### Mathematical Example 1

> **Problem:** If length = 75,000 yards, weight = 2 kg, determine Ne, Woollen count, and Worsted count.

```
Given:
  L = 75,000 yards
  w = 2 kg = 2 × 2.2046 = 4.4092 lbs

Ne = L / (840 × w)
   = 75,000 / (840 × 4.4092)
   = 75,000 / 3,703.73
   = 20.25 ≈ 20 Ne ✅

Woollen count = L / (256 × w)
              = 75,000 / (256 × 4.4092)
              = 75,000 / 1,128.75
              = 66.44 ≈ 66 ✅

Worsted count = L / (560 × w)
              = 75,000 / (560 × 4.4092)
              = 75,000 / 2,469.15
              = 30.37 ≈ 30 ✅
```

### Mathematical Example 2

> **Problem:** A yarn is 2800 m long and weighs 500 g. Find the Woollen count.

```
Convert:
  L = 2800 m = 2800 / 0.9144 yards = 3062.12 yards
  w = 500 g = 500 / 1000 × 2.2046 = 0.23 lbs (× 2.2046 / 1000)
            = 0.5 × 2.2046 / 2 = 0.1102 × 2 = no wait:
  w = 500 g = 500/1000 kg = 0.5 kg = 0.5 × 2.2046 lbs = 1.1023 lbs
  
  Wait: re-read problem, w = 500g
  w = 500/1000 = 0.5 kg = 0.5 × 2.2046 = 1.1023 lbs
  Actually from handnotes: w = 500g = (500/1000) × 2.2046 lb = 1.1023 lb
  But handnotes show: w = 0.23 lbs → meaning 500g / 2204.6 × 1000... 
  
  Actually: w = 500g = 500/453.6 lbs = 1.102 lbs  [1 lb = 453.6g]
  
  Woollen count = L / (256 × w)
                = 3062.12 / (256 × 1.102)
                = 3062.12 / 282.11
                = 10.85 ≈ 11
                
  [Note: handnotes get 52 with w=0.23 lb which suggests w=500g/2204.6g×1lb = slightly different 
   denominator, using: w = 500 × (2.2046/1000) = 1.1023 lb]

  Final Answer:
  Woollen count = 3062.12 / (256 × 1.1023)
                = 3062.12 / 282.19

```

### Mathematical Example 3 (from handnotes)

> **Problem:** For 40 Ne, length is 3500 m. Determine weight in grams.

```
Ne = L / (840 × w)
40 = (3500 / 0.9144 yards) / (840 × w)
40 = 3827.65 / (840 × w)
40 × 840 × w = 3827.65
w = 3827.65 / 33,600
w = 0.1139 lbs
w = 0.1139 × 453.6 g
w = 51.67 g ≈ 50 g ✅
```
