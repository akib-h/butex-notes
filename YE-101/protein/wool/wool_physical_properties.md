## 2. Length and Fineness

- **Length:** 5–12 cm (preferred for most versatile yarn manufacturing)
  - Can range from 5 cm (finest Merino) to 35 cm (coarsest wools)
  - Merino: 60–100 mm | Lincoln: 75–250 mm

- **Fineness (Diameter):**
  - Fine Merino: **17–25 µm** (not very long, but very fine)
  - Lincoln (coarse): around **40 µm** (longer: 75–250 mm)
  - Range: 14 µm → 45 µm

> An important characteristic of wool is its **fineness** — the smaller the diameter, the **softer and more comfortable** the fabric, especially when worn next to the skin.

---

## 3. Crimp

- **Crimp** = natural waviness of wool fibre
- Fine wools: up to **100 crimps per 10 cm**
- Coarse wools: as few as **4 crimps per 10 cm**
- Best Merino: **30 waves per inch**

**Why crimp is important:**

1. Prevents fibres from aligning too closely → **creates air spaces** (≈2/3 of volume)
2. Air spaces → **thermal insulation**
3. Crimp gives **elasticity** to the fibre
4. Creates **air pockets** that act as thermal barriers, retaining body heat in cold conditions

### Mathematical Relationship:

```
As diameter (D) increases → Crimps per unit length (C) decreases

Empirical approximation:
C ∝ 1/D

Fine (14 µm)  → 100 crimps/10cm
Coarse (35 µm) → 4 crimps/10cm

Ratio check:
C_fine / C_coarse = 100 / 4 = 25
D_coarse / D_fine = 35 / 14 = 2.5

(Not perfectly linear, but qualitatively inverse)
```

## 4. Tenacity / Strength

- **Dry tenacity:** 8.8–15 cN/tex (1.0–1.7 g/den)
- **Wet tenacity:** 7–14 cN/tex (0.8–1.6 g/den)
- **From handnotes:** 1–35 g/denier (wide range depending on breed)

> **Wool is a comparatively WEAK fibre** — its tenacity is low compared to cotton, flax, silk.

### Why Wool is Weak (from Physical Perspective):

When wool **absorbs moisture:**
```
Water molecules + H-bonds → break hydrogen bonds
Water molecules + salt linkages → hydrolyze salt linkages (in amorphous regions)
Result: swelling of fibre + loss of tenacity (wet strength < dry strength)
```

**Tenacity Comparison:**

```
Polypropylene filament  ≈ 529 mN/tex   (strongest)
Nylon staple            ≈ 441 mN/tex
Polyester staple        ≈ 353 mN/tex
Cotton                  ≈ 353 mN/tex
Acrylic staple          ≈ 265 mN/tex
Wool                    ≈ 176 mN/tex   (weakest common fibre)
```

### Calculation Example:

> **Problem:** Convert wool tenacity from g/denier to cN/tex.

```
Given: Tenacity = 1.5 g/denier

Conversion:
  1 g/denier = 8.82 cN/tex (standard conversion factor)

  Tenacity = 1.5 × 8.82 = 13.23 cN/tex

OR using:
  1 cN/tex = 0.1133 g/denier
  So: 1.5 g/den × (1/0.1133) = 13.24 cN/tex ✅
```

## 5. Elongation

- **Elongation at break (dry):** 25–35%
- **Elongation at break (wet):** 25–50%
- **From handnotes:** 20–30%

### Stress-Strain Curve: Three Regions

```
Stress (mN/tex)
  │
  │    Hookean  │  Yield Region  │  Post-yield
  │    Region   │  (2% - 30%)    │  (>30%)
  │   (0 - 2%)  │                │
  │  ╱──────────╱                │
  │ ╱                            ╱──────────
  │╱─────────────────────────────
  └────────────────────────────────────────── Strain (%)
  0          2%             25-30%       >30%
```

**Region 1 — Hookean Region (0–2% strain):**
- Reversible deformations
- Bond angles and bond lengths change
- Fully elastic; Hooke's Law applies
- α-Keratin structure remains intact

**Region 2 — Yield Region (2–25/30% strain):**
- **α-helices of micro-fibrils unfold** and are replaced by **β-pleated sheets**
- Progressive uncoiling of the helical polymer
- Largely recoverable if not taken too far

**Region 3 — Post-yield Region (>30% strain):**
- Some **irreversible degradation** occurs
- Permanent structural damage
- Recovery incomplete

## 6. Elasticity and Resiliency

- Wool has **very good elastic recovery** and **excellent resiliency**
- **Elastic recovery:** 65% for 20% extension; almost **100% for short extensions** (<2%)

**Source of elasticity (two factors):**
1. **Crimped configuration** of wool fibre (physical spring effect)
2. **α-Keratin configuration** of the wool polymer (molecular spring effect)

**Inter-polymer forces that allow return to α-Keratin:**
```
Di-sulphide bonds + Salt linkages + Hydrogen bonds
→ collectively pull polymers back to helical (α-keratin) configuration
→ fibre recovers its original length
```

### Mathematical Example:

```
A wool fibre is extended by 20%:
  Original length L₀ = 100 mm
  Extended length   = 120 mm (20% extension)
  Extension         = 20 mm

Elastic recovery = 65% for 20% extension

  Recovery length = 65% of 20 mm = 13 mm
  Remaining permanent set = 20 - 13 = 7 mm

  Final length after recovery = 100 + 7 = 107 mm
  Permanent set % = (7/20) × 100 = 35%
```

## 7. Specific Gravity

- **Specific gravity = 1.32** (same as density in g/cm³ for water = 1 g/cm³ reference)
- Wool is a **light-weight fibre**

**Comparison:**

| Fibre | Specific Gravity |
|---|---|
| Cotton | 1.52–1.54 |
| Jute | 1.5 |
| **Wool** | **1.32** |
| Silk | 1.34 |
| Flax | 1.54 |
| Polyester | 1.38 |
| Nylon | 1.14 |

> Lower specific gravity → **lighter fabric for same volume** → more comfortable for garments.

### Calculation Example:

```
If a wool fabric has volume = 200 cm³
Specific gravity of wool = 1.32

Mass of wool = Volume × Density
             = 200 cm³ × 1.32 g/cm³
             = 264 g

Same volume of cotton (sp. gr. = 1.54):
Mass = 200 × 1.54 = 308 g

Wool is 308 - 264 = 44 g lighter for the same volume!
```

## 8. Moisture Regain and Water Absorption

- **Moisture regain:** **16%** (standard conditions: 65% RH, 20°C)
- **Water absorption:** Up to **16–18% of its weight**
- Wool is the **most hydrophilic of all textile fibres**
- Can absorb up to **30% of its weight in moisture** without feeling wet

**Why wool absorbs so much moisture:**

```
1. Polar peptide groups (C=O, N–H) attract water molecules
2. Salt linkages (ionic groups) attract water molecules
3. High amorphous content (70–75%) allows water to penetrate easily
→ Water enters the amorphous polymer system readily
```

### Moisture Regain Calculation:

```
Moisture regain (MR%) = [(wet weight - dry weight) / dry weight] × 100

Example:
  Dry weight of wool = 50 g
  MR% = 16%

  Wet weight at std. conditions = 50 × (1 + 16/100) = 50 × 1.16 = 58 g
  Moisture absorbed = 58 - 50 = 8 g

Conditioned weight formula:
  Conditioned weight = Bone dry weight × (1 + MR%/100)
```

## 9. Lustre

- Wool fibres have **natural lustre**
- Lustre varies based on the type of wool
- Depends largely on the **nature of the fibre surface** (scale arrangement)
- Finer wools tend to have more lustre
- Coarse wools (like Lincoln) have a **silky lustre** due to large, flat scales

---

## 10. Colour

- Natural colour: **off-white** or **creamy**
- Cause: **Di-sulphide (S=S) bonds** act as chromophores, giving slight yellow tinge
- After bleaching: **white** colour achieved
- Wool is **easy to dye** — accepts acid, reactive, mordant, and pre-metallised dyes

---

## 11. Dimensional Stability

- Wool has **BAD / poor** dimensional stability
- This is due to the tendency of wool to **felt** (irreversible shrinkage)
- Felting is caused by the directional frictional effect of scales

---

## 12. Crystallinity

- Crystallinity: **20–25%** (some sources: 25–30%)
- Amorphousness: **70–75%**
- This high amorphous content makes wool very receptive to:
  - Moisture
  - Dyes
  - Chemical agents

---

## 13. Stress-Strain Behaviour

```
At temperatures > 200°C: tensile properties and durability decrease significantly
Great decrease in tensile properties seen at temperatures > 200°C
```

Effect of moisture on mechanical behaviour:
```
Dry wool → higher tenacity, lower elongation
Wet wool → lower tenacity (~80-90% of dry), higher elongation
           (salt linkages broken by water → polymers slide more easily)
```

## 14. Thermal Insulation — Why Wool Keeps Us Warm

(QB 23, 22, 19, 16 from handnotes)

Wool keeps us warm due to its **unique structure and properties**:

1. **Natural Insulation:** Wool fibres trap air within their crimped structure, creating insulating layers that prevent heat loss

2. **Crimp and Elasticity:** The crimp in wool fibres creates air pockets that act as thermal barriers, retaining body heat in cold conditions

3. **Moisture Absorption (Exothermic Reaction):** Wool absorbs up to **30% of its weight in moisture without feeling wet**. As it absorbs moisture, it **releases heat through an exothermic reaction** — keeping the wearer warm!
   ```
   Wool + H₂O → Heat released (exothermic)
   ΔH < 0 (heat of sorption)
   ```

4. **Breathability:** Wool allows **excess heat and moisture to escape**, maintaining a comfortable temperature (thermoregulation)

5. **Slow Heat Conduction:** Wool **conducts heat slowly**, reducing the loss of body warmth in cold environments
