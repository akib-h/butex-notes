## Q1. Names of Jute Producing Countries [2 Marks]

Jute — often called the **"Golden Fiber"** — is primarily grown in warm, humid climates with monsoon rainfall of 160–200 cm per year. Almost **85% of the world's jute cultivation** is concentrated in the **Ganges Delta**, shared between India and Bangladesh.

### 🌍 Major Jute Producing Countries

| Rank | Country | Annual Production (approx.) | Key Regions |
|------|---------|---------------------------|-------------|
| 1 | 🇮🇳 **India** | ~1,968,000 tonnes | West Bengal, Assam, Bihar, Odisha, Meghalaya |
| 2 | 🇧🇩 **Bangladesh** | ~1,681,939 tonnes | Tangail, Dhaka, Jessore, Jamalpur, Faridpur |
| 3 | 🇺🇿 **Uzbekistan** | ~20,000 tonnes | Central agricultural regions |
| 4 | 🇨🇳 **China** | ~15,700–29,628 tonnes | Hunan, Guangdong, Yunnan provinces |
| 5 | 🇳🇵 **Nepal** | ~14,890 tonnes | Terai plains, Biratnagar |
| 6–10 | Myanmar, Thailand, Egypt, South Sudan, Kenya | Smaller scale | — |

### Key Facts
- **India** is the world's **largest producer** of raw jute, contributing over **50%** of global production.
- **Bangladesh** is the world's **largest exporter** of jute fiber, accounting for over **70%** of global jute exports.
- Bangladesh produces the **highest quality jute fiber** (Jat jute) in the world.
- Together, **India, Bangladesh, and China** account for over **90%** of global jute output.

## Q2. Chemical Composition of Jute Fiber [2 Marks]

Jute is a **lignocellulosic bast fiber** obtained from the plant *Corchorus capsularis* (White jute) and *Corchorus olitorius* (Tossa jute). Its chemical composition is not perfectly uniform — it varies depending on soil conditions, climate, plant maturity, and retting method.

### 🧪 Chemical Composition Table

| Chemical Constituent | Percentage (%) | Function/Role |
|----------------------|----------------|---------------|
| **α-Cellulose** | 58–73% | Main structural component; provides tensile strength and stiffness |
| **Hemicellulose** | 13.6–24% | Branched polysaccharide; contributes to flexibility and elasticity |
| **Lignin** | 12–16% | Complex organic polymer; provides rigidity, UV & fungal resistance; makes jute coarser than flax |
| **Pectin** | 0.2–0.5% | Binding material between fiber bundles (removed during retting) |
| **Wax/Fat** | 0.4–0.8% | Surface coating |
| **Protein** | 0.8–2.5% | Minor constituent |
| **Mineral Matter (Ash)** | 0.6–1.2% | Inorganic residue |
| **Moisture** | ~12–14% | Natural regain |

> 📌 **The three principal chemical constituents of jute are: α-Cellulose, Hemicellulose, and Lignin.**

### 🔬 Structural Notes

- Jute's **higher lignin content** compared to flax and ramie makes it coarser and harder to process into fine yarns.
- Cellulose exists in both **crystalline** (ordered hydrogen bonds) and **amorphous** (disordered) regions within the fiber.
- The high lignin content gives jute its characteristic **golden-brown color** and "Golden Fiber" name.
- The molecular formula of cellulose: **(C₆H₁₀O₅)ₙ**

## Q3. Morphological Structure of Wool Fiber [4 Marks]

Wool fiber is an **animal (protein-based) natural fiber** primarily obtained from the fleece of sheep (*Ovis aries*). Its main chemical component is **keratin**, a fibrous structural protein rich in the amino acid **cysteine**, cross-linked by **disulfide (–S–S–) bonds**.

### 🐑 Overview of Wool Fiber

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Fiber type | Natural protein (animal) fiber |
| Main component | Keratin (α-keratin & β-keratin) |
| Fiber diameter | 10–50 micrometers (µm) |
| Fiber length | 5 cm (finest) to 35 cm (coarsest) |
| Color | Off-white to light cream |
| Cross-section shape | Oval to circular |
| Crimp | Fine wool: ~10 crimps/cm; Coarse: ~4 crimps/cm |

---

### 🔬 Morphological Structure of Wool Fiber

The microscopic structure of wool fiber consists of **three primary layers**:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                 WOOL FIBER CROSS-SECTION                │
│                                                         │
│   ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐   │
│   │         1. CUTICLE (Outer Scale Layer)          │   │
│   │  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐   │   │
│   │  │      2. CORTEX (Middle Layer ~90%)       │   │   │
│   │  │  ┌───────────────────────────────────┐  │   │   │
│   │  │  │    3. MEDULLA (Central Core)       │  │   │   │
│   │  │  │    (Present in coarse wools)       │  │   │   │
│   │  │  └───────────────────────────────────┘  │   │   │
│   │  └─────────────────────────────────────────┘   │   │
│   └─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘   │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

---

### Layer 1: 🐚 CUTICLE (Outer Layer)

The cuticle is the **outermost protective layer** consisting of flat, overlapping **scale-like cells** that project from the fiber surface like tiles on a roof.

**Sub-layers of the Cuticle:**

| Sub-layer | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Epicuticle** | Thin outer membrane (~2.5 nm thick); waxy coating; hydrophobic; repels liquid water but allows vapor absorption |
| **Exocuticle** | High-sulfur, highly cross-linked protein layer; very resistant to chemical attack |
| **Endocuticle** | Intermediate layer; lower sulfur content; connects to cortex |

**Key Features of Cuticle:**
- Scales point **away from the root** toward the tip
- Creates the **Differential Frictional Effect (DFE)**: fiber moves more easily tip-to-root than root-to-tip
- Responsible for wool's ability to **felt**
- Protects against chemical, mechanical, and pathogen damage

---

### Layer 2: 🧬 CORTEX (Middle Layer)

The cortex is the **largest component**, making up approximately **80–90% of the total fiber mass**. It determines the main physical and chemical properties of wool including resilience, crimp, and softness.

**Sub-types of Cortical Cells:**

| Type | Location | Properties |
|------|----------|------------|
| **Orthocortex** | One side of fiber | Intermediate filaments (IFs) arranged in spirals; more extensible; absorbs more dye |
| **Paracortex** | Opposite side | IFs arranged parallel to fiber axis; higher cystine content; more stable; less extensible |

> 💡 The **bilateral arrangement** of orthocortex and paracortex on opposite sides of the fiber — with different moisture absorption behavior — is what causes wool's **natural crimp**!

**Internal Hierarchy of the Cortex:**

```
Cortical Cell
    └── Macrofibrils (~0.3–0.5 µm diameter)
            └── Microfibrils (~7.5 nm diameter)
                    └── Protofibrils (2 × α-helical coiled-coil dimers)
                            └── Wool Polymer (Keratin protein chains)
```

---

### Layer 3: 🫧 MEDULLA (Central Core)

- Present mainly in **coarse and carpet wool fibers**; absent in fine (Merino) wool
- Comprises a central core of cells arranged along the fiber axis between the cortical cells
- Can be **continuous** (throughout the fiber) or **intermittent** (in patches)
- Determines the **luster** of the wool fiber
- Highly medullated fibers are generally of lower textile quality

## Q4. Define: Retting and Carbonizing [2 Marks]

---

### 🌊 RETTING

#### Definition
**Retting** is a controlled biological or chemical process used to **separate the bast fibers** from the non-fibrous materials (woody tissues, pectin, gums) in the stalks of fiber-yielding plants such as jute, flax, hemp, ramie, and nettle. The process primarily works by **degrading the pectic binding compounds** that cement the fiber bundles to the stalk, without significantly damaging the cellulosic fiber itself.

> *"Retting employs the action of bacteria and moisture on plants to dissolve or rot away much of the cellular tissues and gummy substances surrounding bast-fibre bundles, thus facilitating separation of the fibre from the stem."* — Britannica

#### Why is Retting Done?
- To remove **pectin, lignin, and hemicellulose** binding materials
- To allow **clean separation** of spinnable bast fibers from the woody core (called *shive* or *hurd*)
- To prepare fibers for subsequent processing: carding, spinning, and weaving

#### Types of Retting

| Type | Method | Duration | Quality | Remarks |
|------|--------|----------|---------|---------|
| **Water Retting** | Stalks submerged in rivers/ponds/tanks | 8–14 days | High quality, uniform | Anaerobic bacteria degrade pectin; produces odor; causes water pollution |
| **Dew (Field) Retting** | Stalks spread on grass fields; exposed to dew, air, fungi | 2–5 weeks | Moderate quality, darker fiber | Lower cost; inconsistent; climate-dependent |
| **Chemical Retting** | Treated with NaOH or other alkali solutions | 2–36 hours | Controlled, consistent | Faster; used in modern industrial processing |
| **Enzymatic Retting** | Polygalacturonases & pectinases applied | Hours | High quality, eco-friendly | Still being scaled up commercially |

#### Process (Water Retting of Jute)

```
Harvest jute stalks (after flowering, before seed set)
        │
        ▼
Bundle stalks → Submerge in slow-moving water
        │
        ▼
Bacteria degrade pectin & bind materials (~20–25 days)
        │
        ▼
Test for retting completion
        │
        ▼
Remove bundles → Wash thoroughly in clean water
        │
        ▼
Sun-dry for 2–3 days → Tie into bundles for market
```

#### ⚠️ Important Caution
- **Under-retting:** Fibers are not fully separated → poor quality, requires extra labor
- **Over-retting:** Cellulase enzymes from bacteria damage the cellulose fibers → weakened, low-quality fiber

### 🔥 CARBONIZING (Carbonisation)

#### Definition
**Carbonizing** (also spelled *Carbonising*) is a **chemical process** applied exclusively to **wool and other animal (protein) fibers** to remove **vegetable impurities** — such as seeds, burrs, grass, straw, and other plant fragments — from raw wool. It exploits the chemical difference between the **protein structure of wool** (which is resistant to acids) and the **cellulosic nature of vegetable matter** (which is degraded by strong acids).

> *"Carbonising is a continuous process which combines scouring to remove the wool grease and a chemical process which removes vegetable matter such as seeds, burs and grass."* — The Woolmark Company

#### Why is Carbonizing Necessary?
Raw wool collected from grazing sheep contains significant amounts of plant debris that cannot be fully removed by mechanical methods (burr-picking, carding) alone. Without carbonizing, vegetable matter would cause:
- Uneven dyeing (white resist spots)
- Weakened yarn and fabric
- Poor aesthetic quality of the final textile

#### The Carbonizing Process (Step by Step)

```
Step 1: SCOURING
   Raw wool is washed with non-ionic detergent to remove
   wool grease (lanolin), suint (sweat), dirt, and sand
        │
        ▼
Step 2: ACIDIZING (Immersion in Acid)
   Wool is immersed in dilute Sulphuric Acid (H₂SO₄)
   solution: 5–7% concentration at 20–30°C
   [AlCl₃ or HCl gas can also be used]
        │
        ▼
Step 3: SQUEEZING & DRYING
   Excess acid is removed by squeezing/centrifuging
   Wool is dried at 65–90°C to concentrate the acid
        │
        ▼
Step 4: BAKING (Carbonizing)
   Dried wool is baked at 95–125°C for ~1 minute
   ✦ Acid chars/carbonizes the cellulosic vegetable matter
   ✦ Wool (keratin protein) remains intact
        │
        ▼
Step 5: CRUSHING
   Wool passes through heavy fluted metal rollers
   Charred, brittle vegetable matter is crushed to dust
        │
        ▼
Step 6: DE-DUSTING
   Wool passes through a rotating shaker/de-duster
   Charred dust is mechanically removed
        │
        ▼
Step 7: NEUTRALIZING
   Wool (now acidic) is immersed in alkaline solution:
   Sodium Carbonate (Na₂CO₃), Ammonia, or NH₄ Acetate
   pH brought back to neutral (~7)
        │
        ▼
Step 8: RINSING & BLEACHING
   Rinsed with water + small amount of detergent
   Optional bleaching with H₂O₂ (Hydrogen Peroxide)
        │
        ▼
Step 9: FINAL DRYING
   Wool is dried and ready for further processing
   (carding, spinning, weaving)
```

#### Underlying Chemical Principle

Vegetable matter is composed of **cellulose: (C₆H₁₀O₅)ₙ** — which contains hydrogen and oxygen in the same ratio as water. Upon acid treatment and heating:

```
Cellulose (VM) + H₂SO₄ + Heat → Charred Carbon (C) + H₂O
Wool Keratin (Protein) + Dilute H₂SO₄ → Remains INTACT ✓
```

This **differential stability** of proteins vs. cellulose toward mineral acids is the chemical basis of carbonizing.

#### Comparison: Retting vs. Carbonizing

| Feature | Retting | Carbonizing |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| Applied to | Plant/bast fibers (Jute, Flax, Hemp) | Wool / Animal fibers |
| Purpose | Separate fiber from plant stalk | Remove vegetable impurities from wool |
| Agent used | Bacteria, water, enzymes, or chemicals | Dilute H₂SO₄ (sulfuric acid) |
| Mechanism | Biodegradation of pectin | Acid hydrolysis + thermal charring of cellulose |
| End product | Clean bast fibers ready for spinning | Clean, VM-free wool fiber |
| Temperature | Ambient (water/dew retting) or controlled (chemical) | 65–125°C |
