## Q1. Write down the composition of keratin. `[2 marks]`

### ✅ Answer

**Keratin** is the primary structural protein found in wool fiber. It is a complex fibrous protein made up of **amino acids** linked together by peptide bonds.

### Chemical Composition of Keratin:

| Element | Percentage (approx.) |
|---|---|
| Carbon (C) | ~50% |
| Hydrogen (H) | ~7% |
| Nitrogen (N) | ~16–17% |
| Oxygen (O) | ~21–23% |
| Sulfur (S) | ~3–4% |

> 🔑 **Key Point:** The presence of **sulfur** (in the form of the amino acid **cystine**) is what makes wool keratin unique. Sulfur creates **disulfide bonds (–S–S–)** between polypeptide chains, giving wool its strength, elasticity, and resilience.

### Amino Acid Composition of Keratin:
Keratin is made up of **~18–20 amino acids**, with the most important being:
- **Cystine** — provides disulfide cross-links (responsible for curl and strength)
- **Glutamic acid** — most abundant amino acid
- **Arginine**
- **Leucine**
- **Serine**

### Structure:
Keratin exists in an **alpha-helical coiled structure**, where:
- **α-Keratin** → helical form (found in unstretched wool)
- **β-Keratin** → extended/pleated sheet form (after stretching)

This transformation is reversible, which gives wool its **elastic memory**.

---

## Q2. Which property is responsible for the elasticity of wool fiber? What is wool carbonizing? `[2 marks]`

### ✅ Answer — Part A: Property Responsible for Elasticity

The **elasticity of wool fiber** is primarily due to:

1. **Disulfide (–S–S–) cross-linkages** between cystine residues in the keratin protein chains
   - These covalent bonds act like molecular springs
   - When stretched, the α-helix unfolds; when released, the bonds pull it back

2. **Alpha-helical coiled structure** of keratin
   - The coil-spring arrangement of polypeptide chains stores mechanical energy

3. **Hydrogen bonds and salt linkages** between adjacent polypeptide chains also contribute to elastic recovery

> **Wool can be stretched up to 30% of its length when dry, and up to 70% when wet, and still recover its original form.**

---

### ✅ Answer — Part B: Wool Carbonizing

**Carbonizing** (also spelled *carbonising*) is a **chemical scouring process** used to remove **vegetable matter** (such as burrs, seeds, grass, and straw) from raw wool.

#### Process:
1. Wool is treated with a **dilute solution of sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄)** — typically 4–7% concentration
2. The wool is then **dried and baked** at high temperatures (approximately **100–130°C**)
3. The acid **hydrolyzes and carbonizes** (chars) the cellulosic/vegetable impurities, converting them to brittle carbon
4. The carbonized matter is then **crushed and removed** by passing the wool through rollers
5. The wool is **neutralized** (using alkali like Na₂CO₃) and washed

#### Why is it possible?
- Wool (protein fiber) is **resistant to dilute acids**
- Vegetable matter (cellulose) is **degraded by acids** — hence selective removal is possible

#### Uses:
- Wool from heavily contaminated fleeces (especially from sheep in arid regions) undergoes carbonizing before further processing

## Q3. Mention the classification of wool by fleece. `[2 marks]`

### ✅ Answer

Wool is classified by **fleece type** based on the quality, fineness, and condition of the wool when shorn:

### Classification of Wool by Fleece:

| Fleece Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **1. Lamb's Wool (First Clip)** | Obtained from lambs at their first shearing (usually 6–8 months old). Very soft, fine, and has a natural tapered tip. Highest quality. |
| **2. Hogget Wool** | Obtained from young sheep (hoggs) at their first shearing after maturity. Finer than subsequent clips. |
| **3. Wether Wool** | From male castrated sheep (wethers). Strong, even fiber. Commonly used in apparel. |
| **4. Fleece Wool (Clip Wool)** | Obtained by shearing adult sheep. The most common and commercially important type. Can be shorn once or twice per year. |
| **5. Pulled Wool (Skin Wool)** | Wool removed from the skin of slaughtered sheep using depilatory chemicals. Lower quality than shorn wool. |
| **6. Dead Wool** | Wool removed from the skin of sheep that died naturally. Weakest quality; lacks luster and strength. |
| **7. Cotted Wool (Matted Wool)** | Wool fibers that have become entangled due to disease, poor condition, or neglect. Difficult to process. |
| **8. Taglocks** | Inferior, heavily contaminated, stained, or matted portions of the fleece. Sorted out as a lower grade. |

> 💡 **Note:** When grading wool, factors like **fineness (micron count)**, **staple length**, **strength**, **color**, and **vegetable matter content** are also considered alongside fleece type.

## Q4. Write down the physical properties of Jute (just mention, no need to describe): `[2 marks]`

### ✅ Answer

#### a) Specific Gravity of Jute
**1.48 g/cm³**

#### b) DP (Degree of Polymerization) of Lignin in Jute
The DP (Degree of Polymerization) of lignin in jute is approximately **1,000–3,500** monomer units.
*(Note: Jute contains about 11–13% lignin, which is a complex phenylpropanoid polymer)*

#### c) Elasticity of Jute
Jute has **very low elasticity** — approximately **1–2% elongation at break**
*(Jute is a rigid, stiff fiber with poor elastic recovery)*

#### d) Moisture Regain of Jute
**13.75%** (standard condition: 65% RH, 20°C)
*(Jute is highly hygroscopic — one of the most moisture-absorbing natural fibers)*

## Q5. Describe the dew retting of jute. `[2 marks]`

### ✅ Answer

**Dew retting** (also called **field retting**) is a biological method of separating jute fibers from the non-fibrous portions (stem/woody core) of the jute plant using **natural dew, rain, and microbial action**.

### Process:

1. **Harvesting:** Jute plants are cut at or near the ground level when they are in early flower stage.
2. **Field Spreading:** The cut jute stalks are spread **thinly on the ground** in fields or meadows.
3. **Dew & Rain Exposure:** Morning dew, rain, and humid air keep the stalks moist.
4. **Microbial Action:** Naturally occurring **fungi, bacteria** (especially *Clostridium* sp. and *Bacillus* sp.) attack the **pectins and non-fibrous tissues** binding the fibers to the woody core.
5. **Duration:** The process takes **3–4 weeks** depending on weather conditions.
6. **Separation:** After sufficient decomposition, fibers are stripped from the stalks by hand or mechanical means.
7. **Washing & Drying:** Fibers are washed in clean water and sun-dried.

### Advantages:
- No water body required (unlike water retting)
- Less water pollution
- Lower cost

### Disadvantages:
- Very **slow process** (3–4 weeks vs ~15–20 days for water retting)
- **Quality is inferior** — fibers may be discolored or unevenly retted
- Dependent on **weather conditions**
- Requires large land area

### Comparison with Water Retting:
| Feature | Dew Retting | Water Retting |
|---|---|---|
| Medium | Dew/rain | Stagnant/slow water |
| Duration | 3–4 weeks | 15–20 days |
| Quality | Lower | Higher |
| Water Pollution | Minimal | High |
| Cost | Lower | Slightly higher |

## Q1. Why is silk known as "the Queen of All Fibers"? `[2 marks]`

### ✅ Answer

Silk is called **"the Queen of All Fibers"** because of its exceptional combination of beauty, luxury, strength, and performance properties that no other natural fiber can match:

| Property | Why it Makes Silk "Royal" |
|---|---|
| **Lustrous Appearance** | The triangular prism-like cross-section of silk filaments refracts light, giving a brilliant, natural sheen unmatched by any other fiber |
| **Soft Hand-feel** | Exceptionally smooth and soft texture against the skin — the definition of luxury |
| **Strength** | Silk is the **strongest natural fiber** — stronger than steel of the same diameter (tensile strength ~3.5–5 g/denier) |
| **Lightness** | Despite its strength, silk is extremely light and delicate |
| **Natural Temperature Regulation** | Keeps wearer cool in summer, warm in winter |
| **Moisture Absorption** | Absorbs up to **11% of its weight** in moisture without feeling wet |
| **Natural Protein Fiber** | Biocompatible with human skin; used in medical sutures |
| **Drape & Elegance** | Superior draping quality used in high-end fashion globally |
| **Historical & Cultural Value** | Associated with royalty, wealth, and status for over 5,000 years (Silk Road era) |

> 🌟 The combination of **unrivaled luster, superior strength, elegant drape, and centuries of royal association** justifies silk's title as the "Queen of All Fibers."

---

## Q2. Define: Sericulture, Degumming `[2 marks]`

### ✅ Answer

#### 🐛 Sericulture:

**Sericulture** is the **science and art of raising silkworms** (*Bombyx mori*) for the purpose of producing raw silk. It encompasses all activities from:
- Cultivation of **mulberry trees** (food source for silkworms)
- Rearing of **silkworm larvae**
- Care during **cocoon formation**
- **Harvesting of cocoons** for silk extraction

> *From Latin: "Sericum" (silk) + "cultura" (cultivation)*
> Sericulture has been practiced for over **5,000 years**, originating in ancient China.

---

#### 🫧 Degumming:

**Degumming** is the process of **removing the natural gum (sericin)** that coats raw silk filaments.

- Raw silk contains two proteins: **Fibroin** (the actual silk fiber, ~70–80%) and **Sericin** (the gum, ~20–25%)
- Sericin makes raw silk stiff, dull, and harsh to touch
- **Degumming** removes sericin by boiling silk in **mild alkaline solution** (soap + water, or Na₂CO₃ solution) at ~95°C for 1–2 hours
- After degumming, silk becomes **soft, lustrous, and lightweight**
- The weight loss after degumming is called **"boil-off loss"** (~20–30%)

## Q3. List the five amino acids available in silk. `[2 marks]`

### ✅ Answer

Silk fibroin is a protein composed of **~18 amino acids**. The five most abundant and important ones are:

| # | Amino Acid | Approximate % in Fibroin |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Glycine (Gly)** | ~44–46% (most abundant) |
| 2 | **Alanine (Ala)** | ~28–30% |
| 3 | **Serine (Ser)** | ~11–12% |
| 4 | **Tyrosine (Tyr)** | ~5–6% |
| 5 | **Valine (Val)** | ~2–3% |

> 💡 **Key Fact:** The regular repeating sequence of **Glycine–Alanine–Glycine–Alanine–Glycine–Serine** gives silk its characteristic **β-pleated sheet structure**, which is responsible for silk's high tensile strength and inextensibility.

The remaining amino acids (Glutamic acid, Aspartic acid, Leucine, etc.) make up the amorphous regions of the silk fibroin.

## Q5. Write down the chemical composition of silk. `[2 marks]`

### ✅ Answer

Silk is a **natural protein fiber** secreted by silkworm (*Bombyx mori*) larvae. Its chemical composition includes:

### Elemental Composition:

| Element | Percentage (approx.) |
|---|---|
| Carbon (C) | ~48–50% |
| Hydrogen (H) | ~6–7% |
| Nitrogen (N) | ~17–19% |
| Oxygen (O) | ~25–27% |
| Sulfur (S) | ~0.15–0.5% (trace) |

> Note: Unlike wool keratin, silk contains **very little sulfur** — hence no significant disulfide bonds, giving silk lower elasticity than wool.

### Protein Composition of Raw Silk:

| Component | % in Raw Silk | Nature |
|---|---|---|
| **Fibroin** | 70–80% | Core structural protein; gives strength and luster |
| **Sericin** | 20–25% | Gum coating; holds two fibroin filaments together; removed by degumming |
| **Wax** | ~1.5% | Surface lubricant |
| **Ash (mineral matter)** | ~1.0% | Inorganic salts |
| **Colouring matter** | ~0.2% | Natural pigments |

### Molecular Structure:
- **Fibroin** has a **β-pleated sheet** secondary structure
- Regular repeating peptide sequence: **(Gly–Ala)ₙ** with occasional Serine
- Molecular weight of fibroin: ~200,000–350,000 Daltons
- **No disulfide bonds** (unlike wool) — hence smooth, less extensible

## 🐑 Wool — Extra Practice

### EQ-W1. What is the morphological structure of wool fiber? Describe each part briefly.

**Answer:**
Wool fiber has a complex three-part morphological structure:

1. **Cuticle (Epicuticle + Exocuticle + Endocuticle):**
   - The outermost layer consisting of **overlapping, scale-like cells** pointing toward the fiber tip
   - These scales give wool its **felting property** (fibers interlock irreversibly when agitated in hot soapy water)
   - The epicuticle is a thin hydrophobic membrane providing water repellency

2. **Cortex:**
   - The main bulk (~90%) of the fiber
   - Composed of two types of cells: **Ortho-cortex** and **Para-cortex**
   - The asymmetric arrangement of these two cortical types causes the natural **crimp (waviness)** of wool
   - Contains the keratin proteins organized in microfibrils → macrofibrils

3. **Medulla:**
   - A central canal (hollow core) present only in **coarser wool fibers**
   - Absent in fine wools (like Merino)
   - Acts as air space, contributing to insulation

---

### EQ-W2. What is "wool grading"? Mention two systems used for grading wool.

**Answer:**
**Wool grading** is the process of sorting and classifying raw wool according to its quality, fineness, staple length, and other characteristics to determine its commercial value and suitable end use.

**Two major grading systems:**

1. **Bradford Count System (British Count):**
   - Expressed as the number of **hanks of yarn** (560 yards each) that can be spun from one pound of clean wool
   - Higher count = finer wool (e.g., 80s wool is finer than 40s)
   - Example: Merino wool = 60s–80s; carpet wool = 20s–36s

2. **Micron System (IWTO Standard):**
   - Measures the **average fiber diameter in micrometers (µm)**
   - Lower micron = finer fiber
   - Example: Ultrafine Merino < 17.5 µm; Medium wool = 22–26 µm; Carpet wool > 35 µm

---

### EQ-W3. What is wool felting? What causes it?

**Answer:**
**Felting** is the irreversible **intermatting and interlocking of wool fibers** when subjected to heat, moisture, and mechanical agitation simultaneously, resulting in a dense, compact fabric (felt).

**Causes:**
- The **scale structure** on the cuticle layer of wool fibers — scales point toward the tip
- When agitated, fibers move preferentially toward their root ends (differential friction effect — **DFE**)
- Fibers become tangled and locked together permanently
- Heat causes the keratin to swell; moisture plasticizes the fiber; friction causes irreversible interlocking

**Industrial Use:** Felted wool is used for hats, industrial filters, musical instrument pads (piano keys), insulation.

---

### EQ-W4. What is the moisture regain of wool? Why does wool absorb so much moisture?

**Answer:**
- **Moisture regain of wool:** **13–18%** (standard: ~16–17% at 65% RH, 20°C)
- Wool is one of the most hygroscopic natural fibers

**Why such high moisture absorption?**
1. **Hydrophilic amino acid side groups** (–OH, –COOH, –NH₂, –CONH–) in keratin readily form hydrogen bonds with water molecules
2. The **amorphous regions** of the fiber protein are accessible to water
3. The **cortex cells** can swell in moisture — wool absorbs moisture without feeling wet on the surface
4. This moisture absorption generates **heat (heat of absorption/sorption)** — this is why wool garments keep you warm even when slightly damp

## 🌿 Jute — Extra Practice

### EQ-J1. Write the chemical composition of jute fiber.

**Answer:**
Jute is a **lignocellulosic bast fiber** (neither pure cellulose like cotton, nor protein like silk/wool). Its chemical composition:

| Component | Percentage |
|---|---|
| **Cellulose** | 58–63% |
| **Hemicellulose** | 20–24% |
| **Lignin** | 11–13% |
| **Ash** | ~1% |
| **Water soluble matter** | ~1.5% |
| **Fats & Wax** | ~0.5% |
| **Moisture** | 13.75% (at standard conditions) |

> 🔑 The presence of **lignin** is what makes jute different from cotton — lignin causes yellowing/degradation over time and makes jute stiffer and less dye-receptive.

---

### EQ-J2. What is retting? Name the types of retting used for jute.

**Answer:**
**Retting** is the process of **separating the bast fibers** of jute from the surrounding non-fibrous tissues (woody core, bark, etc.) using **water and microbial action**.

Enzymes (pectinases, hemicellulases) produced by microorganisms break down the **pectin and gummy substances** binding the fiber bundles to the stem.

**Types of Retting:**
1. **Water Retting (Pool/River Retting)** — Most common in Bangladesh; stalks submerged in slow-moving water for 15–20 days
2. **Dew Retting** — Stalks spread on fields; relies on dew and rain; takes 3–4 weeks
3. **Chemical Retting** — Using chemicals (NaOH, Na₂CO₃) to speed up the process; faster but expensive
4. **Steam/Pressure Retting** — High-pressure steam used; industrial method; very fast
5. **Enzymatic Retting** — Using purified pectinase enzymes; eco-friendly and gives high-quality fiber

## 🐛 Silk — Extra Practice

### EQ-S1. Describe the life cycle of Bombyx mori.

**Answer:**
*Bombyx mori* (mulberry silkworm) undergoes **complete metamorphosis (holometabolism)** with four distinct stages:

```
Egg → Larva (Silkworm/Caterpillar) → Pupa (Cocoon) → Adult Moth
```

| Stage | Duration | Key Activity |
|---|---|---|
| **Egg (Ovum)** | 10–14 days | Female moth lays ~400–600 eggs; eggs overwinter |
| **Larva (Silkworm)** | 25–35 days | Caterpillar hatches; eats mulberry leaves voraciously; molts 4 times (5 instars) |
| **Pupa (Cocoon)** | 10–14 days | Mature larva spins a cocoon using a single continuous silk thread (600–1500 m long); pupates inside |
| **Adult (Moth)** | 5–10 days | Moth emerges (for breeding) or cocoon is killed (for silk extraction); mates and lays eggs |

> ⚠️ For silk production, cocoons are killed (by steam or hot air) **before the moth emerges** to prevent it from breaking the continuous silk filament.

---

### EQ-S2. What is reeling of silk? Describe briefly.

**Answer:**
**Reeling** is the process of **unwinding the continuous silk filament** from killed cocoons and combining several filaments into a single thread of raw silk (called *grege* or *raw silk*).

**Process:**
1. Killed cocoons are **softened in hot water** (80–90°C) to dissolve the sericin slightly and allow unwinding
2. The **filament end** of each cocoon is located using a brush
3. Filaments from **5–8 cocoons** are combined and drawn together through a guide
4. The combined filaments are wound onto a **reel** to form raw silk
5. The sericin in softened state acts as a **natural adhesive**, binding the filaments together as they dry

> Raw silk produced by reeling has a diameter equivalent to combining 5–8 filaments → gives required count/thickness.

### EQ-S4. What is the Silk Road? How is it related to silk trade history?

**Answer:**
The **Silk Road** was an ancient network of **trade routes** connecting **China to the Mediterranean** (and beyond), spanning approximately **7,000 km**, used from around **130 BCE to 1453 CE**.

**Connection to Silk:**
- China monopolized silk production for over **1,000 years** — exporting silk to Rome, Persia, India, and beyond in exchange for gold, spices, and other goods
- The term "Silk Road" was coined by German geographer **Ferdinand von Richthofen** in 1877
- The **secret of sericulture** (silkworm eggs and mulberry seeds) was eventually smuggled to Byzantine Empire (~550 CE), ending China's monopoly
- Bangladesh and Bengal region were part of the **maritime Silk Road**, trading Bengal silk (*Muslin* and silk fabrics) with Arab and European traders
