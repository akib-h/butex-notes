## 1. Textile Fiber — Basics & Classification

---

### 1.1 What is a Textile Fiber? / Define Textile Fiber.

A **textile fiber** is the basic raw material used in the manufacture of yarn and fabric. It is a slender, thread-like unit of material that possesses sufficient length, fineness, strength, and flexibility to be spun or twisted into a yarn and subsequently woven, knitted, or otherwise fabricated into a textile structure.

**Key characteristics required:**
- High length-to-width (aspect) ratio (minimum ~100:1)
- Adequate tensile strength
- Flexibility / pliability
- Cohesiveness / spinnability
- Uniformity

---

### 1.2 "All fibers are not textile fibers" — Explain.

All fibrous materials have a thread-like structure, but **not every fiber is suitable for textile use**. To qualify as a textile fiber, a material must meet specific requirements:

| Requirement | Why Needed |
|---|---|
| Sufficient length (≥ 1 cm for staples) | For spinning into yarn |
| Fineness | For soft, flexible fabrics |
| Strength (tenacity) | To withstand processing & wear |
| Flexibility | Prevents breaking during bending |
| Uniformity | For regular yarn structure |
| Cohesiveness | Fibers must cling together during spinning |

**Example:** Asbestos is a fiber but is not used for apparel because it is rigid and carcinogenic. Human hair has a fiber-like structure but is too coarse and lacks the required cohesiveness for spinning. Hence, **though sheep wool is a textile fiber, human hair is not.**

---

### 1.3 State the Basic Requirements / Properties of Textile Fibers.

#### Primary (Essential) Properties:
1. **High Length-to-Diameter Ratio** — At least 100:1 (cotton ~1000:1)
2. **Tensile Strength** — Minimum 1 g/denier to survive processing
3. **Flexibility / Pliability** — Fiber must bend without breaking
4. **Cohesiveness / Spinning Quality** — Ability of fibers to cling together
5. **Uniformity** — Consistent diameter and length

#### Secondary (Desirable) Properties:
1. **Elasticity** — Ability to return to original length after stretching
2. **Moisture Regain (MR)** — Ability to absorb moisture
3. **Luster** — Appearance and light-reflecting quality
4. **Dyeability** — Ability to accept dye
5. **Fineness** — Fine fibers give soft, smooth fabrics
6. **Color** — Preferably white or near-white for easy dyeing

### 1.5 Compare Natural Fiber vs Man-Made Fiber.

| Property | Natural Fiber | Man-Made Fiber |
|---|---|---|
| Source | Nature (plant, animal, mineral) | Chemical synthesis or regeneration |

| Moisture Regain | High (cotton 8.5%, wool 17%) | Low (polyester 0.4%) |
| Strength | Moderate | Generally high |
| Elasticity | Generally low (except wool) | High (especially nylon, spandex) |
| Thermal resistance | Moderate | Variable |
| Biodegradability | Fully biodegradable | Non-biodegradable (mostly) |
| Cost | Subject to harvest variability | Consistent and controllable |
| Feel / Handle | Soft, comfortable | Can feel synthetic/harsh |
| Chemical resistance | Varies by fiber | Generally good |
| Examples | Cotton, Jute, Wool, Silk | Polyester, Nylon, Viscose |

### 1.6 Distinguish Between Staple Fiber and Filament Fiber.

| Property | Staple Fiber | Filament Fiber |
|---|---|---|
| Length | Short (2–50 cm) | Very long (continuous, hundreds of meters) |
| Origin | All natural fibers (except silk), cut synthetic | Silk (natural), all synthetic as produced |
| Yarn structure | Requires spinning; hairy, bulky yarn | Can be used directly; smooth, lustrous yarn |
| Fabric feel | Soft, warm | Smooth, cool |
| Strength | Lower yarn strength | Higher yarn strength |
| Examples | Cotton, Jute, Wool, Hemp | Silk, Nylon filament, Polyester filament |

---

### 1.7 Differences Between Crystallinity and Amorphousness of Textile Fibers.

| Property | Crystalline Region | Amorphous Region |
|---|---|---|
| Molecular arrangement | Highly ordered, parallel | Random, disordered |
| Density | High | Low |
| Strength | High | Low |
| Moisture absorption | Low | High (accessible to water) |
| Dye uptake | Poor (dye molecules can't penetrate) | Good |
| Flexibility | Rigid | Flexible |

> **Note:** Most textile fibers are **semi-crystalline** — they have both regions. Cotton has ~70% crystallinity; wool ~25–30%.

---

### 1.8 What is Moisture Regain (MR) and Moisture Content (MC)?

- **Moisture Regain (MR):** The weight of moisture in a fiber expressed as a percentage of the **bone-dry (oven-dry) weight** of the fiber.

$$MR(\%) = \frac{W_{wet} - W_{dry}}{W_{dry}} \times 100$$

- **Moisture Content (MC):** The weight of moisture expressed as a percentage of the **wet (conditioned) weight**.

$$MC(\%) = \frac{W_{wet} - W_{dry}}{W_{wet}} \times 100$$

| Fiber | Standard MR (%) |
|---|---|
| Cotton | 8.5 |
| Jute | 13.75 |
| Flax | 12.0 |
| Wool | 17.0 |
| Silk | 11.0 |
| Viscose | 13.0 |
| Polyester | 0.4 |
| Nylon 6,6 | 4.0 |

---

### 1.9 Compare Hydrophilic vs Hydrophobic Fibers.

| Property | Hydrophilic Fiber | Hydrophobic Fiber |
|---|---|---|
| Water affinity | High — absorbs water readily | Low — repels water |
| MR | High (> 8%) | Low (< 2%) |
| Comfort | High (breathable, absorbent) | Low |
| Drying time | Slow | Fast |
| Static buildup | Low | High |
| Dyeability | Easy with water-based dyes | Requires special disperse dyes |
| Examples | Cotton, Linen, Wool, Silk, Rayon | Polyester, Polypropylene, Acrylic |

### 1.10 Importance of Textile Fibers.

1. **Basic raw material** for all textile products — clothing, home textiles, technical textiles.
2. **Economic importance** — major source of export earnings (e.g., jute and cotton in Bangladesh).
3. **Employment** — millions employed in fiber production, spinning, weaving, finishing.
4. **Agriculture** — cotton, jute, flax are important agricultural crops.
5. **Industrial uses** — geotextiles, composites, medical textiles, filtration.
6. **Cultural significance** — traditional textiles tied to regional identity.

## 2. Cotton Fiber

---

### 2.1 Morphological Structure of Cotton Fiber (with Sketch)

Cotton fiber is a single-celled seed hair. Its morphological structure has the following layers from outside to inside:

```
CROSS-SECTION VIEW (mature cotton):
┌────────────────────────────┐
│  Cuticle (wax + pectin)    │  ← outermost layer; waxy, protects fiber
├────────────────────────────┤
│  Primary Wall              │  ← thin cellulose layer with spiral fibrils
├────────────────────────────┤
│  Secondary Wall (S1,S2,S3) │  ← thick; 90% of fiber mass; crystalline cellulose
│  (fibrillar structure)     │     fibrils wound at ~25°–35° angle (winding angle)
├────────────────────────────┤
│  Tertiary Wall (thin)      │  ← inner most cellulose layer
├────────────────────────────┤
│        Lumen               │  ← central hollow channel; contains dried
│     (hollow canal)         │     cell sap; degree of collapse = maturity
└────────────────────────────┘

LONGITUDINAL VIEW:
   ~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~   ← Twisted ribbon shape (convolutions)
   Natural twist (right and left spirals alternate)
   Immature: flat ribbon; Mature: kidney/bean shaped cross-section
```

**Key morphological features:**
- **Convolutions:** Natural twists in the fiber (~3.9–6.5 twists/cm); give cohesiveness; absent in immature fiber
- **Lumen:** The hollow center; in mature fiber it collapses, giving a kidney-shaped cross-section
- **Fibrils:** Sub-microscopic cellulose chains wound in a spiral in the secondary wall
- **Winding angle:** ~20–30° from fiber axis; affects strength and extensibility

---

### 2.2 Chemical Composition of Cotton Fiber

| Component | Percentage (%) |
|---|---|
| Cellulose | 88–96 |
| Protein | 1.0–1.9 |
| Pectic substances | 0.4–1.2 |
| Wax | 0.4–1.0 |
| Ash (minerals) | 0.7–1.6 |
| Water (moisture) | 6–8 |
| Others (pigments, sugars) | ~0.5 |

**Chemical structure of cellulose:**
- Cellulose is a linear polysaccharide made of **β-D-glucose** units linked by **β-1,4-glycosidic bonds**.
- Degree of Polymerization (DP) of native cotton cellulose: **~10,000–15,000**
- Empirical formula of repeating unit (cellobiose): **(C₆H₁₀O₅)ₙ** or more precisely: each glucose unit = C₆H₁₀O₅

```
         OH  OH                OH  OH
          |   |                 |   |
   ─O─CH₂─CH─CH─O─CH─CH₂─O─CH₂─CH─CH─O─CH─CH₂─O─
          |   |   |   |         |   |   |   |
         OH   O   OH  OH       OH   O   OH  OH
              │                     │
          (1→4) β linkage       (1→4) β linkage
```

---

### 2.3 Chemical Properties of Cotton Fiber

1. **Effect of acids:** Cotton is **degraded by acids**. Concentrated acids (HCl, H₂SO₄) hydrolyze glycosidic bonds rapidly. Dilute acids at high temperature also cause damage. Cotton is more sensitive to acids than wool.
2. **Effect of alkalis:** Cotton is **resistant to alkalis**. NaOH swells cotton (basis of mercerization — improves luster, strength, dye uptake). Cotton is stable in dilute and concentrated alkalis.
3. **Effect of oxidizing agents:** Strong oxidizers (H₂O₂, NaOCl at high concentrations) degrade cotton (oxycellulose formation). Controlled bleaching with H₂O₂ is safe.
4. **Effect of reducing agents:** Generally resistant.
5. **Effect of heat:** Yellows at 120°C; degrades at 150°C; burns readily (no melting); leaves gray ash.
6. **Flammability:** Burns readily with afterglow; smells of burning paper; leaves soft gray ash — typical of cellulosic fibers.
7. **Moisture:** Highly hygroscopic; MR = 8.5%; **wet strength increases ~20%** (hydrogen bonding with water strengthens inter-fibrillar bonding).
8. **Organic solvents:** Generally resistant — basis for dry-cleaning safety.

---

| Property | Value / Description |
|---|---|

### 2.6 What is Organic Cotton?

**Organic cotton** is cotton grown without the use of synthetic pesticides, herbicides, or artificial fertilizers. It is produced using natural methods that maintain soil health, ecosystem balance, and biodiversity. It is certified by international standards (e.g., GOTS — Global Organic Textile Standard, OCS). Key features:
- No GMO (Genetically Modified Organism) seeds
- Rainwater irrigation preferred over flooding
- Safer for farmers, consumers, and environment
- Usually lower yield but premium market value

---

### 2.7 Define Lint and Linters. Compare Between Them.

- **Lint:** The long fibers (staple) attached to the cotton seed after opening of the boll. These are the commercially valuable textile fibers separated during ginning. Staple length > 15 mm.

- **Linters:** Short, fuzzy fibers that remain clinging to the cottonseed after the lint has been removed by ginning. They are too short to be spun into yarn directly. Length: 1–6 mm (1st cut) and 1–3 mm (2nd cut).

| Property | Lint | Linters |
|---|---|---|
| Length | > 15 mm (staple) | 1–6 mm |
| Obtained | First ginning | After de-linting/second ginning |
| Use | Yarn and fabric manufacture | Rayon, cellulose acetate, paper, explosives (nitrocellulose), medical cotton |
| Cellulose purity | ~88–96% | ~80–90% |
| Commercial value | High | Moderate (industrial) |

---

### 2.8 What is Ginning? Working Procedure of Saw Ginning Machine.

**Ginning** is the mechanical process of separating cotton lint (long fibers) from the cottonseeds.

#### Saw Ginning (McCarthy Gin / Modern Saw Gin):

**Components:**
- Seed roll box
- Saw cylinder (circular saws mounted on a shaft)
- Huller ribs (grid bars)
- Brush/air doffing system
- Lint chute

**Working Procedure:**
```
Seed Cotton (raw) → Feed Hopper
        ↓
    Seed Roll Box
   (seed cotton accumulates between grid bars and saw cylinder)
        ↓
    Saw Cylinder rotates
   (saw teeth grip lint fibers and pull them THROUGH the grid bars/ribs)
   (seeds are TOO LARGE to pass through the ribs → seeds fall out)
        ↓
    Lint on saw teeth is removed by:
      - High-speed Brush Roller (mechanical doffing), OR
      - Air blast (pneumatic doffing)
        ↓
    Clean Lint → Baling
    Seeds → Seed outlet → Oil extraction / Linters
```

**Capacity:** ~14–20 bales/day per stand.
**Suitable for:** American Upland cotton (medium-long staple).
**Advantage:** High output, efficient.
**Disadvantage:** Cuts long fibers; not suitable for extra-long staple (ELS) cotton.

---

### 2.9 Faults of Ginning

1. **Neps** — Small tangled fiber knots formed due to rough handling
2. **Broken seeds** — Cause seed coat fragments (SCF) contamination in lint
3. **Cutting of fibers** — Saw teeth cut long fibers; reduces staple length
4. **High moisture ginning** — Leads to fiber damage and discoloration
5. **Low moisture ginning** — Excessive static, fiber breakage
6. **Foreign matter contamination** — Dust, leaves, stems mixing with lint
7. **Loss of fibers** — Some lint remains attached to seeds (unseparated lint)

---

### 2.10 Cotton Grading System

Cotton is graded based on three principal characteristics:
1. **Staple Length** — measured by HVICC (High Volume Instrument)
2. **Grade** — combination of color (whiteness, brightness), leaf (trash) content, and preparation (ginning quality)
3. **Micronaire** — fineness and maturity combined measure

**USDA Grade Standards (descending quality):**
Good Middling → Strict Middling → Middling → Strict Low Middling → Low Middling → Strict Good Ordinary → Good Ordinary

---

### 2.11 End Uses of Cotton Fiber

**Apparel:** shirts, dresses, underwear, socks, T-shirts, jeans, uniforms
**Home textiles:** bed sheets, towels, curtains, upholstery
**Industrial:** filters, conveyor belts, tarpaulins, nets
**Medical:** surgical cotton, bandages, gauze (from linters)
**Technical:** tire cord (mercerized cotton), canvas, ropes
**Chemical:** linters → cellulose acetate, nitrocellulose (explosives), viscose rayon

## 3. Jute Fiber

---

### 3.1 What is Jute? / Classification of Jute.

**Jute** is a natural bast fiber obtained from the skin (bast) of the jute plant. It is a ligno-cellulosic fiber — composed of cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin. Bangladesh is the world's largest jute-growing nation. The fiber is also called the **"Golden Fiber"** due to its golden-brown color and economic importance.

#### Classification of Jute (By Species):
```
JUTE FIBER
│
├── White Jute (Corchorus capsularis)
│   └── Round-podded; grown in low-lying flooded areas
│
└── Tossa Jute / Bangla Jute (Corchorus olitorius)
    └── Long-podded; superior quality; stronger and softer
```

#### Classification by Quality Grade (Kutcha Baling System):
| Grade | Mark |
|---|---|
| Special | S |
| Superior | HS |
| A-grade | A |
| B-grade | B |
| C-grade | C |
| D-grade (lowest) | D |

#### Classification by Geographical Distribution in Bangladesh:
- **Faridpur type** (White jute — best quality)
- **Dacca type** (Tossa)
- **Narayanganj type**
- **Chittagong type**

---

### 3.2 Morphological Structure of Jute Fiber

#### Macro Structure (Longitudinal view):
```
Jute Plant Stem Cross-Section:
┌──────────────────────────┐
│  Epidermis               │  ← outer protective layer
├──────────────────────────┤
│  Cortex                  │
├──────────────────────────┤
│  Bast (Phloem) ←── FIBER │  ← fiber bundles here
├──────────────────────────┤
│  Cambium                 │
├──────────────────────────┤
│  Xylem (woody core)      │
├──────────────────────────┤
│  Pith                    │
└──────────────────────────┘
```

#### Micro Structure:
- **Ultimate cell (single fiber cell):** polygonal cross-section; 1–4 mm long; 0.01–0.02 mm wide
- **Cell wall layers:** Primary wall (thin cellulose + pectin + lignin) and Secondary wall (cellulose fibrils)
- **Lumen:** Narrow but present; contains dried protoplasm
- **Node / Dislocation:** Weak transverse lines visible under microscope

```
Longitudinal microscopic view:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
   ← multicellular in nature →
```

---

### 3.3 Chemical Composition of Jute Fiber

| Component | Percentage (%) |
|---|---|
| α-Cellulose | 58–63 |
| Hemicellulose | 20–24 |
| Lignin | 12–15 |
| Pectin | 0.2–0.4 |
| Water-soluble matter | 1.1–1.5 |
| Fat & Wax | 0.3–0.5 |
| Moisture | ~12 |
| Ash | 0.5–1.0 |

> **Note:** Unlike cotton (almost pure cellulose), jute contains significant lignin (~12–15%) which makes it stiffer and less flexible. Lignin is the binding agent in the middle lamella of cells.

---

### 3.4 What is Lignin?

**Lignin** is a complex aromatic polymer found in plant cell walls, particularly in woody tissues and bast fibers. It acts as the **cement or binding material** between cellulose fibers, giving rigidity to the plant stem. It is a 3-dimensional network polymer of phenylpropanoid units (coniferyl, sinapyl, p-coumaryl alcohols).

**Properties of Lignin:**
- Resistant to most chemicals
- Causes yellowing of jute under light (photo-degradation of lignin)
- Non-cellulosic; does not contribute to textile properties
- High lignin = stiffer, harsher fiber
- Reduces flexibility and dyeability

---

### 3.5 Chemical Properties of Jute Fiber

1. **Effect of acids:** Jute is degraded by acids (cellulose component hydrolyzed). Hot dilute acid or cold concentrated acid causes serious degradation. Less acid-resistant than cotton.
2. **Effect of alkalis:** Moderate resistance. NaOH causes swelling but also partial dissolution of hemicellulose and lignin. Strong alkali degrades jute.
3. **Effect of oxidizing agents:** Bleaching agents damage jute more than cotton due to oxidation of lignin producing colored compounds (chromophores).
4. **Effect of reducing agents:** More stable than to oxidizers.
5. **Effect of heat:** Discolors at 120°C; decomposes above 150°C.
6. **Moisture:** MR = 12–13%; jute is hygroscopic.
7. **Micro-organisms:** Susceptible to mildew and bacterial attack (especially during retting).

---

### 3.6 Identification of Jute Fiber

1. **Microscopic test:**
   - *Cross-section:* Polygonal with thick walls and narrow lumen; cells in bundles
   - *Longitudinal:* Node-like (dislocation zones) marks; multicellular

2. **Burning test:**
   - Burns readily with flame; smells like burning wood/paper; leaves fluffy gray ash (lignin + cellulose)
   - Burns more rapidly than cotton due to lignin

3. **Chemical test (Phloroglucinol + HCl test):**
   - Add phloroglucinol solution followed by conc. HCl to fiber
   - Jute turns **cherry red / crimson** due to presence of lignin (**lignin test** — positive for jute, negative for cotton)

4. **Feel:** Rough, stiff compared to cotton or silk

5. **Color:** Natural golden brown / yellowish

---

### 3.7 Defects of Jute Fiber

1. **Runners / Specky jute:** Pieces of hard, poorly-retted jute still attached to the root
2. **Dead fiber:** Over-retted; very weak, disintegrated fiber
3. **Crop:** Short fibers at root end
4. **Knotty fiber:** Tangled or knotted fiber
5. **Heart damage (Kacha):** Fibrous material not properly separated from woody core
6. **Rooty jute:** Excessive root matter attached to fiber
7. **Sandy / Weedy jute:** Sand, leaves, and foreign matter mixed
8. **Mossy jute:** Covered with moss or fungal growth (microbial contamination)
9. **Discolored jute:** Off-color due to improper retting or storage
10. **Cutchy jute:** Under-retted (incomplete retting); fiber not fully separated

---

### 3.8 Jute Retting Process (with Types)

**Retting** is the biological process by which the gum (pectin, hemicellulose, lignin) binding the bast fibers to the stem is decomposed by microbial action (bacteria, fungi) in water, thereby releasing the fibers.

```
Jute stalks harvested
        ↓
Bundle stalks and immerse in slow-flowing or stagnant water
        ↓
Microbial activity (Clostridium, Bacillus species) breaks down pectin & lignin
        ↓
Duration: 8–30 days (depends on temperature, water quality)
        ↓
Test for completion: Bark separates cleanly from core
        ↓
Extract fibers by hand (stripping/ribboning)
        ↓
Wash and dry in sun
```

#### Types of Retting:
| Type | Method | Quality |
|---|---|---|
| **Water retting** | Submersion in ponds, rivers, lakes | Most common in Bangladesh |
| **Dew retting** | Spreading on field; dew + microbes do the work | Slow; used for flax mainly |
| **Chemical retting** | NaOH/Na₂CO₃ solution | Faster; used industrially |
| **Steam retting** | Steam + chemicals | Very fast; not common for jute |

**Best water type:** Slightly acidic, running water gives superior fiber. Stagnant water = darker, weaker fiber.

---

### 3.9 Diversified Uses of Jute / End Uses

**Traditional:**
- Hessian (burlap) — sacking, bags
- Sacking cloth — packaging grain, sugar, cement
- Carpet backing (primary and secondary)
- Ropes, twine, cordage

**Diversified / Modern Uses ("Jutton" products):**
- **Jute-cotton blended yarn** ("Jutton") for apparels
- Geotextiles — erosion control, road reinforcement
- Jute composites — automotive door panels, packaging boards
- Jute nonwoven — insulation material
- Jute paper — specialty paper
- Biochar from jute sticks — fuel, soil amendment
- Shopping bags, fashion accessories (eco-friendly)
- Agronet — crop protection

---

### 3.10 "Jutton" — Definition

**"Jutton"** refers to a blended yarn or fabric made by combining **jute and cotton** fibers together. By blending, the resulting product acquires properties intermediate between the two fibers: better softness (from cotton) and cost-effectiveness plus environmental benefits (from jute). It is used for apparels, home furnishing, and industrial applications.

---

### 3.11 Jute Cultivation Process

1. **Land preparation:** Well-drained, loamy soil; pH 6–7; plowing and harrowing
2. **Sowing:** March–April (Tossa) or April–May (White); broadcast or row sowing; seed rate 5–7 kg/acre
3. **Germination:** 5–7 days
4. **Thinning/weeding:** At 4–6 weeks to remove excess plants
5. **Irrigation:** Requires adequate moisture; no waterlogging during early growth
6. **Fertilization:** N, P, K fertilizers; green manuring
7. **Maturity:** 100–120 days (4–5 months); harvested at early pod stage (before full maturity) for best fiber
8. **Harvesting:** Plants cut near ground level by sickle
9. **Retting:** Bundles immersed in water 8–30 days
10. **Stripping/washing/drying:** Manual fiber extraction

## 4. Flax / Linen Fiber

---

### 4.1 What is Flax / What is Linen?

- **Flax fiber** is a natural bast fiber extracted from the stem of the flax plant (*Linum usitatissimum*). It is one of the oldest textile fibers known to humanity (> 30,000 years of use).
- **Linen** is the name given to the textile fabric or yarn made from flax fiber. So: **Flax = fiber; Linen = fabric/yarn from flax.**

**Major flax-producing countries:** France (largest), Belgium, Netherlands, Russia, China, Egypt, Belarus, Ukraine, Poland.

---

### 4.2 Morphological / Microstructure of Flax Fiber

```
CROSS-SECTION of flax ultimate cell:
┌───────────────────────────────┐
│  Primary Wall (pectin + cellulose)   │
├───────────────────────────────┤
│  Secondary Wall               │
│  (highly crystalline cellulose;│
│   fibrils ~6° to fiber axis)   │  ← very low winding angle → high strength
├───────────────────────────────┤
│  Lumen (very narrow; oval/     │
│         polygonal shape)       │
└───────────────────────────────┘

Polygonal cross-section with 5–6 sides (pentagonal/hexagonal)
Thick cell wall; very narrow lumen → strong fiber

LONGITUDINAL VIEW:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
 Nodes (joints) seen at intervals
 No convolutions (unlike cotton)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
```

**Key structural features:**
- Polygonal cross-section
- Very narrow lumen
- Thick secondary wall with nearly parallel fibrils (low angle → very high strength)
- Transverse markings (nodes) visible under microscope
- Ultimate cells held together by pectin in fiber bundles

---

### 4.3 Chemical Composition of Flax Fiber

| Component | Percentage (%) |
|---|---|
| Cellulose | 70–80 |
| Hemicellulose | 14–20 |
| Lignin | 2–3 |
| Pectin | 1.8–2.3 |
| Wax / Fat | 1.2–1.7 |
| Water-soluble matter | 3–5 |
| Moisture | ~10–12 |

> Flax has much **less lignin than jute** (~2–3% vs ~12–15%), which is why linen is softer and more pliable than jute.

### 4.4 Physical Properties of Flax Fiber

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Length (ultimate cell) | 17–30 mm |
| Length (technical fiber/bundle) | 30–100 cm |
| Fineness | 12–17 micron |
| Tenacity | 5.5–6.5 g/denier (one of the strongest natural fibers) |
| Elongation at break | 1.5–3.3% (very low — rigid) |
| Elastic recovery | Poor (~65% at 2% extension) |
| Moisture Regain | 12% |
| Density | 1.50 g/cm³ |
| Color | Light gray to yellowish |
| Luster | High natural luster (silky appearance) |
| Feel | Smooth, cool to touch (good thermal conductivity) |

---

### 4.5 Chemical Properties of Flax Fiber

1. **Acids:** Hydrolyzed by acids (cellulosic fiber); more resistant than jute due to higher cellulose content. Concentrated H₂SO₄ dissolves flax; dilute acids weaken it.
2. **Alkalis:** Good resistance; NaOH mercerizes flax (improves luster and dye uptake). Strong alkali at high temperature may degrade.
3. **Oxidizing agents:** Resistant to dilute bleach; strong oxidizers damage cellulose.
4. **Sunlight:** Moderate resistance; prolonged UV exposure causes degradation.
5. **Heat:** Good heat resistance; can be ironed at high temperature (linen is ironed at 230°C) — characteristic distinguishing feature.
6. **Micro-organisms:** Susceptible to mildew (like all cellulosics).
7. **Wet strength:** Increases by ~20% when wet (like cotton).

---

### 4.6 Thermal Characteristics of Flax

- Flax withstands **high ironing temperatures** (230°C); recommended iron setting = "linen" (highest).
- Does not melt (unlike synthetics).
- Decomposes at > 200°C (prolonged exposure).
- Burns readily with flame; leaves fluffy ash; smells of paper.
- Good thermal conductor → feels cool to touch → ideal for summer clothing.

---

### 4.7 Retting of Flax Fiber

Retting is the microbial decomposition of pectin that binds flax fiber bundles to the stem, releasing the fibers.

#### Types of Retting:
| Type | Method | Duration | Quality |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Dew retting** | Spread stalks on grass fields; dew + fungi (Cladosporium) act | 4–6 weeks | Best quality linen; golden color |
| **Water retting** (river/pond) | Submerged in slow-flowing water; bacteria act | 10–14 days | Good quality |
| **Tank retting** | Concrete tanks with warm water (20–35°C) | 5–7 days | Controlled |
| **Chemical retting** | Boiling with NaOH or Na₂CO₃ | Hours | Fast; some fiber damage |
| **Steam retting** | Steam pressure + chemicals | Minutes–hours | Industrial |
| **Enzymatic retting** | Pectinase enzymes | Fastest; most eco-friendly | Highest quality |

---

### 4.8 Flax Production Process

```
1. SOWING: Spring (Feb–April); broadcast or drill seeding; 115–130 kg seed/ha
         ↓
2. GROWTH: ~90–100 days to maturity; thin stems = finer fiber (dense sowing)
         ↓
3. HARVESTING: Pulled by roots (not cut) to preserve full length; when lower 1/3 of stem is yellow
         ↓
4. RIPPLING: Seed pods removed by passing through coarse metal comb
         ↓
5. RETTING: (see above — dew, water, or chemical retting)
         ↓
6. DRYING: Dried in fields or kilns (moisture reduced to ~10%)
         ↓
7. BREAKING/SCUTCHING: Wooden rollers crush woody core (shives); paddle removes shives
         ↓
8. HACKLING (COMBING): Fiber drawn through sets of metal combs to:
         - Remove short fibers (tow)       → Tow = short, tangled flax fibers
         - Align long fibers (line)         → Line = long, parallel aligned fibers
         ↓
9. SPINNING: Line flax → wet spinning (fine linen yarn)
             Tow flax → dry spinning (coarser yarn)
```

**"Line" vs "Tow":**
- **Line:** Long, parallel, combed flax fibers (premium; fine linen)
- **Tow:** Short, tangled fibers removed during hackling (coarser; used for canvas, rope)

---

### 4.9 Cottonization of Flax Fiber

**Cottonization** is the process of converting flax fiber into short fibers of cotton-like length (15–50 mm) so they can be processed on **cotton spinning machinery**.

**Methods:**
1. **Mechanical cottonization:** Cutting, carding, and opening on modified machinery to achieve short-staple bundles.
2. **Chemical cottonization:** Treatment with steam, NaOH, or enzymes to separate fiber bundles into individual ultimate cells (lignin and pectin removal).
3. **Enzymatic cottonization:** Using cellulase, pectinase, or xylanase enzymes — eco-friendly and gentle.

**Purpose:**
- Utilize cotton machinery infrastructure
- Produce blended cotton-linen yarns
- Improve softness of flax products
- Reduce processing cost

---

### 4.10 End Uses / Defects of Flax Fiber

**End Uses:**
- Apparel: summer dresses, shirts, suits, handkerchiefs
- Home textiles: bed sheets, tablecloths, towels, kitchen towels
- Industrial: canvas, sailcloth, fire hose, tarpaulins
- Paper: currency notes, cigarette paper, bibles (thin, high-quality paper)
- Technical: composite materials (flax-reinforced polymers)

**Defects of Flax:**
1. **Barré** — uneven retting causing uneven fiber separation
2. **Tangled/knotty fiber** — poor hackling
3. **Short fiber (tow excess)** — poor quality retting/scutching
4. **Discoloration** — improper retting or drying
5. **Brittleness** — over-retting (fiber weakened by excessive microbial action)
6. **Shive contamination** — woody core particles not removed during scutching

## 5. Wool Fiber

---

### 5.1 What is Wool Fiber?

**Wool** is the natural protein fiber obtained from the fleece (coat) of domesticated sheep (*Ovis aries*). It is a **keratin protein fiber** composed of amino acid chains. Wool is the most important animal fiber in terms of volume and variety of uses.

**Wool-producing countries (major):** Australia (largest), China, New Zealand, Argentina, South Africa, UK, Russia, India, Uruguay.

---

### 5.2 Morphological Structure of Wool Fiber

```
CROSS-SECTION of wool fiber:
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│           CUTICLE (outer layer)             │
│  Scale-like cells overlapping like roof     │
│  tiles; pointing toward tip of fiber        │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│              CORTEX (main body)             │
│  ┌────────────────┬──────────────────────┐  │
│  │  Ortho-cortex  │  Para-cortex         │  │
│  │ (more reactive;│ (less reactive;      │  │
│  │  helix spring) │  straight)           │  │
│  └────────────────┴──────────────────────┘  │
│  Bilateral arrangement → natural crimp       │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│           MEDULLA (if present)              │
│  Hollow core; not in fine wools             │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────┘

SURFACE (longitudinal view):
▲▲▲▲▲▲▲▲▲▲▲▲▲▲▲▲▲▲▲▲▲▲▲▲▲▲
  Scale structure (overlapping)
  ← responsible for felting →
  Scale frequency: 6–9/mm (fine wool), 3–5/mm (coarse)
```

**Key features:**
- **Scales (cuticle):** Overlapping like fish scales; cause **felting** due to directional friction effect (DFE); scale edge points toward tip.
- **Bilateral cortex:** Two halves (ortho + para) respond differently to moisture → creates natural **crimp/curl**.
- **Crimp:** Natural waviness; fine wool has higher crimp frequency (9+ per cm); coarse wool has less.
- **Medulla:** Present in coarser/hair fibers; absent in fine Merino.

---

### 5.3 Chemical Composition of Wool Fiber

| Component | Percentage (%) |
|---|---|
| Protein (Keratin) | ~97 |
| Lipids (wax/fat) | ~1–2 |
| Ash (minerals, sulfur) | ~0.5–1.0 |
| Water | ~10–17 |

**Wool fiber is composed of KERATIN**, a complex protein formed from 20 different amino acids. Key amino acids:
1. Glycine (Gly)
2. Alanine (Ala)
3. Valine (Val)
4. Leucine (Leu)
5. Serine (Ser)
6. **Cystine** (Cys–Cys) ← most important; forms disulfide (–S–S–) bonds — responsible for elasticity and set
7. Lysine (Lys)
8. Glutamic acid (Glu)
9. Arginine (Arg)
10. Tyrosine (Tyr)

**Chemical structure of keratin:**
- Polypeptide chains in **α-helix** configuration held together by:
  1. **Disulfide bonds (–S–S–):** Cystine cross-links; main stabilizer; broken by permanent waving chemicals
  2. **Hydrogen bonds:** Maintain helix; broken by water (hence wool stretches when wet)
  3. **Salt (ionic) bonds:** –NH₃⁺ ... OOC–; broken by acids or bases
  4. **Hydrophobic interactions:** Van der Waals forces between non-polar groups

---

### 5.4 Chemical Properties of Wool Fiber

1. **Effect of acids:** Wool is more resistant to acids than alkalis. Dilute cold acids are tolerated well. Concentrated H₂SO₄ dissolves wool. **Carbonizing** uses this property: acid (H₂SO₄) destroys vegetable matter (cellulose) without harming wool.
2. **Effect of alkalis:** Wool is highly sensitive to alkalis. Dilute NaOH swells wool; concentrated solutions or hot dilute solutions dissolve it (peptide bond hydrolysis). Even mild soap (if alkaline) can damage wool.
3. **Oxidizing agents:** Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) used for careful bleaching; chlorine (Cl₂) modifies scales (basis of machine-washable Superwash wool).
4. **Reducing agents:** NH₃, thioglycolate → breaks disulfide bonds; used in permanent waving and relaxing.
5. **Heat:** Wool scorches at 130°C; chars above 175°C; burns with difficulty, self-extinguishes. Burns with odor of burning hair; leaves crushable black ash.
6. **Water:** Wool is hygroscopic; MR = 17% (highest of all natural fibers). Wet strength decreases (~30%). Wool felts when agitated wet.
7. **Sunlight:** Photodegradation; yellowing and strength loss due to destruction of tryptophan amino acid by UV.

### 5.6 Wool Production Process (Shearing to Finished Fiber)

```
SHEEP REARING
     ↓
SHEARING (clipping fleece with electric shears or blade; once yearly; Spring)
     ↓
WOOL CLASSING / SORTING (skirting: removing stained edges; fleece graded by class)
     ↓
SCOURING (washing with hot detergent solution to remove lanolin/wool grease, 
          suint (dried sweat), and vegetable matter; 50–60°C; 
          removes ~40–60% of raw wool weight = yolk/grease)
     ↓
CARBONIZING (if vegetable matter remains: treated with H₂SO₄ 5–7%; 
             dried; baked at 110°C; vegetable matter carbonizes and crumbles;
             neutralized with Na₂CO₃ or NH₃)
     ↓
RINSING / DRYING
     ↓
DYEING (if required at fiber stage = stock dyeing)
     ↓
OILING (adding spinning lubricant)
     ↓
CARDING (disentangling fibers; forms sliver for woollen system, OR
          → COMBING for worsted system: removes short fibers = noils; aligns long fibers)
     ↓
SPINNING → WOOLLEN YARN (from carded web) or WORSTED YARN (from combed sliver)
```

---

### 5.7 Definitions: Shearing, Sorting, Grading, Scouring, Carbonizing, Garnetting

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Shearing** | The act of cutting the fleece from a living sheep using electric or mechanical clippers; done once per year in spring |
| **Sorting** | Dividing a single fleece into different quality sections based on fineness and length (done within one fleece; more detailed than classing) |
| **Grading / Classing** | Grouping fleeces of similar quality together for commercial sale; based on fiber diameter, length, color, vegetable matter content |
| **Scouring** | Washing raw wool to remove grease (lanolin), suint, dirt, and vegetable matter using hot water and detergent (NaOH, Na₂CO₃, or biodegradable detergents) |
| **Carbonizing** | Treating wool with dilute sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) to destroy residual vegetable matter (burrs, seeds, leaf) without damaging the wool protein; fiber is baked, then crushed, neutralized |
| **Garnetting** | Mechanical process of tearing apart old woolen fabrics, rags, or waste into individual fibers for reuse (recycled wool = "shoddy" or "mungo") |
| **Felting** | Irreversible entanglement of wool fibers due to mechanical agitation in hot, moist conditions; caused by scale structure + crimp; exploited in felt fabric making |

### 5.9 Carbonizing Process (detailed)

1. Pre-treat scouring if needed
2. Impregnate wool in 5–7% H₂SO₄ solution (cold)
3. Squeeze out excess acid
4. **Dry** at 60–70°C (to concentrate acid)
5. **Bake** at 100–120°C for 3–5 minutes (cellulose/lignin in plant material is hydrolyzed and carbonized; forms brittle, friable material)
6. **Crush** the charred vegetable matter through crushing rollers
7. **Dust** off carbonized particles
8. **Neutralize** with Na₂CO₃ or ammonia
9. Wash and dry — clean wool ready for processing

---

### 5.10 Felting of Wool

Felting occurs due to:
1. **Scale structure (cuticle):** Scales point toward the fiber tip; when fibers rub against each other, scales interlock — **directional friction effect (DFE):** friction is greater in the "against scale" (root-to-tip) direction than "with scale."
2. **Crimp:** Natural waviness increases fiber entanglement.
3. **Conditions:** Heat + moisture + mechanical agitation → scales open, interlock, and fiber migration is irreversible.

**Applications of felting:** Felt hats, felt fabrics, piano hammer felts, felted slippers.
**Anti-felting treatments:** Chlorine-Hercosett treatment (destroys scales); polymer resin coating; enzyme treatment (subtilisin — degrades scale protein).

## 6. Silk Fiber

---

### 6.1 Silk — Overview / "The Queen of All Fibers"

Silk is a natural continuous protein filament produced by the **silkworm** (*Bombyx mori*) during its cocoon-spinning stage. It is called the **"Queen of Textile Fibers"** because:
1. **Lustrous appearance** — brilliant sheen due to triangular prism-like cross-section that refracts light
2. **Finest natural filament** — 9–11 μm diameter; continuous length of ~600–900 m per cocoon
3. **Unique handle** — soft, smooth, cool, pleasant "scroop" sound on rubbing
4. **High strength** — one of the strongest natural fibers (tenacity ~4–5 g/den)
5. **Excellent dyeability** — vibrant colors due to protein fiber
6. **Comfort** — excellent moisture management; warm in winter, cool in summer
7. **Historical significance** — prized luxury fabric for millennia (Silk Road trade route)

---

### 6.2 Chemical Composition of Silk

Silk fiber has two components:
1. **Fibroin** (~75–80%): The actual fiber protein; forms the silk filament; insoluble in water; β-pleated sheet structure; composed of glycine (~44%), alanine (~28%), serine (~12%), and other amino acids.
2. **Sericin** (~20–25%): Gum protein that holds twin filaments of fibroin together; soluble in hot water — removed by degumming; less valuable.
3. **Wax / fat** (~0.5–1.5%)
4. **Ash / minerals** (~1.0%)
5. **Water** (~10%)

| Component | % |
|---|---|
| Fibroin | 72–81 |
| Sericin | 19–28 |
| Fat/wax | 0.5–1.5 |
| Ash | ~1.0 |
| Water | ~10 |

---

### 6.3 Sericulture (Silk Cultivation and Manufacturing)

**Sericulture** is the practice of rearing silkworms (*Bombyx mori*) for the purpose of producing raw silk.

```
SERICULTURE FLOW CHART:

1. MULBERRY CULTIVATION
   Planting and maintaining Mulberry (Morus alba) trees
   → Leaves are the SOLE food of Bombyx mori larvae
         ↓
2. SILKWORM REARING (Sericultural stage)
   Eggs (laid by moth) → Incubated at 25°C, 80% RH
         ↓
3. LARVAL STAGES (Instars I–V)
   Larvae hatch → Feed on mulberry leaves
   5 instars separated by 4 molts
   Duration of larval stage: ~26–28 days
   ↓ (at end of 5th instar)
4. SPINNING / COCOONING
   Mature larva spins cocoon around itself
   Secretes fibroin from 2 spinneret glands + sericin from sericterium
   Cocoon spun in figure-8 motion; ~1500 m total filament
   Duration: 3–4 days
         ↓
5. STIFLING (killing pupa)
   Cocoons placed in hot air (80–100°C) or steam to kill pupa
   (Must be done before adult moth emerges — would cut silk threads)
         ↓
6. SORTING / GRADING of cocoons
         ↓
7. REELING
   Cocoons softened in hot water (90–95°C)
   Filament end found; 3–8 cocoons' filaments combined/twisted
   Reeled onto reel as "raw silk" (grège)
   Single reeled filament too thin → 3–8 cocoon filaments combine = 1 thread
         ↓
8. THROWING (Twisting)
   Several reeled threads twisted together for strength → "thrown silk"
   Types: Organzine (for warp), Tram (for weft)
         ↓
9. DEGUMMING
   Remove sericin by boiling in soap/water solution (98–100°C, 1–2 hrs)
   Weight loss: 20–25%; fiber becomes softer and more lustrous → "spun silk" softness
         ↓
10. DYEING / WEAVING → FINISHED SILK FABRIC
```

---

### 6.4 Degumming Process of Silk

**Degumming** is the removal of the sericin gum from raw silk to reveal the soft, lustrous fibroin underneath.

**Methods:**
| Method | Process |
|---|---|
| **Soap degumming** | Boiling in 0.5–1.5% Marseille soap + Na₂CO₃ at 90–98°C for 1–2 hrs; most common |
| **Alkali degumming** | Na₂CO₃ solution (milder); less damaging to fiber |
| **Acid degumming** | Dilute HCl or H₂SO₄; hydrolyzes sericin; faster but risky |
| **Enzymatic degumming** | Protease enzymes; eco-friendly; gentle; precise control |

**After degumming:** Weight loss 20–25%; fiber becomes softer, more lustrous, and more flexible. 

> **Weighted silk:** After degumming, silk may be treated with metal salts (tin salts) to restore lost weight — but this weakens the fiber and is largely discontinued.

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Length (single cocoon) | 600–1500 m (continuous filament) |
| Fineness | 9–11 μm |
| Tenacity (dry) | 4.0–5.0 g/denier |
| Tenacity (wet) | ~20% weaker (opposite to cotton) |
| Elongation at break | 10–25% |

| Luster | Brilliant, silky sheen |

---

### 6.6 Chemical Properties of Silk Fiber

1. **Acids:** More resistant than wool; conc. HCl/H₂SO₄ dissolves silk.
2. **Alkalis:** Sensitive to alkalis; NaOH damages fibroin; silk should be washed with neutral pH.
3. **Oxidizing agents:** H₂O₂ used for bleaching (carefully at low temperature).
4. **Enzymes:** Protease enzymes degrade silk fibroin (silk pepsin = sericin degradation).
5. **Heat:** Yellows above 80°C; decomposes above 170°C; no melting; burns like wool (hair odor, crushable ash).
6. **Water:** Wet strength decreases by ~20% (unlike cotton which gains); good moisture regain.
7. **Sunlight:** Damaged by UV light more than wool; loses strength and yellows.
8. **Perspiration (sweat):** Damages silk (both acid sweat and alkaline deodorants); never allow prolonged contact.

---

### 6.7 End Uses of Silk

**Apparel:** Sarees, scarves, ties, evening wear, lingerie, wedding dresses, kimono
**Home textiles:** Cushion covers, curtains, lampshades, bed covers
**Technical textiles:** Sutures for surgery (silk is biocompatible), parachute cord (historically), bicycle tires (historically)
**Industrial:** Insulation for electrical equipment (historically)

### 6.9 Bombyx mori — Short Note

*Bombyx mori* is the domesticated silkworm moth; it has been so thoroughly domesticated over 5,000 years that it cannot survive in the wild. Key characteristics:
- **Only food source:** Mulberry leaves (*Morus alba*) — hence "mulberry silk"
- **Life cycle (holometabolous):** Egg → Larva (5 instars) → Pupa (in cocoon) → Adult moth
- **Total life cycle:** ~35–45 days at 25°C
- **Silk production:** 1 cocoon ≈ 600–1500 m filament; raw silk from 3000–5000 cocoons = 500 g silk
- Does not fly (wings vestigial); does not eat as adult (mouthparts degenerate)

### 7.1 Wool vs Silk Fiber

| Property | Wool | Silk |
|---|---|---|
| Source | Sheep (animal hair) | Silkworm cocoon (insect filament) |
| Protein type | Keratin (α-helix) | Fibroin (β-sheet) |
| Cross-section | Roughly circular (with medulla sometimes) | Triangular prism |
| Length | Staple (5–35 cm) | Filament (600–1500 m per cocoon) |
| Luster | Low to moderate; dull | Brilliant, silky |
| Elasticity | Excellent (best natural fiber elasticity) | Good but less than wool |
| Strength (dry) | 1.5–2.5 g/den (weaker) | 4.0–5.0 g/den (stronger) |
| Wet strength | Decreases ~30% | Decreases ~20% |
| Moisture Regain | 17% | 11% |
| Chemical resistance | Sensitive to alkalis; good in acids | Sensitive to both acids and alkalis |
| Felting | Yes (scale structure) | No felting |
| Flammability | Self-extinguishes; smells of burning hair | Self-extinguishes; smells of burning hair |
| Feel | Soft, warm, sometimes scratchy | Smooth, cool, luxurious |
| Cost | Moderate | High (luxury) |

**Which is more elastic? → WOOL.** Wool has superior elasticity (~99% recovery at 2% extension) due to the coiled α-helix keratin structure and disulfide cross-links. Silk (β-sheet structure) is less elastic.

## 8. Minor / Miscellaneous Fibers

---

### 8.1 Hemp Fiber

**Source:** Bast fiber from *Cannabis sativa* (industrial hemp, with < 0.3% THC)
**Origin:** Central Asia; major producers: China (largest), France, Canada, Ukraine

**Physical Structure:**
- Ultimate cell: 5–55 mm long; 10–30 μm diameter
- Polygonal cross-section; similar to jute but better quality
- Nodes (dislocations) visible under microscope

**Physical Properties:**
| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Tenacity | 5.0–6.5 g/denier (stronger than jute) |
| Elongation | 1–2% |
| Moisture Regain | 12% |
| Density | 1.48 g/cm³ |
| Color | Yellowish-gray to green |

**Chemical Composition:**
- Cellulose: 70–74%, Hemicellulose: 18–22%, Lignin: 3–5%, Pectin: ~1%, Wax: ~0.8%

**End Uses:**
- Ropes, cordage, twine
- Canvas, sailcloth, tarpaulins
- Paper (specialty)
- Geotextiles
- Composite materials (hempcrete, biocomposites)
- Eco-friendly packaging
- Blended yarn with cotton/polyester for fashion

---

### 8.2 Ramie Fiber

**Source:** Bast fiber from *Boehmeria nivea* (China grass) or *Boehmeria tenacissima*; also called "China grass" or "grass linen"

**Production stages:**
1. Harvesting (3–6 times per year — perennial plant)
2. **Decortication:** Mechanical removal of bark from stems
3. **Degumming (retting):** Chemical (NaOH) or biological removal of gum, pectin, and woody matter
4. **Washing, drying, hackling**

**Properties:**
| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Tenacity | 6–7 g/denier — **STRONGEST natural cellulosic fiber** |
| Elongation | 3.5% |
| Moisture Regain | 12–13% |
| Luster | High; lustrous |
| Cross-section | Irregular; kidney-shaped lumen |
| Stiffness | Very high; least elastic natural fiber |

**Key Facts:**
- Strongest natural textile fiber (wet or dry)
- Poor elasticity; breaks if bent sharply
- Resistant to mildew and bacteria (naturally)
- Difficult to spin due to brittleness and short ultimate cell length

**End Uses:**
- Blended with cotton, wool, or polyester for clothing
- Industrial use: fire hoses, canvas, thread
- Fishing nets (high wet strength)
- Specialty paper (currency notes)

---

### 8.3 Coir Fiber

**Source:** Fibrous material from the husk (mesocarp) of the coconut fruit (*Cocos nucifera*)

**Types:**
1. **Brown coir:** From mature, ripe coconut husks; stronger and coarser
2. **White coir:** From immature (green) husks; finer, spun into yarn

**Extraction:**
1. Husks soaked in fresh or seawater for 6–12 months (**retting**)
2. Pound or machine-beat retted husks to separate fibers from pith
3. Wash, dry, and process

**Properties:**
| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Length | 10–30 cm (brown); 5–10 cm (white) |
| Tenacity | 1.0–1.5 g/denier (weakest natural fiber) |
| Elongation | 15–25% (highest among natural fibers) |
| Moisture Regain | 13.5% |
| Density | 1.40 g/cm³ |
| Color | Golden-brown |

**Chemical Composition:**
- Cellulose: 36–43%, Lignin: 41–45% (very high — most lignified natural fiber), Hemicellulose: 0.25%, Pectin: 3–4%

> Coir has the **highest lignin content** of any commercial natural fiber — makes it very resistant to biodegradation and sea water.

**End Uses:**
- Doormats, floor matting
- Ropes, brushes, brooms
- Geotextile/erosion control nets
- Upholstery stuffing, mattress padding
- Horticulture (coir pots, growing medium)

---

### 8.4 Sisal Fiber

**Source:** Leaf fiber from *Agave sisalana* plant; native to Mexico; major producers: Brazil, Tanzania, Kenya, China

**Extraction:**
1. Leaves harvested (3–4 feet long)
2. **Decortication:** Leaves crushed and scraped (mechanically) to remove pulp
3. Fibers washed, dried, and brushed

**Properties:**
| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Length | 60–150 cm |
| Tenacity | 3.0–5.0 g/denier |
| Elongation | 2–3% |
| Moisture Regain | 11% |
| Color | Cream to pale yellow |
| Luster | Good |

**End Uses:**
- Agricultural twine, binder twine
- Ropes, cables, cordage
- Carpet and mat backing
- Geotextiles
- Composite reinforcement
- Dartboards (sisal fibers are self-healing)

---

### 8.5 Banana Fiber

**Source:** Pseudo-stem (trunk) of the banana plant (*Musa* spp.); extracted from leaf sheaths; by-product of banana fruit industry

**Fiber Extraction Techniques:**
1. **Manual extraction:** Hand-scraping the outer sheath of the banana pseudo-stem
2. **Mechanical extraction (Decortication):** Spindle-driven machines or drum scrapers crush the sheaths and scrape away the pulp to leave fibers; washed and sun-dried
3. **Chemical retting:** NaOH solution treatment to remove non-fibrous material (lignin, hemicellulose) followed by washing
4. **Enzymatic extraction:** Pectinase/cellulase enzymes — eco-friendly method

**Properties:**
| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Length | 45–225 cm |
| Tenacity | 3.0–5.5 g/denier |
| Elongation | 1–3.5% |
| Moisture Regain | ~8.5% |
| Density | 1.35 g/cm³ |
| Color | Creamy white to light brown |

**End Uses:**
- Ropes, mats, bags
- Eco-fashion textiles (blended yarn)
- Specialty paper (Japanese washi from Musa textile)
- Composite materials (biocomposites)
- Geotextiles

---

### 8.6 Pineapple Fiber (PALF — Pineapple Leaf Fiber)

**Source:** Leaves of the pineapple plant (*Ananas comosus*); by-product of pineapple fruit cultivation; major producers: Philippines, India, Thailand

**Advantages of PALF:**
1. High cellulose content (70–82%) → good tensile strength
2. Fine, soft, silky texture
3. High moisture absorption
4. Eco-friendly — uses agricultural waste
5. Biodegradable
6. Can be blended with cotton, silk, polyester
7. Traditional Philippine textile: **Piña cloth** (luxury fabric)

**Disadvantages of PALF:**
1. Low productivity (extraction is labor-intensive)
2. Limited availability (seasonal, regional)
3. Poor elongation; brittle
4. Inconsistent fiber properties batch-to-batch
5. Poor durability if not treated; susceptible to mildew
6. Higher processing cost compared to conventional fibers

---

### 8.7 Asbestos Fiber

**Source:** Naturally occurring **mineral fiber**; the only naturally occurring mineral textile fiber. Found in metamorphic rocks.

**Types of Asbestos:**
1. **Serpentine group:**
   - **Chrysotile (White asbestos)** — most common (90%+ of use); *Mg₃(Si₂O₅)(OH)₄*; curly/wavy fibers; less hazardous than amphiboles
2. **Amphibole group:**
   - **Crocidolite (Blue asbestos)** — most hazardous; *Na₂(Fe²⁺₃Fe³⁺₂)(Si₈O₂₂)(OH)₂*
   - **Amosite (Brown/Gray asbestos)** — *Mg-Fe silicate*
   - **Anthophyllite** — *Mg silicate*
   - **Tremolite**
   - **Actinolite**

**Key Properties:**
- **Incombustible / Non-flammable** — withstands temperatures > 1000°C
- High tensile strength
- Poor electrical and thermal conductivity
- Acid-resistant (chrysotile less so than amphiboles)
- Flexible enough to be spun (chrysotile only)

**End Uses of Asbestos:**
1. **Fireproof fabrics:** Fire blankets, protective clothing for firefighters
2. **Insulation:** Heat and electrical insulation in buildings and ships
3. **Brake linings and friction materials**
4. **Asbestos cement:** Roofing sheets, pipes
5. **Gaskets and packing** in industrial machinery
6. **Theater curtains** (fire-resistant)
7. **Boiler lagging** and furnace linings

> ⚠️ **HEALTH WARNING:** All types of asbestos are now classified as **Group 1 carcinogens** (IARC). Inhalation of asbestos fibers causes **mesothelioma** (lung cancer), **asbestosis**, and lung cancer. Asbestos is banned in most countries. It is only discussed here for academic purposes.

### 8.8 Kapok Fiber

**Source:** Seed fiber from the Kapok tree (*Ceiba pentandra*); grows in tropical regions (Indonesia, Philippines, West Africa, Bangladesh)

**Properties:**
| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Length | 10–30 mm |
| Diameter | 20–40 μm |
| Density | 0.35 g/cm³ (lightest textile fiber) |
| Luster | Silky |
| Color | Yellow/brown |
| Hollow core | ~80% hollow (very high — excellent insulation) |

**Key characteristics:**
- Cannot be spun easily due to very short length, extremely smooth surface (no cohesiveness), and fragile nature
- Highly **buoyant** (hollow cells trap air)
- Excellent **thermal and acoustic insulation**
- Water-resistant (waxy coating)

**End Uses:**
- Life jackets, life buoys (buoyancy)
- Pillow and cushion stuffing
- Thermal insulation (sleeping bags, quilts)
- Acoustic insulation panels
- Oil-spill cleanup (oleophilic and hydrophobic)

### 8.9 Defining Key Terms Summary Table

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Sericulture** | Science and practice of rearing silkworms to produce raw silk |
| **Reeling** | Unwinding continuous silk filament from the cocoon by softening in hot water and winding onto reels |
| **Throwing** | Twisting and plying of reeled silk threads to form yarn suitable for weaving |
| **Degumming** | Removing sericin (gum) from raw silk to reveal soft, lustrous fibroin |
| **Stifling** | Killing of the pupa inside the cocoon (by heat or gas) before adult moth emerges and cuts the silk thread |
| **Cottonization** | Converting bast fibers (flax, hemp, ramie) into short-staple form for processing on cotton machinery |
| **Retting** | Microbial decomposition of pectic substances binding bast fiber bundles to stem, releasing the fibers |
| **Scutching** | Mechanical beating to remove woody shives from retted flax/hemp stalks |
| **Hackling** | Drawing flax/hemp fibers through metal-toothed combs to separate long (line) from short (tow) fibers |
| **Ginning** | Mechanical separation of cotton lint from cottonseed |
| **Lint** | Long cotton fibers attached to the cottoneed; used for textile manufacture |
| **Linters** | Very short fibers remaining on cottonseeds after ginning; used for industrial cellulose products |
| **Jutton** | Blended yarn/fabric of jute and cotton |
| **Mercerization** | Treatment of cotton with cold concentrated NaOH (18–25°); increases luster, strength, and dye uptake |
| **Lignin** | Complex aromatic polymer in plant cell walls; acts as cement; responsible for stiffness in jute |
| **Hemicellulose** | Non-cellulosic polysaccharide in plant cell walls; branched, amorphous; filler |
| **Keratin** | Structural protein of wool, hair, and nails; α-helix secondary structure; rich in cystine |
| **Fibroin** | Structural protein of silk fiber; β-pleated sheet; very high glycine/alanine content |
| **Sericin** | Gum protein surrounding fibroin in raw silk; removed by degumming |
| **Linen** | Textile fabric/yarn produced from flax fiber |
| **Line (flax)** | Long, parallel, aligned flax fibers after hackling — premium quality |
| **Tow (flax)** | Short, tangled fibers removed during hackling — lower quality |
| **MR** | Moisture Regain — moisture expressed as % of dry weight |
| **MC** | Moisture Content — moisture expressed as % of wet weight |
| **Organic cotton** | Cotton grown without synthetic pesticides/fertilizers; GMO-free; certified |

```

Stifling (heat kills pupa) → Reeling (hot water, 90°C) →
```
