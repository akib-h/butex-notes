## 1. Textile & Textile Fibre Basics

### What is Textile?
A **textile** is any flexible material made by interlacing, weaving, knitting, knotting, or felting fibres. The word comes from the Latin *texere* (to weave). Textiles include fabrics used for clothing, home furnishings, industrial products, and technical applications.

---

### Define Textile Fibre
A **textile fibre** is a fine, hair-like unit of raw material that is capable of being spun into yarn or made directly into a fabric. To qualify as a textile fibre, it must:
- Be long enough to be spun or interlaced
- Be flexible and fine (not stiff or coarse)
- Have adequate tensile strength
- Possess good cohesiveness (ability to cling together)

---

### "All Fibres Are Not Textile Fibres" — Explain
Not every fibre in nature can be used for textile purposes. A fibre must meet specific criteria to be called a **textile fibre**:

| Criterion | Explanation |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Length** | Must be long enough (minimum ~15 mm) to be spun |
| **Fineness** | Must be thin enough to make soft fabric |
| **Strength** | Must withstand spinning and weaving stress |
| **Flexibility** | Must bend without breaking |
| **Cohesiveness** | Fibres must hold together during spinning |
| **Uniformity** | Consistent diameter for even yarn |

**Example:** Glass fibre exists naturally but is too brittle and lacks cohesiveness. Asbestos fibre is a mineral fibre — it can be used in limited textile applications but lacks the softness, flexibility, and dyeability of most textile fibres. Cotton, jute, silk, and wool all meet these criteria fully.

---

### Basic Requirements of a Textile Fibre
1. **Adequate length** — minimum staple length for spinning
2. **Fineness** — measured in microns or denier
3. **Strength (Tenacity)** — must resist breaking during processing
4. **Elasticity & Flexibility** — for durability in use
5. **Cohesiveness** — natural crimp or scale helps fibres stick together
6. **Chemical stability** — resistance to acids, alkalis, heat
7. **Absorbency** — for comfort and dyeability
8. **Uniformity** — consistent properties throughout

---

### Classification of Textile Fibres (with Examples)

```
TEXTILE FIBRES
│
├── NATURAL FIBRES
│   ├── Vegetable / Cellulosic (from plants)
│   │   ├── Seed fibre     → Cotton, Kapok
│   │   ├── Bast fibre     → Jute, Flax, Hemp, Ramie
│   │   ├── Leaf fibre     → Sisal, Pineapple (PALF), Abaca
│   │   └── Fruit fibre    → Coir (coconut)
│   │
│   ├── Animal / Protein
│   │   ├── Wool (sheep)
│   │   ├── Silk (silkworm)
│   │   └── Specialty hair → Cashmere, Mohair, Angora
│   │
│   └── Mineral
│       └── Asbestos
│
└── MAN-MADE / SYNTHETIC FIBRES
    ├── Regenerated cellulosic → Viscose Rayon, Modal, Lyocell
    ├── Synthetic polymers     → Polyester, Nylon, Acrylic, Polypropylene
    └── Mineral (man-made)    → Fiberglass, Carbon fibre
```

---

### Difference Between Natural and Man-Made Fibre

| Property | Natural Fibre | Man-Made Fibre |
|----------|--------------|----------------|
| Source | Plants, animals, minerals | Chemicals, polymers |
| Comfort | Highly comfortable | Often less breathable |
| Moisture absorption | Generally high | Generally lower |
| Biodegradability | Biodegradable | Mostly non-biodegradable |
| Cost | Depends on availability | Usually more consistent |
| Uniformity | Variable (natural variation) | Very uniform |
| Strength | Moderate to good | Can be engineered to be very high |
| Examples | Cotton, Jute, Wool, Silk | Polyester, Nylon, Rayon |

## 2. Cotton Fibre

### Botanical Name and Classification

| Item | Details |
|------|---------|
| **Botanical Name** | *Gossypium* spp. |
| **Family** | Malvaceae |
| **Kingdom** | Plantae |
| **Type** | Seed / Cellulosic fibre |

**Main Species:**
- *Gossypium hirsutum* — Upland cotton (~90% of world production)
- *Gossypium barbadense* — Sea Island / Egyptian cotton (long staple, high quality)
- *Gossypium arboreum* — Tree cotton (India/Pakistan)
- *Gossypium herbaceum* — Levant cotton (Africa/Asia)

---

### Cotton Growing Areas / Producing Belt
Cotton grows in tropical and subtropical regions (called the **Cotton Belt**):

- **USA:** Texas, Mississippi, California (Upland cotton)
- **China:** Xinjiang, Hebei
- **India:** Maharashtra, Gujarat, Andhra Pradesh, Punjab
- **Pakistan:** Punjab, Sindh
- **Egypt:** Nile Delta (Egyptian long-staple)
- **Brazil, Uzbekistan, Turkey, Australia** also major producers

> Cotton requires **warm climate, 200+ frost-free days, 500–750 mm rainfall** and **well-drained soil**.

---

### Chemical Composition of Cotton Fibre

| Component | Percentage |
|-----------|-----------|
| Cellulose | 88–96% |
| Moisture | 6–8% |
| Proteins | 1–1.5% |
| Wax & Fats | 0.5–1% |
| Ash (minerals) | 1–1.8% |
| Pectic substances | 0.7–1.2% |

> **Key point:** Cotton is almost **pure cellulose** — the chemical formula of cellulose is **(C₆H₁₀O₅)ₙ**

---

### Physical Properties of Cotton Fibre

| Property | Details |
|----------|---------|
| **Colour** | White to creamy white |
| **Lustre** | Dull (unless mercerized) |
| **Length** | 10–65 mm (staple) |
| **Fineness** | 12–35 microns |
| **Tenacity (dry)** | 3–5 g/denier |
| **Tenacity (wet)** | Stronger when wet (increases ~20%) |
| **Moisture regain** | 7–8.5% |
| **Elongation at break** | 3–10% |
| **Elastic recovery** | Poor — prone to wrinkling |
| **Density** | 1.52 g/cm³ |
| **Cross-section** | Kidney/bean-shaped with central lumen |
| **Longitudinal view** | Twisted ribbon (convolutions) |

---

### Chemical Properties of Cotton Fibre

| Property | Details |
|----------|---------|
| **Effect of acids** | Damaged by strong acids; dilute hot acids hydrolyse cellulose |
| **Effect of alkalis** | Resistant — strong alkali (NaOH) causes mercerization |
| **Oxidising agents** | Ozone, bleach can oxidize and weaken the fibre |
| **Reducing agents** | Generally resistant |
| **Dyeability** | Dyed with direct, vat, reactive, and sulphur dyes |
| **Heat resistance** | Can withstand up to ~150°C; decomposes above 250°C |
| **Biological attack** | Attacked by mildew and mould |
| **Sunlight** | Gradual degradation |

---

### Cross-Section / Morphological Structure of Cotton Fibre

```
Cross Section (mature cotton):
        ___________
       |           |  ← Primary Wall (outer)
       |  _______  |
       | |       | |  ← Secondary Wall (S1, S2, S3 layers of cellulose)
       | | LUMEN | |  ← Central Lumen (hollow canal - collapsed in mature fibre)
       | |_______| |
       |___________|

Longitudinal View:
  ~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~  ← Twisted ribbon (convolutions)
  Appears like a flattened, twisted tube
```

**Parts of Cotton Fibre:**
1. **Cuticle** — thin waxy outer coating
2. **Primary Wall** — cellulose network (outer layer)
3. **Secondary Wall (Winding)** — thick cellulose layer (gives strength)
4. **Lumen** — hollow central canal (contains cell sap when alive; collapses when dried)

---

### Lint and Linters

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Lint** | The long, spinnable fibres attached to the cotton seed. These are the main fibres used in textile production. Length: 20–65 mm |
| **Linters** | The very short fibres (~1–6 mm) remaining on the seed after lint has been removed by ginning. Not spinnable but used in: paper making, plastics, gun cotton, upholstery stuffing, and as a source of pure cellulose |

---

### End Uses of Cotton Fibre
- **Apparel:** T-shirts, shirts, denim, underwear, socks
- **Home textiles:** Bedsheets, towels, curtains
- **Industrial:** Canvas, tarpaulins, filter cloth
- **Medical:** Bandages, surgical cotton, gauze
- **Technical:** Tyre cord, conveyor belts (historically)
- **Linters use:** Paper, rayon, explosives (gun cotton)

---

### Faults/Defects of Cotton Fibre

| Defect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Neps** | Small tangled knots of fibre; cause fabric pilling |
| **Motes** | Immature seed fragments in the fibre |
| **Trash** | Leaf, stem, and seed coat fragments |
| **Short fibres** | Below spinnable length; weaken yarn |
| **Immature fibres** | Thin-walled fibres that don't dye properly |
| **Dead fibres** | Totally immature, no secondary wall; appear white/grey |
| **Stickiness** | Due to honeydew from insects — clogs machinery |
| **Ginning defects** | Broken fibres, excessive short fibres from harsh ginning |

## 3. Ginning

### Define Ginning
**Ginning** is the mechanical process of separating cotton lint (seed fibre) from the cotton seed and removing impurities (leaves, husks, dirt) from the raw cotton after harvesting.

---

### Classification of Ginning

```
GINNING
│
├── 1. Roller Ginning
│   ├── Foot Roller Gin (hand-operated)
│   ├── McCarthy Gin (single roller)
│   └── Double Roller (DR) Gin
│
└── 2. Saw Ginning
    ├── Single Breast Saw Gin
    └── Double Breast Saw Gin
```

---

### Objects (Objectives) of Ginning
1. To **separate lint from seed** without damaging the fibre
2. To **clean** the cotton by removing foreign matter
3. To **preserve fibre quality** (length, strength, fineness)
4. To **prepare cotton** for baling and transport
5. To **recover seeds** for oil extraction and planting

---

### Working Principle of Saw Ginning Machine (with Sketch)

**Principle:** The saw gin uses rotating circular saw blades to pull cotton lint from seeds. The seed is too large to pass through grid bars, so only fibres are pulled through.

**Main Components:**
- Huller ribs (grid bars) spaced narrower than seed
- Rotating saw cylinder with many circular saw blades
- Brush/air system to remove lint from saws
- Seed outlet

**Working:**
1. Seed cotton fed into the gin stand
2. Rotating saw blades grip fibres and pull them through narrow grid bars
3. Seeds are too large to pass — separated and fall out
4. Lint on the saws is removed by a rotating brush or air blast
5. Clean lint is delivered to the lint room

```
           FEED
            ↓
    ┌───────────────────┐
    │  Rotating Saws ⚙️  │ ← Saws pull lint through grid bars
    │  =========         │
    │  Grid/Huller Ribs  │ ← Seed can't pass through (too big)
    └───────────────────┘
         ↓         ↓
       LINT       SEEDS
    (to baler)  (to seed bin)
```

---

### Differentiate Between Saw Ginning and Roller Ginning

| Feature | Saw Ginning | Roller Ginning |
|---------|------------|---------------|
| **Mechanism** | Rotating circular saws grip fibres | Leather/rubber roller pulls fibres by friction |
| **Speed** | High (faster output) | Slow |
| **Fibre length** | More fibre breakage | Less fibre breakage; good for long-staple |
| **Suitable for** | Short-medium staple (Upland cotton) | Long-staple cotton (Egyptian, Sea Island) |
| **Nep formation** | More neps | Fewer neps |
| **Seed damage** | Seeds may be crushed | Seeds remain intact |
| **Capacity** | Higher | Lower |
| **Cost** | Higher initial cost | Lower cost |
| **Quality** | Slightly lower | Better quality lint |

## 4. Jute Fibre

### Why is Jute Called a Bast Fibre?
Jute is called a **bast fibre** because it is extracted from the **bast** (phloem/inner bark) of the jute plant stem. Bast fibres run along the length of the stem between the outer bark and the woody core (xylem). Other bast fibres include flax, hemp, and ramie.

### Define Bast Fibre
**Bast fibre** is a plant fibre collected from the phloem (the "bast") or skin of the plant. These fibres support the plant's vascular system and are extracted by a process called **retting** (microbial decomposition of pectin binding the fibres to the stem).

---

### Botanical Name and Classification of Jute

| Item | Details |
|------|---------|
| **Botanical Name** | *Corchorus capsularis* (White jute) and *Corchorus olitorius* (Tossa jute) |
| **Family** | Malvaceae (Tiliaceae) |
| **Type** | Bast / Cellulosic fibre |
| **Classification** | Natural → Vegetable → Bast fibre |

---

### Chemical Composition of Jute Fibre

| Component | Percentage |
|-----------|-----------|
| Cellulose | 58–72% |
| Hemicellulose | 13–21% |
| Lignin | 12–15% |
| Pectin | 0.2–0.5% |
| Water-soluble matter | 1.5% |
| Fats & Wax | 0.5% |
| Moisture | 12–14% |

> **Important:** Jute contains **lignin** which makes it stiffer and less flexible than cotton. The high lignin content also makes it less durable and photo-degradable.

---

### Physical Properties of Jute Fibre

| Property | Details |
|----------|---------|
| **Colour** | Golden to brown (white jute) or reddish-brown (tossa) |
| **Lustre** | Silky, bright lustre |
| **Length** | 1.5–3.5 metres (plant); technical fibre: 150–300 cm |
| **Fineness** | 1.5–2 denier |
| **Tenacity** | 3–4.5 g/denier |
| **Moisture regain** | 13.75% |
| **Elongation** | 1.7% |
| **Density** | 1.48 g/cm³ |
| **Stiffness** | High (due to lignin) |
| **Cross-section** | Polygonal cells with central lumen |

---

### Jute Retting Process
**Retting** is the process of separating jute fibres from the stem using microbial action.

**Steps:**
1. Harvested jute stalks are **bundled** and submerged in slow-moving water (rivers, ponds)
2. Water temperature should be **29–32°C**
3. **Bacteria and fungi** break down pectin and other binding materials
4. Process takes **10–30 days** depending on water temperature
5. Fibres are then **stripped** from the stem by hand
6. Fibres are **washed**, dried, and graded

**Types of Retting:**
- **Water retting** — submersion in water (most common)
- **Dew retting** — exposure to dew and rain (less common)
- **Chemical retting** — using alkalis to speed up process

---

### Classification of Jute Fibre
**By Species:**
- White jute (*C. capsularis*) — grown in flooded areas
- Tossa jute (*C. olitorius*) — superior quality, softer

**By Grade (Kutcha Grading):** Based on strength, colour, root content, defects:
- Top Crossing, Middle Crossing, Bottom Crossing

---

### Kutcha Grading System of Jute
**Kutcha grading** is done at the field level by traders. Fibres are graded by:
1. **Colour** — bright golden preferred
2. **Strength** — pulled by hand to test
3. **Fibre length** — longer is better
4. **Root content** — less root = higher grade
5. **Cleanliness** — absence of defects

**Kutcha Grades (from best to worst):**
- **W/Special, W/Fine, W/Good** (White jute grades)
- **TD-5, TD-4, TD-3** (Tossa jute grades)

---

### Pucca Grading System of Jute
**Pucca grading** is the standardised official grading done at the mill or market level based on Indian Jute Standards:

Key parameters:
- **Strength** — tested by standard method
- **Fineness** — measured in denier
- **Colour** — assessed visually
- **Defects** — proportion of rotten, immature, or dirty fibre
- **Moisture content** — standardised

Pucca grades: **W (Fine), W (Good), W (Ordinary)** for white jute; similar for tossa.

---

### Diversification of Jute
Jute is being diversified beyond traditional sacking and bags into:
1. **Geotextiles** — erosion control, road construction
2. **Composites** — jute-reinforced plastic panels for cars
3. **Fashion textiles** — sarees, upholstery, bags
4. **Decorative fabrics** — carpets, curtains
5. **Pulp and paper** — newsprint and specialty paper
6. **Agrotextiles** — crop protection nets

---

### End Uses of Jute Fibre
- **Traditional:** Gunny bags, sacks, ropes, twines, hessian cloth
- **Modern:** Shopping bags, floor coverings, geotextiles
- **Industrial:** Packing material, tarpaulins
- **Home:** Carpets, rugs, curtains

## 8. Asbestos Fibre

### What is Asbestos? Mention the Source

**Asbestos** is a naturally occurring **mineral fibre** (silicate mineral) found in rock formations. It belongs to the serpentine and amphibole mineral groups. It is the only natural **mineral textile fibre**.

**Source:** Mined from metamorphic rocks — primarily found in:
- Canada (Quebec)
- Russia and Kazakhstan
- South Africa, China, Brazil
- India (Rajasthan, Andhra Pradesh)

**Types of Asbestos:**
1. **Chrysotile (White asbestos)** — most common; serpentine group; curly fibres
2. **Amosite (Brown asbestos)** — amphibole group
3. **Crocidolite (Blue asbestos)** — most hazardous; amphibole group
4. **Tremolite, Actinolite, Anthophyllite** — less common types

> ⚠️ **Health Warning:** Asbestos fibres cause **mesothelioma** (lung cancer), asbestosis, and lung disease. Its use is **banned or severely restricted** in most countries.

---

### Physical Properties of Asbestos Fibre

| Property | Details |
|----------|---------|
| **Colour** | White, grey, brown, or blue depending on type |
| **Lustre** | Silky to dull |
| **Length** | 1–50 mm (short to medium) |
| **Tenacity** | Low to moderate |
| **Thermal resistance** | Excellent — withstands 500–800°C or higher |
| **Fire resistance** | Non-flammable (naturally fireproof) |
| **Electrical resistance** | Good insulator |
| **Chemical resistance** | Resistant to most chemicals and acids |
| **Flexibility** | Poor — brittle compared to organic fibres |
| **Density** | 2.4–3.3 g/cm³ (much heavier than plant fibres) |
| **Moisture regain** | Very low |
| **Spinning ability** | Can be spun, especially chrysotile |

**End Uses (Historical):**
- Fireproof fabric, gloves, aprons
- Insulation material in buildings and pipes
- Brake pads and clutch facings
- Roofing materials, floor tiles

## 14. Wool Fibre

### What is Raw Wool?
**Raw wool** (also called **grease wool** or **fleece**) is the wool sheared from a sheep before any cleaning or processing. It contains:
- Wool fibre (~30–50%)
- **Lanolin** (natural grease/wax): 10–25%
- Suint (dried sweat salts): 5–10%
- Vegetable matter (grass, seeds, straw)
- Dirt and dust

Raw wool must be **scoured** (washed) to remove lanolin and impurities before spinning.

---

### Felting Properties of Wool in Wet and Dry Conditions

**Felting** is the irreversible matting and interlocking of wool fibres into a compact fabric due to the unique structure of wool scales.

**Why does wool felt?**
Wool fibres have **overlapping scales (corticular scales)** on their surface. Under agitation + moisture + heat, these scales lock together like hooks, and the fibres cannot separate. This is called the **Differential Friction Effect (DFE)**.

#### Felting in WET Conditions:
- Water **swells** the wool fibre (high moisture regain: 13–18%)
- Hot water + agitation causes scale interlocking rapidly
- Alkaline conditions (soap) speed up felting further
- Felting is **faster and more pronounced** in wet conditions
- This is why wool fabrics **must not be machine washed** (they shrink/felt)
- Wet felting is used commercially to make **felt fabric** (hats, padding)

#### Felting in DRY Conditions:
- Felting still occurs but is **much slower**
- Requires mechanical pressure and friction (not water)
- Scales do not swell/open as easily
- Used in **needlefelt** (needles mechanically interlock dry fibres)
- Industrial dry felting produces nonwovens for carpet backing, insulation

| Condition | Rate | Mechanism | Example |
|-----------|------|-----------|---------|
| Wet | Fast | Scale interlocking + swelling | Shrinkage in washing |
| Dry | Slow | Mechanical friction only | Needlefelt fabrics |

## 15. Silk & Sericulture

### Discuss Sericulture

**Sericulture** is the art and science of cultivating silkworms (*Bombyx mori*) for the production of silk fibre. It is one of the oldest textile crafts, originating in China ~4,500 years ago.

**Steps in Sericulture:**

#### 1. Mulberry Cultivation (Host Plant)
- Mulberry (*Morus alba*) leaves are the sole food for silkworms
- Requires well-drained soil, warm climate
- Leaves harvested at 35–40 days growth

#### 2. Egg Production (Seed Production)
- Female silkworm moths lay ~300–400 eggs
- Eggs are stored at controlled temperatures until hatching

#### 3. Silkworm Rearing (Larval Stage)
- Eggs hatch into **larvae (caterpillars)** after 10–14 days
- Larvae (silkworms) are fed fresh mulberry leaves **5–6 times daily**
- Duration: ~25–30 days
- Silkworms go through **4 moults (instars)** growing from 3mm to 7–8 cm

#### 4. Spinning the Cocoon
- Mature silkworm selects a spot and spins cocoon using **fibroin** (core protein) and **sericin** (gum-like coating)
- Cocoon formed in **2–3 days**; one cocoon = ~300–1500 metres of continuous filament

#### 5. Stifling (Pupa killing)
- Cocoons are heated (steam/hot air/sun-drying) at ~80°C to kill the pupa inside
- Prevents moth from breaking the silk filament

#### 6. Reeling
- Cocoons are softened in hot water (90–95°C) to dissolve sericin
- The **filament is located and unwound** from several cocoons simultaneously
- Multiple threads twisted together form a **raw silk thread (reeled silk)**

#### 7. Processing
- Raw silk is degummed, dyed, and woven into silk fabric

**Major Silk-Producing Countries:** China (70%), India, Japan, Korea, Brazil

**Types of Silk:**
- **Mulberry silk** — from *Bombyx mori* (finest quality)
- **Tasar silk** — from *Antheraea mylitta* (wild silkworm)
- **Eri silk** — from *Samia cynthia* (Assam, India)
- **Muga silk** — from *Antheraea assamensis* (Assam; golden colour)

## 16. Yarn Types

### Differentiate Between Woollen and Worsted Yarn

| Feature | Woollen Yarn | Worsted Yarn |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| **Fibre length** | Short staple wool (25–75 mm) | Long staple wool (75–150 mm+) |
| **Combing** | Not combed | Combed to remove short fibres and align |
| **Fibre arrangement** | Random, not parallel | Parallel and orderly |
| **Yarn texture** | Soft, fuzzy, bulky | Smooth, fine, compact |
| **Weight** | Heavier | Lighter for same thickness |
| **Lustre** | Low | High (due to parallel fibres) |
| **Warmth** | Warmer (more air trapped) | Less warm |
| **Durability** | Less durable | More durable |
| **Count range** | Coarser counts | Finer counts |
| **End uses** | Overcoats, blankets, tweeds, knitwear | Suits, dress fabric, gabardine, crepe |
| **Processing steps** | Carding → Spinning | Carding → Combing → Drawing → Spinning |

## 17. Cotton Grading

### What is Grading? Mention the American Grading System

**Grading** is the process of classifying cotton into different quality categories based on its physical properties. Proper grading ensures fair trade and correct industrial use.

**Factors assessed in grading:**
1. **Colour** — brightness and whiteness
2. **Trash content** — non-lint material
3. **Staple length** — measured in inches or mm
4. **Fibre strength** — grams per tex
5. **Micronaire (fineness)** — measured by air permeability
6. **Moisture content**

---

### American Grading System (USDA Standards)

The **USDA (United States Department of Agriculture)** established the official American Cotton Grading system:

#### Colour Grades (based on colour and trash):
| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Good Middling (GM)** | Bright white; very clean |
| **Strict Middling (SM)** | White; very slight spotted |
| **Middling (M)** | White; slight spotted — **benchmark/standard grade** |
| **Strict Low Middling (SLM)** | Slightly grey or spotted |
| **Low Middling (LM)** | Light spotted or tinged |
| **Strict Good Ordinary (SGO)** | Spotted or grey |
| **Good Ordinary (GO)** | Spotted, grey, or tinged |
| **Below Good Ordinary** | Inferior quality |

> **Middling** is the **base or universal standard** grade.

#### Leaf Grade:
Grades 1–7 based on the amount of leaf/trash content (1 = least trash).

#### USDA Official Standards:
- Use a combination of **colour grade + leaf grade** (e.g., "Middling, Leaf Grade 3")
- Modern grading uses **High Volume Instrument (HVI)** testing for objectivity

## ⚡ KEY POINTS TO REMEMBER FOR EXAM

1. **Cotton cross-section** = kidney/bean-shaped with central lumen
2. **Jute = bast fibre** because extracted from phloem of stem
3. **Lint** = long spinnable fibres; **Linters** = very short residual fibres after ginning
4. **Saw ginning** = faster, more neps, for short staple; **Roller ginning** = slower, fewer neps, for long staple
5. **Sericulture** = complete process of silk production from silkworm rearing to reeling
6. **Woollen** = short fibres, random, fuzzy, warm; **Worsted** = long fibres, combed, smooth, fine
7. **Cotton is stronger when wet** (same as flax and ramie)
8. **Kapok cannot be spun** — too short and smooth
9. **Asbestos** = only mineral textile fibre; classified as carcinogen; banned in many countries
10. **Felting** in wool is due to **Differential Friction Effect** — scales lock in wet + heat + agitation
11. **Middling** = standard benchmark grade in American cotton grading
12. **Jute retting** = microbial decomposition of pectin to free bast fibres
13. **PALF** = Pineapple Leaf Fibre; very fine and lustrous
14. **Coir** = from coconut husk; best natural water-resistant fibre
15. **Ramie** = strongest among bast fibres; also bacteriostatic
