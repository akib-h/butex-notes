Fibre Classification

A textile fibre must be long, fine, strong, flexible, and cohesive. Not all natural fibres qualify — see the full classification below.

🌿 Natural Fibre Classification — Schematic

✅ Requirements for Textile Fibre

  • Length ≥ 15 mm for spinning
  • Fineness (measured in microns)
  • Tenacity / tensile strength
  • Flexibility — bends without breaking
  • Cohesiveness — fibres cling together
  • Moisture absorption / dyeability
  • Chemical stability

🌿 Cellulosic Fibres

  • Base chemical: (C₆H₁₀O₅)ₙ
  • Stronger when wet
  • Attacked by acids
  • Resistant to alkalis
  • Biodegradable
  • Good moisture absorption
  • Examples: Cotton, Jute, Flax

🐑 Protein Fibres

  • Base: amino acid chains
  • Weaker when wet
  • Resistant to dilute acids
  • Damaged by strong alkalis
  • Felts under heat + agitation
  • Excellent moisture regain
  • Examples: Wool, Silk

Cotton & Ginning Machinery

Cotton is a seed fibre (~88–96% pure cellulose). Ginning is the mechanical process of separating seed cotton into lint fibres and seeds. Two main types: Saw Gin and Roller Gin.

Cotton Fibre — Cross Section & Longitudinal Structure

⚙ Ginning — Step by Step Process

Seed cotton → Drying / Pre-cleaning → Gin stand (Saw or Roller) → Fibre (lint) separated from seeds → Cleaning of lint → Bale pressing → Seeds sent for oil/meal extraction

✦ Saw Gin

  • Uses rotating saw-toothed cylinders
  • Saw teeth pull fibres through narrow slots
  • Seeds are too large to pass through
  • Faster — used for short/medium staple
  • Produces more neps (fibre entanglement)
  • ~90% of world ginning is saw ginning

✦ Roller Gin (McCarthy)

  • Leather roller rotates against stationary knife
  • Fibres gripped by roller, seeds held back by knife
  • Slower — used for long staple cotton
  • Fewer neps — better fibre quality
  • Used for Egyptian, Sea Island varieties
  • Higher fibre quality retention

Jute Extraction & Retting

Jute is a bast fibre obtained from the phloem (inner stem bark) of Corchorus capsularis (White jute) and C. olitorius (Tossa jute). The extraction process is called retting.

Jute Fibre Extraction — Process Flow

Types of Retting

  • Water retting — Submerged in slow rivers/ponds (most common)
  • Dew retting — Left on fields; slower, needs more land
  • Chemical retting — NaOH + enzymes; faster, controlled quality
  • Steam retting — High-pressure steam; industrial use

Jute Properties

  • Golden, silky lustre → "Golden Fibre"
  • High lignocellulosic content (lignin: 12–15%)
  • Poor elastic recovery — stiff
  • Good tensile strength
  • Poor moisture resistance (absorbs moisture)
  • Main use: Bags, sacking, rope, geotextiles

Bast Fibres — Flax, Hemp & Ramie

Bast fibres come from the phloem (inner bark) of plant stems. All share a similar extraction method involving retting → scutching → hackling. They are all cellulosic and stronger when wet.

Flax (Linen) — Processing Machinery

Flax / Linen — Bast Fibre Extraction Process

🌿 Flax (Linen)

  • Linum usitatissimum
  • Strongest when wet (gains 20%)
  • High lustre, cool to touch
  • Poor crease recovery
  • Good abrasion resistance
  • Used: Linen fabric, paper, nets

🌿 Hemp

  • Cannabis sativa
  • Coarser than flax
  • Very strong and durable
  • Rot-resistant, saltwater-resistant
  • Naturally antimicrobial
  • Used: Rope, canvas, sacking

🌿 Ramie

  • Boehmeria nivea
  • Longest and strongest bast fibre
  • Silky, high lustre
  • Bacteriostatic (resists bacteria)
  • Poor elasticity — brittle
  • Used: Industrial fabrics, mixed yarns

Coir, Sisal & Leaf Fibres

Coir is a fruit fibre from the coconut husk. Sisal and pineapple (PALF) are leaf fibres from Agave and Ananas plants respectively. All are coarse cellulosic fibres.

Coir Fibre — Extraction Process from Coconut Husk

🥥 Coir

  • From: Coconut (Cocos nucifera) husk
  • Fruit fibre — coarse and stiff
  • Best water-resistant natural fibre
  • High lignin (45%) — very resistant
  • Longest retting (6–12 months)
  • Uses: Doormats, geotextiles, rope

🌵 Sisal

  • From: Agave sisalana leaves
  • Hard leaf fibre
  • Stiff, strong, saltwater-resistant
  • Low moisture absorption
  • Leaf is decorticated (scraped)
  • Uses: Marine rope, mats, bags

🍍 Pineapple (PALF)

  • From: Ananas comosus leaves
  • Very fine and lustrous
  • Soft — can be woven into fine fabric
  • Called "Piña cloth" (Philippines)
  • Used in fashion and luxury textiles
  • Also used in composites

Wool Fibre Structure & Felting

Wool is a protein fibre (keratin) from sheep fleece. Its unique scale structure gives it the ability to felt — irreversibly interlocking under heat + moisture + agitation.

Wool Fibre — Cross Section & Scale Structure

💧 Wet Felting

  • Water swells fibre (13–18% MR)
  • Hot water + agitation + alkali (soap)
  • Scales open and interlock rapidly
  • FASTER — more pronounced
  • Why wool shrinks in washing machine
  • Used to make hat felt, padding

🔧 Dry Felting (Needlefelt)

  • No water — only mechanical pressure
  • Barbed needles push fibres through web
  • Scales interlock mechanically
  • SLOWER — less pronounced
  • Used for carpet backing, insulation
  • Industrial nonwoven production

Silk & Sericulture

Sericulture is the cultivation of silkworms (Bombyx mori) for silk production. One cocoon yields 300–1500 m of continuous filament — the only natural filament fibre.

Sericulture — Complete Process Flow

🪱 Bombyx mori Details

  • Sole food: Mulberry leaves (Morus alba)
  • 4 larval moults (instars)
  • Grows: 3mm → 7–8 cm
  • Cocoon spun in 2–3 days
  • Fibre: Fibroin (core) + Sericin (gum)
  • Raw silk: 5–8 cocoons reeled together

🧵 Types of Silk

  • Mulberry — Bombyx mori; finest quality
  • Tasar — Antheraea mylitta; wild silk
  • Eri — Samia cynthia; Assam, India
  • Muga — A. assamensis; golden colour
  • China = 70% of world production
  • Only natural filament fibre

Yarn Types — Woollen vs Worsted

Both woollen and worsted yarns are made from wool, but differ fundamentally in fibre length used, processing steps, and final properties. This is a key exam topic.
