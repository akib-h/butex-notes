## 1. Classification by Source / Animal Breed

Wool is classified by the animal from which it is obtained and the sheep breed:

**Major breeds of sheep:**

| Class | Breed | Origin | Staple Length | Fineness (µm) | Crimp | Uses |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Very Fine | Merino, Finest breeds | Australia, South Africa, USSR | 62–100 mm | 15–23 (finest) | Highly crimped | Comfort wear, baby wear |
| Fine | Rambouillet, Southdown, Corriedale | Argentina, Uruguay | 50–100 mm | 18–25 | Normal crimped | Fleece felts |
| Medium | Crossbred sheep | USA, South Africa, England | 38–50 mm | 27–31 | Medium crimped | Heavier, robust |
| Long & Coarse | Leicester, Longwool | USA, Canada, UK | 80–150 mm | 32–55 (medium coarse) | Fewer crimps | Good multi-purpose |
| Coarse | Lincoln, Romney, Karakul | Argentina, Uruguay, New Zealand, UK | >150 mm | >35 (coarse) | Low crimp, straight | Carpets, traditional |
| Medium | Cheviot | — | 75–125 mm | 27–33 | 50–56 crimps | Good for blending |

**Other animal wool sources:**

1. **Merino** — Extremely fine (17–25 µm), soft, comfortable, contains lanolin (antibacterial)
2. **Alpaca** — Soft, durable, luxurious, silky; naturally water-repellent; difficult to ignite
3. **Llama / Vicuna / Guanaco** — Live in Andes mountains of South America; shorn every two years; fine, soft, lightly crimped, very warm
4. **Camel** — Collected every year; fine, soft, lightly crimped; beige in colour; used for outerwear
5. **Cashmere** — From cashmere goat in Mongolia, Himalayas, Tibet, Nepal, Pakistan; most expensive hair fibre; very fine, soft, bright, lustrous
6. **Mohair** — Special type of Angora goat from Texas, South Africa, Turkey; shorn twice a year; longer, strong, lightly curled, silky lustre
7. **Angora Rabbit** — Farmed in Europe and East Asia; shorn up to 4 times per year; fine, very light, excellent at absorbing moisture vapour

## 2. Classification by Fibre Fineness & Breed

This is the most scientific classification. Fineness is measured in **microns (µm)**:

```
Very Fine  :  14–23 µm  →  Merino
Fine       :  18–25 µm  →  Rambouillet, Southdown
Medium     :  27–31 µm  →  Crossbred
Coarse     :  32–55 µm  →  Leicester, Lincoln
Carpet     :  >35 µm    →  Karakul, Romney
```

### Mathematical Relationship: Crimp vs Diameter

> As diameter increases, crimp frequency **decreases** (inverse relationship):

```
Fine wool   (14 µm)  → 100 crimps per 10 cm
Coarse wool (35 µm)  → 4 crimps per 10 cm
```

**Formula (Empirical):**
```
Crimps per unit length ∝ 1/diameter
```

This means: **the finer the wool, the more crimps, the softer the fabric**.

## 3. Classification by Fibre Extraction Method

This classification is based on **how** the wool is removed from the animal:

### i. Virgin Wool (New Wool / Lamb's Wool)
- Wool collected from a **lamb's first shearing** (6–8 months old)
- The **softest and finest** wool produced
- High quality; high price
- Also called **"lamb's wool"** specifically when from 7–9 month old lambs
- First fleece shorn/trimmed from a living, healthy animal

### ii. Recovered Wool
- Wool that has **already been made into yarn, fabric, or even worn garments** — then recycled
- Also called **"reclaimed wool"** or **"shoddy"**
- Formed by recycling process; 3 sub-types:

| Sub-type | Source | Fibre Damage |
|---|---|---|
| **Shoddy** | Fabric NOT excessively milled or felted during manufacture | Less damage |
| **Mungo** | Cloth that HAS been milled or felted during manufacture | More damage |
| **Extract** | Wool recovered from cotton or rayon blends by **carbonizing** | Variable |

**Carbonizing (for Extract):** Chemical process using H₂SO₄ to destroy cellulosic content, leaving only wool fibres.

### iii. All Wool
- Wool that **contains a proportion of recovered wool**
- Sometimes called "all wool" to indicate blended composition

### iv. Pulled Wool (Skin Wool)
- Wool pulled from the **pelt (skin) of slaughtered sheep** using various chemicals
- Fibres are of **low quality** → produce low-grade cloth

### v. Dead Wool (Fallen Wool)
- Wool obtained from **sheep that have died of age or accidentally killed**
- Decidedly **inferior in grade**; used in low-grade cloth

### vi. Cotty Wool
- Obtained from sheep **exposed to severe weather conditions**
- Severe weather hampers fleece quality → **poor grade, hard and brittle**

### vii. Taglocks
- **Torn, ragged, or discoloured parts** of a fleece
- Very **low quality**

### viii. Weathered Wool
- Collected from sheep **older than 14 months** after the first shearing
- Contains soil and dust; lower quality than lamb's wool

## 4. Classification by Fleece (Age of Sheep)

| Type | Age of Sheep | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Lamb's Wool** | 6–8 months | First fleece; softest, finest; very high quality |
| **Hogget Wool** | 12–14 months | First adult fleece; high strength; used for warp yarns |
| **Weathered Wool** | >14 months | After first shearing; contains soil & dust |
| **Pulled Wool** | Slaughtered | From pelt; low quality |
| **Dead Wool** | Died naturally | Inferior quality |

---

## 5. Classification by Fleece Grade

According to fleece type, wool is classified into **5 classes**:

1. **Fine / Merino Wool** → Class One Wool
2. **Medium** → Class Two Wool
3. **Crossbred** → Class Three Wool
4. **Long** → Class Four Wool
5. **Coarse (Carpet Wool)** → Class Five Wool
