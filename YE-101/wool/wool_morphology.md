## 1. Macro-structure of Wool

The macro-structure describes the **visible and measurable physical features** of the wool fibre.

### i. Size

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Length | 5–12 cm (common range) |
| Length (rare/coarse) | Up to 35 cm |
| Diameter (fine) | 14 µm |
| Diameter (coarse) | 35–45 µm |
| Slenderness ratio (fine, short) | 2500:1 |
| Slenderness ratio (coarse, long) | 7500:1 |

> Wool is classified as a **medium staple fibre** (5–12 cm preferred for versatile textile manufacturing).

**Slenderness Ratio Example:**
```
Fine Merino fibre:
  Length = 80 mm = 80,000 µm
  Diameter = 20 µm
  Slenderness ratio = 80,000 / 20 = 4000:1

Coarse Wool:
  Length = 150 mm = 150,000 µm
  Diameter = 40 µm
  Slenderness ratio = 150,000 / 40 = 3750:1
```

---

### ii. Shape

- **Longitudinal view:** Cylindrical shape with overlapping surface scales
- **Cross-sectional view:** **Round or oval** shape
- The fibre is **crimped** — not straight

---

### iii. Colour

- Natural colour: **Off-white or creamy**
- Cause of off-white colour: **Di-sulphide (S=S) bonds** in the keratin act as **chromophores** — they modify incident light so reflected light has a slight yellow tinge
- When wool is bleached → colour becomes **white**
- Cream to dark cream colour can result from **polymer degradation** at the surface (wool polymer is sensitive to atmospheric oxygen and air pollutants)

---

### iv. Surface

- Surface is **serrated / porous / scaly**
- Covered with **overlapping epithelial cells** (scales)
- Scales point **toward the tip** of the fibre
- This scaly surface causes **felting** and the **Directional Frictional Effect (DFE)**

```
ROOT END ─────────────────────► TIP END
          [scale][scale][scale]
          ◄ less friction (rootward direction)
          ► more friction (tipward direction)
```

---

### v. Crimp

**Crimp** = Natural waveness of wool fibre

| Wool Type | Diameter | Crimp Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Fine Merino | 14 µm | Up to 100 crimps per 10 cm |
| Coarse | 35 µm | Up to 4 crimps per 10 cm |

**Key relationship:**
```
As fibre diameter INCREASES → crimp per unit length DECREASES
(inverse proportionality)

Crimps per unit length ∝ 1/diameter
```

**Why crimp matters:**
- The crimped configuration **prevents fibres from aligning too closely** when spun
- Result: wool textile has **air spaces occupying ~2/3 of volume**
- The warmth of wool fabric is **more due to trapped air** than the fibre itself
- Crimp gives wool its **elasticity and resiliency**
- Best Merino wool: **30 waves per inch**

**Mathematical Example — Air Space in Wool:**
```
If total volume of wool fabric = 100 cm³
And air spaces = 2/3 of volume

Air space volume = (2/3) × 100 = 66.67 cm³
Fibre volume     = (1/3) × 100 = 33.33 cm³

This high air content is why wool is an excellent thermal insulator.
```

## 2. Micro-structure of Wool

Wool fibre grows from the **follicle of sheep's skin**. It is a highly complex skin tissue. It can be divided into **three sections**:

```
                    ┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
                    │  WOOL FIBRE CROSS-SECTION            │
                    │                                       │
                    │   ┌─────────────────────────────┐    │
                    │   │       CUTICLE               │    │
                    │   │  (Epicuticle/Exo/Endo)       │    │
                    │   │   ┌─────────────────────┐   │    │
                    │   │   │      CORTEX         │   │    │
                    │   │   │  ┌───────────────┐  │   │    │
                    │   │   │  │   MEDULLA     │  │   │    │
                    │   │   │  │  (if present) │  │   │    │
                    │   │   │  └───────────────┘  │   │    │
                    │   │   └─────────────────────┘   │    │
                    │   └─────────────────────────────┘    │
                    └─────────────────────────────────────┘
```

---

### i. Cuticle

The cuticle is the **layer of overlapping epithelial cells surrounding the wool fibre**. There are **3 sub-layers**:

#### a) Epicuticle (Outermost)
- **Waxy layer** — protective layer
- **Water repellent** (hydrophobic)
- Has **microscopic pores**
- The outermost cover of the wool fibre

#### b) Exocuticle (Middle)
- Formed by **overlapping epithelial cells**
- **Largely responsible for felting shrinkage**
- High in **cystine content** (sulphur-containing amino acid → forms di-sulphide cross-links)
- This high cross-linking = **greater chemical stability**

#### c) Endocuticle (Innermost cuticle layer)
- **Intermediate, cementing layer**
- Bonding between epithelial cell and cortex

---

### ii. Cortex

- Forms about **90% of the fibre volume**
- Consists of **spindle-shaped cortical cells** (countless long cells)
- Composed of **2 distinct sections:**

#### a) Ortho-cortex
- Contains **ortho cortical cells**
- **Less cystine** (fewer di-sulphide bonds)
- **Easier to dye** (absorbs more dye)
- **Less chemically and mechanically stable**

#### b) Para-cortex
- Contains **para cortical cells**
- **More cystine** (more di-sulphide bonds = S–S bonds)
- **Harder to dye** (less dye absorption)
- **More chemically and mechanically stable**
- More flexible, chemically and mechanically strong

```
Ortho-cortex:  less cystine → fewer S-S bonds → more dye uptake → easier to dye
Para-cortex:   more cystine → more S-S bonds  → less dye uptake → harder to dye
```

> Because of the difference in ortho and para cortex, wool fibre is called a **bicomponent fibre**. The ortho and para cortex **spiral around one another** — this helical spiralling is the source of wool's natural crimp at the molecular/cellular level.

**Fine wool fibres:** ~20 cortical cells across diameter
**Coarse wool fibres:** ~50 cortical cells across diameter

---

### iii. Medulla (Fibril)

- **Coarser fibres** have a **hollow space running lengthwise** through the center → this is the medulla
- Fine quality wool fibres generally **do not** have a medulla
- The presence of medulla reduces fibre strength and dyeability

---

## 3. Fibrillar Hierarchy

The cortical cells of wool fibre have a **hierarchical fibrillar structure**:

```
WOOL POLYMER (α-Keratin polymer)
       ↑ 3 polymers spiral together
PROTO-FIBRIL (~500 nm long, ~2 nm diameter)
       ↑ 11 proto-fibrils form each micro-fibril
MICRO-FIBRIL (~5 nm diameter)
       ↑ hundreds of micro-fibrils form each macro-fibril
MACRO-FIBRIL (100–200 nm diameter)
       ↑ numerous macro-fibrils + protein matrix = cortical cell
CORTICAL CELL (spindle-shaped)
       ↑ countless cortical cells = cortex
CORTEX (90% of fibre volume)
       ↑ + cuticle
WOOL FIBRE
```

**Specific dimensions:**
- Macro-fibril: 100–200 nm diameter
- Micro-fibril: ~5 nm diameter
- Proto-fibril: ~500 nm length, ~2 nm diameter
- Each micro-fibril: 11 proto-fibrils
- Each proto-fibril: 3 wool polymer (keratin) chains spiralling

> The **fibrillar and spiralling structure** within cortical cells contributes to **flexibility, elasticity, and durability** of wool fibre.

## 4. Felting — The DFE Principle

**Felting** = the **irreversible shrinkage** of the length, breadth, and thickness of the material.

**Cause:** The **serrated surface** of wool fibre formed by overlapping epithelial cells (exocuticle scales). Because of this serrated structure:

```
ROOT END ────────────────────────── TIP END
         [▲][▲][▲][▲][▲][▲][▲]
         scales point toward tip

→ Moving rootward: LESS friction (scales lay down flat)
→ Moving tipward:  MORE friction (scales catch and grip)
```

This difference in surface friction between the two directions is the **Directional Frictional Effect (DFE)**.

**Mechanism of Felting:**
1. During washing/mechanical agitation in aqueous solution
2. Fibre edges can only migrate in the **rootward direction** (DFE)
3. Migrated fibres, due to scale structure, **interlock with each other**
4. Fibres **cannot return** to original position
5. Result: **irreversible compaction** of fabric

**Effects of Felting:**
- Closes up fabric structure → more compact
- Increased rigidity
- Disadvantage for woollen goods requiring frequent laundering

**Felting enhanced by:**
- Heat
- Acid
- Alkali
- Mechanical agitation

## 5. Topography / Microscopic View

### Longitudinal View:
- Overlapping surface cell structure (epithelial cells / scales)
- Scales point toward tip of fibre
- Cylindrical appearance with surface irregularities

### Cross-sectional View:
- **Round or oval** shape
- Shows: Epidermis (cuticle) → Cortex → Medulla (if present)

```
Cross-section diagram:
```
