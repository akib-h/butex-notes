## Experiment No. 07 (0-1)

### Determination of the Focal Length of a Convex Lens by the Displacement Method

**Practical Date:** 10/03/2024 | **Submit Date:** 17/03/2024

---

### Theory

If the object and the image screen are placed on an optical bench such that the distance $D$ between them is greater than four times the focal length $f$ of a given convex lens, there will be two different positions of the lens for which an equally sharp image is obtained on the screen. Let these positions be $L_1$ and $L_2$, and let the displacement between them be:

$$x = L_1 - L_2$$

From the lens equation:

$$\frac{1}{v} - \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1}{f}$$

Since $u + v = D$:

$$\frac{1}{D-u} - \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1}{f}$$

Applying sign convention ($u$ is negative):

$$\frac{1}{D-u} + \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1}{f}$$

$$\Rightarrow u^2 - uD + Df = 0$$

Solving the quadratic equation gives two values of $u$ corresponding to the two lens positions:

$$u_1 = \frac{D}{2} - \frac{\sqrt{D^2 - 4Df}}{2} \quad \text{(position } L_1\text{)}$$

$$u_2 = \frac{D}{2} + \frac{\sqrt{D^2 - 4Df}}{2} \quad \text{(position } L_2\text{)}$$

Then:

$$x = L_1 - L_2 = u_1 - u_2 = \pm\sqrt{D^2 - 4Df}$$

$$\Rightarrow x^2 = D^2 - 4Df$$

$$\boxed{f = \frac{D^2 - x^2}{4D} \text{ cm}}$$

**Power of the lens:**

$$P = \frac{100}{f'(\text{cm})} \text{ dioptres}$$

where $f'$ = mean focal length.

**Symbols:**
- $f$ = focal length of the lens
- $D$ = distance between object and image screen
- $x$ = displacement of the lens between positions $L_1$ and $L_2$
- $P$ = power of the lens
- $f'$ = mean focal length

---

### Apparatus

Optical bench, image screen, convex lens, light source.

---

### Procedure

1. The approximate focal length of the lens was first determined by holding it in front of a distant bright lamp (or in sunlight) and obtaining a sharply focused image on a piece of paper.

2. The object and the image screen were placed on their respective stands at a distance somewhat greater (by ~5 cm) than four times the focal length. The object was placed near one end of the optical bench and kept fixed throughout.

3. The cross-wire of the object screen was illuminated. The lens was brought close to the object and gradually moved away until a real, inverted, and magnified image was sharply focused on the screen. This operation was repeated three times; the mean of the three readings gave position $L_1$.

4. The lens was moved further away from the object until another sharply defined, real, inverted, but reduced image appeared on the screen — this gave position $L_2$.

5. The positions of the object and image screen were noted. Their difference gave the apparent distance $D$; the distance $L_1 - L_2$ gave the displacement $x$.

6. The whole operation was repeated at least 5 times with varying values of $D$.

7. $f$ was calculated for each set of data; the mean $f'$ was determined, and the power $P$ was calculated from $f'$.

### Calculation

$$f_1 = \frac{D_1^2 - x_1^2}{4D_1} = \frac{(89)^2 - (17.4)^2}{4 \times 89} = 21.396 \text{ cm}$$

$$f_2 = \frac{(90)^2 - (17.5)^2}{4 \times 90} = 21.645 \text{ cm}$$

$$f_3 = \frac{(92)^2 - (19.1)^2}{4 \times 92} = 22.009 \text{ cm}$$

$$f_4 = \frac{(94)^2 - (22.5)^2}{4 \times 94} = 22.154 \text{ cm}$$

$$f_5 = \frac{(98)^2 - (30.4)^2}{4 \times 98} = 22.142 \text{ cm}$$

**Mean focal length:**

$$f' = \frac{21.396 + 21.645 + 22.009 + 22.154 + 22.142}{5} = 21.8692 \text{ cm}$$

**Power of the lens:**

$$P = \frac{100}{f'} = \frac{100}{21.8692} = 4.573 \text{ dioptres}$$

**Percentage of Error:**

$$\% \text{ Error} = \left|\frac{21.8692 - 20}{20}\right| \times 100\% = 9.346\%$$

---

### Result

The focal length of the given convex lens is **21.8692 cm** with a **9.346% error**. The power of the lens is **4.573 dioptres**.

---

### Discussion

1. The formula $f = \dfrac{D^2 - x^2}{4D}$ is valid only when $D > 4f$, since the value of $x$ diminishes with $D$ and becomes zero when $D = 4f$. On the other hand, $D$ should not be made too large.

2. The value of $D$ should be increased in steps of 4–5 cm, since a small change in $D$ causes a large change in $x$.
