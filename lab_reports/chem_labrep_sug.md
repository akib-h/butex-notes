---

#### 🔬 Structural Details

**Lead Acetate — Pb(CH₃COO)₂ · 3H₂O**
```
      CH₃                CH₃
       |                  |
   C=O  →  Pb²⁺  ←  O=C
       |                  |
      O⁻                O⁻
```
> Lead acetate is also called "sugar of lead" due to its sweet taste — **extremely toxic; do not ingest.**

---

**Ferrous Sulphate — FeSO₄ · 7H₂O**

The hydrated salt has an octahedral coordination geometry:

```
         [Fe(H₂O)₆]²⁺  ·  SO₄²⁻  ·  H₂O
```

- Fe²⁺ is coordinated by 6 water molecules
- One additional water molecule is present in the lattice
- On heating, loses water stepwise: 7H₂O → 4H₂O → H₂O → anhydrous FeSO₄

---

**Mohr's Salt — (NH₄)₂Fe(SO₄)₂ · 6H₂O**

> Mohr's salt is preferred over FeSO₄ in volumetric analysis because it is **more stable** in air — Fe²⁺ in FeSO₄ oxidises readily to Fe³⁺, while Mohr's salt resists aerial oxidation, giving more accurate titration results.

---

**Chrome Alum — KCr(SO₄)₂ · 12H₂O**

General formula for alums: **M⁺M³⁺(SO₄)₂ · 12H₂O**

Where:
- M⁺ = monovalent cation (K⁺)
- M³⁺ = trivalent cation (Cr³⁺)

### Part (2) — Preparation and Standardization of 0.1 M HCl Solution

#### ⚗️ Background

Hydrochloric acid (HCl, M = 36.46 g/mol) is a **secondary standard** — concentrated HCl is volatile and its exact concentration varies between batches. Therefore, approximate solutions must be **standardized** against a primary standard.

---

#### 📐 Step 1: Calculate the Volume of Concentrated HCl Required

**Given:**
- Density of concentrated HCl ≈ 1.18 g/mL
- Assay (purity) ≈ 35–38% w/w (use 37% for calculation)
- Molarity of concentrated HCl:

$$M = \frac{1000 \times \rho \times \text{Assay\%}}{M_r} = \frac{1000 \times 1.18 \times 0.37}{36.46} \approx 11.97 \text{ M}$$

**To prepare 1 litre of 0.1 M HCl:**

Using the dilution formula: $C_1V_1 = C_2V_2$

$$V_1 = \frac{C_2 \times V_2}{C_1} = \frac{0.1 \times 1000}{11.97} \approx 8.35 \text{ mL}$$

---

#### 🧫 Step 2: Preparation Procedure

**Materials Required:**
- Concentrated HCl (37%, density 1.18 g/mL)
- 1000 mL volumetric flask
- Distilled water (freshly boiled and cooled)
- Fume hood
- Measuring cylinder / pipette

**Procedure:**

1. Take approximately **800 mL of distilled water** in a 1000 mL volumetric flask.
2. In a fume hood, carefully measure **8.35 mL of concentrated HCl** using a pipette or measuring cylinder.
3. Add the acid **slowly to the water** (never water to acid — exothermic reaction!).
4. Mix well and allow to cool to room temperature.
5. Make up the volume to **1000 mL** with distilled water.
6. Stopper and mix thoroughly by inversion several times.
7. Label the bottle as **"~0.1 M HCl (Unstandardized)"**.

> ⚠️ **Safety:** Always work in a fume hood. Wear gloves, lab coat, and eye protection. Add acid to water, not water to acid.

---

#### 📏 Step 3: Standardization of 0.1 M HCl

##### Primary Standard Used: **Anhydrous Sodium Carbonate (Na₂CO₃)**

**Why Na₂CO₃?**
- Anhydrous Na₂CO₃ (M = 105.99 g/mol) is a primary standard — it is available in high purity, is stable, non-hygroscopic when dried, and has a high molar mass giving low weighing error.

**Reaction:**

$$\text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3 + 2\text{HCl} \longrightarrow 2\text{NaCl} + \text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{CO}_2 \uparrow$$

**Indicator:** Methyl Orange (colour change: yellow → orange-red at pH ~4)
*Alternatively:* Mixed indicator (methyl red + bromocresol green): grey-green → pink

---

##### Standardization Procedure:

**Preparation of Primary Standard:**
1. Dry anhydrous Na₂CO₃ in an oven at **250–300°C for 1–2 hours** to remove any absorbed moisture.
2. Cool in a desiccator.
3. Accurately weigh approximately **0.5300 g** (≈ 5 millimoles) of anhydrous Na₂CO₃ on an analytical balance (record to 4 decimal places).
4. Dissolve in ~50 mL distilled water in a 250 mL conical flask.

**Titration:**
1. Fill a burette with the ~0.1 M HCl solution. Note the initial burette reading.
2. Add **2–3 drops of methyl orange** indicator to the Na₂CO₃ solution.
3. Titrate with HCl until the solution turns from **yellow to orange-red** (endpoint).
4. Record the volume of HCl used (V mL).
5. Repeat the titration at least **three times** until concordant results (within ±0.1 mL) are obtained.

**Calculation of Exact Molarity:**

$$\text{Moles of Na}_2\text{CO}_3 = \frac{\text{mass (g)}}{105.99 \text{ g/mol}}$$

$$\text{Moles of HCl} = 2 \times \text{Moles of Na}_2\text{CO}_3$$

$$\text{Molarity of HCl} = \frac{\text{Moles of HCl}}{\text{Volume of HCl (L)}}$$

**Example Calculation:**

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Mass of Na₂CO₃ | 0.5300 g |
| Moles of Na₂CO₃ | 0.5300 / 105.99 = 0.005000 mol |
| Moles of HCl | 2 × 0.005000 = 0.01000 mol |
| Volume of HCl used | 99.5 mL = 0.0995 L |
| **Exact Molarity of HCl** | **0.01000 / 0.0995 = 0.1005 M** |

---

##### Concordant Results Table (Sample):

| Titration | Initial Burette (mL) | Final Burette (mL) | Volume HCl (mL) |
|-----------|----------------------|--------------------|-----------------|
| 1 (rough) | 0.00 | 10.20 | 10.20 |
| 2 | 0.00 | 9.95 | 9.95 |
| 3 | 0.00 | 9.97 | 9.97 |
| 4 | 0.00 | 9.96 | 9.96 |
| **Mean** | — | — | **9.96** |

> ✅ Titrations 2, 3, and 4 are concordant (within ±0.02 mL). Titration 1 (rough) is excluded.

---

#### 📊 HCl Standardization Flowchart

```
  Concentrated HCl (37%, d = 1.18 g/mL)
           │
           │  Calculate: C₁V₁ = C₂V₂
           │  V₁ = 8.35 mL per litre
           ▼
```

### Part (2) — Preparation and Standardization of 0.1 M NaOH Solution

#### ⚗️ Background

Sodium hydroxide (NaOH, M = 40.00 g/mol) is a **secondary standard**. It cannot be used directly as a primary standard because:

- NaOH is **hygroscopic** — absorbs moisture from air
- It absorbs atmospheric **CO₂**, forming Na₂CO₃:

$$2\text{NaOH} + \text{CO}_2 \longrightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3 + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$

- Commercial NaOH pellets/flakes contain impurities (Na₂CO₃, NaCl, water)

Therefore, NaOH solutions must be **standardized** against a suitable primary standard.

---

#### 📐 Step 1: Calculate the Mass of NaOH Required

**To prepare 1 litre of 0.1 M NaOH:**

$$\text{Mass} = M \times V \times M_r = 0.1 \times 1 \times 40.00 = 4.00 \text{ g}$$

Since NaOH is impure, weigh approximately **4.2–4.5 g** to compensate for impurities.

---

#### 🧫 Step 2: Preparation Procedure

**Materials Required:**
- NaOH pellets (analytical grade)
- 1000 mL volumetric flask
- CO₂-free distilled water (freshly boiled and cooled)
- Polypropylene (plastic) storage bottle — NaOH attacks glass!
- Analytical balance

**Procedure:**

1. **Prepare CO₂-free water:** Boil distilled water for 15–20 minutes. Cool under CO₂-free conditions (seal with NaOH guard tube or use nitrogen blanket). This prevents absorption of CO₂ into NaOH solution.
2. Quickly weigh approximately **4.2 g of NaOH pellets** on an analytical balance (weigh rapidly — NaOH absorbs moisture).
3. Dissolve the pellets in ~200 mL of CO₂-free distilled water in a beaker. The dissolution is **highly exothermic** — allow to cool.
4. Transfer quantitatively to a **1000 mL volumetric flask**.
5. Make up to the mark with CO₂-free distilled water.
6. Stopper and invert several times to mix.
7. Store in a **plastic (polypropylene) bottle** fitted with a soda lime guard tube.
8. Label as **"~0.1 M NaOH (Unstandardized)"**.

> ⚠️ **Safety:** NaOH is corrosive. Wear gloves and eye protection. Dissolution is exothermic — cool before transferring to volumetric flask.
>
> ⚠️ **Note:** Do not store NaOH in glass-stoppered bottles — NaOH etches glass and seals the stopper.

---

#### 📏 Step 3: Standardization of 0.1 M NaOH

Two primary standards are commonly used:

---

##### Option A — Primary Standard: **Oxalic Acid (H₂C₂O₄ · 2H₂O)**

- M = 126.07 g/mol
- Stable, crystalline, easily dried, high purity available

**Reaction:**

$$\text{H}_2\text{C}_2\text{O}_4 + 2\text{NaOH} \longrightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{C}_2\text{O}_4 + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}$$

**Indicator:** Phenolphthalein (colourless → pale pink, endpoint at pH ~8.3–10)

**Procedure:**
1. Dry oxalic acid crystals at **60°C** (not higher — decomposes above 70°C). Cool in desiccator.
2. Accurately weigh approximately **0.6303 g** (≈ 5 millimoles) of oxalic acid.
3. Dissolve in ~50 mL of distilled water in a 250 mL conical flask.
4. Add **2–3 drops of phenolphthalein** indicator.
5. Titrate with ~0.1 M NaOH from a burette until a **pale pink colour** persists for 30 seconds.
6. Record the volume. Repeat for concordant results.

**Calculation:**

$$\text{Molarity of NaOH} = \frac{2 \times \text{moles of oxalic acid}}{\text{volume of NaOH (L)}}$$

$$= \frac{2 \times \frac{0.6303}{126.07}}{V_{NaOH} \text{ (L)}}$$

---

##### Option B — Primary Standard: **Potassium Hydrogen Phthalate (KHP, KHC₈H₄O₄)**

- M = 204.22 g/mol *(high molar mass = lower weighing error)*
- Excellent primary standard: stable, non-hygroscopic, high purity

**Reaction:**

$$\text{KHC}_8\text{H}_4\text{O}_4 + \text{NaOH} \longrightarrow \text{KNaC}_8\text{H}_4\text{O}_4 + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$

(1:1 mole ratio)

**Indicator:** Phenolphthalein

**Procedure:**
1. Dry KHP at **110–120°C for 2 hours**. Cool in desiccator.
2. Accurately weigh approximately **0.8000–1.0000 g** of KHP.
3. Dissolve in 50 mL CO₂-free distilled water.
4. Add 2 drops of phenolphthalein indicator.
5. Titrate with NaOH to a **faint permanent pink endpoint**.
6. Repeat titrations for concordant results.

**Calculation:**

$$\text{Molarity of NaOH} = \frac{\text{mass of KHP (g)}}{204.22 \times V_{NaOH} \text{ (L)}}$$

---

### Practice Q2 — Preparation of 0.1 M H₂SO₄

**Question:** How would you prepare 250 mL of 0.1 M H₂SO₄ from concentrated sulphuric acid (density = 1.84 g/mL, 98% w/w)?

**Solution:**

**Step 1: Molarity of concentrated H₂SO₄**

$$M = \frac{1000 \times 1.84 \times 0.98}{98.07} = 18.38 \text{ M}$$

**Step 2: Volume required (C₁V₁ = C₂V₂)**

$$V_1 = \frac{0.1 \times 250}{18.38} = 1.36 \text{ mL}$$

**Step 3: Procedure**
1. Take ~200 mL distilled water in a 250 mL volumetric flask.
2. Carefully add **1.36 mL of concentrated H₂SO₄** to the water (always acid to water!).
3. Allow to cool. Make up to 250 mL.

> ⚠️ Concentrated H₂SO₄ addition to water is **highly exothermic**. Never add water to acid!

### Practice Q3 — Standardization Calculation

**Question:** 0.4800 g of anhydrous Na₂CO₃ was dissolved in water and titrated against HCl solution. It required 45.20 mL of HCl to reach the endpoint with methyl orange indicator. Calculate the exact molarity of the HCl.

**Solution:**

**Reaction:** Na₂CO₃ + 2HCl → 2NaCl + H₂O + CO₂

$$\text{Moles of Na}_2\text{CO}_3 = \frac{0.4800}{105.99} = 4.529 \times 10^{-3} \text{ mol}$$

$$\text{Moles of HCl} = 2 \times 4.529 \times 10^{-3} = 9.058 \times 10^{-3} \text{ mol}$$

$$\text{Molarity of HCl} = \frac{9.058 \times 10^{-3}}{45.20 \times 10^{-3}} = \boxed{0.2004 \text{ M}}$$

### Practice Q4 — Standardization of NaOH using KHP

**Question:** 0.9163 g of KHP (M = 204.22 g/mol) was titrated with NaOH solution. It required 44.80 mL of NaOH. Calculate: (a) the molarity of NaOH, and (b) the mass of NaOH per litre.

**Solution:**

**Reaction:** KHC₈H₄O₄ + NaOH → KNaC₈H₄O₄ + H₂O (1:1 molar ratio)

**(a) Molarity:**

$$\text{Moles of KHP} = \frac{0.9163}{204.22} = 4.487 \times 10^{-3} \text{ mol}$$

$$\text{Moles of NaOH} = 4.487 \times 10^{-3} \text{ mol}$$

$$\text{Molarity of NaOH} = \frac{4.487 \times 10^{-3}}{44.80 \times 10^{-3}} = \boxed{0.1002 \text{ M}}$$

**(b) Mass of NaOH per litre:**

$$\text{Mass} = 0.1002 \times 40.00 = \boxed{4.008 \text{ g/L}}$$

### Practice Q5 — Error Analysis

**Question:** A student prepared 0.1 M NaOH but did not use CO₂-free water. After standardization, the molarity was found to be 0.0985 M instead of 0.1000 M. Explain the source of error and calculate the percentage error.

**Solution:**

**Source of Error:**
CO₂ dissolved in water reacts with NaOH:

$$2\text{NaOH} + \text{CO}_2 \longrightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3 + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$

This consumes NaOH, reducing its effective concentration. Na₂CO₃ in the NaOH solution also causes a **double endpoint** problem with phenolphthalein, as Na₂CO₃ is a weaker base and only partially neutralized at the phenolphthalein endpoint.

**Percentage Error:**

$$\% \text{ Error} = \frac{|0.1000 - 0.0985|}{0.1000} \times 100 = \boxed{1.5\%}$$

### Practice Q6 — Primary vs Secondary Standards

**Question:** Distinguish between primary and secondary standards, giving two examples of each used in acid-base titrimetry.

**Solution:**

| Feature | Primary Standard | Secondary Standard |
|---------|------------------|--------------------|
| Purity | Very high (≥99.9%) | May contain impurities |
| Stability | Stable in air and storage | May degrade (absorb H₂O, CO₂) |
| Molar mass | Preferably high (reduces weighing error) | Variable |
| Can be weighed directly? | Yes | No — must be standardized |
| Examples (acid-base) | Na₂CO₃, KHP, oxalic acid, KIO₃ | HCl, H₂SO₄, NaOH, KOH |

**Properties of an ideal primary standard (mnemonic — PASH):**
- **P**urity — ≥99.9%
- **A**vailability — easily obtainable at low cost
- **S**tability — in solid state and in solution
- **H**igh molar mass — reduces relative weighing error

### Practice Q7 — Indicator Selection

**Question:** Why is methyl orange used for HCl/Na₂CO₃ titrations but phenolphthalein for NaOH/oxalic acid titrations?

**Solution:**

**Methyl Orange (pH range: 3.1–4.4; yellow to red):**
- The product of HCl + Na₂CO₃ neutralization is NaCl + CO₂ + H₂O
- The solution at equivalence point contains dissolved CO₂ → mildly **acidic** (pH ~3.8)
- Methyl orange changes colour in this acidic range → suitable endpoint

**Phenolphthalein (pH range: 8.2–10.0; colourless to pink):**
- The product of NaOH + oxalic acid is Na₂C₂O₄ (sodium oxalate) + H₂O
- Na₂C₂O₄ is the salt of a weak acid + strong base → **alkaline** at equivalence point (pH ~8.7)
- Phenolphthalein changes colour in this alkaline range → suitable endpoint

> 🔑 **Key Rule:** Choose an indicator whose colour change pH range encompasses the equivalence point pH of the specific titration.
